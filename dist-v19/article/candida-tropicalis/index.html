Candida Tropicalis

Candida tropicalis is an opportunistic fungal pathogen and occasional gut commensal. It is the second or third most common Candida species causing invasive candidiasis, after C. albicans and C. glabrata. Within the gut mycobiome, C. tropicalis has been specifically associated with Crohn's disease, where it participates in interkingdom biofilm formation with bacterial pathogens.

Metal Dependencies

Like other Candida species, C. tropicalis relies on zinc, iron, and copper for growth and virulence. It expresses secreted aspartyl proteases (SAPs) and other zinc-dependent enzymes involved in host tissue invasion and immune evasion. Iron acquisition via siderophore-like systems is essential for its growth in the host environment.

Key Associations: Crohn's Disease

C. tropicalis is one of the most-cited fungi in the Crohn's disease mycobiome literature. Key findings:

  • Elevated in Crohn's disease — mycobiome studies consistently find elevated C. tropicalis abundance in CD patients compared to healthy controls
  • Interkingdom biofilm with Serratia marcescens and E. coli — C. tropicalis forms triple-species biofilms with Serratia marcescens and adherent-invasive E. coli (AIEC) that are more pathogenic and harder to eradicate than any single species alone; this is a documented example of functional shielding
  • *Anti-Saccharomyces cerevisiae antibody (ASCA) connection — ASCA, a diagnostic marker for Crohn's disease, cross-reacts with C. tropicalis cell wall antigens, suggesting C. tropicalis* may be an actual ASCA antigen in some CD patients
  • Family clustering — Crohn's patients and their healthy relatives both show elevated C. tropicalis, suggesting a genetic or environmental susceptibility to this organism

Virulence Features

  • Biofilm formation — strong biofilm former on mucosal surfaces and medical devices
  • Secreted aspartyl proteases — degrade host proteins and extracellular matrix, facilitating invasion
  • Phenotypic switching — shifts between yeast and pseudohyphal forms in response to environmental signals (pH, temperature, nutrient availability)
  • Iron scavenging — expresses multiple iron acquisition systems; competes effectively with bacteria for luminal iron
