Amyloid Beta

Amyloid-beta (Aβ) is a peptide derived from the proteolytic cleavage of amyloid precursor protein (APP), predominantly 40–42 amino acids in length. Its aggregation into extracellular plaques in the brain is the defining neuropathological feature of Alzheimer's disease. However, amyloid-beta is also a normal physiological peptide with antimicrobial functions — making its relationship to infection and the gut microbiome mechanistically significant.

Normal Function

Amyloid-beta is not intrinsically pathological. In physiological concentrations it functions as:

  • An antimicrobial peptide (AMP) — Aβ42 has documented activity against bacteria, fungi, and viruses; it appears to trap pathogens in fibrillar nets similar to neutrophil extracellular traps (NETs)
  • A metal-binding peptide — Aβ binds zinc and copper with high affinity; this may serve a protective role sequestering metals from pathogens (fitting the nutritional immunity framework)
  • A synaptic regulator — at low concentrations, Aβ modulates synaptic transmission and memory consolidation

Metal-Driven Aggregation

The metallomic signature of Alzheimer's disease (elevated brain iron, copper, and zinc in plaques) converges directly on amyloid-beta:

  • Zinc and copper bind to Aβ, promoting its aggregation from soluble monomers into toxic oligomers and insoluble fibrils
  • Iron catalyzes oxidative damage via Fenton chemistry at the Aβ plaque surface, generating hydroxyl radicals that damage surrounding neurons
  • Mis-metallation — when toxic metals (lead, cadmium) occupy the copper- and zinc-binding sites on Aβ, they alter its conformation and aggregation kinetics, potentially accelerating plaque formation (mis metallation)

The Infection Hypothesis

A growing body of evidence suggests that amyloid-beta plaque formation may be a misdirected antimicrobial response:

  • Aβ production is upregulated in response to bacterial LPS, viral infection, and fungal exposure
  • Brain infections (HSV-1, H. pylori, bacterial translocation across a disrupted blood-brain barrier) may trigger Aβ overproduction as an innate immune defense
  • The gut-brain axis connects gut dysbiosis to brain Aβ burden — systemic LPS from Gram-negative gut pathobionts (E. coli, H. pylori) stimulates neuroinflammation and may drive chronic Aβ overproduction

This positions amyloid-beta accumulation not as a primary cause of AD but as a chronic innate immune response to gut-derived pathogenic stimuli, where the antimicrobial defense becomes pathological through overactivation.

Gut Microbiome Connection

  • Germ-free mice show reduced Aβ plaque burden; conventionalization with dysbiotic microbiota increases it
  • Specific gut bacteria (E. coli K12 expressing curli fibers, Salmonella) produce functional amyloids that can cross-seed mammalian amyloid aggregation via the gut-brain axis
  • helicobacter pylori infection (enriched in AD signatures) is associated with elevated serum amyloid and increased AD risk in epidemiological studies
