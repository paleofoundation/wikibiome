## Entities (95)

### Metals & Metalloids (17)

| Entity | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| [[nickel]] | Central entity of the wiki. Group 1 carcinogen, most common contact allergen, essential cofactor for pathogen virulence (H. pylori urease/hydrogenase), and emerging cardiometabolic concern. |
| [[arsenic]] | Metalloid carcinogen acting through proliferative signaling, SAM depletion, and DNA repair inhibition rather than direct DNA damage. |
| [[chromium]] | Cr(VI) is the genotoxic carcinogen of the trio: Cr-DNA adducts, mismatch repair inhibition, and the ascorbate reduction paradox. |
| [[cadmium]] | Purely toxic heavy metal; metalloestrogen, nephrotoxin, and the best-studied model for probiotic gut-barrier protection against metal toxicity. |
| [[lead]] | Purely toxic; exerts harm through calcium mimicry, heme biosynthesis disruption, and oxidative stress. Neurodevelopmental damage persists at levels once deemed safe. |
| [[mercury]] | Most toxic heavy metal by weight. Methylmercury binds thiol groups, depletes glutathione, and crosses both blood-brain and placental barriers. |
| [[copper]] | Essential but dual-natured: elevated in nearly every disease state in this wiki (PCOS, breast cancer, RA, AMI) yet depleted in neurodegenerative brain tissue. |
| [[zinc]] | Most widely utilized metal cofactor in biology (~10% of the proteome). Deficiency is a remarkably consistent feature across cancer, PCOS, ASD, PPD, and IBD. |
| [[iron]] | Most abundant transition metal in the body. Drives ferroptosis via Fenton chemistry; the hepcidin-ferroportin axis is central to nutritional immunity. |
| [[manganese]] | Essential cofactor for Mn-SOD (SOD2) with a narrow therapeutic window; excess causes manganism, a well-characterized parkinsonism-like syndrome. |
| [[aluminum]] | Non-essential metal with no known biological role. Neurotoxic at elevated doses; major contaminant in infant formulas and baby foods. |
| [[selenium]] | Primarily protective: backbone of the selenoproteome (GPx, TrxR, deiodinases). Significance lies more in consequences of deficiency than excess. Highest organ concentration in thyroid. |
| [[magnesium]] | Essential macromineral cofactor for 600+ enzymatic reactions. Deficiency linked to insulin resistance, PPD, cardiovascular risk, and muscle cramps. |
| [[cobalt]] | Essential as the core of vitamin B12 (cobalamin); excess is toxic. Relevant to prosthetic joint metallosis and B12-dependent microbial pathways. |
| [[tin]] | Low inherent toxicity as inorganic Sn, but organotin compounds (tributyltin) are potent endocrine disruptors. Acute exposure from canned foods. |
| [[molybdenum]] | Essential trace element as cofactor for xanthine oxidase, sulfite oxidase, and aldehyde oxidase. Molybdopterin cofactor in some bacterial sulfate reductases. |

## Concepts (44)

| Concept | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| [[oxidative-stress]] | Unifying mechanism across all metal toxicity: ROS generation via Fenton chemistry, glutathione depletion, and SOD/catalase inhibition. |
| [[metal-carcinogenesis]] | Three paradigms -- epigenetic (Ni), proliferative (As), genotoxic (Cr) -- illustrating diversity of metal-induced cancer mechanisms. |
| [[epigenetic-modifications]] | Primary carcinogenic mechanism for Ni and As: DNA hypermethylation, histone H3K9 dimethylation, and gene silencing without direct DNA damage. |
| [[DNA-damage]] | Primary Cr(VI) mechanism (Cr-DNA adducts, crosslinks, MMR inhibition); notably absent for Ni and As. |
| [[hypoxic-signaling]] | HIF-1a stabilization under normoxic conditions -- a hallmark unique to Ni carcinogenesis, driving VEGF and glycolytic shift. |
| [[ferroptosis]] | Iron-dependent regulated cell death via lipid peroxide accumulation when GPX4 fails. Links iron overload to neurodegeneration, cancer, and gut pathology. |
| [[mis-metallation]] | Displacement of correct metal cofactors by toxic metals ("biochemical identity theft") -- fundamental mechanism of metal toxicity. |
| [[metalloestrogens]] | Metals (especially Cd, Ni) that activate ERa and/or GPR30 to mimic estradiol, linking metal exposure to breast cancer, PCOS, and endometriosis. |
| [[metallomics]] | Systematic multi-element profiling (ICP-MS) of biological specimens as disease biomarkers across plasma, urine, brain tissue, and tumors. |
| [[gut-metal-microbiome]] | Bidirectional interaction: metals reshape microbial communities while microbiota modulates metal absorption, speciation, and toxicity. |
| [[nutritional-immunity]] | Host strategy of withholding essential metals from pathogens. Well-established for Fe and Zn; underexplored for Ni. |
| [[nickel-allergy]] | Most frequent contact allergen worldwide. Type IV hypersensitivity via TLR-4. Lifelong once sensitized; triggered by skin and dietary exposure. |
| [[dietary-nickel-exposure]] | Food is the primary non-occupational Ni exposure route. Children and nickel-sensitized individuals are highest-risk groups; TDI often exceeded. |
| [[nickel-neurotoxicity]] | Chronic Ni exposure causes anxiety, depression, and memory impairment through hippocampal oxidative stress and neurotransmitter disruption. |
| [[nickel-reproductive-toxicity]] | Maternal Ni exposure linked to congenital heart defects, placental barrier disruption, and fetal tissue accumulation. |
| [[metabolic-syndrome]] | Emerging Ni-MetS link via NHANES epidemiology (blood pressure, fasting glucose, lipids), though findings remain inconclusive at low doses. |
| [[environmental-metal-exposure]] | How toxic metals reach people: dietary ingestion, occupational inhalation, consumer products, soil/fertilizer contamination, and air pollution. |
| [[metal-dependent-virulence]] | How Ni/Fe/Zn/Cu/Mn serve as essential cofactors for pathogen virulence factors. Environmental metals tip the host-pathogen balance. |
| [[siderophores-metallophores]] | Small molecules pathogens secrete to scavenge metals: iron siderophores and emerging nickelophores (staphylopine, pseudopaline, yersiniabactin). |
| [[matrix-metalloproteases]] | Zn-dependent endopeptidases for ECM degradation. Bridges pathogen tissue invasion and host disease (cancer, endometriosis, RA, neurodegeneration). |
| [[inter-kingdom-metal-shielding]] | Metal-mediated cooperation in polymicrobial communities: biofilm metal dynamics, siderophore sharing, Candida-bacteria interactions. |
| [[pathogen-metal-acquisition]] | Molecular machinery for metal import (ABC/NiCoT/TonB), storage (Hpn/ferritins), regulation (NikR/Fur/Zur), and export (CznABC/CzcCBA). |
| [[urease]] | Ni-metalloenzyme (urea to ammonia + CO2). Most widespread Ni-dependent virulence factor: 14+ pathogens use it. Nickel-free diet doubles H. pylori eradication. |
| [[hydrogenase]] | [NiFe] hydrogenases power pathogen energy metabolism. In H. pylori, drives CagA translocation. S. Typhimurium triple mutant 100% avirulent. |
| [[glyoxalase]] | Ni-dependent GloI detoxifies methylglyoxal in pathogens. Human GloI uses Zn -- this metal selectivity divide makes pathogen GloI a selective drug target. |
| [[biofilm]] | Structured microbial communities in extracellular matrix. Metal-dependent: require Zn, Mn, Fe for structural integrity. Catheter encrustation, chronic wounds. |
| [[calprotectin]] | S100A8/A9 heterodimer; dominant host metal-sequestering protein. Coordinates Zn, Mn, and Ni at infection sites. Key effector of nutritional immunity. |
| [[developmental-metal-vulnerability]] | Critical windows (fetal, infant, pubertal) of heightened susceptibility to metal toxicity due to immature detoxification and rapid growth. |
| [[dysbiosis]] | Microbial community imbalance. Metal exposure drives dysbiosis by selectively eliminating metal-sensitive commensals and enriching metal-tolerant pathobionts. |
| [[glutathione]] | Primary intracellular thiol antioxidant. Depleted by toxic metals (Hg, Cd, As, Ni) via direct thiol binding. Cross-disease signature in metal toxicology. |
| [[gut-brain-axis]] | Bidirectional communication between gut microbiota and CNS via vagal, immune, and metabolite pathways. Metals reshape gut communities, altering brain signaling. |
| [[hepcidin]] | Master iron-regulatory hormone. Elevated in infection/inflammation (functional anemia); distinguishes true iron deficiency from iron sequestration. |
| [[inflammation]] | Chronic low-grade inflammation driven by metal-induced NF-kB activation, NLRP3 inflammasome, and LPS translocation from metal-damaged gut barrier. |
| [[insulin-resistance]] | Impaired cellular response to insulin. Linked to Zn depletion (ZnT8), Cd/As interference with GLUT4, and metal-induced gut dysbiosis driving endotoxemia. |
| [[lactoferrin]] | Iron-binding glycoprotein of innate immunity. Sequesters Fe from pathogens at mucosal surfaces. Therapeutic potential in endometriosis and gut infections. |
| [[mediterranean-diet]] | Dietary pattern rich in polyphenols, fiber, and antioxidants. Modulates metal absorption and gut microbiome composition. Studied in PCOS and endometriosis. |
| [[neurodegeneration]] | Progressive neuronal loss involving metal dyshomeostasis: Fe accumulation (ferroptosis), Cu/Zn-SOD dysfunction, Mn toxicity, and Al neurotoxicity. |
| [[nf-kappa-b]] | Master transcription factor for inflammatory and immune genes. Activated by metal-induced oxidative stress (Ni, Cd, As) and pathogen-associated molecular patterns. |
| [[probiotics]] | Live microorganisms conferring health benefits. Metal-binding capacity is double-edged: beneficial for toxic metal detox, but may reduce essential element bioavailability. |
| [[short-chain-fatty-acids]] | Acetate, propionate, and butyrate -- primary products of bacterial fiber fermentation. Critical molecular link between diet, gut microbiome, and host physiology. Depletion is near-universal across all diseases in this wiki. |
| [[tmao]] | Gut microbiome-derived metabolite (trimethylamine N-oxide) and one of the strongest microbial biomarkers for CVD risk. Exemplifies how microbial metabolism of dietary choline/carnitine generates systemically toxic products. |
| [[neuroinflammation]] | Chronic CNS inflammatory activation driven by microglia, astrocytes, and peripheral immune infiltration. Convergent mechanism across AD, PD, MS, and ASD; the brain-side consequence of gut-brain axis disruption by metals. |
| [[tryptophan-metabolism]] | Essential amino acid metabolized via kynurenine, serotonin, and microbial indole pathways. Balance profoundly influenced by gut microbiome, inflammation, and metal cofactors. Critical gut-brain axis node. |
| [[bile-acid-metabolism]] | Host-microbe co-metabolism of cholesterol-derived bile acids creating a signaling network affecting lipid metabolism, glucose homeostasis, immune regulation, and cancer risk. Altered in CVD, IBD, MS, and CRC. |
