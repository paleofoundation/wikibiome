## Entities (32)

Metals, metalloids, pathogens, and organisms. Each page covers chemistry/biology, exposure/acquisition, toxicological/virulence mechanisms, and disease associations.

| Entity | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| [[nickel]] | Central entity of the wiki. Group 1 carcinogen, most common contact allergen, essential cofactor for pathogen virulence (H. pylori urease/hydrogenase), and emerging cardiometabolic concern. |
| [[arsenic]] | Metalloid carcinogen acting through proliferative signaling, SAM depletion, and DNA repair inhibition rather than direct DNA damage. |
| [[chromium]] | Cr(VI) is the genotoxic carcinogen of the trio: Cr-DNA adducts, mismatch repair inhibition, and the ascorbate reduction paradox. |
| [[cadmium]] | Purely toxic heavy metal; metalloestrogen, nephrotoxin, and the best-studied model for probiotic gut-barrier protection against metal toxicity. |
| [[lead]] | Purely toxic; exerts harm through calcium mimicry, heme biosynthesis disruption, and oxidative stress. Neurodevelopmental damage persists at levels once deemed safe. |
| [[mercury]] | Most toxic heavy metal by weight. Methylmercury binds thiol groups, depletes glutathione, and crosses both blood-brain and placental barriers. |
| [[copper]] | Essential but dual-natured: elevated in nearly every disease state in this wiki (PCOS, breast cancer, RA, AMI) yet depleted in neurodegenerative brain tissue. |
| [[zinc]] | Most widely utilized metal cofactor in biology (~10% of the proteome). Deficiency is a remarkably consistent feature across cancer, PCOS, ASD, PPD, and IBD. |
| [[iron]] | Most abundant transition metal in the body. Drives ferroptosis via Fenton chemistry; the hepcidin-ferroportin axis is central to nutritional immunity. |
| [[manganese]] | Essential cofactor for Mn-SOD (SOD2) with a narrow therapeutic window; excess causes manganism, a well-characterized parkinsonism-like syndrome. |
| [[aluminum]] | Non-essential metal with no known biological role. Neurotoxic at elevated doses; major contaminant in infant formulas and baby foods. |
| [[selenium]] | Primarily protective: backbone of the selenoproteome (GPx, TrxR, deiodinases). Significance lies more in consequences of deficiency than excess. Highest organ concentration in thyroid. |
| [[helicobacter-pylori]] | Most nickel-dependent human pathogen. Requires Ni for both urease (acid survival) and [NiFe]-hydrogenase (energy metabolism, CagA translocation) in the gastric niche. |
| [[staphylococcus-aureus]] | Staphylopine metallophore competes with calprotectin for Ni/Zn. Ni-urease for skin/kidney/biofilm. IsdB heme piracy. MRSA metal-antibiotic co-resistance. |
| [[pseudomonas-aeruginosa]] | Pyoverdine/pyochelin dual-use siderophores: Fe acquisition AND extracellular toxic metal shielding. Pseudopaline nickelophore. Ni-GloI. |
| [[salmonella-typhimurium]] | Four [NiFe] hydrogenases; triple mutant 100% avirulent. Macrophage SCV survival via H₂-powered respiration. |
| [[candida-albicans]] | Metal biosorption, heavy metal-enhanced virulence in immunocompromised hosts. C. neoformans Ni-urease drives brain invasion. |
| [[proteus-mirabilis]] | Ni-urease as master CAUTI virulence factor. Crystalline biofilms, struvite stones. HypB accessory protein (39% histidine). |
| [[escherichia-coli]] | NikABCDE paradigm for Ni import. Yersiniabactin dual Fe/Ni metallophore in UPEC. Ni transport upregulated during UTI. |
| [[enterococcus]] | 120-year metal-antibiotic resistance co-selection on mobile elements. Cd reprograms 47% of genome. Hospital-acquired infection threat. |
| [[streptococcus-pneumoniae]] | Fe required for viability. Faces BOTH metal starvation AND Zn/Cu intoxication from neutrophils. PsaA Mn transporter as vaccine candidate. |
| [[endometriosis]] | Chronic estrogen-dependent inflammatory disease. 90.3% nickel allergy prevalence. First complete disease signature with all 9 Karen's Brain primitives active. |
| [[pcos]] | Polycystic ovary syndrome. Elevated Cu (meta-analytic), depleted Zn, elevated Ni in erythrocytes of obese patients. Metal-driven oxidative stress and endocrine disruption. |
| [[type-2-diabetes]] | Metabolic disease with disturbed trace element homeostasis: Zn depleted (ZnT8), Fe elevated (ferritin), Cr depleted, weak urinary Ni association. Gut microbiome mediates metal-diabetes pathways. |
| [[alzheimers-disease]] | Neurodegenerative disease with Fe, Cu, and Zn dyshomeostasis in brain tissue. Ferroptosis, amyloid-metal interactions, and gut-brain axis connections. |
| [[parkinsons-disease]] | Neurodegenerative disease linked to Mn (manganism), Fe (neuromelanin/ferroptosis), and pheomelanin-metal interactions. Gut-brain axis and microbiome involvement. |
| [[breast-cancer]] | Elevated Cu, Cd (metalloestrogen), and Ni in tumor tissue and biofluids. Cu/Zn ratio as diagnostic biomarker. Metalloestrogen-ERa activation pathway. |
| [[chronic-kidney-disease]] | Progressive nephropathy driven by Pb, Cd accumulation. Heavy metals both cause and consequence of declining renal function. Environmental CKDu in endemic regions. |
| [[rheumatoid-arthritis]] | Autoimmune joint disease with elevated Cu, depleted Se/Zn. Cu drives inflammation via ceruloplasmin; environmental metal exposure as risk factor. |
| [[multiple-sclerosis]] | Autoimmune demyelinating disease. Thin metal signature but strong gut-brain axis connection. CNS iron accumulation in progressive MS. Probiotic interventions improve EDSS. |
| [[postpartum-depression]] | Perinatal psychiatric condition. Zn depletion strongest association (100 mg/day supplementation reduced PPD risk 75%). Elevated Cu/Zn ratio as risk marker. |
| [[autism-spectrum-disorder]] | Neurodevelopmental condition with consistent metal dyshomeostasis: elevated toxic metals (Pb, Hg, Al), depleted essential metals (Zn, Se). Gut-brain-metal axis. |

## Concepts (38)

Mechanisms, pathways, and cross-cutting themes that connect the entities and sources.

| Concept | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| [[oxidative-stress]] | Unifying mechanism across all metal toxicity: ROS generation via Fenton chemistry, glutathione depletion, and SOD/catalase inhibition. Role differs by metal and context. |
| [[metal-carcinogenesis]] | Three paradigms -- epigenetic (Ni), proliferative (As), genotoxic (Cr) -- illustrating the diversity of metal-induced cancer mechanisms. |
| [[epigenetic-modifications]] | Primary carcinogenic mechanism for Ni and As: DNA hypermethylation, histone H3K9 dimethylation, and gene silencing without direct DNA damage. |
| [[DNA-damage]] | Primary Cr(VI) carcinogenic mechanism (Cr-DNA adducts, crosslinks, MMR inhibition); notably absent for Ni and As. |
| [[hypoxic-signaling]] | HIF-1a stabilization under normoxic conditions -- a hallmark unique to Ni carcinogenesis, driving VEGF, glycolytic shift, and tumor angiogenesis. |
| [[ferroptosis]] | Iron-dependent regulated cell death via lipid peroxide accumulation when GPX4 fails. Links iron overload to neurodegeneration, cancer, and gut pathology. |
| [[mis-metallation]] | Displacement of correct metal cofactors by toxic metals ("biochemical identity theft") -- one of the most fundamental and pervasive mechanisms of metal toxicity. |
| [[metalloestrogens]] | Metals (especially Cd, Ni) that activate ERa and/or GPR30 to mimic estradiol, linking metal exposure to breast cancer, PCOS, and endometriosis. |
| [[metallomics]] | Systematic multi-element profiling (ICP-MS) of biological specimens as disease biomarkers, spanning plasma, urine, brain tissue, and tumor samples. |
| [[gut-metal-microbiome]] | Bidirectional interaction: metals reshape microbial communities and metabolism, while the microbiota modulates metal absorption, speciation, and toxicity. |
| [[nutritional-immunity]] | Host strategy of withholding essential metals from pathogens. Well-established for Fe and Zn; underexplored but potentially powerful for Ni. |
| [[nickel-allergy]] | Most frequent contact allergen worldwide. Type IV hypersensitivity via TLR-4 activation; lifelong once sensitized, triggered by both skin and dietary exposure. |
| [[dietary-nickel-exposure]] | Food is the primary non-occupational Ni exposure route. Children and nickel-sensitized individuals are the highest-risk groups; TDI often exceeded. |
| [[nickel-neurotoxicity]] | Chronic Ni exposure causes anxiety, depression, and memory impairment through hippocampal oxidative stress and neurotransmitter disruption. |
| [[nickel-reproductive-toxicity]] | Maternal Ni exposure linked to congenital heart defects, placental barrier disruption, and fetal tissue accumulation. |
| [[metabolic-syndrome]] | Emerging Ni-MetS link via NHANES epidemiology (blood pressure, fasting glucose, lipids), though findings remain inconclusive at low environmental doses. |
| [[environmental-metal-exposure]] | How toxic metals reach people: dietary ingestion, occupational inhalation, consumer products (tampons, formula), soil/fertilizer contamination, and air pollution. |
| [[metal-dependent-virulence]] | How Ni/Fe/Zn/Cu/Mn serve as essential cofactors for pathogen virulence factors (urease, hydrogenase, SOD, metalloproteases). Environmental metals tip the host-pathogen balance. |
| [[siderophores-metallophores]] | Small molecules pathogens secrete to scavenge metals: iron siderophores (enterobactin, pyoverdine) and emerging nickelophores (staphylopine, pseudopaline, yersiniabactin). |
| [[matrix-metalloproteases]] | Zn-dependent endopeptidases for ECM degradation. Bridges pathogen tissue invasion and host disease (cancer metastasis, endometriosis, RA, neurodegeneration). |
| [[inter-kingdom-metal-shielding]] | Metal-mediated cooperation in polymicrobial communities: biofilm metal dynamics, siderophore sharing, Candida-bacteria interactions, NEC as paradigm case. |
| [[pathogen-metal-acquisition]] | The cellular machinery for metal import (ABC/NiCoT/TonB), storage (Hpn/ferritins), regulation (NikR/Fur/Zur), and export (CznABC/CzcCBA). The molecular arms race. |
| [[urease]] | Ni-metalloenzyme catalyzing urea → ammonia + CO₂. The most widespread Ni-dependent virulence factor: 14+ pathogens use it for acid survival, biofilm, brain invasion, stone formation, and more. Nickel-free diet doubles H. pylori eradication. |
| [[hydrogenase]] | [NiFe] hydrogenases power pathogen energy metabolism. In H. pylori, hydrogenase drives CagA translocation (cancer pathway). S. Typhimurium triple mutant is 100% avirulent. H₂ is chronically available in the human gut. |
| [[glyoxalase]] | Ni-dependent GloI detoxifies methylglyoxal in pathogens (P. aeruginosa, Neisseria, all Enterobacteriaceae, Leishmania). Human GloI uses Zn — this metal selectivity divide makes pathogen GloI a selective drug target. |
| [[biofilm]] | Structured microbial communities embedded in extracellular matrix. Metal-dependent: require Zn, Mn, Fe for structural integrity. Catheter encrustation, chronic wounds, CF lung. |
| [[calprotectin]] | S100A8/A9 heterodimer; dominant host metal-sequestering protein. Coordinates Zn, Mn, and Ni at infection sites. Key effector of nutritional immunity. |
| [[developmental-metal-vulnerability]] | Critical windows (fetal, infant, pubertal) of heightened susceptibility to metal toxicity due to immature detoxification, rapid growth, and higher relative exposure. |
| [[dysbiosis]] | Microbial community imbalance. Metal exposure drives dysbiosis by selectively eliminating metal-sensitive commensals and enriching metal-tolerant pathobionts. |
| [[glutathione]] | Primary intracellular thiol antioxidant. Depleted by toxic metals (Hg, Cd, As, Ni) via direct thiol binding. Depletion is a cross-disease signature in metal toxicology. |
| [[gut-brain-axis]] | Bidirectional communication between gut microbiota and CNS via vagal, immune, and metabolite pathways. Metals reshape gut communities, altering brain-directed signaling. |
| [[hepcidin]] | Master iron-regulatory hormone. Elevated in infection/inflammation (functional anemia); distinguishes true iron deficiency from iron sequestration as host defense. |
| [[inflammation]] | Chronic low-grade inflammation driven by metal-induced NF-kB activation, NLRP3 inflammasome, and LPS translocation from metal-damaged gut barrier. |
| [[insulin-resistance]] | Impaired cellular response to insulin. Linked to Zn depletion (ZnT8), Cd/As interference with GLUT4, and metal-induced gut dysbiosis driving endotoxemia. |
| [[lactoferrin]] | Iron-binding glycoprotein of innate immunity. Sequesters Fe from pathogens at mucosal surfaces. Therapeutic potential in endometriosis and gut infections. |
| [[mediterranean-diet]] | Dietary pattern rich in polyphenols, fiber, and antioxidants. Modulates metal absorption and gut microbiome composition. Studied in PCOS and endometriosis. |
| [[neurodegeneration]] | Progressive neuronal loss involving metal dyshomeostasis: Fe accumulation (ferroptosis), Cu/Zn-SOD dysfunction, Mn toxicity (manganism), and Al neurotoxicity. |
| [[nf-kappa-b]] | Master transcription factor for inflammatory and immune genes. Activated by metal-induced oxidative stress (Ni, Cd, As) and by pathogen-associated molecular patterns. |

## Disease Signatures (4)

| Signature | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| [[endometriosis]] | First complete signature (28 papers). All 9 primitives active. Metallomic: Ni (77.8%), Fe (70.4%), Pb (59.3%) elevated, glutathione depleted. Key pathogens: E. coli (primary), B. fragilis, GBS, F. nucleatum, C. albicans (functional shielding). Validated interventions: low-Ni diet, EcN 1917, lactoferrin, NAC. STOPs: iron and zinc supplementation. HBOT promising. |
| [[crohns-disease]] | Built from 129 papers. Metal dyshomeostasis → dysbiosis → barrier dysfunction → inflammation. ZIP8 A391T demonstrates dysbiosis precedes inflammation. Key depletion: F. prausnitzii (most consistent across all cohorts). Key pathogens: AIEC, F. nucleatum, Enterococcus, R. gnavus. SCFA/bile acid/tryptophan metabolite collapse. STOPs: oral iron, broad-spectrum antibiotics. |
| [[graves-disease]] | Built from 21 papers. Metal-driven dysbiosis masquerading as primary autoimmunity. Metallomic: Pb (76%), I (67%), Se depleted (43%), Fe dysregulated (38%). Bacteroides-Proteobacteria-Streptococcus consortium drives Th17/Treg imbalance, intestinal permeability, and molecular mimicry with TPO. Estrobolome drives 3:1 female predominance. Validated: berberine+methimazole, selenium supplementation. 7 of 9 primitives active. |
