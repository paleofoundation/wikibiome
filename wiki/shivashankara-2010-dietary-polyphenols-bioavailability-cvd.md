# Shivashankara & Acharya 2010 -- Dietary Polyphenols Bioavailability and CVD

## Key Claims

- Only ~5% of dietary [[polyphenols]] are absorbed in the duodenum; over 95% pass to the colon where they are fermented by the [[gut microbiome]], producing microbial metabolites absorbed as mammalian conjugates
- Polyphenols reduce [[cardiovascular disease]] risk by inhibiting [[LDL oxidation]], promoting vasodilation, and reducing [[platelet aggregation]]
- Bioavailability ranking: isoflavones (highest) > gallic acid > flavanols (catechins) > flavanones > flavonol (quercetin) > proanthocyanidins/galloylated catechins/anthocyanins (lowest)
- Fermentation of soy products with *Bacillus subtilis* or *Aspergillus* fungi increases isoflavone bioavailability; enzyme digestion with beta-glycosidase increases soy milk isoflavones
- Food preparation methods significantly alter polyphenol content: cutting increases cyanidin in lettuce, steaming increases polyphenols in broccoli, boiling causes loss of anthocyanins

## Methods/Findings

- **Type**: Narrative review
- **Key mechanisms**:
  - Flavonoid glycosides are absorbed to a limited extent and cleaved by gut bacterial or human intestinal beta-glucosidases
  - Colonic microflora are the major metabolizers of phenolic acids (chlorogenic acid, caffeic acid, ellagic acid)
  - Polyphenols inhibit LDL oxidation via NADH oxidase reduction and increased glutathione reductase activity
  - Endothelium-dependent vasodilation enhanced through NO synthase activation and peroxynitrite scavenging
  - Grape polyphenols cause dose-dependent improvement in brachial artery flow-mediated dilation
  - Tea and cocoa polyphenols inhibit LDL oxidation and improve endothelial function independently of plasma antioxidant activity
- **Polyphenols and platelet aggregation**: Grape juice, grape seed/skin, and cocoa suppress platelet aggregation; polyphenol supplements can inhibit platelet function
