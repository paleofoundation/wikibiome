## Key Findings

- **Oral microbiome dysbiosis disrupts vascular nitric oxide (NO) homeostasis.** Anaerobic Gram-negative periodontopathogens such as [[porphyromonas-gingivalis]] displace commensal nitrate-reducing bacteria, eliminating dietary nitrate reduction to nitrite and NO — a vasodilatory and anti-inflammatory molecule essential for endothelial health ([[gualtero-2023-oral-microbiome-inflammation-vascular-diseases-review]], expert-opinion).

- **Bacteremia from periodontal disease directly damages vascular endothelium.** Periodontal pathogens translocate to the peripheral circulation during routine activities (chewing, brushing, dental procedures), invading endothelial cells, activating macrophage-monocyte inflammatory cascades (IL-6, IL-8, IL-1beta, TNF-alpha, MCP-1), and promoting foam cell formation in atherosclerotic plaques ([[gualtero-2023-oral-microbiome-inflammation-vascular-diseases-review]], expert-opinion).

- ***P. gingivalis* outer membrane vesicles (OMVs) and gingipains are key virulence factors.** OMVs carry LPS, gingipains, and other toxins that cleave endothelial adhesion molecules (PECAM-1/CD31, VE-cadherin/CD144), suppress eNOS expression via ERK1/2 and p38 MAPK pathways, and increase endothelial permeability ([[gualtero-2023-oral-microbiome-inflammation-vascular-diseases-review]], expert-opinion).

- **Periodontal treatment improves cardiovascular markers.** Clinical evidence from RCTs shows intensive periodontal treatment (IPT) reduces high-sensitivity CRP, improves flow-mediated dilation (FMD), and reduces the risk of cardiovascular events over 6+ months of follow-up ([[gualtero-2023-oral-microbiome-inflammation-vascular-diseases-review]], expert-opinion).

- **Periodontitis induces a prothrombotic state.** Periodontal bacteria (*P. gingivalis*, *F. nucleatum*, *A. actinomycetemcomitans*, *S. sanguinis*) activate platelets via direct receptor binding, fibrinogen/fibronectin bridging, LPS, and OMVs, increasing clot formation risk ([[gualtero-2023-oral-microbiome-inflammation-vascular-diseases-review]], expert-opinion).

## Methods

This is a **narrative review** (not a systematic review or meta-analysis). The authors synthesized evidence from preclinical studies (cell culture, animal models), epidemiological studies, clinical trials, and prior systematic reviews/meta-analyses. No formal search protocol, inclusion/exclusion criteria, or risk-of-bias assessment is reported. The paper cites 157 references spanning basic science through clinical intervention studies.

## Detailed Summary

### Oral Dysbiosis and Nitric Oxide

The oral microbiome plays a critical role in systemic nitric oxide homeostasis. Commensal bacteria on the tongue and in the oral cavity reduce dietary nitrates to nitrites, which are further converted to NO in the stomach and circulation. When oral dysbiosis increases anaerobic Gram-negative periodontopathogens such as *P. gingivalis* (which cannot reduce nitrate), nitrate-reducing commensals are displaced. This loss of the enterosalivary nitrate-nitrite-NO pathway reduces NO bioavailability, contributing to endothelial dysfunction, hypertension, and vascular disease ([[gualtero-2023-oral-microbiome-inflammation-vascular-diseases-review]]).

Chlorhexidine mouthwash studies demonstrate this mechanism: even at low concentrations, chlorhexidine inhibits nitrate-reducing activity and the *Veillonella dispar* population, though salivary nitrite production was somewhat preserved. Tongue cleaning affects the tongue microbiome and can alter systolic blood pressure in normotensive individuals ([[gualtero-2023-oral-microbiome-inflammation-vascular-diseases-review]]).

### Periodontal Disease as Chronic Inflammatory Driver

Periodontitis affects more than 700 million people worldwide, with severe forms affecting 11.2% of the global population. The review describes it as a chronic multifactorial inflammatory disease driven by a biofilm that generates diverse grades of tissue destruction. Periodontal bacteria including *P. gingivalis*, *T. forsythia*, *T. denticola*, and *A. actinomycetemcomitans* are considered the "red complex" and associated pathogens driving disease progression ([[gualtero-2023-oral-microbiome-inflammation-vascular-diseases-review]]).

### Translocation and Vascular Invasion

*P. gingivalis* is the most frequent organism detected during oral bacteremia in periodontitis patients. It has been found in amniotic fluid, placental tissues, brains, cerebrospinal fluid, and vascular tissues. Routes of systemic entry include:
- Direct translocation through ulcerated periodontal pockets during chewing, brushing, scaling
- Phagocyte/dendritic cell pathway transport
- Intracellular survival and dissemination (achieved by *P. gingivalis* and *F. nucleatum*)

*P. gingivalis* can invade multiple cell lines including human umbilical vein endothelial cells (HUVECs), KB cells, and oral epithelial cells. Invasion of endothelial cells and phagocytic cells within atheroma is critical for atherosclerosis progression ([[gualtero-2023-oral-microbiome-inflammation-vascular-diseases-review]]).

### Endothelial Dysfunction Mechanisms

The review details multiple pathways through which periodontopathogens cause endothelial dysfunction:

1. **LPS-mediated inflammation**: Bacterial LPS activates TLR2 and TLR4 on endothelial cells, triggering NF-kB signaling, proinflammatory cytokine release (IL-6, IL-8, TNF-alpha), and upregulation of adhesion molecules (ICAM-1, VCAM-1, E-selectin)
2. **OMV-mediated damage**: Outer membrane vesicles from *P. gingivalis* cleave PECAM-1 and VE-cadherin, suppress eNOS via ERK1/2 and p38 MAPK, and increase endothelial permeability
3. **Gingipain proteases**: Cysteine proteases (Rgp and Kgp) from *P. gingivalis* degrade complement factors, sequester and internalize bacteria by complement receptor, and promote tissue invasion
4. **Oxidative stress**: Periodontal infection increases reactive oxygen species (ROS), oxidizes LDL to ox-LDL, and accelerates foam cell formation and atherogenesis
5. **eNOS suppression**: *P. gingivalis* OMVs induce suppression of endothelial nitric oxide synthase through ERK1/2 and p38 MAPK pathways, reducing NO production

### Macrophage-Monocyte Activation

In atherosclerosis-related endothelial dysfunction, monocytes migrate through the endothelium, differentiate into macrophages in the subendothelial tissue, and convert to foam cells. LPS from periodontal bacteria accelerates foam cell formation. *A. actinomycetemcomitans* LPS synergistically interacts with complement to activate inflammatory cascades. The review notes that *A. actinomycetemcomitans* had a greater proinflammatory capacity than *P. gingivalis* in HCAEC models, as evidenced by reduced eNOS expression and increased NOS2 and COX-2 expression ([[gualtero-2023-oral-microbiome-inflammation-vascular-diseases-review]]).

### Prothrombotic State

Periodontal bacteria activate platelets through multiple mechanisms:
- Direct bacterial binding to platelet receptors
- Indirect binding via fibrinogen, fibronectin, and von Willebrand factor bridges
- LPS, gingipains, and OMV interactions
- *P. gingivalis*, *F. nucleatum*, *A. actinomycetemcomitans*, and *S. sanguinis* are the most commonly associated periodontopathogens with platelet activation

### Association with Cardiovascular Diseases

The review cites multiple systematic reviews and meta-analyses demonstrating:
- Periodontitis is an independent predictor of myocardial infarction risk
- Patients with periodontitis had CRP levels more than 90% higher than controls
- Periodontal treatment reduced CRP for up to 180 days
- IPT reduced systolic and diastolic blood pressure in hypertensive and prehypertensive individuals (evidence from 8 RCTs)
- A strong positive association between subgingival colonization by *P. gingivalis*, *T. forsythia*, *T. denticola*, and *A. actinomycetemcomitans* and arterial hypertension (multiethnic cohort study)

### Hypertension and the Renin-Angiotensin System

The review discusses a "two-hit" hypothesis: chronic immune activation at sites of inflammation exacerbates responses to low-dose angiotensin II, linking periodontitis-driven chronic inflammation to hypertensive responses. *P. gingivalis* or its LPS induces proinflammatory cytokines and angiotensin II in HCAECs, contributing to both endothelial dysfunction and hypertension ([[gualtero-2023-oral-microbiome-inflammation-vascular-diseases-review]]).

### Nitrate-Reducing Bacteria and Blood Pressure

Population-level data (NHANES III) demonstrates significant associations between elevated blood pressure and antibodies against *Campylobacter rectus*, *Veillonella parvula*, and *Prevotella melaninogenica* — with *C. rectus* showing the strongest association. Higher counts of *P. intermedia*, *P. gingivalis*, and *F. nucleatum* were associated with high blood pressure in periodontitis patients ([[gualtero-2023-oral-microbiome-inflammation-vascular-diseases-review]]).

## Relevance to Cureva

This review supports several intervention and STOP hypotheses for conditions involving endothelial dysfunction:

**Intervention hypotheses:**
- **Periodontal treatment as vascular intervention**: The evidence from RCTs showing improved FMD and reduced CRP after IPT supports periodontal treatment as a validated intervention for cardiovascular risk reduction. This extends to erectile dysfunction via the shared endothelial dysfunction mechanism.
- **Nitrate-reducing probiotic restoration**: If oral dysbiosis depletes nitrate-reducing commensals, restoring them (or protecting them from displacement) could improve NO bioavailability — relevant to hypertension, ED, and CVD signatures.
- **Targeted anti-biofilm strategies**: Interventions that suppress *P. gingivalis* without disrupting nitrate-reducing commensals would be preferable to broad-spectrum antimicrobials.

**STOP signals:**
- **Chlorhexidine mouthwash**: While used for oral hygiene, chlorhexidine inhibits nitrate-reducing bacteria even at low concentrations, potentially worsening NO-dependent vascular conditions. This is a candidate STOP for patients with hypertension or ED.

**Triangle evidence:**
- The review provides strong I-to-f (periodontal treatment reduces inflammatory markers) and moderate I-to-D (periodontal treatment improves cardiovascular outcomes) evidence. The f-to-D link (endothelial dysfunction drives CVD/ED) is well established from other literature.

## Contradictions / Tensions

- The review acknowledges that while epidemiological evidence strongly links periodontitis to CVDs, **the Periodontitis and Vascular Events (PAVE) study found no difference in cardiovascular events between periodontally treated and control patients over 24 months** — though a subgroup with elevated CRP at baseline did show significant reduction. This tension between mechanism-rich evidence and inconsistent clinical trial results is a recurring theme.
- The review notes chlorhexidine's dual role: beneficial for periodontal treatment but potentially harmful for nitrate-reducing capacity. This creates a tension in clinical recommendations.
- *A. actinomycetemcomitans* showed greater proinflammatory capacity than *P. gingivalis* in some endothelial cell models, which complicates the field's heavy focus on *P. gingivalis* as the primary periodontopathogen of concern.

## Open Questions

1. **Does periodontal treatment improve erectile dysfunction outcomes?** The shared endothelial dysfunction mechanism is well-supported mechanistically, but no direct clinical trial evidence is cited linking periodontal treatment to ED improvement.
2. **Can targeted probiotics restore oral nitrate-reducing capacity** without promoting periodontal pathogen growth? What species would be optimal?
3. **What is the threshold of periodontal disease severity** required to produce clinically meaningful vascular effects? Is subclinical periodontitis sufficient?
4. **How do iron-dependent virulence mechanisms of oral pathogens interact with systemic iron status?** The review mentions siderophores and iron-related virulence but does not deeply explore the metallomics of oral dysbiosis.
5. **Does the oral microbiome composition directly predict cardiovascular or ED risk** independently of traditional risk factors? Prospective cohort data with oral microbiome profiling is lacking.
