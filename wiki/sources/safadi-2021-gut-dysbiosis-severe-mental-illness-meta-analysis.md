# Gut dysbiosis in severe mental illness and chronic fatigue: a trans-diagnostic meta-analysis

## Summary

This systematic review and meta-analysis investigated proxy biomarkers of gut [[dysbiosis]] in patients with severe mental illness and chronic fatigue. Nineteen studies in the meta-analysis (N = 2758 patients, N = 1847 controls) assessed zonulin, lipopolysaccharide, endotoxin antibodies, sCD14, LBP, and alpha-1-antitrypsin as markers of [[dysbiosis]]-driven gut barrier breakdown.

## Key Findings

- Elevated zonulin in patients vs controls (SMD = 0.97; 95% CI 0.10-1.85; P = 0.03) indicating gut barrier compromise
- Elevated lipopolysaccharide (SMD = 0.77; 95% CI 0.42-1.12; P < 0.01)
- Elevated anti-endotoxin antibodies in bipolar disorder, depression, schizophrenia, and chronic fatigue (SMD = 0.99; P < 0.01)
- Elevated sCD14 in bipolar, depression, schizophrenia, and chronic fatigue (SMD = 0.54; P < 0.01)
- Elevated LBP (SMD = 0.87; P < 0.01) and alpha-1-antitrypsin (SMD = 1.23; P < 0.01)
- Gut [[dysbiosis]] biomarkers positively correlated with severity of sickness behavior
- "Sickness behavior" symptoms (fatigue, anhedonia, social withdrawal) overlap with negative symptoms of schizophrenia

## Relevance to Gut-Metal-Microbiome Axis

This meta-analysis provides quantitative evidence that gut barrier compromise is a feature of schizophrenia and related psychiatric conditions. Elevated zonulin indicates a "leaky gut" that permits systemic entry of bacterial endotoxins and dietary metals ([[iron]], [[lead]], [[cadmium]]). The elevated LPS and sCD14 confirm bacterial translocation that drives [[neuroinflammation]]. The overlap between "sickness behavior" and negative symptoms of schizophrenia strengthens the case for [[immune-balance]] disruption as a core pathogenic mechanism. [[Short-chain-fatty-acids]] depletion from [[dysbiosis]] may remove protective barrier maintenance, amplifying metal and endotoxin translocation.

## Limitations

- High heterogeneity in some analyses
- Cross-sectional data limits causal inference
- Medication effects not fully controlled
