# Hoang 2023 -- Dysbiotic Microbiome Variation in CRC Linked to Lifestyles and Metabolic Diseases

## Key Claims

- The Anna Karenina principle (AKP) effect was found for history of smoking, alcohol consumption, and diabetes (higher intra-sample similarity in exposed groups), while obesity and hypertension showed the anti-AKP effect (lower intra-sample similarity in exposed groups)
- Non-smokers, non-drinkers, obese individuals, hypertensive subjects, and non-diabetic individuals showed stronger network connectivity of microbial communities compared to their counterparts
- Table 3 identifies differentially abundant bacteria by lifestyle/metabolic disease: smokers enriched in *Bacilli/Streptococcaceae*; alcohol consumers in *Gammaproteobacteria/Enterobacteriaceae*; obese patients in *Betaproteobacteria/Lachnospiraceae*; diabetic patients in *Elusimicrobia/Peptostreptococcaceae*
- Diversity of food and nutrient intake did not shape the microbial diversity between individuals (pProcrustes > 0.05), suggesting lifestyle factors have more impact than diet composition per se
- *Firmicutes* appeared as the central phylum mostly connected with other phyla; *Bacteroidetes* and *Firmicutes* (the two most abundant phyla) were negatively correlated (rho = -0.16 to -0.06)

## Methods/Findings

- **Design**: Cross-sectional study of 331 CRC patients from Seoul National University Hospital (2017-2019); fecal samples pre-surgery
- **Methods**: 16S rRNA sequencing (V3-V4), COZINE network analysis, AKP effect assessment via Ruzicka similarity index, LEfSe for differential abundance, Procrustes analysis for diet-microbiome association
- **Key insight**: Modifiable factors (smoking, alcohol, metabolic disease) contribute to microbial stochasticity that may influence CRC outcomes
