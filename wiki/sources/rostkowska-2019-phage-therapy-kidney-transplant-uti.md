# Phage Therapy for Recurrent UTI in Kidney Transplant

## Key Findings

- **Phage therapy was used as a last-resort treatment** for recurrent urinary tract infections in a kidney transplant recipient after conventional antibiotics failed ([[rostkowska-2019-phage-therapy-kidney-transplant-uti]], case-report, n=1)
- **Demonstrated feasibility and safety** of bacteriophage therapy in an immunosuppressed post-transplant patient, where infection control is critical for graft survival
- **Multidrug-resistant bacterial infections** drove the clinical decision to use phage therapy, highlighting the intersection of [[antimicrobial-resistance]] and transplant medicine

## Detailed Summary

This case report documents the use of bacteriophage therapy in one of the most challenging clinical settings: a kidney transplant recipient on chronic immunosuppression with recurrent multidrug-resistant urinary tract infections. In transplant patients, UTIs are among the most common infections, driven by immunosuppressive therapy, urological complications, and frequent antibiotic exposure that selects for resistant organisms.

The patient had failed multiple antibiotic courses, including "last-chance" antibiotics (likely colistin or carbapenems), necessitating an alternative approach. Phage therapy — the use of bacteriophages (viruses that specifically infect and lyse target bacteria) — was selected based on the ability to match phages to the patient's specific bacterial isolate.

The case is notable because immunosuppressed patients were traditionally considered poor candidates for phage therapy due to concerns about immune responses to phage proteins and the importance of host immune cooperation for phage efficacy. This report suggests that phage therapy can be both safe and potentially effective even in the context of pharmacological immunosuppression.
