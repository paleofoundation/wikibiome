# Why This Paper Matters

While not exclusively about metals, this review provides the strategic framework for understanding why metal-targeting antimicrobials represent a promising new therapeutic avenue. Parkhill and Johnson document the crisis in antibacterial drug discovery -- 4.95 million deaths associated with [[antimicrobial-resistance]] in 2019, with resistance emerging to every clinically approved antibiotic (half-life of ~15 years before resistance is detected). The paper argues that integrating chemical biology with molecular genetics can identify vulnerable targets, and metalloenzymes and metal homeostasis systems are among the most promising. For the wiki, this paper explains why disrupting [[metal-homeostasis]] through [[mis-metallation]]-inducing compounds is a viable antimicrobial strategy and how modern genetic tools (CRISPRi, chemical genomics) can systematically identify metal-dependent vulnerabilities.

# Key Claims

- **Antibacterial drug discovery has reached a plateau** while resistance emergence is constant -- there is now resistance to every clinically approved antibiotic class
- **The half-life of a drug class** before resistance detection is ~15 years (95% CI: 10-20 years), following an exponential distribution
- **Target-based drug discovery has largely failed** for antibiotics -- high-throughput biochemical screening of individual targets has produced no clinical antibacterial candidates
- **69% of current antibacterial drugs** are natural products derived from soil microbes, but rediscovery rates now require screening ~10^7 new species per new compound
- **Chemical genomics** -- combining genetic perturbations with chemical screening -- can identify metal-dependent vulnerabilities and synergistic drug combinations
- **Loss-of-function dominance** determines target attractiveness: metal homeostasis genes are often "vulnerable" (modest inhibition causes catastrophic fitness loss)
- **Resistance evolution** is governed by both drug chemistry and target biology -- metal-based antimicrobials may have lower resistance propensity because metals target multiple pathways simultaneously
- **Co-selection** between antibiotic resistance and metal resistance genes has been documented, with resistance genes coexisting for 30,000+ years in environmental samples

# Methods & Findings

## The Drug Discovery Crisis

- In the last 10 years, all 19 approved new antibacterial drugs belonged to existing classes (except pretomanid 2019 and delamanid 2014)
- Of 7 antibiotics in Phase III trials (end 2022), only gepotidacin and zoliflodacin have new mechanisms of action
- The proportion of synthetic compounds has increased from 44% to 61%, and novel mechanisms from 28% to 65% in clinical trials from 2011-2022

## Genetic Tools for Metal Target Discovery

- **Gene essentiality screens**: Transposon insertion and gene deletion libraries exist for E. coli, B. subtilis, S. aureus, Salmonella, and others
- **CRISPRi**: Enables titratable knockdown of metal homeostasis genes to assess vulnerability -- two S. aureus asRNA assays identified 150 and 658 essential genes respectively
- **Chemical-genetic interactions**: Combining gene deletions/knockdowns with chemical stressors identifies metal-dependent resistance and synergistic drug pairs
- **PROSPECT assay**: Combines sensitized strains with chemical libraries for screening -- identified M. tuberculosis MshC knockdown as isoniazid-resistant, revealing mycothiol biosynthesis as a resistance pathway

## Resistance and Metal Co-selection

- Genes encoding resistance to beta-lactams, tetracyclines, and glycopeptides found in 30,000-year-old environmental samples
- Antibiotic synthesis and resistance mechanisms were likely driven by early microbial competition
- Resistance can be generic (reduced influx, efflux), target-specific, or class-specific
- Mobile genetic elements transmit both antibiotic and metal resistance, enabling [[co-selection]]

## Implications for Metal-Targeting Antimicrobials

- Multi-target mechanisms (like metal disruption) may reduce resistance emergence
- Gene-gene interactions can identify synthetic lethality -- e.g., combining metal chelation with existing antibiotics
- Loss-of-function dominance screening can identify metal homeostasis genes where partial inhibition causes disproportionate fitness loss
