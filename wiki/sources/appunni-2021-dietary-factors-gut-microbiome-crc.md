# Emerging Evidence on the Effects of Dietary Factors on the Gut Microbiome in Colorectal Cancer

## Key Claims

- Dietary fibers reaching the colon undigested are metabolized by gut microbiome into SCFAs (butyrate, propionate, acetate) which exhibit anti-inflammatory properties, maintain mucosal integrity, and retain microbial diversity; diets low in fiber predispose to CRC via reduced SCFA production
- Table 1 comprehensively catalogs the CRC tumorigenicity of diverse microbial taxa: *F. nucleatum* is pro-tumorigenic through multiple mechanisms (miR21/TLR4/NF-kB, FadA/CHK2, ULK1/ATG7 autophagy), while *Akkermansia*, *Desulfovibrio*, *Clostridium XIVa*, and *Anaerotripes* are anti-tumorigenic
- Red meat and processed meat promote CRC through generation of N-nitroso compounds, heterocyclic amines, and heme iron-mediated genotoxicity; the gut microbiome metabolizes these into more harmful compounds
- Polyphenols from berries, tea, and mango function as prebiotics, increasing *Bifidobacterium*, *Lactobacillus*, and *Akkermansia* while alleviating inflammation; curcumin specifically increases *Lactobacillales* and decreases *Coriobacteriales* in CRC mouse models
- Alcohol consumption increases *Bacteroidetes* and *Ruminococcaceae* while decreasing *Firmicutes* overall, and alcohol is metabolized to acetaldehyde, a DNA-damaging intermediate, via gut microbiome pathways

## Methods/Findings

- **Design**: Narrative review integrating evidence on dietary factors, gut microbiome modulation, and CRC development/prevention
- **Key dietary factors reviewed**: Dietary fiber, red/processed meat, alcohol, phytomolecules (polyphenols, curcumin), prebiotics, probiotics, synbiotics, postbiotics, EVOO, n-3 PUFAs
- **Figure 1**: Host diet-outcome model showing beneficial (fiber, phytochemicals, EVOO, prebiotics) vs. harmful (low fiber, processed food, red meat, obesity) dietary factors on gut microbiome
- **Figure 2**: Detailed pathway diagram of healthy vs. at-risk diet influence on intestinal epithelium through gut microbiome intermediaries
