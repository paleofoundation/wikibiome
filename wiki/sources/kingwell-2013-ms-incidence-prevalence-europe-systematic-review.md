# Incidence and Prevalence of Multiple Sclerosis in Europe: A Systematic Review

## Summary

This systematic review of 123 European studies (1985-2011) provides a comprehensive assessment of [[multiple-sclerosis]] incidence and prevalence across European regions, including quality assessment of methodology.

## Key Findings

- Study estimates were highly heterogeneous, even within regions or countries (I-squared >= 84.4%, p<0.0001)
- Prevalence and incidence estimates tended to be higher in more recent studies
- Higher in Nordic countries and northern British Isles
- Female:male ratio as high as 3:1 with rare exceptions
- Few studies examined ethnicity
- Only 37% of studies provided standardized estimates
- Epidemiological data at national level was uncommon
- Large areas of Europe were unrepresented or under-represented

## Quality Assessment

- Quality was generally higher in more recent studies using current diagnostic criteria
- Marked geographical disparities in available data
- Lack of standardization hampers inter-study comparisons

## Relevance to Gut-Metal-Microbiome Axis

The geographical variation in MS prevalence across Europe provides a framework for investigating environmental determinants including [[heavy-metals]] exposure and [[gut-microbiome]] composition. The Nordic countries with highest MS prevalence also have distinct dietary patterns, industrial histories, and environmental metal contamination profiles. The increasing incidence over time could reflect changing environmental exposures (including dietary metals, processed food consumption, and antibiotic use) that alter [[gut-microbiome]] ecology. The sex ratio differences may relate to differential metal accumulation and hormonal effects on the [[gut-brain-axis]].

## Limitations

- Data only up to 2011
- High heterogeneity in methodology
- Many European regions not represented
- Does not address environmental or microbiome factors
