# Progression of Parkinson's Disease Is Associated with Gut Dysbiosis: Two-Year Follow-Up Study

## Summary

This longitudinal study tracks gut [[microbiome]] changes over a two-year period in [[parkinsons-disease]] patients, providing rare prospective evidence that [[dysbiosis]] deepens in parallel with clinical disease progression.

## Key Findings

- Gut microbiome composition shifted significantly over two years in PD patients
- Disease progression (worsening motor and non-motor scores) correlated with deepening dysbiosis
- SCFA-producing bacteria decreased further over the follow-up period
- Pro-inflammatory taxa showed progressive enrichment
- Microbial diversity declined over time in PD patients but remained stable in controls
- The rate of microbiome change predicted the rate of clinical progression

## Relevance to Gut-Metal-Microbiome Axis

This is one of few longitudinal studies confirming that gut [[dysbiosis]] in [[parkinsons-disease]] is progressive, not static. The finding that microbiome deterioration tracks with clinical worsening supports the hypothesis that dysbiosis is an active driver (not merely a consequence) of PD progression. In the metallomic framework, progressive depletion of SCFA producers means progressive loss of the gut's metal-buffering capacity, leading to accelerating [[iron]] dysregulation. The correlation between microbiome change rate and clinical progression rate suggests that interventions targeting the microbiome-metal axis could potentially slow disease.

## Limitations

- Two-year follow-up may not capture full disease trajectory
- Medication changes during follow-up are a major confounder
- Relatively small longitudinal cohort
- Diet and lifestyle changes during follow-up not fully controlled
