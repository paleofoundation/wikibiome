## Key Claims

- Reduction of dietary nickel intake may benefit some nickel-sensitive patients with endogenous pattern dermatitis
- 58 of 90 patients (64%) benefited in the short term from a [[low-nickel-diet]]; 40 of 55 long-term respondents (73%) showed marked clearing or reduced severity
- Patients with strongly positive patch tests to nickel had less benefit from the diet than those with moderately positive patch tests
- Specific foods, menstrual period, psychological stress, and sweating were identified as aggravating factors

## Methods/Findings

- **Design**: Open prospective diet trial in a private dermatology practice in Aalborg, Denmark
- **Population**: 770 patients (64 men, 706 women) seen from 1986-1990 with nickel allergy; 216 suspected endogenous cause underwent oral challenge with 2.5 mg nickel; 96 reacted to nickel but not placebo; 90 completed the diet trial
- **Protocol**: Patients followed the diet for 1-2 months initially; questionnaire follow-up sent 1-3 years after diet initiation; 55 long-term questionnaires evaluated
- **Short-term results**: 58 (64%) obviously benefited; 15 (17%) possibly benefited; 17 (19%) did not benefit
- **Long-term results (55 patients, mean follow-up 1.8 years)**: 10 had no dermatitis or only occasional eruptions; 30 had less severe, fewer, shorter flares; mean age 32 years; mean dermatitis duration 3.8 years before diet
- **Dermatitis types responding**: Recurrent vesicular hand eczema (67.7% long-term effect); anogenital dermatitis (100%); vasculitis-like (66.7%); other types (87.5%)
- **Aggravating factors** (reported on 3+ separate occasions): Specific foods (31/40 with long-term effect), menstrual period (7/40), psychological stress (13/40), sweating (10/40)
- **Diet appendix**: Detailed patient-friendly diet instructions provided
