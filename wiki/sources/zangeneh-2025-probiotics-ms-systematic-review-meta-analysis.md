# The Potential Effectiveness of Probiotics in Reducing Multiple Sclerosis Progression in Preclinical and Clinical Studies

## Summary

This systematic review and meta-analysis evaluated 23 studies (17 animal, 6 human) on [[probiotics]] in [[multiple-sclerosis]] and EAE, covering literature from 2004 to August 2024. It is the first worldwide meta-analysis specifically quantifying probiotic effects on cytokine levels, immune cell percentages, and clinical outcomes.

## Key Findings

### Animal Studies (EAE)
- [[probiotics]] significantly decreased IFN-gamma production (Std diff in means: -2.492 by ELISA, -2.453 by RT-PCR)
- Significant decrease in IL-17 levels with probiotic treatment
- Increased IL-10 (anti-inflammatory) production
- Decreased TNF-alpha levels
- Increased percentage of regulatory T cells (Tregs) and CD4+CD25+ cells
- Significant reduction in clinical EAE scores

### Human Studies
- In all 6 human studies, single or multiple [[probiotics]] decreased disease severity and changed gut microbiota population
- *Bifidobacterium*, *Prevotella*, and *Lactobacillus* can decrease Th1/Th17 ratio while inducing IFN-gamma and IL-17 levels
- [[probiotics]] can modulate immune cells and cytokines, consequently decreasing EAE signs and symptoms

### Key Probiotic Strains
- *Lactobacillus plantarum*, *Bifidobacterium lactis*, *Lactobacillus acidophilus*, *Lactobacillus casei* showed beneficial effects
- *Prevotella* from human gut suppresses EAE induction
- Changes in intestinal microbiota composition are associated with high levels of intestinal Th17 cells and enhanced disease activity in MS

## Relevance to Gut-Metal-Microbiome Axis

This meta-analysis supports the [[gut-metal-microbiome]] model by demonstrating that:

1. **Restoring beneficial bacteria reverses MS-associated immune dysregulation** - if [[heavy-metals]] deplete these protective species, [[probiotics]] could partially compensate
2. **Treg/Th17 rebalancing** by probiotics mirrors the immune correction needed after metal-induced [[dysbiosis]]
3. **The gut microbiota is a modifiable target** for MS therapy, supporting the concept that environmental exposures (including metals) that disrupt the microbiome are upstream disease drivers
4. **Specific strains** (*Lactobacillus*, *Bifidobacterium*) that show benefit are among those most sensitive to [[cadmium]], [[lead]], and [[nickel]] exposure

## Limitations

- Significant heterogeneity between studies (high I-squared values)
- Only 6 human studies available; most evidence from EAE models
- Probiotic strains, doses, and durations varied widely
- Publication bias possible
- Human studies were small with short follow-up
