## Detailed Summary

This conference presentation summarized emerging evidence that metformin, the most widely prescribed anti-diabetic drug, exerts a significant portion of its therapeutic effect through modulation of the gut microbiome. Prior to this recognition, many studies reporting "T2D-associated microbiome signatures" were confounded by metformin use among diabetic participants. The Gothenburg group and others demonstrated that when metformin users are separated from non-users, the T2D microbiome signature changes substantially.

Metformin's microbiome effects include increased abundance of short-chain fatty acid producers, modulation of bile acid metabolism through altered bacterial bile salt hydrolase activity, and enhancement of mucin-degrading bacteria like [[akkermansia-muciniphila]]. These microbial changes improve glucose tolerance, gut barrier integrity, and GLP-1 secretion, contributing to metformin's therapeutic profile.

The concept of pharmacomicrobiomics — understanding how the microbiome transforms drugs and how drugs transform the microbiome — has implications beyond diabetes, potentially relevant to any orally administered medication.
