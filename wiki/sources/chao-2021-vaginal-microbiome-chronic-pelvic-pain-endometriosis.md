# Key Claims

- EM/AM-associated chronic pelvic pain (CPP) has significantly higher vaginal microbiome alpha diversity than CPPS without EM/AM and women without CPPS
- *Clostridium butyricum*, *Clostridium disporicum*, *Alloscardovia omnicolens*, and *Veilonella montpellierensis* were enriched in EM/AM-associated CPP
- *Lactobacillus jensenii*, *Lactobacillus reuteri*, and *Lactobacillus iners* were significantly reduced in EM/AM-CPP patients
- When *Clostridium disporicum* exceeded 0.001105% and *L. reuteri* was below 0.1911349%, diagnostic sensitivity was 81.08% and specificity 52.0%
- Combining vaginal biomarkers with serum CA125 increased sensitivity to 89.19%

# Methods/Findings

- **Study design**: Case-control at Peking Union Medical College Hospital
- **Participants**: 37 EM/AM-CPP (group A), 25 CPPS without EM/AM (group B), 66 normal controls (group C)
- **Methods**: 16S rRNA V4 sequencing (Illumina HiSeq2500), PICRUSt functional profiling
- **Key results**:
  - Group A had 626 species; Group B had 409; Group C had 465
  - ANOSIM: R=0.122, P=0.002 between groups A and C
  - LEfSe identified distinct clades for each group
  - PICRUSt: 7 differentially regulated pathways between the 3 groups
  - Prospective validation: sensitivity 70%, specificity 53.62% for combined biomarkers

# Relevance to Wiki

This study identifies vaginal microbiome-based [[biomarkers]] that can differentiate [[endometriosis]]/adenomyosis-associated chronic pelvic pain from other causes. The depletion of protective *Lactobacillus* species and enrichment of anaerobic pathogens in EM/AM patients aligns with broader [[dysbiosis]] patterns in endometriosis. The combination of microbial markers with CA125 for non-invasive diagnosis represents a practical clinical application of microbiome research in [[endometriosis]].
