# Key Claims

- Dietary polyphenols (berries, green tea, curcumin, stilbenes) have been most extensively tested in rodent colitis models, with the majority showing inhibition of colitis
- EGCG (green tea) shows a U-shaped dose-response: low doses (0.01-0.1%) are protective, but high doses (0.5-1%) exacerbate colitis in rodents
- Translating polyphenol doses from rodent models to human equivalent doses reveals that supplemental doses are most likely required -- dietary intake alone is insufficient
- Curcumin, quercetin, and resveratrol reduce colonic [[inflammation]] by suppressing NF-kappaB, reducing TNF-alpha, IL-6, and MPO activity, and enhancing barrier function
- Species differences in xenobiotic metabolism and microbiota limit direct translation from rodent models to human clinical applications

# Methods/Findings

- **Study type**: Comprehensive review of polyphenol studies in rodent IBD models (DSS, TNBS, IL-10-/-, Mdr1a-/- models)
- **Polyphenol classes tested** (Table 1): Anthocyanins, flavan-3-ols, isoflavones, proanthocyanidins, flavanones, flavonols, stilbenes, curcuminoids, hydroxycinnamates, ellagitannins
- **EGCG dose-response** (Figure 2A): Human equivalent doses <500 mg/day protective; >2000 mg/day potentially detrimental
- **Green tea extract** (Figure 2B): Similar U-shaped curve; doses <1000 mg/day HED protective
- **Quercetin**: 50 mg/kg oral in BALB/c mice inhibited DSS-colitis; reduced IL-1beta, IL-6, TNF-alpha; naringenin at 0.3% similarly effective
- **Resveratrol**: Reduced colitis in DSS and TNBS models; decreased NF-kappaB binding; but variable effects across dose ranges
- **Key limitation**: Polyphenols are extensively catabolized by gut microbiota, and microbial metabolites may have higher anti-inflammatory activity than precursors
