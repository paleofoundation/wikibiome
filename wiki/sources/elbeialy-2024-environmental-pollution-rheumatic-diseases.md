# Key Claims

- [[lead]], [[cadmium]], and [[chromium]] are significantly elevated in both RA and FMS patients compared to controls (p < 0.001); [[aluminum|aluminium]] shows no significant difference
- [[vitamin-d-supplementation]] is significantly decreased in RA (21.84 ng/ml) and FMS (23.93 ng/ml) vs controls (27.99 ng/ml)
- PTH is significantly increased in RA (77.03 pg/ml) vs both FMS and controls (p < 0.001)
- Heavy metals correlate directly with disease activity (DAS28) in RA and symptom severity (SSS, WPI, VAS) in FMS
- Vitamin D inversely correlates with all heavy metals and disease activity measures in both conditions
- Chronic vitamin D3 deficiency and secondary hyperparathyroidism may play important roles in RA and FMS pathogenesis

# Methods/Findings

- **Design**: Comparative analytical case-control study; 60 adults (20 RA, 20 FMS, 20 controls), Al-Zahraa University Hospital, Cairo, Egypt (Nov 2021 - Sept 2023)
- **Same study cohort** as the Haddad 2024 preprint, published as peer-reviewed version in BMC Rheumatology
- **Measurements**: Serum Pb, Al, Cd, Cr by ELISA; vitamin D3 and PTH
- RA group: Lead 9.66, Cd 0.055, Cr 0.53, Al 4.99 ug/dl
- No significant difference between RF positive and negative RA patients for metal levels
- Correlation analysis: VitD inversely correlated with PTH (r=-0.885), Lead (r=-0.969), Cd (r=-0.901), Cr (r=-0.925) in RA
- Lead, Cd, Cr directly correlated with DAS28, tender/swollen joints, PGA in RA (p < 0.001)
