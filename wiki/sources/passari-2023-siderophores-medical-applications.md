## Key Findings

- **Siderophores have therapeutic applications beyond antimicrobials** — desferrioxamine (DFO) inhibits *P. falciparum* growth by iron deprivation; multiple siderophore classes show anticancer activity ([[passari-2023-siderophores-medical-applications]], expert-opinion).
- **Enterobactin causes [[iron]]-depletion-driven cancer cell apoptosis** — bacterial siderophores can be repurposed to starve tumors of iron, paralleling nutritional immunity.
- **Siderophore-hapten conjugates function as uropathogenic [[escherichia-coli]] vaccines** — exploiting obligate siderophore expression to target immune responses specifically to pathogens.
- **Iron chelation therapy (DFO) has antimalarial activity** by depriving *Plasmodium falciparum* of the iron it requires for intraerythrocytic development.

## Detailed Summary

Passari et al. review the expanding medical applications of siderophores — small molecules evolved by bacteria to scavenge iron from the environment — arguing that these molecules have therapeutic potential far beyond their role in infection.

**Antimalarial application.** Desferrioxamine (DFO, trade name Desferal), originally developed as a bacterial siderophore from *Streptomyces pilosus*, has demonstrated antimalarial activity in both in-vitro and clinical studies. *P. falciparum* requires iron for its intraerythrocytic development — the parasite's rapid multiplication inside red blood cells demands iron for ribonucleotide reductase and other essential enzymes. DFO chelates iron in the parasite's environment, starving it of this essential cofactor. This is effectively [[nutritional-immunity]] applied pharmacologically to a eukaryotic pathogen, paralleling the bacterial applications described in [[caruffo-2020-deferiprone-nutritional-immunity-piscirickettsia]].

**Anticancer mechanisms.** The review highlights multiple pathways by which siderophores exert anticancer effects: (1) **Direct iron deprivation** — enterobactin and pyoverdine can deplete iron from cancer cells, triggering apoptosis through mechanisms related to ferroptosis but distinct from it (iron-depletion-driven rather than iron-excess-driven cell death); (2) **ROS generation** — some siderophore-iron complexes undergo redox cycling that generates reactive oxygen species selectively in cancer cells; (3) **Anti-angiogenic effects** — iron chelation by siderophores inhibits prolyl hydroxylase domain enzymes, affecting HIF-1α signaling and vascular endothelial growth factor production.

**Vaccine platform.** A particularly innovative application uses siderophore-hapten conjugates as vaccines against uropathogenic *[[escherichia-coli]]* (UPEC). The rationale is elegant: since UPEC must express siderophore receptors (IroN for salmochelin, FyuA for yersiniabactin) to survive in the iron-restricted urinary tract, antibodies targeting siderophore-receptor complexes will recognize every virulent strain. This exploits the same obligate iron dependency that [[chairatana-2015-salmochelin-pathogen-selective-killing]] leverages for drug delivery.

**Imaging applications.** Siderophores can be loaded with radiometals (68Ga, 89Zr) for PET imaging of bacterial infections, enabling non-invasive detection of infection sites by tracking where bacteria are actively scavenging iron — a diagnostic application of the same siderophore biology.
