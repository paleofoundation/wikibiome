# Diet in Parkinson's Disease: Critical Role for the Microbiome

## Summary

This review examines how dietary patterns influence [[parkinsons-disease]] outcomes through modulation of the gut [[microbiome]]. It covers the Mediterranean diet, ketogenic diet, high-fiber diets, and specific dietary components (polyphenols, omega-3 fatty acids) and their effects on PD-associated microbial taxa and metabolites.

## Key Findings

- Mediterranean diet adherence correlates with reduced PD risk and less severe symptoms
- High-fiber diets increase [[short-chain-fatty-acids]] production and support SCFA-producing bacteria depleted in PD
- Ketogenic diets show preliminary benefits for PD motor symptoms, possibly through beta-hydroxybutyrate's neuroprotective effects
- Polyphenols (from berries, green tea, coffee) promote growth of beneficial gut bacteria and have anti-inflammatory properties
- Protein intake timing affects levodopa absorption and should be managed in PD patients
- Dietary patterns that reduce gut [[dysbiosis]] may slow PD progression via the [[gut-brain-axis]]
- [[Probiotics]] and [[prebiotics]] represent targeted dietary interventions for PD microbiome modulation

## Relevance to Gut-Metal-Microbiome Axis

Diet is the primary modifiable input to the gut-metal-microbiome axis. Dietary fiber intake directly determines [[butyrate]] production, which regulates [[iron]] absorption through HIF-2alpha. The Mediterranean diet, rich in polyphenols, provides natural metal chelators that can bind [[iron]], [[nickel]], and [[lead]] in the gut lumen, reducing their absorption. Conversely, Western diets high in processed foods increase [[nickel]] and other metal exposure while reducing fiber intake. The ketogenic diet's effects on PD may partly operate through altered [[iron]] metabolism, as ketone bodies influence hepcidin expression. Dietary protein competition with levodopa for absorption also involves metal-dependent amino acid transporters (DMT1) that co-transport [[iron]] and [[manganese]], creating a direct link between diet, metal absorption, and PD medication efficacy.

## Limitations

- Review article; does not present primary data
- Most dietary intervention studies in PD are small and short-term
- Adherence to dietary changes is challenging in PD patients with dysphagia and motor impairments
- Optimal dietary recommendations for PD remain undefined
