# Effects of Trace Elements on Endocrine Function and Pathogenesis of Thyroid Diseases — A Literature Review

## Key Findings

- **Iron is essential for TPO activity** and thyroid hormone synthesis; Fe deficiency reduces plasma T3/T4 and increases TSH; 58% of [[hashimotos-thyroiditis]] patients have Fe deficiency anemia and 75% have vitamin D deficiency; MRI studies show increased Fe deposition in the brain of GD patients with ophthalmopathy ([[brylinski-2025-trace-elements-thyroid-diseases]], expert opinion)
- **Cadmium disrupts thyroid hormone metabolism** through dual mechanisms: inhibiting hepatic 5'-monodeiodinase (5'-D) activity (interfering with T4-to-T3 conversion, causing T4 increase with no T3 change) and triggering UGT activity (increasing T3/T4 metabolism and lowering plasma concentrations); Cd also decreases thyroid follicle size and epithelial thickness ([[brylinski-2025-trace-elements-thyroid-diseases]], expert opinion)
- **Selenium builds all three deiodinases** (DIO1, DIO2, DIO3) essential for T4-to-T3 conversion, and is a component of glutathione peroxidases (GPx) and thioredoxin reductase (TrxR) protecting thyrocytes from oxidative damage; Se deficiency is a risk factor for both Hashimoto's and Graves' disease ([[brylinski-2025-trace-elements-thyroid-diseases]], expert opinion)
- **Cobalt irradiation treats severe Graves' ophthalmopathy** with an overall response rate of 96% and patient satisfaction of 98%; however, Co also causes hypothyroidism by decreasing iodine uptake in the thyroid gland, and nickel-cobalt cross-allergy increases contact allergy risk threefold ([[brylinski-2025-trace-elements-thyroid-diseases]], expert opinion)
- **Copper has opposing effects by disease state**: increased serum Cu appears positively associated with hyperthyroidism in GD; low Cu levels may contribute to Graves' orbitopathy; cuproptosis (Cu-dependent cell death via FDX1) is an emerging mechanism in thyroid cancer; the disulfiram/Cu (DSF/Cu) complex inhibits cancer stem cell activity ([[brylinski-2025-trace-elements-thyroid-diseases]], expert opinion)

## Methods

- **Study type**: Comprehensive literature review covering 13 trace elements (Fe, Cu, Co, I, Mn, Zn, Ag, Cd, Hg, Pb, Se, plus Ca indirectly via Cu) across 7 thyroid disease categories (Graves' disease, Hashimoto's thyroiditis, hypothyroidism, autoimmune thyroiditis, thyroid nodules, thyroid cancer, postpartum thyroiditis)
- **Structure**: Organized element-by-element, with subsections for each thyroid disease
- **Comprehensive Table 1**: Maps each trace element to its mechanism of action, influence on TH synthesis/metabolism, and additional information
- **Coverage**: 54 pages with extensive referencing across clinical, animal, and in vitro studies

## Detailed Summary

**Iron and thyroid.** Fe enters the composition of TPO (heme-dependent enzyme). TPO catalyzes H2O2-dependent oxidation reactions converting iodide to iodonium intermediate compound. Fe deficiency results in decreased TPO activity, consequent decrease in T3/T4 synthesis, and compensatory TSH increase. Studies show Fe levels positively correlated with FT3 and FT4 in pregnant women. In GD specifically: MRI showed increased Fe deposition in the brain of patients with associated ophthalmopathy; GD patients are at additional risk of autoimmune atrophic gastritis (impairing Fe absorption). In thyroid cancer: Fe may facilitate tumor cell proliferation through hepcidin (increasing intracellular Fe); GPX4 identified as ferroptosis regulator.

**Copper and thyroid.** Cu stimulates T4 production and controls calcium levels in the body. Cu/Zn ratio is higher in thyroid cancer patients. In GD: increased serum Cu appears positively associated with hyperthyroidism; in stable euthyroidism or subclinical post-treatment GD, no Cu difference; low Cu may contribute to orbitopathy. In HT: patients showed higher serum Cu, lower Cu/Zn ratio. Cuproptosis: a new form of Cu-dependent cell death via FDX1 and lipoylation pathway; DSF/Cu complex kills thyroid cancer cells through ROS-dependent suppression of MAPK/ERK and PI3K/AKT pathways.

**Cobalt and thyroid.** Co is a component of vitamin B12. In GD: Co-60 irradiation (external beam radiotherapy, EBRT) for severe GO achieves 96% response rate, 98% patient satisfaction; relatively safe with low risk of radiation retinopathy. Co causes hypothyroidism by decreasing iodine uptake; hip replacement prostheses with Co-chromium can release Co into bloodstream causing thyroid dysfunction (treatable with chelation: EDTA, DMPS, dimercaprol). Nickel-Co cross-allergy increases contact allergy risk 3-fold.

**Iodine and thyroid.** Iodine is a component of T3/T4 structure. Recommended daily intake: 150 ug/day for adults, 250 ug for pregnant women. Excess I inhibits TH synthesis (Wolff-Chaikoff effect) via XBP1-mediated repression of NIS, TPO, Tg, and NKX2.1 genes. In GD: higher incidence linked to higher dietary I levels; iodine-induced hyperthyroidism (Jod-Basedow) in iodine-deficient populations; I-131 radioactive therapy achieves 88.88% remission at 12 months. In HT: excess I causes increased pyroptosis via NF-kappaB/NLRP3 inflammasome; promotes Th1 and Th17 cell accumulation in thyroid. Complex dose-response: I at low concentrations activates AKT/ERK in thyroid cancer cells (promoting growth), while high concentrations inhibit migration.

**Manganese and thyroid.** Mn deficiency increases hepatic 5'-deiodinase activity and relative T3 concentration. Excessive Mn enlarges the thyroid gland. Elevated Mn in autoimmune hypothyroidism may be an indicator of disease risk. In thyroid cancer: higher blood Mn levels; Mn-SOD reduced in affected tissues; MnO2/CDDP@PDA-Cy5.5 nanoparticles show anticancer potential.

**Zinc and thyroid.** Zn deficiency affects TRH, TSH, T3, and T4 production; Zn is a cofactor for >300 metalloenzymes. Insufficient Zn supplementation causes hypothyroidism. The relationship between Zn and thyroid function is mutual. Zn is important for thymus function and T cell regulation.

**Cadmium and thyroid.** Cd decreases thyroid follicle size and epithelial thickness. Cd inhibits 5'-monodeiodinase: T4 levels rise with no T3 change. Cd exposure triggers UGT activity, increasing T3/T4 metabolism and lowering plasma concentrations. Cd decomposition in the brain may affect HPT axis extrusion.

**Mercury and thyroid.** MeHg interferes with TSH production. Inorganic Hg inhibits TPO production. Both MeHg and inorganic compounds inhibit Tg iodination. In gold miners occupationally exposed: TSH slightly elevated, T3/T4 significantly reduced in exposed group. A meta-analysis showed Hg in blood could increase TSH and FT4 while negatively affecting T4.

**Lead and thyroid.** Pb prevents deiodination, obstructing T4-to-T3 conversion. TSH is down while T4 is rising. Pb disrupts cellular energy production and causes DNA damage in thyroid cells.

## Relevance to Cureva

- Fe supplementation in AITD: adequate Fe may prevent autoimmune thyroiditis in women of reproductive age, but Fe feeds tumor cells via hepcidin — context-dependent intervention
- Se supplementation: component of GPx, TrxR, and DIOs; Se deficiency is a risk factor for both HT and GD — triangle evidence for Se intervention
- STOP signal: unmonitored Fe supplementation in thyroid cancer patients could feed ferroptosis resistance and tumor growth
- Co-60 for severe GO: 96% response rate provides intervention evidence, though Co-induced hypothyroidism is a trade-off
- Cd/Hg exposure assessment should be part of AITD workup — environmental trigger identification

## Contradictions / Tensions

- Cu effects are inconsistent: some studies show positive association between Cu and hyperthyroidism, others find no difference in Cu between hypothyroid and autoimmune thyroiditis groups; Cu may be an acute-phase reactant confounding observational studies
- Iodine has a complex U-shaped relationship: both deficiency and excess increase AITD risk through different mechanisms
- Hg effects on thyroid are inconsistent between meta-analyses — some show TSH increase while others show TSH and FT4 changes in opposite directions
- Fe and Cu dual roles — essential for thyroid function but also exploitable by tumors (ferroptosis resistance, Cu-dependent tumor survival)
