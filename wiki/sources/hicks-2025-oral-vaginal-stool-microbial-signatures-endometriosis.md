# Key Claims

- Oral and stool microbiota composition differed significantly between endometriosis patients, non-endometriosis patients, and healthy controls (PERMANOVA p<0.05)
- Vaginal microbiota showed no significant compositional differences between groups
- *Fusobacterium* was enriched in oral samples of patients with moderate/severe [[endometriosis]]
- *Escherichia* was enriched in vaginal samples of endometriosis patients, consistent with the [[bacterial-contamination-hypothesis]]
- *Lactobacillus* was more prevalent in stool of endometriosis patients, potentially reflecting estrobolome activity

# Methods/Findings

- **Study design**: Prospective cross-sectional pilot study, Nepean Hospital, Australia
- **Participants**: 64 women (19 healthy controls, 24 confirmed no-endometriosis, 21 confirmed endometriosis)
- **Methods**: Self-collected oral, stool, and vaginal samples; 16S rRNA V3-V4 sequencing (Illumina MiSeq)

 - Stool: PERMANOVA p=0.003 (HC vs N-ENDO), p=0.042 (oral)
 - Alpha diversity: borderline differences in oral (p<0.1) and significant in stool (p<0.05)
 - 36 differentially abundant microbial features across all three body sites
 - Eight taxonomic features differed between minimal/mild vs moderate/severe endometriosis
 - *Actinomyces* enriched in stool of minimal/mild; *Cardiobacterium* in oral of minimal/mild
 - *Phascolarctobacterium* enriched in stool of endometriosis patients

# Relevance to Wiki

This multi-site microbiome study uniquely demonstrates that oral and stool microbiota may offer better diagnostic potential for [[endometriosis]] than vaginal samples. The enrichment of *Fusobacterium* in oral samples (a periodontal pathogen) suggests a link between endometriosis and periodontal disease. The *Escherichia* enrichment in vaginal samples aligns with the LPS/TLR4 pathway of endometriosis pathogenesis. These findings support non-invasive microbiome-based diagnostic approaches for a disease currently requiring surgical confirmation.
