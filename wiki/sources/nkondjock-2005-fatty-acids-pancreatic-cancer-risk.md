# Key Claims

- Statistically significant inverse associations were observed between [[pancreatic cancer]] risk and palmitate (OR 0.73, P-trend=0.02), stearate (OR 0.70, P-trend=0.02), oleate (OR 0.75, P-trend=0.04), total saturated FAs (OR 0.67, P-trend=0.01), and monounsaturated FAs (OR 0.72, P-trend=0.02)
- Among obese subjects (BMI >30), saturated and monounsaturated [[fatty acids]] showed strongly protective associations (saturated FAs OR 0.35, P-trend=0.002; monounsaturated FAs OR 0.32, P-trend=0.001)
- Substituting polyunsaturated or monounsaturated FAs for saturated FAs may reduce pancreatic cancer risk independently of total energy intake, particularly among obese subjects

# Methods/Findings

- **Study design**: Population-based case-control study; 462 PC cases and 4,721 frequency-matched controls across 8 Canadian provinces, 1994-1997
- **Dietary assessment**: Self-administered food frequency questionnaire (FFQ) with 69 food/beverage items
- **Individual fatty acids**: Palmitic acid (OR 0.70, highest vs lowest quartile), stearic acid (OR 0.73), oleic acid (OR 0.75); butyric, lauric, linoleic, alpha-linolenic, EPA, DHA, arachidonic acids showed no significant associations
- **BMI interaction**: Significant interaction between BMI and stearic acid (P=0.04) and oleic acid (P=0.04); among obese: stearic OR 0.36, oleic OR 0.36, saturated OR 0.35, monounsaturated OR 0.32
- **Potential mechanism**: High fat intake may stimulate cholecystokinin (CCK) release promoting pancreatic carcinogenesis; but unsaturated FAs may be weaker CCK stimulators
- **Confounders**: Adjustments for age, province, BMI, smoking, educational attainment, total fat, and total energy intake
