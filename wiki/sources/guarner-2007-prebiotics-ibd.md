# Key Claims

- Prebiotics (inulin, oligofructose) selectively stimulate beneficial bacteria growth and SCFA production in the gut
- Strong experimental evidence from animal models supports that inulin and oligofructose can prevent or mitigate intestinal inflammatory lesions in CD, UC, and pouchitis
- Prebiotics promote Bifidobacterium and Lactobacillus growth, species associated with reduced mucosal [[inflammation]] in IBD animal models
- Clinical results are encouraging but preliminary, with more rigorous trials needed
- Reduced proportion of Firmicutes in CD patients, with altered Bacteroidetes ratios, provides the rationale for prebiotic intervention

# Methods/Findings

- **Study type**: Review of experimental and clinical evidence for prebiotics in IBD
- **Animal models**: Inulin and oligofructose reduced mucosal inflammation in DSS, TNBS, and IL-10 knockout colitis models
- **Mechanism**: Prebiotics increase SCFA (acetate, propionate, butyrate) production; butyrate is primary energy source for colonocytes
- **Clinical evidence**: Preliminary trials in pouchitis (inulin supplementation with reduced PDAI scores) and UC (lactulose enemas)
- **Microbiota**: CD patients show reduced faecal microbial diversity with 30-40% of dominant species being atypical

# Connections

- SCFA production by prebiotic-stimulated bacteria including [[faecalibacterium-prausnitzii]] is central to barrier function in [[crohns-disease]]
- Prebiotic fiber may chelate dietary metals, affecting [[iron]] and [[zinc]] bioavailability in the [[gut-metal-microbiome]]
- [[probiotics]] combined with prebiotics (synbiotics) may offer synergistic benefits for [[dysbiosis]] correction
- Butyrate production connects to [[ferroptosis]] protection through antioxidant effects on colonocytes
- [[bacteroides-fragilis]] polysaccharide interactions with the immune system may be modulated by prebiotic substrate availability
