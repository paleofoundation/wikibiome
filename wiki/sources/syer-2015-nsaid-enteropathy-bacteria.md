# Key Claims

- NSAID-induced small intestinal damage is more common and serious than previously appreciated, occurring with greater frequency than upper GI damage
- Gram-negative bacteria are particularly important in NSAID enteropathy pathogenesis through endotoxin release
- Antibiotic cocktails reduce NSAID-induced damage severity, but individual antibiotics (rifaximin, kanamycin, vancomycin) are inconsistently effective
- [[probiotics]] including Bifidobacterium, Lactobacillus, and [[faecalibacterium-prausnitzii]] show promise in preventing NSAID enteropathy in animal models
- Beneficial probiotic effects appear due to bacterial colonization rather than products released by bacteria

# Methods/Findings

- **Study type**: Review of NSAID enteropathy mechanisms and microbiome interactions
- **Key mechanisms**: NSAIDs impair mucus layer, increase permeability; bacterial LPS activates TLR4/NF-kB pathway; bile acid enterohepatic circulation affected
- **Antibiotic studies**: Cocktail of ampicillin, vancomycin, neomycin, metronidazole reduced naproxen damage; individual antibiotics less effective
- **16S sequencing**: Naproxen shifts microbiota from Firmicutes-dominant to Gram-negative dominated (Verrucomicrobia, Fusobacteria, Proteobacteria)
- **Probiotic evidence**: Bifidobacterium and Faecalibacterium colonization protective in animal models

# Connections

- NSAIDs are commonly used in conditions comorbid with [[crohns-disease]], creating additive enteropathy risk
- NSAID-induced [[dysbiosis]] parallels CD-associated microbiome shifts, with expansion of Proteobacteria and [[escherichia-coli]]
- Barrier disruption by NSAIDs connects to [[inflammation]] pathways shared with IBD
- [[probiotics]] may serve dual purpose: protecting against both IBD flares and NSAID enteropathy
- Metal-dependent bacterial enzymes involved in bile acid metabolism connect to [[gut-metal-microbiome]] interactions
