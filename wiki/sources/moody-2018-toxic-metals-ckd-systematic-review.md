# Key Claims

- [[arsenic]] exposure is moderately associated with CKD; the two highest-quality prospective studies both showed positive associations
- Evidence for [[cadmium]]-CKD association is extremely limited from prospective studies; one large Swedish cohort (n=74,307) found no association with dietary Cd
- Evidence for [[lead]]-CKD association is mixed; two large Swedish cohort studies reported conflicting results on ESRD
- Overall, the literature is small (only 8 studies met inclusion criteria: 3 for As, 2 for Cd, 4 for Pb)
- Low-level exposure to toxic metals may contribute to increased nephrotoxicity with public health significance
- Major methodological gaps: inconsistent CKD definitions, inadequate covariate adjustment, small sample sizes

# Methods/Findings

- **Type**: Systematic review following PRISMA guidelines
- **Search**: PubMed; As and Cd from Jan 2013-April 2018; Pb with no date restriction
- **Inclusion criteria**: Occupational/environmental exposure; individual-level measurements; temporality requirement; CKD per NKF-KDOQI guidelines
- **Arsenic studies**: Hsu et al. (Taiwan, n=6,093, positive dose-response); Zheng et al. (American Indian, n=3,119, borderline significant)
- **Cadmium studies**: Thomas et al. (Sweden, n=74,307, no association with dietary Cd); Sommar et al. (Sweden, n=118 ESRD cases, elevated erythrocyte Cd OR increased)
- **Lead studies**: Large Swedish occupational cohort (n=10,303) found no association; smaller Taiwan study (n=121) found positive association with CKD progression
- Key recommendation: future studies need serial monitoring, harmonized exposure biomarkers, consistent CKD definitions
