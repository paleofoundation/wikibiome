# The Role of the Microbiome in Ovarian Cancer: Oncobiosis and Bacterial Metabolite Signaling

## Key Claims

- Ovarian cancer is characterized by [[dysbiosis]] (oncobiosis) identified in tumor tissue, upper and lower genital tract, serum, peritoneum, and intestines
- Colonization was linked to Gram-negative bacteria with high inflammatory potential; local inflammation participates in carcinogenesis initiation
- Bacterial metabolites (LPS, SCFAs, bile acids, indole derivatives, lysophosphatides, tryptophan metabolites) can exert autocrine, paracrine, and hormone-like effects on ovarian cancer
- The oncobiome and bacterial metabolites play key roles in mediating chemotherapy effectiveness
- Vaginal infections (e.g., Neisseria gonorrhoeae, Chlamydia trachomatis) increase risk of developing ovarian cancer

## Methods/Findings

- **Study type**: Comprehensive review of microbiome-OC interactions
- **Compartments**: Oncobiosis identified in tumor tissue itself, vaginal, cervical, peritoneal, gut, and serum compartments
- **Metabolites covered**: LPS, SCFAs, secondary bile acids, polyamines, indole derivatives, lysophosphatidic acid, tryptophan metabolites
- **Therapeutic implications**: Probiotics, antibiotics, and nutrients discussed as microbiome-modulating strategies
