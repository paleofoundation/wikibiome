# Integrated Analyses of Microbiome and Longitudinal Metabolomic Data Reveal Microbial-Host Interactions on Sulfur Metabolism in Parkinson's Disease

## Summary

This study integrates microbiome and metabolomic data using constraint-based metabolic modeling to identify microbial-host metabolic interactions in [[parkinsons-disease]]. It reveals that sulfur metabolism is a key axis of microbiome-host interaction disrupted in PD.

## Key Findings

- Sulfur amino acid metabolism is significantly altered at the microbiome-host interface in PD
- Hydrogen sulfide production by gut bacteria is reduced in PD, consistent with depletion of sulfate-reducing bacteria
- [[tryptophan-metabolism]] shows altered microbial-host partitioning in PD
- Bile acid metabolism is disrupted through altered microbial bile salt hydrolase activity
- Constraint-based modeling predicts metabolic flux changes between microbiome and host
- Longitudinal metabolomic data show progressive metabolic deterioration
- The integrated approach reveals metabolic interactions invisible to microbiome or metabolomic analysis alone

## Relevance to Gut-Metal-Microbiome Axis

Sulfur metabolism is intimately connected to metal homeostasis. Hydrogen sulfide (H2S) produced by gut bacteria serves as a gasotransmitter that regulates [[iron]] metabolism, and its reduction in PD may contribute to [[iron]] dysregulation. Sulfur-containing amino acids (cysteine, methionine) are precursors to glutathione, the primary cellular defense against metal-induced oxidative stress and [[ferroptosis]]. The PD-associated reduction in microbial sulfur metabolism may deplete the cysteine supply needed for glutathione synthesis, rendering neurons more vulnerable to [[iron]]- and [[manganese]]-catalyzed oxidative damage. Bile acid alterations affect [[iron]] absorption by modulating the expression of intestinal iron transporters. This systems-level analysis demonstrates the value of integrating metabolomic and microbiome data to understand the metal-microbiome axis.

## Limitations

- Computational modeling predictions require experimental validation
- Model assumptions may not capture all biological complexity
- Sulfur metabolism measurements in feces may not reflect mucosal or systemic levels
- Limited by available constraint-based metabolic models
