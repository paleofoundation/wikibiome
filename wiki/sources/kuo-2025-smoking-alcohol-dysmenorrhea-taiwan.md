# Key Claims

- Neither tobacco smoking alone (OR = 1.21, 95% CI: 0.89-1.63) nor alcohol drinking alone (OR = 1.06, 95% CI: 0.53-2.13) significantly increased [[dysmenorrhea]] risk
- However, concurrent use of both alcohol and tobacco dramatically increased risk: adjusted OR = 3.19 (95% CI: 1.51-6.72, p = 0.002)
- The synergistic effect was even more pronounced in women aged 50 and above: OR = 7.26 (95% CI: 1.58-33.45)
- Regular exercise was protective against dysmenorrhea: OR = 0.77 (95% CI: 0.64-0.93)
- Early menarche (<12 years) was associated with increased risk: OR = 1.37 (95% CI: 1.14-1.64)
- Higher education level was paradoxically associated with increased risk: OR = 1.43 (95% CI: 1.19-1.71)

# Methods/Findings

- **Design**: Cross-sectional analysis of the Taiwan Biobank (TWB)
- **Participants**: 8,567 women from 29,621 in the TWB dataset (excluded those without dysmenorrhea data or missing variables)
- **Substance use groups**: Non-users 92.1% (n=7,891); tobacco-only 5.8% (n=496); alcohol-only 1.4% (n=121); both 0.7% (n=59)
- **Dysmenorrhea prevalence**: 7.5% overall (589/8,567)
- **Statistical analysis**: Multivariable logistic regression adjusted for age, residential urbanicity, education, BMI, behavioral factors, gynecological problems, menarche age, parity, and health products

 - Women <50: dual use OR = 2.57 (1.13-5.83)
 - Women >=50: dual use OR = 7.26 (1.58-33.45)
- **Protective factors**: Regular exercise (OR = 0.77), multiparity (OR = 0.64, 95% CI: 0.52-0.77)
- **Risk factors**: Uterine problems (OR = 1.50, 95% CI: 1.23-1.82), early menarche, higher education
- **Mechanisms discussed**: Smoking causes vasoconstriction and increases prostaglandins; alcohol disrupts hormonal balance, stimulates vasopressin release, depletes magnesium, and enhances inflammatory response
- **Limitations**: Cross-sectional design; self-reported data; no differentiation between primary and secondary dysmenorrhea; Taiwanese population may limit generalizability
