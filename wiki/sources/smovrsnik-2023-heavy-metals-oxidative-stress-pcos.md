# Key Claims

- Women with [[pcos]] have increased blood levels of antimony (Sb), [[cadmium]] (Cd), [[lead]] (Pb), [[mercury]] (Hg), arsenic (As), tellurium (Te), and thallium (Tl) and decreased [[zinc]] (Zn) levels compared to controls
- Results for [[copper]] (Cu) are conflicting across studies
- Heavy metals (Sb, Cd, Pb, Hg) and essential elements (Zn, Cr, Se, Ca, Mg, Cu) are associated with metabolic and hormonal characteristics of PCOS
- [[Oxidative stress]] markers (TOS, MDA, hs-CRP, TNF-alpha) are elevated and antioxidant markers (TAS, SOD, OSI) are decreased in PCOS women
- Supplementation with Zn, Cr, Se, Mg, and Ca shows potential benefits for reducing OS and improving endocrinological parameters in PCOS

# Methods/Findings

- **Design**: Systematic review following PRISMA guidelines; registered in PROSPERO (CRD42023418453)
- **Search**: PubMed from January 2008 to April 2023
- **Included studies**: 15 studies meeting inclusion criteria (from 35 initially identified)
- **Study types**: Case-control studies, prospective studies, and randomized double-blind placebo-controlled trials

 - Sb: Higher in PCOS (2.5 +/- 0.23 vs 1.89 +/- 0.31 ppb) in two studies
 - Cd: Elevated in PCOS (1.2 vs 0.7 ppb; 1.75 vs 0.59 ppb) in two studies
 - Pb: Elevated in PCOS (23.1 vs 15.5 ppb; 83.19 vs 36.69 ppb) in two studies
 - Hg: Elevated in PCOS in two studies
 - Te, Tl, Os: Elevated in one study

 - Zn: Consistently lower in PCOS (lower CAT activity, higher MDA)
 - Se supplementation: Higher pregnancy rates, decreased alopecia/hirsutism, lower FSH
 - Cr supplementation: Decreased FBG, insulin, HOMA-IR, TG, VLDL, cholesterol; increased QUICKI
 - Mg supplementation: Reduced weight, BMI, waist circumference, TNF-alpha
 - Ca + vitamin D: Decreased LH and hs-CRP
- **Oxidative stress markers**: Kirmizi et al. (2020) found lower TAS, OSI, SOD and higher TOS, MDA, hs-CRP, TNF-alpha in PCOS vs controls; positive correlation between Sb, Pb, Cd and MDA, TNF-alpha, HOMA-IR
- **Mechanism**: Heavy metals interact with sulfhydryl groups in the non-enzymic antioxidant system (GSH), forming organo-metallic complexes; metals can disrupt insulin gene promoter activity, gonadotropin, and reproductive hormone levels
- **Limitations**: Limited number of studies; heterogeneous methodologies; varying sample types and metal panels
