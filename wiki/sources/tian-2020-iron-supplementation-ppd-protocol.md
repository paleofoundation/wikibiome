# Key Claims

- [[iron]] supplementation has emerged as a **potential means to treat PPD** because of the documented association between iron deficiency anemia, low ferritin, and PPD
- Iron deficiency can change concentrations of cytochrome C, [[dopamine]], [[serotonin]], and gamma-aminobutyric acid, with adverse effects on brain function
- As iron stores decrease, risk of developing PPD increases; many PPD patients have depleted iron stores
- A positive association between early iron supplementation and decreased EPDS scores has been reported
- This is identified as the **first systematic review and meta-analysis protocol** specifically examining iron supplementation RCTs for PPD

# Methods/Findings

- **Design**: Protocol for systematic review and meta-analysis (INPLASY202011007)
- **Eligibility**: All RCTs of iron supplementation for PPD, without language or nationality restrictions
- **Intervention**: Iron supplementation (any route, dosage, or duration) vs. control (psychological interventions, pharmacological, or no treatment)
- **Primary outcome**: EPDS score
- **Secondary outcome**: Improvement rate
- **Databases to search**: PubMed, Embase, Cochrane Library, Web of Science, China National Knowledge Infrastructure, China Science and Technology Journal, Chinese Biomedical Literature Database (through December 31, 2020)
- **Planned analysis**: Review Manager 5.3; RR with 95% CI for dichotomous variables; mean difference with 95% CI for continuous variables; I-squared for heterogeneity; random effects if I-squared > 50%; subgroup analysis for participant characteristics, administration routes, dosage, and duration
- **Risk of bias**: Cochrane risk of bias assessment tool
- **Reporting bias**: Funnel plot and Egger regression if >= 10 trials included
