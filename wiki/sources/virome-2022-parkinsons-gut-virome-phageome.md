# Gut Virome and Phageome in Parkinson's Disease

## Summary

This study characterizes the gut [[virome]] (including the phageome -- bacteriophage component) in [[parkinsons-disease]], contributing to the growing body of evidence that viral communities in the gut are disrupted in PD.

## Key Findings

- The gut virome composition differs significantly between PD patients and healthy controls
- Bacteriophage communities showed altered predator-prey dynamics in PD
- Eukaryotic viruses in the gut were also altered, though less dramatically than phages
- A substantial proportion of viral sequences remained unclassified ("viral dark matter")
- Virome alterations correlated with bacterial dysbiosis patterns, supporting phage-driven bacterial community shifts
- The phageome may represent an earlier and more dynamic indicator of dysbiosis than bacterial composition

## Relevance to Gut-Metal-Microbiome Axis

The gut [[virome]] adds a critical regulatory layer to the metallomic-microbiome framework in [[parkinsons-disease]]. Bacteriophages shape bacterial community composition through selective predation, and this predation is influenced by metal availability (phage enzymes require divalent cations, metal stress can induce prophage activation). The phage-driven depletion of metal-buffering commensals creates a metal-permissive environment for pathobionts. Understanding the virome is essential for a complete picture of how metal [[dyshomeostasis]] drives and sustains [[dysbiosis]] in PD.

## Limitations

- Virome characterization is technically more challenging than bacterial profiling
- High proportion of uncharacterized viral sequences limits interpretation
- Small sample sizes typical of virome studies
- Virome databases are underdeveloped compared to bacterial databases
