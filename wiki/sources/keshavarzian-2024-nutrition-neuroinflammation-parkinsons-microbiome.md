# Nutrition, Neuroinflammation, and the Parkinson's Disease Microbiome

## Summary

This review examines how nutritional factors modulate [[neuroinflammation]] and the gut [[microbiome]] in [[parkinsons-disease]], providing a framework for dietary interventions that target the microbiome-inflammation-neurodegeneration axis.

## Key Findings

- Dietary patterns strongly influence both gut microbiome composition and neuroinflammatory markers in PD
- Mediterranean diet is associated with reduced PD risk and less severe dysbiosis
- Ketogenic diet shows preliminary neuroprotective effects, possibly through microbiome modulation
- Dietary fiber (prebiotics) promotes SCFA production and anti-inflammatory microbiome states
- Polyphenols (from fruits, tea, coffee) have both direct antioxidant and microbiome-modulating effects
- Western diet (high fat, low fiber, processed foods) promotes pro-inflammatory dysbiosis

## Relevance to Gut-Metal-Microbiome Axis

Dietary interventions for [[parkinsons-disease]] can target the metallomic-microbiome axis at multiple points. High-fiber diets bind dietary metals ([[iron]], [[zinc]], [[manganese]]) reducing free metal availability to pathobionts while feeding SCFA producers. Mediterranean diet's high polyphenol content chelates metals and promotes beneficial bacteria. The ketogenic diet may reduce [[iron]]-dependent oxidative stress by shifting energy metabolism away from mitochondrial complex I. Critically, a "low-metal, high-fiber" dietary framework would address both metal exposure and microbiome health simultaneously -- potentially more effective than either approach alone.

## Limitations

- Review article
- Dietary intervention studies in PD are mostly observational or small-scale
- Dietary adherence is challenging for PD patients with motor and cognitive impairment
- Individual nutritional needs vary greatly among PD patients
