# Alterations of the Human Gut Microbiome in Multiple Sclerosis

## Summary

This landmark study used 16S rRNA sequencing to characterize the gut microbiome of 60 [[multiple-sclerosis]] patients and 43 healthy controls from the Brigham and Women's Hospital MS Center. It is one of the foundational papers establishing the MS-microbiome connection, employing both Roche 454 and Illumina MiSeq platforms for cross-validation.

## Key Findings

### Phylum-Level Changes
- Increased Euryarchaeota and Verrucomicrobia in MS patients at the phylum level
- These changes were confirmed by both sequencing platforms

### Genus-Level Alterations
**Increased in MS (disease effect):**
- *Methanobrevibacter* - archaeal methanogen, recruits inflammatory cells and DCs
- *Akkermansia* - mucin-degrading bacterium in phylum Verrucomicrobia

**Decreased in MS (disease effect):**
- *Butyricimonas* - [[short-chain-fatty-acids]]-producing bacterium

**Treatment effect (immunomodulatory therapy):**
- Treated patients showed increased *Prevotella* and *Sutterella*
- Treated patients showed decreased *Sarcina*

### Gene Expression Correlations
- *Methanobrevibacter* and *Akkermansia* positively correlated with genes involved in dendritic cell maturation, interferon signaling, and [[nf-kappa-b]] signaling pathways in circulating T cells and monocytes
- *Butyricimonas* had negative correlations with these inflammatory pathways
- In untreated MS patients, *Methanobrevibacter* and *Akkermansia* had positive correlations with CASP1, TRAF5, and STAT5, while *Butyricimonas* had negative correlations with genes implicated in IFN signaling, IL-2 signaling, and PPAR/RXR activation

### Serologic Analysis
- No significant differences in anti-*Methanobrevibacter* or anti-*Akkermansia* antibody titers between MS patients and controls

### Breath Methane
- MS patients showed elevated breath methane compared to controls (second cohort), consistent with increased gut *Methanobrevibacter*
- *Methanobrevibacter* is the dominant methane-producing archaeon in the human gut

## Mechanistic Insights

- *Methanobrevibacter*-derived lipopolysaccharide (LPS) activates inflammatory cells and DCs
- *Methanobrevibacter* adheres to the mucosa via regulated expression of adhesin-like proteins, placing it close to the gut-associated lymphoid tissue
- *Akkermansia* has been enriched in patients with inflammatory bowel disease and increased expression of resistant immune cells to microbial antigens
- *Butyricimonas* produces butyrate, a [[short-chain-fatty-acids]] with known anti-[[inflammation]] properties that supports Treg differentiation

## Relevance to Gut-Metal-Microbiome Axis

This study is critical for the [[gut-metal-microbiome]] framework because:

1. **Methanobrevibacter** - methanogens are known to be affected by [[heavy-metals]]; their increase in MS may reflect metal-driven competitive advantage over metal-sensitive SCFA producers
2. **Akkermansia increase** - [[cadmium]] exposure specifically upregulates *Akkermansia muciniphila* (as shown in Zhang et al. 2021), and this taxon is consistently increased in MS
3. **Butyricimonas depletion** - SCFA-producing bacteria are particularly sensitive to [[heavy-metals]] toxicity
4. **NF-kB pathway activation** - the [[nf-kappa-b]] signaling correlation with MS-enriched taxa connects to metal-induced inflammatory cascades
5. **Treatment partially normalizes** the microbiome, suggesting the [[dysbiosis]] is a modifiable risk factor

## Limitations

- Cross-sectional design cannot determine causality
- Cohort predominantly Caucasian females; limited generalizability
- 16S rRNA sequencing provides genus-level resolution but limited functional information
- Excluded patients with recent antibiotic use, IBD, or other confounders
