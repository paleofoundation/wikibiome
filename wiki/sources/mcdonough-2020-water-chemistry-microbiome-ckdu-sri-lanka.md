# Water Chemistry and Microbiome in CKDu-Endemic Sri Lanka

## Key Findings

- **Microcystis** — a cyanotoxin-producing cyanobacterium — was frequently detected in household well water in the CKDu-endemic Medawachchiya District, suggesting chronic low-level exposure to microcystins (potent hepatotoxins and potential nephrotoxins).
- The **total microbial content** and composition of drinking water varied substantially between wells and may influence the gut microbiome composition of consumers through daily ingestion.
- Several **bacterial taxa previously associated with CKD patient gut microbiomes** were also detected in household well water, raising the possibility that environmental water sources seed gut dysbiosis in affected communities.
- Water chemistry analysis revealed variable levels of heavy metals and other dissolved constituents, though the relationship between specific water chemistry parameters and CKDu prevalence was complex and multifactorial.
- The study supports the hypothesis that CKDu in Sri Lanka has environmental origins involving multiple interacting exposures (metals, cyanotoxins, microbial contaminants) rather than a single causative agent.

## Methods

**Cross-sectional environmental assessment** of 30 household wells in the Medawachchiya District of Sri Lanka's North Central Province, a region with high CKDu prevalence. Both chemical analysis (major ions, trace metals, nutrients) and microbial analysis (16S rRNA gene sequencing of water samples) were performed. Water microbiome composition was characterized at phylum, family, and genus levels. Chemical parameters were analyzed using standard hydrochemical methods. Correlations between chemical and microbial parameters were examined.

## Detailed Summary

McDonough et al. conducted an integrated chemical and microbiological assessment of household drinking water wells in one of Sri Lanka's CKDu-endemic regions. CKDu — chronic kidney disease of unknown etiology — affects predominantly male agricultural workers in the dry zone of Sri Lanka, and despite decades of research, no single causative agent has been definitively established.

The detection of Microcystis in household wells is significant because microcystins are potent toxins that can cause liver and kidney damage. Chronic low-level exposure through daily drinking water consumption could contribute to nephrotoxicity over years or decades. Microcystis thrives in warm, nutrient-rich (eutrophic) water — conditions common in agricultural areas where fertilizer runoff enriches groundwater.

The finding that CKD-associated bacterial taxa were present in drinking water adds a novel dimension to the gut-kidney axis: rather than dysbiosis arising solely from internal disease processes, the environmental water source may actively introduce pathogenic or dysbiotic microorganisms into the gut. This concept of "environmental seeding" of gut dysbiosis through drinking water is relatively unexplored in the CKD literature.

Water chemistry analysis revealed variable but generally low levels of heavy metals, consistent with other studies that have found the metal burden in CKDu-endemic areas to be above background but below acute toxicity thresholds — suggesting that chronic, low-level multi-metal exposure, potentially combined with cyanotoxins and microbial factors, drives kidney damage through cumulative insult.

## Relevance to WikiBiome

This study is highly relevant to WikiBiome's metallomics framework, demonstrating the **environmental exposure pathway** by which metals, cyanotoxins, and waterborne microorganisms may collectively drive kidney disease. The concept that drinking water serves as both a source of metal exposure AND a vector for gut-dysbiosis-associated microorganisms integrates two key WikiBiome themes.

The CKDu research in Sri Lanka represents a natural experiment in environmental nephrotoxicity where multiple exposures ([[cadmium]], [[lead]], [[arsenic]], cyanotoxins, waterborne pathogens) converge — making it a valuable case study for understanding how environmental factors interact with the gut-kidney axis.

## Open Questions

- Do residents of CKDu-endemic areas with well water containing Microcystis have higher rates of CKDu than those with Microcystis-free wells?
- Can the gut microbiome composition of CKDu patients be directly linked to their household drinking water microbiome?
- What is the relative contribution of chronic metal exposure versus chronic cyanotoxin exposure to CKDu pathogenesis?
