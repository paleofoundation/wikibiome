## Key Findings

- **Two large epidemiological studies** confirmed that prenatal valproic acid (VPA) exposure significantly increases ASD risk in human offspring, providing population-level validation of the animal model ([[chomiak-2013-vpa-asd-lessons-learned|this study]], expert-opinion).
- **Two key neurobiological features** link VPA exposure to ASD phenotype: (1) accelerated early brain overgrowth mirroring the macrocephaly observed in a subset of ASD children, and (2) hyperexcitable neural networks consistent with the high epilepsy comorbidity in ASD.
- **VPA functions as a histone deacetylase (HDAC) inhibitor**, altering gene expression during critical neurodevelopmental windows, which provides a mechanistic framework for understanding how environmental exposures interact with genetic susceptibility in ASD.
- **The VPA animal model** remains one of the most widely used and validated preclinical models of ASD, reproducing behavioral, neuroanatomical, and molecular features of the human condition.

## Methods

- **Study type**: Narrative review synthesizing epidemiological, clinical, and preclinical (animal model) evidence on VPA-ASD associations.
- **Scope**: Covers epidemiological cohort data, VPA teratogenicity mechanisms, neurobiological parallels between VPA-exposed animals and ASD phenotypes, and the utility of the VPA model for ASD research.
- **Evidence base**: Integrates human epidemiological studies, rodent VPA models, and in vitro mechanistic work on HDAC inhibition.

## Detailed Summary

This review examines what the VPA model has taught the field about ASD biology. VPA, an anticonvulsant and mood stabilizer prescribed for epilepsy and bipolar disorder, was recognized as a teratogen decades ago, with fetal valproate syndrome including neural tube defects, craniofacial abnormalities, and cognitive impairment. The ASD connection emerged from clinical observations that children exposed to VPA in utero had elevated rates of autistic features.

The authors highlight two convergent lines of evidence from the VPA model that map onto ASD biology. First, VPA-exposed rodents exhibit early brain overgrowth followed by normalization — a pattern strikingly similar to the accelerated head circumference growth observed in a subset of human ASD cases during the first year of life. Second, VPA produces hyperexcitable cortical networks characterized by increased NMDA receptor-mediated currents and altered excitation/inhibition balance, consistent with the excitation/inhibition imbalance hypothesis of ASD and the high prevalence of seizures in autistic individuals.

The mechanistic pathway through HDAC inhibition is significant because it connects a specific environmental exposure to widespread epigenetic changes during neurodevelopment. This provides a model for understanding how other environmental factors (including heavy metal exposures) might alter gene expression during critical developmental periods.

## Relevance to Cureva

- VPA exposure history should be considered in clinical assessment of ASD patients — a potential "environmental exposure" layer in the ASD signature.
- The HDAC mechanism suggests that interventions targeting epigenetic modifications may be relevant for VPA-exposed ASD subgroups.
- Important STOP context: VPA should be avoided during pregnancy in women at risk for having children with ASD.

## Open Questions

- Whether VPA-induced microbiome alterations (VPA is known to affect gut bacteria in animal models) contribute to the ASD phenotype independently of direct neurological effects.
- Whether the VPA model can be used to test microbiome-targeted interventions for ASD — e.g., whether probiotic or dietary interventions can rescue VPA-induced behavioral deficits.
- How VPA-induced epigenetic changes interact with metal-induced epigenetic modifications in the developing brain.
