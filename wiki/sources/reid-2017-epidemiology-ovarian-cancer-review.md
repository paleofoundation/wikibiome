# Epidemiology of Ovarian Cancer: A Review

## Key Findings

- **Epithelial ovarian cancer** is the most predominant pathologic subtype with five major histotypes (high-grade serous, low-grade serous, endometrioid, clear cell, mucinous), each with distinct epidemiological and molecular profiles (Reid et al. 2017, expert opinion).
- **GWAS identified 29 common susceptibility alleles** including 14 that are subtype-specific, highlighting the molecular heterogeneity of OC.
- **Reproductive and hormonal factors** significantly modulate risk: oral contraceptive use and parity reduce risk, while older age at menopause and hormone replacement therapy confer increased risk.
- **Environmental risk factors** include asbestos and talc powder exposures -- both mineral-based exposures with potential metal contamination (including traces of [[cadmium]] and [[nickel]]).
- **[[endometriosis]] is a recognized risk factor** for clear cell and endometrioid OC histotypes, connecting the endometriosis microbiome signature to ovarian carcinogenesis.

## Methods

- **Study design**: Comprehensive narrative review of OC epidemiology
- **Scope**: Covers incidence, mortality, histotype classification, genetic susceptibility, reproductive risk factors, hormonal influences, and environmental exposures
- **Limitations**: Narrative review without systematic methodology; some environmental exposure data predates modern detection methods

## Detailed Summary

Reid et al. provide a comprehensive overview of ovarian cancer epidemiology, emphasizing the importance of histotype-specific risk factors. The review highlights that OC is not a single disease but a collection of distinct malignancies with different origins, risk profiles, and molecular drivers. From a WikiBiome perspective, the most relevant findings are: (1) the environmental metal exposure pathway through asbestos and talc, which may contain trace [[cadmium]] and [[nickel]]; (2) the hormonal modulation of risk, which connects to the estrobolome concept and metalloestrogen theory; and (3) the endometriosis-OC link, which provides a bridge between the endometriosis microbiome signature and ovarian carcinogenesis. The BRCA-associated high-grade serous subtype has the strongest genetic basis, while environmental and hormonal factors play larger roles in other histotypes.
