# Body Mass Index, Interleukin-6 Signaling and Multiple Sclerosis: A Mendelian Randomization Study

## Summary

This two-sample Mendelian randomization study using data from the IMSGC (14,802 MS cases, 26,703 controls) establishes that genetically predicted increased BMI and IL-6 signaling are both causally associated with higher [[multiple-sclerosis]] risk, and that IL-6 signaling mediates approximately 43% of the BMI-MS association.

## Key Findings

- **BMI and MS risk**: OR = 1.30 (95% CI 1.15-1.47, p = 3.76 x 10^-5)
- **IL-6 signaling and MS risk**: OR = 1.51 (95% CI 1.11-2.04, p = 0.01)
- Higher BMI causally increases IL-6 signaling (beta = 0.37, p = 1.58 x 10^-65)
- In multivariable MR, IL-6 signaling effect persisted after adjusting for BMI (OR = 1.36, p = 0.003)
- BMI effect persisted after adjusting for IL-6 signaling (OR = 1.16, p = 0.046)
- **Proportion mediated by IL-6 signaling**: 43% (95% CI 25-54%)
- No evidence for serum IL-6 levels or CRP levels directly affecting MS risk

## Relevance to Gut-Metal-Microbiome Axis

The BMI-IL-6-MS causal pathway identified here intersects with [[gut-microbiome]] biology at multiple points. Obesity-associated [[dysbiosis]] (reduced *Bacteroidetes*, increased *Firmicutes*) promotes systemic inflammation through increased intestinal permeability and elevated LPS translocation, driving IL-6 production. [[heavy-metals]] accumulate in adipose tissue and further disrupt [[gut-microbiome]] ecology. The finding that IL-6 signaling mediates nearly half of the BMI-MS effect suggests that interventions targeting gut-derived inflammation (including [[probiotics]], dietary modification, or metal chelation) could reduce MS risk in obese individuals by lowering systemic IL-6.

## Limitations

- European ancestry population only
- Cannot determine tissue-specific IL-6 signaling effects
- Does not address microbiome as a mediator of the BMI-MS relationship
