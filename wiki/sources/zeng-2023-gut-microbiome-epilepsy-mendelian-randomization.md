# Roles of Gut Microbiome in Epilepsy Risk: A Mendelian Randomization Study

## Summary

This MR study analyzed the causal relationship between 211 gut microbiome taxa (from MiBioGen consortium, n=18,340) and epilepsy (from FinnGen, n=252,026) to identify specific intestinal flora associated with increased epilepsy risk.

## Key Findings

**Epilepsy (general):**
- Class *Betaproteobacteria* (OR = 1.357, p = 0.001) and Order *Burkholderiales* (OR = 1.336, p = 0.002) were risk factors
- *Eubacterium Xylanophilum Group* was protective (OR = 0.816, p = 0.014)
- *Akkermansia* was a risk factor (OR = 1.184, p = 0.025)

**Generalized epilepsy:**
- *Actinobacteria* increased risk (OR = 1.381, p = 0.042)
- Class *Bacilli* was protective (OR = 0.748, p = 0.033)

**Focal epilepsy:**
- Phylum *Verrucomicrobia* and genus *Akkermansia* strongly associated (OR = 1.697 and 1.739 respectively)
- *Sutterella* also a risk factor (OR = 1.662, p = 0.033)

## Relevance to Gut-Metal-Microbiome Axis

While focused on epilepsy rather than [[multiple-sclerosis]], this study establishes causal relationships between specific gut taxa and neurological disease through the [[gut-brain-axis]]. The identification of *Akkermansia* as a risk factor for epilepsy is notable given its complex role in MS -- often increased in MS patients. The *Burkholderiales* association is relevant because this order includes metal-tolerant species that can thrive in [[heavy-metals]]-contaminated environments. These findings support the broader concept that [[gut-microbiome]] composition causally influences CNS disease, a principle directly applicable to MS.

## Limitations

- Based on 16S rRNA data from MiBioGen, limited taxonomic resolution
- European ancestry populations only
- Does not address metabolite-level mechanisms
