## Key Findings

- **Reduced microbial diversity in COVID-19 patients** — ICU-hospitalized COVID-19 patients showed significantly decreased Chao1 index (species richness) compared to ward-hospitalized COVID-19 patients (p=0.02) and controls (p=0.006). Shannon diversity index followed similar pattern.
- **Dysbiosis signature distinguishes disease severity** — Ward-hospitalized COVID-19 (w-COVID19) and ICU COVID-19 (i-COVID19) patients showed distinct microbial profiles that separated clearly from controls in beta-diversity analysis
- **Proteobacteria bloom in COVID-19** — Proteobacteria (gram-negative potential pathogens) were significantly enriched in w-COVID19 patients compared to controls
- **Taxa alterations correlate with severity** — Specific families including Carnobacteriaceae, Pectobacteriaceae, Moritellaceae, and Selenomonadaceae showed differential enrichment in i-COVID19 vs w-COVID19, suggesting a severity-dependent dysbiosis gradient

## Methods

**Study design:** Cross-sectional case-control  
**Participants:** 15 COVID-19 patients hospitalized in ward for highly infectious disease (w-COVID19), 9 COVID-19 patients in ICU (i-COVID19), 8 healthy controls  
**Sample collection:** Rectal swabs from hospitalized patients; processed within 4 hours  
**Molecular method:** 16S rRNA gene sequencing; V3-V4 region amplification  
**Analysis:** Alpha-diversity indices (Chao1, Shannon), beta-diversity (PCA, Bray-Curtis), taxonomic profiling at phylum/family/genus levels

## Detailed Summary

This cross-sectional study examined the gut microbiota composition in COVID-19 patients across two severity levels (ward vs ICU hospitalization) and compared to healthy controls using 16S rRNA sequencing.

**Key findings by severity:**

1. **Microbial richness and diversity** — ICU-hospitalized COVID-19 patients showed the most severe dysbiosis, with significantly reduced Chao1 index compared to both ward-hospitalized COVID-19 and controls. Shannon diversity index showed the same trend, indicating loss of both species richness and evenness.

2. **Phylum-level alterations:**
   - Proteobacteria: Significantly increased in w-COVID19 vs controls
   - Firmicutes: Reduced in COVID-19 patients
   - Actinobacteria: Increased in w-COVID19
   - Nitrosopira: Increased in i-COVID19
   - Bacteroidetes: Notable alterations in both groups

3. **Family-level dysbiosis:**
   - Carnobacteriaceae, Pectobacteriaceae, Moritellaceae, Selenomonadaceae: Significantly elevated in i-COVID19
   - Erysipelotrichales: Higher in i-COVID19 vs w-COVID19
   - Multiple shared dysbiotic families between w-COVID19 and i-COVID19

4. **Microbiota segregation:** Clear clustering by group in principal component analysis: controls segregated distinctly from both COVID-19 groups, and i-COVID19 showed a distinct profile separating it from w-COVID19.

5. **Taxa sharing:** Venn diagram analysis revealed substantial shared dysbiosis between w-COVID19 and i-COVID19 (shared families/genera), plus unique dysbiotic families in each severity group, suggesting a common dysbiosis foundation with severity-dependent amplifications.

## Relevance to WikiBiome

- Provides early evidence of dysbiosis signature in acute COVID-19 across severity spectrum
- Demonstrates that microbial diversity loss correlates with hospitalization severity
- Identifies specific taxa alterations (Proteobacteria bloom, Bacteroides/Faecalibacterium changes) that appear consistent across multiple COVID-19 studies
- Establishes rectal swab as valid sampling method for COVID-19 microbiota assessment

## Relevance to Cureva

- **Signature layer 2 (taxonomic):** Provides baseline dysbiosis signature in acute COVID-19; relevant for distinguishing acute from post-COVID profiles
- **Severity stratification:** Microbiota diversity correlates with hospitalization severity, suggesting potential biomarker for clinical severity
- **Karen's Brain primitive 1 (metals as selective pressures):** Proteobacteria bloom may reflect metal-selective pressures (Proteobacteria enrichment in hypoxic/dysbiotic states)
- **Gap to long COVID:** This acute-phase dysbiosis study provides comparison point for understanding post-COVID dysbiosis trajectory

## Open Questions

- Do the dysbiosis patterns in acute COVID-19 persist into post-COVID syndrome?
- Which specific metabolites are produced by the enriched Proteobacteria and dysbiotic families?
- Do metal exposures (heavy metals in hospital environments, food) select for the Proteobacteria bloom?
- What interventions can restore diversity most effectively in severity-stratified patient groups?
- How long does microbiota recovery take after acute infection resolution?
