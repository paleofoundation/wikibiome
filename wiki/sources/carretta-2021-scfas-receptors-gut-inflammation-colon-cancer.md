# Participation of Short-Chain Fatty Acids and Their Receptors in Gut Inflammation and Colon Cancer

## Key Findings

- **SCFAs signal through three G-protein-coupled receptors:** FFAR3/GPR41, FFAR2/GPR43, and HCAR2/GPR109A, expressed on gut epithelial cells, neutrophils, eosinophils, dendritic cells, ILC3, and other immune cells ([[carretta-2021-scfas-receptors-gut-inflammation-colon-cancer]], expert-opinion).
- **FFAR2 (GPR43) activation by butyrate induces HDAC inhibition**, promoting epigenetic tumor suppressor function. FFAR2 deficiency promoted colon tumorigenesis through overexpression of cAMP-PKA-CREB and Wnt/beta-catenin pathways with increased HDAC protein expression ([[carretta-2021-scfas-receptors-gut-inflammation-colon-cancer]], expert-opinion).
- **HCAR2 (GPR109A) functions as a suppressor of carcinogenesis**: knockdown/silencing accelerated colonic inflammation and colon cancer progression in multiple experimental models (Singh et al., 2014; Macia et al., 2015). HCAR2 re-expression in silenced colon cancer cell lines abolished NF-kB activation and induced apoptosis ([[carretta-2021-scfas-receptors-gut-inflammation-colon-cancer]], expert-opinion).
- **SCFAs promote Treg differentiation and ILC3 function:** Via FFAR2, SCFAs induce IL-18 secretion through NLRP3 inflammasome activation, enhancing Treg differentiation via IL-10 production. IL-1beta engages IL-1R on ILC3, promoting IL-22 production for tissue integrity and host defense. FFAR2 KO mice showed decreased intestinal IgA production ([[carretta-2021-scfas-receptors-gut-inflammation-colon-cancer]], expert-opinion).
- **GLPG0974 as therapeutic candidate:** A selective FFAR2 antagonist that potently inhibited acetate-induced CD11b expression on human neutrophils both in vitro and ex vivo, without causing side effects. However, this antagonist showed no affinity for mouse or rat FFAR2, making preclinical testing difficult ([[carretta-2021-scfas-receptors-gut-inflammation-colon-cancer]], expert-opinion).

## Detailed Summary

This review systematically examines how SCFAs — the major microbial metabolites from bacterial fermentation of dietary fiber — exert anti-inflammatory and anti-tumor effects through three specific GPCRs in the gut.

**SCFA production and transport:** Acetate (C2), propionate (C3), and butyrate (C4) constitute ~95% of total SCFAs. Colonic concentrations reach 70-100 mM with the ratio of acetate:propionate:butyrate being 60:25:15. SCFAs are absorbed through passive diffusion or via substrate transporters (MCT1/SMCT1) coupled to H+ transport, and the sodium-coupled monocarboxylate transporter 1 (SMCT1; encoded by SLC5A8), which is frequently silenced in colon cancer. SCFAs not metabolized by colonocytes are transported through portal circulation to the liver (concentrations of 264 micro-M acetate, 30 micro-M propionate, 16 micro-M butyrate).

**FFAR2/GPR43 in detail:** Expressed on neutrophils, eosinophils, intestinal epithelial cells, dendritic cells, and ILC3. Activated by acetate and propionate preferentially. Key functions include: (1) neutrophil chemotaxis induction, (2) IL-18 secretion via NLRP3 inflammasome in epithelial cells, (3) Treg cell differentiation via IL-10 from DCs, (4) intestinal IgA promotion, (5) ILC3 activation for IL-22 production. Multiple synthetic agonists have been developed but many display overlapping tissue expression with FFAR3, complicating selective targeting.

**FFAR2 synthetic agonists:** 4-CMTB (allosteric), compound 1 (FFAR2 selective with 200-fold selectivity over FFAR3), AZ1729 (direct allosteric), and phenylacetamide 1. AZ1729 did not increase intracellular calcium but potentiated the propionate response. Compound 1 combined with FFAR2 was a potent activator of superoxide-generating NADPH oxidase. Several agonists show beta-arrestin recruitment properties useful for distinguishing Gi vs. beta-arrestin signaling pathways.

**HCAR2/GPR109A in detail:** Butyrate is the primary ligand (though niacin was the first identified ligand). Expressed on luminal plasma membrane of intestinal epithelial cells (silenced in colon cancer via DNA methylation), DCs, and macrophages. HCAR2 activation: (1) promotes Treg differentiation, (2) inhibits NF-kB, (3) induces apoptosis in colon cancer cells, (4) suppresses carcinogenesis. The Thangaraju group showed HCAR2 re-expression in colon cancer cells abolished NF-kB and induced apoptosis. Bardhan et al. reported IFN-gamma could counteract DNA methylation-mediated HCAR2 silencing as anti-tumor mechanism.

**FFAR3/GPR41:** Less studied in CRC context. Expressed on intestinal epithelial cells and immune cells. Propionate is the most potent ligand. Some evidence suggests FFAR3 activation suppresses neurogenic motility in rat proximal colon.

**SCFA role in IBD:** SCFAs promote synthesis of intestinal mucins and secretion of intestinal epithelial cell mucus, repairing and improving barrier function. They inhibit secretion of proinflammatory factors, increase IL-10 production, and activate Treg cell activity. *Clostridium butyricum* (butyrate-producing probiotic) inhibited intestinal tumor development by modulating Wnt/beta-catenin signaling. Loss of FFAR2 promoted colon tumorigenesis in mice by reducing gut barrier integrity, increasing tumor bacterial load, and altering IL-27 and CD8+ T cell phenotypes.

**ILC3 cells as master regulators:** SCFAs via FFAR2 promote ILC3 activation. ILC3s produce IL-22 for tissue integrity and host defense. The interplay between microbiome-derived metabolites, SCFA receptors, and ILC3 represents an emerging frontier in intestinal immunology.

## Contradictions / Tensions

- **FFAR2 dual role:** FFAR2 activation on neutrophils promotes chemotaxis (potentially pro-inflammatory), while FFAR2 activation on epithelial cells and DCs is anti-inflammatory and anti-tumor. This tissue-specific duality complicates FFAR2-targeting strategies.
- **FFAR2 and FFAR3 overlap:** Both receptors share endogenous ligands (SCFAs) and are expressed in similar tissues, making selective pharmacological targeting challenging.
- **HCAR2 silencing paradox:** HCAR2 is epigenetically silenced in colon cancer via DNA methylation, yet its natural ligand (butyrate) is the primary SCFA in the colon. This suggests a tumor-driven silencing mechanism that removes the protective effect of endogenous butyrate.
