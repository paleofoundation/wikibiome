## Key Findings

- **Urinary nickel in the highest quartile associates with 3.57-fold increased CVD risk** based on NHANES population data ([[liu-2025-nickel-cardio-metabolic-effects]], expert-opinion).
- **Nickel exposure correlates with metabolic syndrome features**: elevated fasting glucose, hypertension, and dyslipidemia.
- **Mechanism involves oxidative stress** through depletion of superoxide dismutase (SOD) and reduced glutathione — consistent with nickel's pro-oxidant role seen across conditions.
- **Dose-response relationship**: cardiovascular risk increases across nickel exposure quartiles, with the highest quartile showing the most dramatic effect.

## Methods

Review and synthesis of epidemiological studies, with primary focus on NHANES (National Health and Nutrition Examination Survey) data analyzing the association between urinary nickel levels and cardiovascular/metabolic outcomes. Evaluates cross-sectional and cohort studies correlating nickel exposure biomarkers (urinary, blood, hair nickel) with cardiovascular disease (CVD), metabolic syndrome, hypertension, dyslipidemia, and insulin resistance. Reviews mechanistic studies on nickel-induced oxidative stress, inflammation, and endothelial dysfunction. <!-- NEEDS VERIFICATION: specific NHANES survey years and sample sizes used -->

## Detailed Summary

Liu et al. review the evidence linking [[nickel]] exposure to cardiovascular and metabolic disease, extending the understanding of nickel toxicity beyond its established roles in allergy, carcinogenesis, and microbiome disruption.

**Population-level cardiovascular risk.** Analysis of NHANES data reveals that individuals in the highest quartile of urinary nickel excretion have a 3.57-fold increased risk of cardiovascular disease compared to the lowest quartile. This is a large effect size for an environmental exposure, comparable to well-established CVD risk factors. The association persists after adjustment for confounders including age, sex, smoking, and socioeconomic status.

**Metabolic syndrome associations.** The review documents associations between nickel exposure and multiple metabolic syndrome components: (1) elevated fasting glucose and insulin resistance, (2) hypertension, (3) dyslipidemia (elevated triglycerides, reduced HDL). These associations suggest that nickel may not merely damage the cardiovascular system directly but may disrupt the metabolic regulatory systems that maintain cardiovascular health.

**Oxidative stress mechanism.** The proposed mechanistic pathway centers on nickel-induced oxidative stress. Nickel depletes superoxide dismutase (SOD) through [[mis-metallation]] — Ni2+ can displace Mn2+ or Cu2+/Zn2+ from SOD active sites, rendering the enzyme inactive ([[robinson-2020-metalation-natures-challenge]]). Nickel also depletes reduced glutathione (GSH), the cell's primary non-enzymatic antioxidant. The combined loss of enzymatic (SOD) and non-enzymatic (GSH) antioxidant capacity leads to accumulation of reactive oxygen species, which damage endothelial cells, oxidize LDL (promoting [[atherosclerosis]]), and activate inflammatory signaling cascades.

**Endothelial dysfunction.** Nickel-induced ROS generation in endothelial cells reduces nitric oxide (NO) bioavailability, impairs vasodilation, and promotes a pro-thrombotic, pro-inflammatory endothelial phenotype. This connects nickel exposure to the earliest stages of atherosclerotic plaque formation.

**Interactions with other metals.** The review examines nickel's interactions with [[cadmium]], [[lead]], and [[arsenic]] — all of which share cardiovascular toxicity through overlapping oxidative stress mechanisms. Co-exposure to multiple toxic metals (the typical human scenario) likely produces synergistic cardiovascular damage. The review also notes that [[zinc]] supplementation may partially protect against nickel toxicity by competing for binding sites and supporting SOD function.

## Open Questions

- Does nickel exposure alter the gut microbiome in ways that independently affect cardiovascular risk (e.g., shifting TMAO-producing species)?
- What are the primary dietary sources of nickel contributing to the NHANES exposure gradient — cocoa, nuts, legumes, whole grains?
- Can zinc supplementation meaningfully reduce nickel-mediated cardiovascular risk in exposed populations?
- Whether the 3.57-fold risk increase is driven by acute nickel toxicity or chronic cumulative oxidative damage.
- How nickel-nickel-dependent gut pathogens (e.g., *H. pylori* urease) interact with systemic nickel exposure levels in the cardiovascular context.
