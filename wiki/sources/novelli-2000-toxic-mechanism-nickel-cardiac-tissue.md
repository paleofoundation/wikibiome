# Toxic Mechanism of Nickel Exposure on Cardiac Tissue

## Why This Paper Matters

This foundational year-2000 study is one of the earliest experimental investigations specifically examining the mechanism of [[nickel]] toxicity on cardiac tissue. It provides direct evidence that nickel-induced cardiac damage is mediated by [[oxidative-stress]], specifically through superoxide radical (O2-) generation, and demonstrates that vitamin E (alpha-tocopherol) supplementation can reverse these toxic effects. The study is important because it established the superoxide-driven pathway for nickel cardiotoxicity at a time when cardiac effects of nickel were poorly understood, predating by two decades the epidemiological studies linking nickel exposure to [[cardiovascular-disease]] reviewed in [[Liu et al. 2025|liu-2025-cardiometabolic-nickel]].

## Key Claims

- Nickel exposure (Ni2+ 100 mg/L from NiSO4 in drinking water) significantly increases lipoperoxide and total lipid concentrations in cardiac tissue
- Nickel increases serum cholesterol (59%), lactate dehydrogenase (LDH, 64%), and alanine transaminase (ALT, 30%) -- markers of cardiac and liver damage
- Vitamin E (alpha-tocopherol, 0.2 mg/200g by oral gavage) significantly reversed all biochemical parameters to near-control values
- SOD activities in the heart were significantly decreased by nickel exposure, while catalase and GSH-Px activities were unchanged
- Superoxide radical (O2-) generated by nickel exposure is of primary importance in the pathogenesis of cardiac damage
- Superoxide anion-induced cell damage may occur before being converted to H2O2 (since SOD was decreased but not catalase/GSH-Px)
- The average daily oral Ni2+ intake in this model was 3.6 mg per day (calculated from drinking water Ni2+ concentration)

## Methods/Findings

- **Study type**: Experimental animal study (rat model)
- **Animals**: 40 male Wistar rats (180-200g, 12 weeks old), divided into 4 groups of 10
 - Group A: control (tap water only)
 - Group B: alpha-tocopherol only (0.2 mg/200g, oral gavage, twice weekly)
 - Group C: NiSO4 only (100 mg Ni/L in drinking water)
 - Group D: NiSO4 + alpha-tocopherol (both treatments combined)
- **Duration**: 15 days

 - Serum: total protein, cholesterol, HDL-cholesterol, LDH, ALT, AST
 - Heart tissue: total protein, glycogen, total lipid, lipoperoxide, LDH, ALT, AST, SOD, GSH-Px, catalase

### Serum Results (Table 2)
- No body weight gain differences between groups
- Cholesterol: 67.3 (control) vs. 107.4 (nickel) mg/dL -- **60% increase**
- LDH: 164.4 (control) vs. 270.7 (nickel) U/L -- **65% increase**
- ALT: 32.8 (control) vs. 42.8 (nickel) U/L -- **30% increase**
- HDL-cholesterol: 43.7 (control) vs. 48.0 (nickel) -- significant increase
- All elevated markers recovered to control values with tocopherol + nickel co-treatment (Group D)

### Cardiac Tissue Results (Table 3)
- Lipoperoxide: 43.0 (control) vs. 34.4 (nickel) then 69.5 (nickel) mmol/g tissue -- significantly increased
- SOD: 51.4 (control) vs. 139.4 (nickel, serum) but heart SOD **decreased** with nickel
- GSH-Px: 4.4 (control) vs. 13.6 (nickel) then 5.4 (nickel) -- significantly decreased in heart
- No alterations in catalase activities
- Glycogen content increased in heart with nickel exposure in presence of vitamin E
- Total lipid concentration increased in nickel-exposed hearts

### Mechanistic Interpretation
- LDH release from cells indicates tissue injury and irreversible morphological alteration (cell membrane damage releases cytoplasmic enzymes)
- Increased serum LDH and ALT suggest myocardial ischemia diagnosis; ALT elevation may indicate cardiac disease
- Nickel increased cholesterol and lipoperoxidation is involved in development of atherosclerosis
- SOD decrease in heart indicates superoxide radical accumulation -- since catalase and GSH-Px were unchanged, the superoxide itself (not H2O2) is the primary damaging species
- Vitamin E as intramembrane antioxidant protects polyunsaturated fatty acids from radical-induced peroxidation
- Lipoperoxide/tocopherol ratio was 29% higher in nickel-alone group vs. control, and 67% lower in tocopherol-treated groups
