## Summary

A comprehensive review of clinical/epidemiological evidence linking low zinc levels to multiple cancer types. The authors summarize recent findings from meta-analyses, prospective studies, and case-control studies demonstrating that zinc deficiency is a widespread feature of various cancers, with potential as a diagnostic biomarker and therapeutic target.

## Key Findings

- Low zinc levels consistently reported across esophageal, breast, liver, lung, gynecological, colon, oral, and prostate cancers
- Esophageal cancer: most extensively studied; zinc deficiency promotes carcinogenesis by altering microRNA expression
- Breast cancer: multiple meta-analyses (926-2369 patients) confirm lower serum zinc in breast cancer patients
- Liver cancer: zinc levels correlate with prognosis; high serum copper-to-zinc ratio predicts worse survival; zinc supplementation may decrease risk of hepatocellular carcinoma development
- Lung cancer: meta-analyses show significantly lower zinc levels in lung cancer patients vs. controls
- Colon cancer: European study of 58,221 participants found significant association between zinc levels and colorectal cancer
- Zinc may serve as a potential biomarker for cancer diagnosis and prognosis prediction
- Preliminary evidence suggests zinc supplementation could be preventive or adjunctive in cancer treatment
- Zinc plays key roles in immunity (T cell differentiation), protein folding, apoptosis (p53 activation), and antioxidant activity (SOD cofactor)

## Relevance to Wiki

Demonstrates that zinc deficiency is a hallmark of multiple cancers, relevant to understanding how heavy metal exposure (particularly cadmium, which competes with zinc for binding sites) may contribute to cancer risk by disrupting zinc homeostasis.
