# Recent Advances in Gut Microbiota and Thyroid Disease: Pathogenesis and Therapeutics in Autoimmune, Neoplastic, and Nodular Conditions

## Key Claims

- A pronounced shift in [[gut microbiome]] composition has been consistently observed in thyroid diseases, with significant reduction in [[Bifidobacterium]], [[Bacillaceae]], [[Megamonas]], and [[Clostridium]], and notable increase in [[Bacteroides]], [[Proteobacteria]], [[Actinobacteria]], [[Desulfobacterota]], and [[Klebsiella]]
- These alterations impact metabolic pathways including [[bile acid]] and [[cytokine]] production, with a decrease in [[short-chain fatty acids]] crucial for immune regulation and thyroid hormone homeostasis
- [[Probiotic]] adjunct therapy can modulate the gut microbiota, with specific strains like [[Lactiplantibacillus plantarum]] 299v and [[Bifidobacterium longum]] demonstrating potential in improving thyroid function
- [[Fecal microbiota transplantation]] (FMT) has emerged as a promising intervention in [[Graves' disease]], recalibrating the gut microbiota and influencing neurotransmitters and trace elements via gut-brain and gut-thyroid axes
- The potential decrease in SCFAs may influence [[T regulatory cells]] (Tregs) leading to Th1/Th2 imbalance contributing to increased pro-inflammatory cytokines including IFN-gamma and TNF-alpha

## Methods/Findings

- **Study type**: Narrative review of experimental studies from the past 5 years
- **Scope**: Gut microbiota in four thyroid diseases: HT, GD, thyroid nodules, thyroid cancer
- **HT findings summary table**: Consistent decrease in Bifidobacterium; increase in Bacteroides, Proteobacteria, Actinobacteria across multiple studies from Egypt, Spain, Brazil, China (2020-2024)
- **GD findings summary table**: Shifts in Firmicutes/Bacteroidetes composition across 14 studies from multiple countries
- **Therapeutic evidence**: Probiotics, FMT, and dietary interventions reviewed; L. plantarum 299v and B. longum shown most promising
