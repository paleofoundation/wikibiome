# Nickel; A Metal with Threats to Human Health, Focusing on Its Intoxication Mechanisms

## Why This Paper Matters

This 2025 review from Human and Experimental Toxicology provides the most current comprehensive synthesis of nickel intoxication mechanisms and -- critically -- the most detailed treatment protocols for nickel poisoning available in the wiki. While other reviews focus on mechanisms or epidemiology, this paper uniquely integrates clinical management (chelation therapy dosing protocols, diagnostic evaluation, organ-specific clinical manifestations) with molecular mechanisms. The inclusion of DDC (diethyldithiocarbamate) and disulfiram dosing tables based on urinary nickel concentration, plus discussion of nanoparticle-based environmental remediation, makes this an essential clinical and translational reference for [[nickel]] toxicology.

## Key Claims

- Beneficial intake of Ni for humans would be <100 ug/day; most individuals achieve this with daily dietary intakes >70 ug/day
- Nickel compounds, whether insoluble (oxides, sulfides) or soluble (sulfates, chlorides, acetates), pose serious risks to respiratory, digestive, and skin systems
- In doses greater than 0.5 gr orally, nickel causes poisoning in humans; consumption close to 1 gr causes death
- Soluble and insoluble nickel compounds are placed in IARC Group 1 (carcinogenic); nickel and its alloys in Group 2B (possibly carcinogenic)
- Nickel accumulates in lungs, thyroid, adrenal glands, brain, kidneys, heart, liver, spleen, and pancreas
- The apoptosis pathway is mitochondrial-mediated and passes through the p53-dependent pathway causing G2/M cell cycle arrest
- Nickel compounds induce HIF-1 and its subunits, affecting genes related to energy metabolism, erythropoiesis, angiogenesis, and tumor cell survival
- Recent studies report IBS-like symptoms in people with nickel sensitivity due to nickel in the diet
- Allergic contact dermatitis affects ~10% of women and ~2% of men

## Methods/Findings

- **Study type**: Narrative review drawing from PubMed, Scopus, citations, and Toxicologic Emergencies reference book (1996-2025)

### Physical-Chemical Properties and Exposure Sources
- 5th most abundant element on Earth; atomic number 28; density 8.912 g/cm3; melting point 1455C; boiling point 2913C
- Five stable isotopes: 58Ni (68.077%), 60Ni, 61Ni, 62Ni, 64Ni
- **Air**: remote areas 0.00001-0.003 ug/m3; urban without factories 0.003-0.03 ug/m3; metallurgical areas 0.07-0.77 ug/m3; nickel-producing/consuming industries up to 100 ug/day
- **Water**: rivers typically 0.3 ppb; seawater 0.5-2 ppb; drinking water <10 ug/L leads to 7.5-15.0 ug daily intake
- **Soil**: concentrations range 3-1000 ppm; highest in clay, sandstone, limestone, shale
- **Food**: key sources include grains, buckwheat, oats, milk, legumes, nuts, oilseeds, fruits, vegetables, cocoa, dark chocolate, seafood; average daily consumption 100-300 ug
- Cooking utensils (stainless steel) can elevate nickel content in food

### Metabolism and Pharmacokinetics (Figure 2)
- **Absorption**: digestive system, respiratory system, skin and appendages
- **Distribution**: lungs, thyroid glands, adrenal glands, brain, kidneys, heart, liver, spleen, pancreas
- **Excretion**: primarily urine (values of 6 ug/24 hour specimen; higher values suggest environmental/occupational exposure); very little in feces
- Nickel transported in blood by albumin, amino acids (histidine), alpha-2-macroglobulin, with help of iron via DMT-1 (divalent metal transporter-1)
- Insoluble nickel particles phagocytosed and stored in cytoplasmic vacuoles near nucleus; soluble compounds can enter nucleus and interact with DNA

### Carcinogenicity Mechanisms (Figure 3 -- detailed pathway diagram)
- **ROS generation**: Fenton and Haber-Weiss reactions producing hydroxyl radicals, superoxide, peroxides, lipid peroxides
- **DNA damage**: strand breaks, base modifications, cross-linking
- **Epigenetic effects**: hypermethylation of DNA, hypermethylation of histones, gene silencing
- **Transcription factors**: p53, NF-kB, AP-1, TGF-beta, NF-AT altered by both soluble and insoluble nickel
- **HIF-1 pathway**: nickel compounds induce HIF-1alpha and HIF-1beta, increasing metabolism, erythropoiesis, VEGF-driven angiogenesis, tumor cell survival

### Teratogenicity
- Nickel carbonyl [Ni(CO)4] and nickel sulfide [Ni3S2] are teratogenic in laboratory animals
- Observed outcomes: cystic lung, exencephaly, anophthalmia, cleft palate, microphthalmia, fetal death, chromosomal damage, spermatogenesis abnormalities, hydrocephaly, exophthalmia, skeletal defects

### Apoptosis Pathways
- Internal (mitochondrial): Cyt C release from mitochondrial intermembrane space triggers caspase cascade
- External: TNF receptor binding activates cell death signals
- Nickel upregulates Bax and increases p53, causing Bax/Bcl-2 ratio imbalance and mitochondrial depolarization

### Organ-Specific Clinical Manifestations
- **Skin**: allergic contact dermatitis (most common); type 4 delayed hypersensitivity; sensitization phase variable in duration
- **Liver**: excessive nickel intake causes liver damage in animals; increased AST, ALT; long-term increases risk of liver cirrhosis; leads to lipid peroxidation, reduced hepatic glutathione levels, and nickel accumulation
- **Pancreas**: acute and subchronic nickel exposure increases blood glucose; impaired insulin secretion
- **Kidney**: even at low doses, nickel has potential to cause significant kidney damage; can progress from acute injury to acute tubulointerstitial nephritis to CKD and ESRD
- **Male reproductive**: disrupts sperm vitality, affects zinc-dependent metabolism essential for sperm quality; nickel-containing binding proteins act as DNA-binding proteins affecting spermatogenesis
- **Respiratory**: inhalation dose of nickel affecting respiratory system is 0.5 mg/m3 per week; exposure to >5.0 mg/m3 for 75 minutes causes lung congestion, emphysema, pulmonary fibrosis, pulmonary edema; reduced FEV1 and FEF25%-75%
- **CNS**: Ni2+ can release dopamine and inhibit glutamate receptors
- **GI tract**: IBS-like symptoms in nickel-sensitive patients due to dietary nickel; described as "brain-gut axis" dysfunction with motility, autonomic reactivity, and altered immune function

### Diagnostic Evaluation
- History and clinical examination are essential; poisoning typically occurs via inhalation or ingestion, warranting consideration of respiratory and digestive system assessment
- Nickel distributes across various organs; blood, serum, and urine samples used for analysis
- Nickel concentration >0.3 ug/L in blood but may exceed 8 ug/L in exposed workers
- Urine concentrations typically <10 ug/L
- Dimethylglyoxime spot test for nickel detection; positive reaction indicated by pink/violet color

### Treatment Protocols

**Chelation Therapy** (Table 1 -- chelating agents):
- DDC (diethyldithiocarbamate): lipophilic chelate, binds divalent nickel, facilitates excretion through urine, bile, sweat; must be administered immediately
- Disulfiram: metabolized to two DDC molecules; 70% protection against nickel carbonyl, DDC provides 100%
- NBG-DTC (N-benzyl-D-glucamine dithiocarbamate): greater efficacy due to lipophilic properties; can penetrate brain
- Other agents: BAL, CaNa2-EDTA, D-Penicillamine (not currently used for nickel)

**DDC Dosing Protocol by Urinary Nickel** (Table 2):
| Urinary Nickel | Dosage |
|---------------|--------|
| <100 ug/L | No treatment recommended |
| 100-500 ug/L | 1 gr orally, then 0.8g at 4h, 0.6g at 8h, 0.4g at 16h; continue 0.4g every 8h until resolution |
| >500 ug/L | Same regimen with increased monitoring |
| Critical conditions | 12.5 mg/kg IV |
