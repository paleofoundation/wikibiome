# Key Claims

- Heavy metals are defined as metallic elements with density >5 g/cm3; they bioaccumulate in the body causing biological and physiological complications
- Heavy metals enter humans via four routes: inhalation, ingestion of contaminated food, drinking contaminated water, and dermal contact
- IARC carcinogenicity classification: Group 1 (carcinogenic) includes aluminium production, arsenic/inorganic compounds, cadmium/cadmium compounds, chromium VI compounds, **nickel compounds**, and nickel refining
- Metal toxicity commonly involves: ROS generation via Fenton reactions, DNA damage (direct and indirect), protein misfolding/aggregation, lipid peroxidation/cell membrane damage
- **[[nickel]]**: Density 8.9 g/cm3, 22nd most abundant metal; absorbed as lipophilic compound via GI tract; carcinogenic mechanisms include NF-kappaB activation, ATF-1 phosphorylation, hypoxia-inducible factor upregulation, epigenetic alterations (histone methylation, long non-coding RNA regulation); causes lung and nasal cancer in occupational settings
- Essential elements (Fe, Cu, Zn, Mn, Co) have U-shaped dose-response curves: both deficiency and excess cause harm

# Methods/Findings

- **Study type**: Comprehensive review of 18 heavy metals covering properties, uses, exposure routes, pharmacokinetics, toxicology processes, and regulatory thresholds
- **Nickel-specific findings**:
  - WHO air quality guideline: 3.8 x 10^-4 ug/m3; EPA drinking water threshold: 0.1 mg/L; OSHA workplace air: 1 mg/m3
  - Absorbed via passive diffusion (low amounts) or active transport with facilitated diffusion (high amounts)
  - Ni carbonyl (Ni(CO)4) is fat-soluble and can cross cell membranes via diffusion
  - NF-kappaB activation by nickel leads to ATF/CREB phosphorylation and TSP1 diminution, enhancing angiogenesis
  - Nickel stimulates methylation of promoter, downregulates MEG3, upregulates hypoxia-inducible factor-1alpha in carcinogenesis
  - Effects: lung embolisms, asthma, allergic reactions, respiratory failure, heart disorders, cancer (lung/nasal)
  - Food sources: baked beans, kidney beans, soybeans, whole wheat, oat, rye, cocoa, chocolate, nuts
- Detailed tables for each metal covering: properties, uses, effects on humans, food sources, and regulatory thresholds (WHO, OSHA, EPA, FDA)
