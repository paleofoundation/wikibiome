# Microbiome-Driven Neuroinflammation and Immune Response in Parkinson's Disease

## Summary

This review examines how the [[gut-microbiome]] modulates immune responses and [[neuroinflammation]] in [[parkinsons-disease]]. It details the pathways through which microbial dysbiosis activates both innate and adaptive immune systems, ultimately contributing to dopaminergic neuronal death.

## Key Findings

- Gut [[dysbiosis]] in PD activates [[toll-like-receptors]] (TLR2, TLR4) on enteric immune cells, triggering pro-inflammatory cascades
- Microbial products (LPS, peptidoglycan) translocate across a compromised gut barrier to activate systemic and central immune responses
- T-cell subsets are altered in PD: Th1 and Th17 cells are expanded while Tregs are reduced
- Microbial metabolites regulate [[microglia]] activation states -- [[dysbiosis]] shifts microglia toward a pro-inflammatory M1 phenotype
- [[alpha-synuclein]] acts as a damage-associated molecular pattern (DAMP) that amplifies microbiome-driven inflammation
- The enteric nervous system serves as a conduit for immune signaling between gut microbiome and brain

## Relevance to Gut-Metal-Microbiome Axis

The immune pathways activated by gut [[dysbiosis]] in [[parkinsons-disease]] are fundamentally intertwined with metal homeostasis. [[tlr4]] activation by bacterial LPS triggers [[hepcidin]] expression, causing [[iron]] retention in macrophages and microglia. This iron-loaded immune cell state promotes oxidative stress via Fenton chemistry and drives [[ferroptosis]] in dopaminergic neurons. The Th17 expansion documented here is particularly relevant because IL-17 signaling upregulates metal-binding [[calprotectin]] (S100A8/A9), which sequesters [[zinc]] and [[manganese]] -- a key [[nutritional-immunity]] response that paradoxically starves beneficial commensals while favoring metal-pirating pathogens.

## Limitations

- Review article with no original data
- Immune mechanisms described are largely from animal models
- Human immune-microbiome interactions in PD are less well characterized
- Therapeutic implications remain speculative
