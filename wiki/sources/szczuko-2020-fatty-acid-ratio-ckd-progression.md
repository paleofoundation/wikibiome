# The C18:3n6/C22:4n6 Ratio as a Lipid Marker of CKD Progression

## Key Findings

- The **C18:3n6/C22:4n6 ratio** (gamma-linolenic acid to adrenic acid) decreases progressively across CKD stages and represents a novel lipid biomarker of [[chronic-kidney-disease]] progression.
- **Total polyunsaturated fatty acids (PUFAs)** decreased with advancing CKD, while **monounsaturated fatty acids (MUFAs)** increased — reflecting a shift in fatty acid metabolism characteristic of chronic inflammatory and catabolic states.
- **EPA (eicosapentaenoic acid) and DHA (docosahexaenoic acid)** — the key anti-inflammatory omega-3 fatty acids — declined with CKD progression, indicating impaired omega-3 metabolism or increased consumption due to chronic oxidative stress.
- The accumulation of adrenic acid (C22:4n6) in advanced CKD may reflect impaired retroconversion or increased elongation of n-6 fatty acids, shifting the balance toward pro-inflammatory eicosanoid production.
- The proposed ratio could complement traditional biomarkers (creatinine, eGFR) for monitoring CKD trajectory, particularly in patients where traditional markers are unreliable.

## Methods

**Cross-sectional study** comparing fatty acid profiles across CKD stages. A total of 149 CKD patients (representing stages 1 through 5D/dialysis) and 43 healthy controls were enrolled. Serum fatty acid composition was determined by **gas chromatography** for 30 individual fatty acids and their derivatives. Patients were stratified by CKD stage (using KDOQI classification), and fatty acid profiles were compared across groups using appropriate statistical tests. Correlations between fatty acid ratios and renal function parameters (eGFR, creatinine, urea) were calculated.

## Detailed Summary

Szczuko et al. investigated changes in the serum fatty acid profile across the spectrum of CKD severity, seeking novel lipid biomarkers that could track disease progression. The key finding was that the ratio of gamma-linolenic acid (GLA, C18:3n6) to adrenic acid (C22:4n6) declined systematically with worsening CKD stage.

This ratio captures a fundamental shift in polyunsaturated fatty acid metabolism: GLA is an early intermediate in the n-6 PUFA pathway with anti-inflammatory properties (via DGLA-derived Series 1 prostaglandins), while adrenic acid is a downstream elongation product that can serve as substrate for pro-inflammatory mediators. A declining ratio therefore reflects a metabolic shift from anti-inflammatory to pro-inflammatory fatty acid balance.

The decline in EPA and DHA with CKD progression is clinically significant because these omega-3 fatty acids are potent anti-inflammatory mediators, and their loss contributes to the chronic inflammatory state that accelerates CKD and its cardiovascular complications. The mechanism of their decline may involve increased oxidative consumption (CKD is a state of heightened oxidative stress), impaired hepatic desaturase activity (required for EPA/DHA synthesis from alpha-linolenic acid), or dietary deficiency.

The increase in MUFAs with CKD progression likely reflects upregulated stearoyl-CoA desaturase (SCD) activity, which converts saturated fatty acids to MUFAs as a compensatory response to inflammation-driven lipid peroxidation.

## Relevance to WikiBiome

This study connects CKD progression to **fatty acid metabolism**, an area where the gut microbiome plays an increasingly recognized role. Gut bacteria modify dietary fatty acids through biohydrogenation, conjugation, and production of unique fatty acids (e.g., conjugated linoleic acid). CKD-associated dysbiosis may therefore alter the pool of fatty acids available to the host, contributing to the shifts documented in this study.

The anti-inflammatory omega-3 depletion is also relevant to the metallomics framework: omega-3 fatty acids are required for resolution of inflammation, and their loss in CKD may impair the host's ability to resolve metal-driven inflammatory responses in the kidney.

## Open Questions

- Does the C18:3n6/C22:4n6 ratio predict CKD progression independently of traditional markers in longitudinal cohorts?
- Do gut microbial alterations in CKD contribute to the observed fatty acid profile changes through microbial lipid metabolism?
- Can omega-3 fatty acid supplementation (EPA/DHA) slow CKD progression, and if so, is the benefit mediated through anti-inflammatory or microbiome-modulating mechanisms?
