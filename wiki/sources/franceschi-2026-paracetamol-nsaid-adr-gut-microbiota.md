## Key Findings

- **ADRs were significantly more frequent in female patients** (61% of 897 total ADR cases were adult females), a pattern observed across both paracetamol and NSAID drug classes ([[franceschi-2026-paracetamol-nsaid-adr-gut-microbiota]], retrospective cohort, n=897).
- **Paracetamol and NSAIDs have distinct toxicity profiles**: paracetamol-related ADRs were dominated by intentional overdose (55.1%) and unintentional overdose (20.6%), while NSAID ADRs were characterized by gastritis (53.4%), allergic reactions (32.8%), GI bleeding (8.2%), and renal failure (1.3%). Gastritis, GI bleeding, and renal failure occurred exclusively in the NSAID group.
- **Bacterial beta-glucuronidases are proposed as a central mechanism** linking gut microbiota to ADR risk for both drug classes: these enzymes deconjugate glucuronide metabolites in the intestinal lumen, regenerating the active parent compound and facilitating enterohepatic recycling that increases tissue exposure.
- **Chronic NSAID exposure alters gut microbial ecology** by reducing commensal taxa (e.g., [[lactobacillus]], [[bifidobacterium]]) and promoting Gram-negative overgrowth, establishing a vicious cycle where dysbiosis amplifies mucosal inflammation and drug toxicity.
- **No significant difference in hospitalization rates** between paracetamol-related and NSAID-related ADRs (38% vs. 27%, p = ns).

## Methods

- **Design**: Retrospective observational study, single-center (Fondazione Policlinico Universitario A. Gemelli IRCCS, Rome).
- **Population**: All patients diagnosed with ADRs presenting to the Emergency Department between 2015 and 2021. Non-pregnant patients meeting inclusion criteria were enrolled. Of 897 total ADR patients, 107 had paracetamol- and/or NSAID-related reactions (29 paracetamol, 73 NSAIDs, 7 both).
- **Variables**: Age, sex, clinical presentation, adverse reaction description, drug involved, overdose status, patient outcome (admission vs. discharge).
- **Statistics**: Descriptive statistics, Student's t-test for continuous variables, p < 0.05 threshold. IBM SPSS v23.0.
- **Ethics**: Approved 03-08-2022 by Ethics Committee (protocol ID 5121, protocol number 0025817/22).
- **Limitations**: No direct microbiome data were collected; gut microbiota role is inferred from published literature only. Single-center design limits generalizability.

## Detailed Summary

Among 897 Emergency Department patients with ADRs over 6 years, 107 cases (62 females; mean age 37 +/- 23 years) were attributed to paracetamol and/or NSAIDs. The NSAIDs most commonly implicated were ketoprofen, diclofenac, aspirin, ibuprofen, coxibs, and nimesulide.

**Paracetamol ADRs (n=29):** Predominantly intentional overdose (16/29, 55.1%), unintentional overdose (6/29, 20.6%), allergic reactions (5/29, 17.2%), and elevated hepatic enzymes (3/29, 10.3%). No gastritis, GI bleeding, or renal failure.

**NSAID ADRs (n=73):** Dominated by gastritis (39/73, 53.4%), allergic reactions (24/73, 32.8%), GI bleeding (6/73, 8.2%), intentional overdose (5/73, 6.8%), elevated hepatic enzymes (4/73, 5.4%), unintentional overdose (4/73, 5.4%), and acute renal failure (1/73, 1.3%).

Voluntary poisoning (p < 0.0001) and unintentional poisoning (p < 0.002) were significantly more frequent with paracetamol. Allergic reactions were significantly more frequent with NSAIDs (p < 0.0001).

The paper's discussion section, while not supported by primary microbiome data from this cohort, synthesizes literature on three microbiota-mediated mechanisms:

1. **Beta-glucuronidase-mediated drug reactivation**: Bacterial [[beta-glucuronidase]] enzymes hydrolyze glucuronide conjugates of both paracetamol and NSAIDs in the intestinal lumen, regenerating the active parent drug. For paracetamol, this increases hepatic burden and NAPQI formation. For NSAIDs, this causes repeated mucosal exposure and local inflammation.

2. **Dysbiosis-driven glutathione depletion**: Altered microbial production of sulphur-containing amino acids (cysteine, methionine) may reduce hepatic [[glutathione]] synthesis, compromising detoxification of paracetamol's toxic metabolite NAPQI.

3. **NSAID-induced dysbiosis vicious cycle**: NSAIDs reduce protective commensals ([[lactobacillus]], [[bifidobacterium]]) that produce short-chain fatty acids (SCFAs), while promoting Gram-negative overgrowth. Increased LPS translocation amplifies systemic inflammation and may contribute to renal toxicity.

The authors propose that sex-dimorphic microbiota composition — premenopausal women having greater abundance of [[bacteroides]] and Firmicutes species involved in bile acid and xenobiotic metabolism — may partly explain the female predominance in ADRs beyond pharmacokinetic differences alone.

## Relevance to WikiBiome

- **[[beta-glucuronidase]]**: This study reinforces the central role of bacterial beta-glucuronidases in drug metabolism and toxicity, directly relevant to the existing concept page. The enzyme's ability to reactivate glucuronide-conjugated drugs in the gut lumen is a key mechanism connecting microbiota to pharmacovigilance.
- **[[lactobacillus]]** and **[[bifidobacterium]]**: Further evidence that chronic NSAID use depletes these protective commensals, contributing to barrier dysfunction and increased GI permeability — relevant to entity pages on both taxa.
- **[[glutathione]]**: The proposed link between dysbiosis-driven depletion of sulphur amino acids and reduced glutathione synthesis provides a microbiota-mediated mechanism for hepatotoxicity vulnerability.
- **Pharmacomicrobiomics concept**: Supports a concept page on drug-microbiota interactions and microbiota-informed pharmacovigilance as an emerging field.

## Relevance to Cureva

- **STOP signal potential**: The finding that NSAIDs cause dysbiosis (depleting [[lactobacillus]]/[[bifidobacterium]], promoting Gram-negative overgrowth) while simultaneously relying on an intact mucosal barrier for safety suggests a potential STOP for chronic NSAID use in patients with pre-existing dysbiosis or conditions where gut barrier integrity is already compromised (e.g., IBD signatures, [[endometriosis]]).
- **Intervention hypothesis**: Bacterial beta-glucuronidase inhibitors could theoretically reduce ADR severity for both paracetamol and NSAIDs by blocking enterohepatic drug recycling. Probiotic supplementation with [[lactobacillus]] or [[bifidobacterium]] is proposed by the authors as a strategy to bolster mucosal defense.
- **Sex-stratified risk**: The female predominance in ADRs, potentially mediated by sex-dimorphic microbiota, is relevant to Cureva's approach to personalized intervention — particularly for conditions with strong female predominance (endometriosis, PMDD, fibromyalgia) where NSAID use is common.

## Contradictions / Tensions

- The paper's microbiota discussion is entirely literature-derived — **no microbiome data were collected from the study cohort**. The authors acknowledge this as a major limitation. Claims about microbiota-mediated ADR mechanisms in this specific population remain speculative.

## Open Questions

1. Would prospective microbiome sequencing of ADR patients reveal specific microbial signatures predictive of paracetamol hepatotoxicity or NSAID enteropathy?
2. Can bacterial beta-glucuronidase inhibitors (already explored for irinotecan and mycophenolate) reduce NSAID-induced GI toxicity in clinical trials?
3. Does the sex-dimorphic microbiota composition causally drive the female ADR predominance, or is it merely correlated with other physiological sex differences?
4. How does pre-existing dysbiosis from conditions like IBD or endometriosis interact with NSAID-induced dysbiosis to compound ADR risk?
5. Could microbiota profiling prior to chronic NSAID therapy serve as a practical risk stratification tool?
