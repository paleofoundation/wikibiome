## Key Claims

- 25 popular baby foods consumed in Italy (produced in EU) were analyzed for 30 elements; none posed a health hazard for infants based on PTWI comparisons
- Toxic element intakes were always below safety limits: the most concentrated toxic elements never exceeded 86% of PTWI
- Baby food provides approximately 70% of the adequate intake (AI) for Mn, Cu, Fe, Zn, Ca, K, and P for infants aged 6-12 months
- [[nickel]] in powdered milk (sample 7) showed the highest daily intake (EDI) at 9.43 ug/kg bw/day, representing 85.7% of the PTWI-derived daily risk estimator (11 ug/kg bw/day)
- Thirteen elements (Cr, Co, Ba, Cd, Ce, La, Pb, Rb, Tl, Te, Ti, Th, U) were below limits of detection in all samples
- [[mercury]] was highest in a cereal cream sample, where relevant intake exceeded the daily risk estimator (PTDI)
- Sb and Hg were detectable in 100% of samples; Sr in 96%, Sn in 68%; Al 36%, Ni 16%, As 12%

## Methods/Findings

- **Design**: 25 baby foods from Italian supermarkets (10 powdered milks, 13 homogenized foods, 3 cereal creams) for children 0-6 months
- **Analysis**: ICP-AES and ICP-MS after EPA 3050B acid digestion; Hg by AAS (EPA 7470); three methods covering 30 elements
- **Essential elements**: Powdered milk highest for K, Ca, P, S, Fe, Cu, Zn; cereal cream highest for Si and Mn; cheese highest for Mg
- **Toxic elements (Table 5)**: Al <2.5 mg/kg in all powdered milks; As <0.1 mg/kg in powdered milks; Ni <0.5 mg/kg in most samples (0.5 mg/kg in sample 7 powdered milk); Hg ranged 0.011-0.064 mg/kg
- **Risk assessment**: EDI compared to PTWI/PTDI; Ni EDI up to 9.43 ug/kg bw/day (85.7% of PTWI); Al EDI up to 13.1 ug/kg bw/day (4.58% of PTWI); Hg in cereal cream at ~100% of PTDI; As at 6.68% of PTWI
- **Nutritional adequacy**: Baby food consumption contributes 0.25-18.1% of PRI for Fe; 0.72-69% for Zn; 0.75-33% for Cu
