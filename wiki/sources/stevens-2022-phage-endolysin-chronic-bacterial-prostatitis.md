## Key Findings

- **First reported clinical use of a phage endolysin (ORF28 lysin) for chronic bacterial prostatitis (CBP)**, resulting in bacteriological eradication of *[[enterococcus-faecalis]]* and significant symptom improvement (NIH-CPSI score reduced from 24 to 6) ([[stevens-2022-phage-endolysin-chronic-bacterial-prostatitis]], case report, n=1).
- **Biofilm-mediated antibiotic resistance** was the likely driver of treatment failure: over 6 years the patient experienced recurring *E. faecalis* prostatitis despite repeated courses of Unasyn, Avelox, fosfomycin, linezolid, and piperacillin-tazobactam. MIC values progressively increased, and the strain developed resistance to oxacillin, cefoxitin, gentamicin, and tetracycline ([[stevens-2022-phage-endolysin-chronic-bacterial-prostatitis]], case report).
- **Whole bacteriophage therapy (Sekstafag cocktail and custom 3-phage cocktail) failed**, likely due to poor penetration of intact phages through rectal mucosa into prostatic tissue. Purified endolysin (a smaller protein, 46.1 kDa) succeeded where whole phages did not ([[stevens-2022-phage-endolysin-chronic-bacterial-prostatitis]], case report).
- **MBL2 genetic deficiency** identified via whole exome sequencing (heterozygous p.Arg52Cys variant, genotype A/D) with serum MBL level of 375 ng/ml (normal >2,880), providing a mechanistic explanation for why this patient was predisposed to recurrent bacterial infection ([[stevens-2022-phage-endolysin-chronic-bacterial-prostatitis]], case report).
- **No adverse reactions** were reported during or after endolysin administration, supporting the safety profile of phage-derived lytic enzymes ([[stevens-2022-phage-endolysin-chronic-bacterial-prostatitis]], case report).

## Methods

- **Study design:** Single-patient case report documenting a 6-year clinical course (2016–2022).
- **Patient:** 39-year-old Slovakian man with chronic pelvic pain syndrome / chronic bacterial prostatitis caused by *E. faecalis*.
- **Diagnosis:** Confirmed by repeated culture of expressed prostatic secretion and ejaculate; ultrasonography and MRI confirmed prostatitis. Severity tracked using the NIH Chronic Prostatitis Symptom Index (NIH-CPSI).
- **Genetic workup:** Whole exome sequencing (WES) on NovaSeq 6000 platform identified MBL2 variant; confirmed by immunological assay of serum MBL levels.
- **Endolysin preparation:** ORF28 lysin from phage phiEf11, cloned and expressed in *E. coli* BL21/DE3 via pGEX4T2 vector with GST tag, purified by glutathione affinity chromatography and thrombin cleavage. Final concentration 0.8 mg/ml, confirmed by SDS-PAGE at 46.1 kDa.
- **Sensitivity testing:** Spot testing of serial dilutions on soft-agar lawns of patient's *E. faecalis* strain 587A2 demonstrated lytic activity down to 0.4 microgram/ml.
- **Endolysin administration:** Diluted 1:5 in saline, applied rectally (5 ml every 12 hours for 7 days). Two treatment courses: initial 2 doses in late 2021 (limited supply), full 7-day course in July 2022.
- **Phage cocktail (failed):** Three newly isolated phages (vB_Efa_VP14, vB_Efa_VP15, vB_Efa_VP16, genus Efquatrovirus) at 10^9 PFU/ml applied rectally twice daily for 2 weeks — no clinical effect.

## Detailed Summary

**Clinical course:** The patient presented in October 2016 with neuropathic perineal pain radiating to the scrotum and anogenital area. Urological examination revealed an enlarged and painful prostate confirmed by ultrasonography and MRI. Cultures of expressed prostatic secretion and ejaculate confirmed *[[enterococcus-faecalis]]* infection.

**Phase 1 — Antibiotic-treatable infections (2016–2019):** Initial treatment with Unasyn (ampicillin-sulbactam 750 mg twice daily, 14 days) achieved bacteriological clearance but clinical symptoms persisted (average CPSI ~16 during remission). Over 3 years, the patient experienced at least 3 exacerbations annually, with CPSI reaching 26+ during flares. *E. faecalis* was confirmed at every flare. Avelox was tried but caused tachycardia and severe headaches after 14 days.

**Phase 2 — Escalating resistance (2019–2020):** By March 2019, *E. faecalis* MIC values began increasing. By June 2020, the clinical microbiology laboratory reported resistance to oxacillin, cefoxitin, gentamicin, and tetracycline. Aggressive combination therapy was attempted: linezolid 600 mg twice daily + ampicillin 2g every 6 hours (week 1), then linezolid + fosfomycin 8g every 8 hours (week 2), then fosfomycin monotherapy 8g every 8 hours (weeks 3–4). Disease subsided but recurred after 1 year. Biofilm formation was considered the principal mechanism of treatment failure.

**Phase 3 — Alternative therapies (2020–2022):** Bacteriophage therapy (Sekstafag commercially available cocktail + custom 3-phage cocktail targeting the patient's own strain) was applied rectally twice daily for 2 weeks — no clinical effect. Bacteriophages likely could not penetrate through rectal mucosa to reach the prostate. An autologous *E. faecalis* vaccine was also used without lasting benefit. Probiotics (lactobacilli) were tried without success. By late 2021, the patient developed severe allergic reactions (whole-body itching and exanthema) to both Unasyn and fosfomycin, eliminating the remaining antibiotic options.

**Phase 4 — Endolysin therapy (2021–2022):** With all antibiotics and phage therapy having failed, the ORF28 phage endolysin was applied. Initial application of 2 rectal doses in late 2021 provided symptom relief but insufficient supply prevented a full course. In July 2022, with CPSI at 24 and *E. faecalis* reconfirmed, a 7-day course of endolysin (1 ml diluted 1:5 in saline, 5 ml rectally every 12 hours) achieved significant clinical improvement and bacteriological eradication. Post-treatment CPSI dropped to 6 (pain score 0, urinary symptom score 3, quality-of-life score 3). Ejaculate was sterile; expressed prostatic secretion showed only coagulase-negative staphylococci (normal flora). No adverse reactions reported.

**Genetic finding:** WES revealed a heterozygous pathogenic variant in *MBL2* (c.154C>T, p.Arg52Cys, rs5030737) — a D-allele variant causing mannose-binding lectin (MBL) deficiency. Serum MBL was 375 ng/ml versus normal >2,880 ng/ml. MBL is a key component of innate immunity that activates the lectin complement pathway; its deficiency increases susceptibility to bacterial infections, particularly in the urogenital tract.

## Relevance to Cureva

- **Intervention hypothesis — phage endolysin therapy:** Purified endolysins may be superior to whole-phage therapy for infections in anatomically sequestered sites (prostate, biofilms) because their smaller molecular size (~46 kDa vs. ~100 nm phage particles) allows better tissue penetration. This is a novel drug-repurposing/biologic-intervention concept for recalcitrant biofilm-associated infections.
- **STOP signal — repeated antibiotic cycling in biofilm-mediated CBP:** This case demonstrates that repeated antibiotic courses without biofilm disruption drive resistance evolution and can culminate in allergic sensitization, eliminating remaining treatment options. Conventional antibiotic-based management of recurrent CBP may be counterproductive when biofilm is the underlying mechanism.
- **Personalized medicine angle:** WES-guided identification of immune deficits (MBL2 deficiency) could inform which patients are at high risk for chronic relapsing infections and would benefit from alternative antimicrobial strategies.
- **Karen's Brain Primitive 4 (Microbial Metal Dependencies as Achilles' Heels):** While no specific metal dependency is identified for *E. faecalis* in this paper, the Achilles' heel concept applies structurally — the endolysin targets a specific vulnerability (peptidoglycan bonds) that the organism cannot evolve resistance to as readily as it does to antibiotics.
- **Karen's Brain Primitive 5 (Two-Sided Ecological Engineering):** The endolysin selectively eliminates *E. faecalis* without disrupting commensal flora (evidenced by normal coagulase-negative staphylococci persisting post-treatment), exemplifying targeted pathogen suppression.
- **Karen's Brain Primitive 6 (Interkingdom Relationships and Functional Shielding):** The biofilm context is relevant — *E. faecalis* biofilm in prostatic acini functions as a physical shield against both antibiotics and immune effectors.

## Contradictions / Tensions

- **Phage therapy vs. endolysin therapy:** Previous case reports (Letkiewicz 2009, Johri 2021) reported successful phage therapy for CBP, but in this patient whole phages applied rectally were completely ineffective while the purified endolysin protein succeeded. The authors suggest this is due to differential tissue penetration — phage particles (~100 nm) may not cross rectal mucosa as effectively as a soluble 46 kDa protein. This does not contradict the earlier reports (which used different delivery routes) but adds nuance about route-dependent efficacy.
- **Preprint status:** This is a Research Square preprint (posted November 4, 2022) and has not undergone formal peer review. Findings should be interpreted with appropriate caution pending peer-reviewed publication.

## Open Questions

1. **Tissue penetration mechanism:** How does rectally applied endolysin reach the prostate? The authors hypothesize absorption through the rectal venous plexus to the prostatic venous plexus but have no direct evidence (no detection of endolysin in ejaculate or prostatic tissue).
2. **Durability of response:** The paper documents the immediate post-treatment period but does not report long-term follow-up. Does the *E. faecalis* biofilm eventually re-establish?
3. **Resistance to endolysins:** Can *E. faecalis* develop resistance to the ORF28 endolysin? Lysins target highly conserved peptidoglycan bonds, making resistance theoretically unlikely, but this has not been tested longitudinally in this patient.
4. **Generalizability:** Would endolysin therapy work for other CBP-causing organisms (Gram-negative Enterobacteriaceae), or is it limited to Gram-positive targets where the peptidoglycan layer is externally accessible?
5. **MBL2 deficiency prevalence in CBP patients:** Is innate immune deficiency (lectin pathway) a common predisposing factor for chronic prostatitis? This could identify a subpopulation that benefits most from alternative antimicrobial strategies.
6. **Metal ecology:** The paper does not investigate metal concentrations in prostatic tissue or their relationship to *E. faecalis* biofilm formation. Given that zinc is normally concentrated in prostatic fluid and has antimicrobial properties, the metal ecology of this infection niche is unexplored and could be relevant to the WikiBiome framework.
