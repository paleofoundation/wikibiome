## Key Findings

- **Siderophore-antibiotic conjugates exploit bacterial iron transport** to bypass the outer membrane barrier that defeats conventional antibiotics in MDR Gram-negatives ([[carvalho-2014-siderophores-trojan-horses-mdr]], expert-opinion).
- **BAL30072 (sulfactam siderophore)** shows synergy with carbapenems against MDR [[acinetobacter-baumannii]] and [[pseudomonas-aeruginosa]], demonstrating clinical-stage viability of the Trojan horse concept.
- **Active siderophore-mediated transport achieves MICs 100-fold lower** than passive diffusion, turning the pathogen's own iron hunger against it.
- **Three Trojan horse strategies** distinguished: (1) siderophore-antibiotic conjugates for targeted delivery; (2) siderophore analogs that block iron uptake competitively; (3) toxic metal-siderophore complexes (e.g., gallium) that poison iron-dependent metabolism.
- **Species-specific targeting** is achievable because different pathogens use different siderophore receptors — enabling precision antimicrobials that spare commensals.

## Detailed Summary

This Frontiers in Microbiology review establishes the theoretical and practical framework for exploiting bacterial iron acquisition as a drug delivery mechanism. The core insight is that MDR Gram-negative pathogens have an impermeability problem: their outer membranes exclude most antibiotics, which is why carbapenems (small enough to pass through porins) are often the last resort. However, these same pathogens have evolved high-affinity iron uptake systems using siderophores — small molecules that chelate environmental iron and are actively transported through dedicated outer membrane receptors. The Trojan horse strategy conjugates antibiotics to siderophore structures, hijacking this active transport to bypass the permeability barrier entirely. The review details several clinical-stage compounds: **BAL30072**, a sulfactam siderophore that showed potent activity against MDR *A. baumannii* and *P. aeruginosa*, with synergy when combined with carbapenems; and the monosulfactam BAL30072's efficacy against carbapenem-resistant strains. The authors distinguish three flavors of the strategy: (1) siderophore-drug conjugates for targeted delivery; (2) competitive siderophore analogs that block natural siderophore uptake; and (3) toxic metal complexes (e.g., [[gallium]]-siderophore) that exploit iron transport to deliver a poisonous metal mimic. The 100-fold MIC reduction achieved by active transport vs. passive diffusion underscores the potency advantage. The species-specificity of siderophore receptors enables precision targeting: a siderophore conjugate designed for *P. aeruginosa*'s pyoverdine receptor will not affect *E. coli* or gut commensals using different siderophore systems.
