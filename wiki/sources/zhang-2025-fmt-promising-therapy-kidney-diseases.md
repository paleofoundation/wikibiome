# FMT Is a Promising Therapy for Kidney Diseases

## Key Findings

- **FMT targets the gut-kidney axis through three primary mechanisms**: renin-angiotensin system (RAS) inhibition, inflammation attenuation, and intestinal barrier restoration — addressing core pathogenic pathways in [[chronic-kidney-disease]].
- **FMT has shown therapeutic promise across multiple CKD subtypes**: diabetic nephropathy, IgA nephropathy, membranous nephropathy, and focal segmental glomerulosclerosis (FSGS), suggesting that gut [[dysbiosis]] contributes to diverse CKD etiologies.
- **Most published evidence remains preclinical** (animal models), with limited but encouraging data from early clinical trials and case reports.
- **The broad applicability across CKD subtypes** suggests that gut microbiome disruption is a shared downstream pathway regardless of the initial renal insult.
- Future research directions include AI-guided personalized FMT design, optimal donor selection, and standardized protocols.

## Methods

- **Design**: Systematic review of FMT research in kidney diseases.
- **Scope**: Covers preclinical animal models and available clinical data across CKD subtypes.
- **Diseases covered**: Diabetic nephropathy, IgA nephropathy, membranous nephropathy, FSGS, and general CKD.
- **Journal**: Frontiers in Medicine (2025).

## Detailed Summary

This systematic review by Zhang et al. comprehensively surveys the evidence for FMT as a therapeutic strategy across the spectrum of kidney diseases. The authors organize their findings by disease subtype, demonstrating that FMT has been investigated in diabetic nephropathy (where it reduces proteinuria and inflammatory markers), IgA nephropathy (where it may modulate mucosal immune responses driving IgA overproduction), membranous nephropathy (where it could address autoimmune-driven glomerular damage), and FSGS (where it may reduce proteinuria through barrier restoration). The mechanistic triad of RAS inhibition, anti-inflammatory effects, and barrier restoration provides a coherent framework for understanding FMT's therapeutic potential. RAS modulation is particularly interesting because the gut microbiome has been shown to influence angiotensin-converting enzyme activity and angiotensinogen production, creating a microbial pathway for blood pressure and renal hemodynamic regulation. The review acknowledges the field's key limitation: the paucity of randomized controlled clinical trials in human CKD patients.
