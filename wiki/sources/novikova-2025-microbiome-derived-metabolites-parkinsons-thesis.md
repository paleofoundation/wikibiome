# Microbiome-Derived Metabolites in Parkinson's Disease (Thesis)

## Summary

This doctoral thesis provides a comprehensive investigation of [[microbiome-derived-metabolites]] in [[parkinsons-disease]], integrating metabolomic profiling with microbiome sequencing to map the metabolic communication between gut microbes and the host nervous system.

## Key Findings

- Systematic metabolomic profiling revealed widespread alterations in microbial metabolites in PD
- SCFA depletion (particularly butyrate) was the most consistent finding across PD cohorts
- Tryptophan metabolite diversion from serotonin pathway to kynurenine pathway was documented
- Bile acid transformation by gut bacteria was disrupted, with functional consequences for gut barrier and neuroinflammation
- p-Cresol and other uremic toxin-like metabolites were elevated in PD
- Metabolite-microbe correlation analysis identified specific bacterial producers of PD-relevant metabolites

## Relevance to Gut-Metal-Microbiome Axis

This thesis provides granular metabolomic data connecting [[microbiome-derived-metabolites]] to [[parkinsons-disease]] pathogenesis. Many of the altered metabolites have direct metal interactions: butyrate regulates [[iron]] absorption through HIF signaling, bile acids complex with [[iron]] and [[copper]], tryptophan metabolites (quinolinic acid) chelate [[iron]] and catalyze Fenton chemistry, and p-cresol inhibits dopamine synthesis (a process requiring [[iron]]-containing tyrosine hydroxylase). The thesis demonstrates that the metabolomic output of the PD gut is fundamentally shaped by metal-microbe interactions.
