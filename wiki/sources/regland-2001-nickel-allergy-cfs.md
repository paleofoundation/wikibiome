## Key Claims

- 52% of 204 women with chronic fatigue and muscle pain had a positive history of nickel contact dermatitis, which is comparable to prevalence in women with CFS and positive skin patch test (52%)
- Nickel allergy significantly impacted treatment response: 37% of non-allergic patients were good responders to Staphylococcus vaccine vs only 16% of allergic patients (p<0.0009)
- Among allergic smokers, only 6% were good responders, suggesting cigarette smoke (which contains trace nickel) synergistically worsens nickel allergy outcomes
- Exposure to nickel through dietary intake or cigarette smoke inhalation may trigger systemic nickel allergy and contribute to chronic fatigue and muscle pain syndromes

## Methods/Findings

- **Design**: Analysis of data from clinical trials of Staphylococcus vaccine (Staphypan Berna) at Goteborg University, Sweden
- **Population**: 204 women with chronic fatigue and muscle pain (mean age 48, range 21-73); diagnosed with both fibromyalgia and CFS criteria; no autoimmune disorder
- **Assessment**: Treatment outcome rated by Comprehensive Psychopathological Rating Scale (CPRS); good response = 50%+ decrease in total CPRS score at 6 months; nickel allergy assessed by history of skin hypersensitivity to metal objects
- **Results by subgroup**: Non-allergic non-smokers 39% good response; non-allergic smokers 30%; allergic non-smokers 21%; allergic smokers 6% (p=0.02 Chi-square)
- **Case reports**: (1) 36-year-old nickel-sensitive smoker with fatigue/muscle pain - improved dramatically after quitting smoking and reducing dietary nickel; relapsed after resuming smoking. (2) 54-year-old non-smoker with CFS/fibromyalgia/IBS - improved after adopting low-nickel diet
- **Discussion**: MELISA (lymphocyte proliferation test) found 81% (13/16) of vaccine non-responders reacted to nickel; dietary nickel intake estimated 200-300 ug/day, potentially reaching 900+ ug with high-nickel foods; cigarette smoke contains trace metals including nickel
