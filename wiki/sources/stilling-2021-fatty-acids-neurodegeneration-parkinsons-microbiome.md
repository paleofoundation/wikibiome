# Fatty Acid Metabolism and Neurodegeneration in Parkinson's Disease

## Summary

This study examines the relationship between fatty acid metabolism, particularly long-chain polyunsaturated fatty acids, and neurodegeneration in [[parkinsons-disease]]. It integrates evidence from clinical cohorts, animal models, and in vitro studies to connect lipid metabolism with PD pathology.

## Key Findings

- DHA (docosahexaenoic acid) and other omega-3 fatty acids are depleted in PD brain tissue
- Lipid peroxidation products (4-HNE, MDA) are elevated in PD substantia nigra
- Omega-3 fatty acid supplementation shows neuroprotective effects in PD animal models
- Fatty acid composition of neuronal membranes influences susceptibility to oxidative damage
- Gut microbiome alterations in PD affect fatty acid metabolism through multiple pathways
- Reduced SCFA production in PD impairs colonocyte metabolism and gut barrier function
- Fatty acid-based interventions may complement standard PD pharmacotherapy

## Relevance to Gut-Metal-Microbiome Axis

Lipid peroxidation is the biochemical execution step of [[ferroptosis]], making fatty acid metabolism directly relevant to [[iron]]-mediated neurodegeneration in [[parkinsons-disease]]. [[Iron]] catalyzes the peroxidation of membrane PUFAs through Fenton chemistry, and the elevated lipid peroxidation products in PD brain tissue confirm active [[ferroptosis]]. The depletion of DHA in PD brain paradoxically makes remaining membranes more vulnerable, as compensatory enrichment with arachidonic acid increases ferroptotic susceptibility. Gut microbiome changes in PD affect this process by: (1) reducing [[butyrate]] that upregulates GPX4 (the key anti-ferroptotic enzyme); (2) altering bile acid profiles that regulate [[iron]] absorption; (3) shifting gut fatty acid metabolism. [[Manganese]] also catalyzes lipid peroxidation in dopaminergic neurons, compounding the [[iron]]-dependent damage.

## Limitations

- Integrative review of diverse data types
- Direct measurement of brain lipids limited to post-mortem studies
- Omega-3 supplementation trials in PD have shown mixed results
- Relationship between gut and brain fatty acid pools is complex
