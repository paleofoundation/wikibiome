## Key Findings

- **Dietary fiber supplementation significantly reduced indoxyl sulfate** (SMD −0.55, 95% CI −1.05 to −0.05, P=0.03) across 10 RCTs pooling 292 CKD patients.
- **p-Cresyl sulfate was also significantly reduced** (SMD −0.47, 95% CI −0.80 to −0.14, P<0.01).
- Secondary outcomes showed reductions in **BUN and uric acid**, though effects on creatinine and eGFR were not significant.
- **Indoxyl sulfate reduction was more pronounced in dialysis patients** compared to pre-dialysis CKD, suggesting greater benefit in advanced disease where uremic toxin burden is highest.
- This is the **highest evidence level** (systematic review of RCTs) supporting a dietary intervention for uremic toxin reduction in CKD.

## Relevance to WikiBiome

Supports the concept that dietary substrate availability shapes microbial metabolite output — fiber feeds SCFA producers and diverts tryptophan metabolism away from indole pathways. Relevant to concept pages on [[bile-acid-metabolism]] and uremic toxin production. Provides the strongest available evidence for a modifiable dietary factor in CKD-associated dysbiosis.

## Relevance to Cureva

Top-tier evidence (Primitive 5: two-sided ecological engineering) for a dietary intervention in CKD. The effect sizes are moderate but clinically meaningful given the low-risk nature of fiber supplementation. The differential response in dialysis vs. pre-dialysis patients informs staging of interventions. Should be triangulated against the CKD signature page — fiber likely works by supporting depleted SCFA producers (Faecalibacterium, Roseburia) identified in Zhang 2023.
