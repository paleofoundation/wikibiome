## Key Findings

- **Candida dominates PPI-treated gastric mucosa**: *Candida* detected in **96.9%** (average 43.59%, median 38.84%) of gastric mucosal samples from PPI-treated GERD patients. Relative abundance of *Candida* significantly higher in PPI groups vs. nt-GERD and HC. Notably, no significant difference between short-term (s-PPI, average 6 months) and long-term (l-PPI, average 2 years) PPI users — colonization reaches maximum within ~2 months.
- **Gastric mucosal fungal community disruption**: Alpha diversity (Shannon, Chao1) significantly reduced in s-PPI group vs. HC (P=0.007, P=0.032). Community structure (Bray-Curtis, Weighted UniFrac) differed significantly between HC and s-PPI (P=0.022) and between l-PPI and nt-GERD (P=0.004).
- **LDA biomarkers in gastric mucosa**: *Candida* was the top biomarker for the PPI group. *Nothojafnea*, *Rhizodermea*, *Ambispora*, *Saccharicola* were more abundant in l-PPI; *Trichoderma*, *Alternaria*, *Aspergillus*, *Monographella*, *Lysurus* more abundant in nt-GERD group.
- **Fecal mycobiota**: Distinct fecal fungal communities in GERD vs. HC regardless of PPI status (ANOSIM R=0.12, P=0.004). Shannon index significantly lower in nt-GERD vs. HC (P=0.021). *Alternaria*, *Aspergillus*, *Mycenella*, *Exserohilum*, *Clitopilus* more abundant in s-PPI; *Alternaria*, *Aspergillus*, *Podospora*, *Phallus*, *Monographella* more abundant in l-PPI vs. nt-GERD.
- **Esophageal mucosal breaks not associated**: No significant difference in *Candida* abundance between GERD patients with vs. without endoscopically visible esophageal lesions — suggesting mucosal fungal colonization is systemic rather than lesion-specific.

## Methods

Cross-sectional observational study. Participants recruited June 2013–July 2014 at Chinese PLA General Hospital (18–75 years, Han ethnicity). Four groups: HC (n=12), nt-GERD [untreated GERD, n=21], s-PPI [short-term PPI, 0.5–1 year average 6 months, n=14], l-PPI [long-term PPI, 1–3 years, average 2 years, n=18]. PPI dose: omeprazole 40 mg/day. Exclusions: gastric cancer, peptic ulcer, hepatobiliary surgery, Barrett's/EAC, pregnancy/lactation, antibiotics/probiotics within 1 month, *H. pylori* positive. Gastric antrum mucosal biopsies and fecal specimens; ITS1-ITS2 gene sequencing; UPARSE-OTU clustering at 97% similarity; 2,091 OTUs (gastric), 2,399 OTUs (fecal). Alpha diversity: Observed features, Shannon, Chao1, Simpson. Beta diversity: Bray-Curtis, weighted UniFrac, Aitchison. LEfSe analysis (LDA>2, P<0.08 gastric; P<0.08 fecal). LinDA for differential abundance.

## Detailed Summary

This study establishes that GERD involves a fungal dysbiosis — independent of bacterial changes — that is further exacerbated by PPI therapy.

**Mechanism of PPI-driven Candida expansion**: PPIs raise intragastric pH, removing the acid barrier that normally prevents *Candida* colonization. Elevated pH also promotes environmental changes favorable to fungal growth and impairs bacterial competition from acid-tolerant bacteria (*Lactobacillus*) that normally suppress *Candida* via competitive exclusion and bacteriocin production.

**Candida and visceral hypersensitivity**: A key clinical implication is that *Candida* dysbiosis may contribute to persistent symptoms in PPI-treated GERD patients. Prior research found that intestinal fungal dysbiosis is associated with visceral hypersensitivity in IBS patients. The authors suggest this mechanism — *Candida* expansion → visceral hypersensitivity → persistent reflux perception despite acid suppression — may explain why many patients on long-term PPIs continue to report symptoms.

**Fecal mycobiota findings reinforce systemic scope**: Distinct fecal fungal communities in GERD independent of PPI status confirm that GERD has a systemic mycobiome signature, not merely a localized esophageal/gastric one. This aligns with growing evidence that GERD should be understood as a systemic condition involving the entire gut ecosystem.

**Interkingdom dimension**: *Lactobacillus* in the stomach normally suppresses *C. albicans* through displacement. PPI therapy depletes acid-tolerant *Lactobacillus* (acid is required for their ecological niche), removing this brake on fungal overgrowth — a classic interkingdom ecological disruption.

## Contradictions / Tensions

- Single center, small n=65; cross-sectional design cannot confirm causality.
- Short (6-month) vs. long-term (2-year) PPI use did not differ significantly in *Candida* colonization — plateau effect within 2 months, consistent with early ecological tipping point.
- Study excluded *H. pylori*-positive patients; in real-world GERD, H. pylori-PPI interaction could modify findings.

## Open Questions

- Does antifungal therapy in PPI-treated GERD patients improve persistent symptoms (testing the visceral hypersensitivity hypothesis)?
- What specific PPI mechanisms (pH change vs. direct fungal effects) drive *Candida* expansion?
- Are fecal *Candida* levels a feasible non-invasive biomarker of gastric *Candida* colonization in GERD?
