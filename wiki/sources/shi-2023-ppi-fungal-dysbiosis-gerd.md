# Key Findings

- **[[candida-albicans]] dramatically increased after PPI use** — detected in 96.9% of gastric mucosal samples from PPI-treated GERD patients, significantly higher than untreated controls [[shi-2023-ppi-fungal-dysbiosis-gerd|cross-sectional, n=65]]
- **PPI promotes Candida colonization** in both short-term and long-term use, suggesting that acid suppression creates a permissive environment for fungal overgrowth
- **Fungal dysbiosis is intrinsic to GERD** — mycobiome alterations were present in GERD patients regardless of PPI status, but PPI further exacerbated the dysbiosis
- **[[alternaria]], [[aspergillus]], and [[trichoderma]]** were among other fungi with altered abundance in GERD gastric mucosa

# Relevance to WikiBiome

- Critical evidence for the [[candida-albicans]] entity page — PPI-mediated Candida expansion is a clinically important iatrogenic effect
- Informs [[aspergillus]] entity page regarding gastric colonization in the context of acid suppression
- Establishes that GERD has a **mycobiome signature** independent of treatment, adding a fungal dimension to the GERD microbiome story
- Supports broader concept of drug-induced dysbiosis — PPIs alter not just bacterial but also fungal ecology

# Relevance to Cureva

- **Primitive 6 (Interkingdom Relationships and Functional Shielding)**: Candida expansion under PPI creates conditions for fungal-bacterial biofilm formation and potential functional shielding of bacterial pathogens in the gastric/esophageal environment
- **STOP signal**: Long-term PPI use in GERD may be counterproductive from a mycobiome perspective — promoting Candida overgrowth that could contribute to persistent symptoms or complications
- Intervention design should consider antifungal co-therapy or PPI alternatives that do not promote fungal colonization
- Baseline fungal dysbiosis in GERD (independent of PPI) suggests the mycobiome should be part of the GERD signature assessment
