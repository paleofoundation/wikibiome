# Socioeconomic Status and CKD: A Meta-Analysis

## Key Findings

- **Lower income** was the socioeconomic indicator most closely associated with [[chronic-kidney-disease]] prevalence and progression, with an odds ratio of 1.34 for CKD in the lowest versus highest income groups.
- **Lower education** was significantly associated with CKD prevalence, likely reflecting the combined effects of reduced health literacy, poorer access to preventive care, and greater exposure to occupational and environmental risk factors.
- **Combined low socioeconomic status** (composite measures of income, education, and occupation) was inversely associated with ESRD outcomes, meaning lower SES individuals were more likely to progress to ESRD and had worse outcomes once on renal replacement therapy.
- The associations were consistent across geographic regions and healthcare systems, suggesting that socioeconomic determinants operate through universal pathways rather than system-specific barriers.
- Potential mediating factors include differential access to healthcare, higher prevalence of CKD risk factors (diabetes, hypertension, obesity) in low SES populations, greater environmental and occupational exposures, and poorer dietary quality.

## Methods

**Systematic review and meta-analysis** of 43 articles examining the relationship between socioeconomic status and CKD. Multiple SES indicators were evaluated: income, education, occupation, and composite SES measures. Random-effects meta-analysis models were used to pool odds ratios across studies. Subgroup analyses were performed by SES indicator type, geographic region, and CKD outcome (prevalence, incidence, progression to ESRD). Heterogeneity was assessed using I-squared statistics. Publication bias was evaluated using funnel plots and Egger's test.

## Detailed Summary

Zeng et al. conducted a comprehensive meta-analysis examining how socioeconomic status influences CKD across the disease spectrum — from prevalence to incidence to progression to ESRD. The analysis of 43 articles provided robust evidence that lower SES is consistently associated with higher CKD burden.

The finding that income was the strongest SES predictor of CKD has multiple mechanistic implications. Lower income is associated with: (1) poorer diet quality — more processed foods, less fresh produce, higher exposure to dietary heavy metals in contaminated inexpensive food sources; (2) greater environmental exposure — residence in areas with higher air pollution, proximity to industrial sites, older housing with lead paint/pipes, and poorer water quality; (3) higher rates of risk factors — diabetes, hypertension, obesity, and smoking are all more prevalent in lower income populations; (4) reduced access to preventive care — later detection of CKD, less aggressive management of risk factors, and delayed nephrology referral.

The education-CKD association likely operates partly through health literacy (ability to understand and act on health recommendations) and partly through correlation with occupational exposures (lower education correlating with manual labor, including jobs with heavy metal and chemical exposure).

The global consistency of these findings underscores that CKD is not merely a medical disease but a disease with deep socioeconomic roots — the same populations most exposed to environmental toxins, heavy metals, and poor-quality diets are the most likely to develop CKD and the least likely to receive adequate treatment.

## Relevance to WikiBiome

This meta-analysis provides critical **epidemiological context** for WikiBiome's environmental exposure narrative. The consistent association between low SES and CKD likely reflects, in part, differential exposure to heavy metals and environmental toxins — populations with lower income tend to live in areas with higher environmental contamination (industrial sites, agricultural pesticides, contaminated water sources) and consume diets with higher metal content (cheap rice with cadmium, canned foods with lead, imported produce with pesticide residues).

The SES-CKD relationship also connects to the gut microbiome: diet quality (heavily influenced by income) is a primary determinant of gut microbial diversity and composition. Lower SES diets — low in fiber, high in processed foods — are associated with the very pattern of dysbiosis (reduced SCFA producers, expanded proteolytic taxa) seen in CKD.

## Open Questions

- What proportion of the SES-CKD association is mediated by differential heavy metal exposure (dietary, occupational, environmental)?
- Does the gut microbiome composition of lower SES populations differ in ways that predispose to CKD (e.g., less fiber-fermenting diversity)?
- Could targeted environmental interventions (water filtration, food safety regulation) in low SES communities reduce CKD incidence more effectively than individual-level medical interventions?
