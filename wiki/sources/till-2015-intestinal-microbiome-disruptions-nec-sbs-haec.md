# Till 2015 — Intestinal Microbiome Disruptions in NEC, SBS, and HAEC

## Key Findings

- **Pre-NEC dysbiosis is detectable before clinical signs** — reduced diversity, Proteobacteria overabundance, and Firmicutes decrease are consistent markers across multiple studies included in this review ([[till-2015-intestinal-microbiome-disruptions-nec-sbs-haec]], narrative review)
- **Dysbiosis type predicts NEC timing**: Firmicutes-type dysbiosis (Clostridia overgrowth) → earlier NEC onset (days 7–21 of life); Proteobacteria-type dysbiosis (Enterobacteriaceae bloom) → later onset (days 19–39) ([[till-2015-intestinal-microbiome-disruptions-nec-sbs-haec]])
- **Clostridia abundance decreases with increasing NEC severity** — raising the question of whether Clostridia are host-protective organisms whose depletion marks a tipping point ([[till-2015-intestinal-microbiome-disruptions-nec-sbs-haec]])
- **No single causative NEC agent identified** across NGS studies — NEC is an ecological collapse rather than a single-pathogen infection ([[till-2015-intestinal-microbiome-disruptions-nec-sbs-haec]])
- **Klebsiella** is responsible for most NICU outbreak-associated NEC cases; it is the most consistently identified pathobiont in severe/surgical NEC ([[till-2015-intestinal-microbiome-disruptions-nec-sbs-haec]])
- **Short bowel syndrome (SBS)**: Lactobacillus overabundance (D-lactic acidosis risk in SBS patients), Proteobacteria enrichment on parenteral nutrition, reduced diversity → liver steatosis and intestinal inflammation ([[till-2015-intestinal-microbiome-disruptions-nec-sbs-haec]])
- **HAEC (Hirschsprung's-associated enterocolitis)**: Candida sp. enrichment (with reduced Malassezia and Saccharomyces), altered Firmicutes/Bacteroidetes ratio; Klebsiella responsible for most HAEC outbreak cases ([[till-2015-intestinal-microbiome-disruptions-nec-sbs-haec]])

## Methods

**Design:** Narrative review. Only studies using next-generation sequencing (NGS) techniques for microbiome characterization were included — standard PCR culture and traditional microbiology studies excluded.

**Scope:** Covers three pediatric surgical gastrointestinal emergencies — necrotizing enterocolitis (NEC), short bowel syndrome (SBS), and Hirschsprung's-associated enterocolitis (HAEC).

**Key limitation:** Narrative review — no systematic search protocol, no meta-analytic pooling, selection bias possible. NGS-only inclusion excludes culture-based evidence. Evidence summaries are expert interpretation of primary literature, not pooled effect sizes. Evidence level appropriately classified as expert-opinion.

## Detailed Summary

This review synthesizes NGS microbiome data across three related pediatric surgical GI emergencies, providing comparative ecological context that single-condition studies cannot offer.

### NEC Microbiome Ecology

The review's most important ecological contribution is the **dysbiosis-type / timing linkage**. Two distinct pre-NEC microbiome patterns have been described in the literature:

1. **Firmicutes-type dysbiosis** (Clostridia overabundance): Associated with earlier NEC onset (days 7–21). This pattern may reflect formula feeding and NICU antibiotic selection pressure in the first week of life, driving rapid Clostridia overgrowth before Proteobacteria establish.

2. **Proteobacteria-type dysbiosis** (Enterobacteriaceae bloom): Associated with later onset (days 19–39). This pattern aligns with the classically described Klebsiella/E. coli bloom and is the more common presentation in most cohort studies.

**The inverse relationship between Clostridia abundance and NEC severity** is mechanistically provocative: non-toxigenic Clostridia may perform protective functions (HMO fermentation, competitive exclusion of Proteobacteria), such that their disappearance is not merely a marker but a cause of escalating pathogenesis.

### SBS Microbiome: Parenteral Nutrition as an Ecological Force

In short bowel syndrome — which occurs in 25–35% of NEC survivors following intestinal resection — parenteral nutrition drives a characteristic dysbiosis: Proteobacteria enrichment with reduced diversity. This creates a cycle where NEC causes SBS, and SBS perpetuates the Proteobacteria ecology that drove NEC. Lactobacillus overabundance in SBS creates an independent risk — D-lactic acidosis from excessive fermentation in the preserved gut segment.

### HAEC: The Fungal Dimension

Hirschsprung's-associated enterocolitis introduces a fungal dimension absent from NEC microbiome studies: Candida sp. enrichment. This is an interkingdom signal — the aganglionic segment in Hirschsprung's disease may create a microenvironment favorable to Candida growth (stasis, fermentable substrates, impaired mucosal immunity). Klebsiella still dominates as the bacterial outbreak pathogen, but the fungal-bacterial interaction in HAEC has not been systematically studied.

### Methodological Note

The review's NGS-only inclusion criterion reflects an important 2015 concern: culture-based NEC studies often identified different organisms than NGS studies, suggesting that the dominant NEC-associated organisms may be non-culturable or that culture selection biases missed the ecologically important taxa.

## Relevance to Cureva

- **STOP**: Treating NEC-associated Clostridia enrichment as pathogenic may be counterproductive — Clostridia depletion correlates with worse NEC outcomes; targeted antibiotics that deplete Clostridia alongside pathobionts may accelerate severity
- **SBS management**: Parenteral nutrition duration should be minimized where clinically possible — PN promotes Proteobacteria dysbiosis and liver steatosis, perpetuating post-NEC intestinal inflammation
- **HAEC antifungal consideration**: Candida enrichment in HAEC suggests antifungal component to HAEC management may be warranted alongside antibacterial treatment

## Contradictions / Tensions

- Firmicutes-type NEC (Clostridia bloom → early onset) contradicts the Clostridium butyricum enrichment finding in Lin 2023 (which found C. butyricum at NEC onset without distinguishing timing). The Firmicutes/Clostridia pattern may be a distinct early-onset NEC subtype; strain-level distinction remains critical.
- Fecal samples may not represent mucosal microbiome (noted explicitly in this review) — all NEC microbiome findings from stool samples carry this limitation.

## Open Questions

- What distinguishes infants who develop Firmicutes-type early NEC vs Proteobacteria-type late NEC — feeding type, gestational age, antibiotic exposure?
- Is Clostridia depletion in severe NEC cause or consequence of disease escalation?
- Does HAEC Candida enrichment respond to antifungal therapy and does this improve HAEC outcomes?
- What is the temporal relationship between Lactobacillus overabundance and D-lactic acidosis in SBS, and can microbiome monitoring identify SBS patients at risk?
