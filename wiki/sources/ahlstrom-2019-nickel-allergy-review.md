# Nickel Allergy and Allergic Contact Dermatitis: A Clinical Review

Ahlström et al. (2019). *Contact Dermatitis* 81:227-241.

## Overview

A comprehensive clinical review establishing that [[nickel]] is the most frequent cause of contact allergy worldwide, covering epidemiology, exposure sources, skin penetration, immunological mechanisms, diagnosis, regulation, and treatment of [[nickel-allergy]].

## Key Findings

### Epidemiology

- Nickel is the single most common contact allergen globally
- Prevalence in Europe: 8-19% of adults, 8-10% of children
- Strong female predominance: women affected 4-10 times more frequently than men, attributed to greater jewelry exposure (ear piercing in particular)
- Prevalence has been influenced by regulatory interventions (see below)

### Exposure Sources

Nickel exposure occurs through a wide range of consumer products and medical devices:

- **Jewelry:** Earrings, necklaces, rings, watches - historically the most important sensitization source
- **Clothing fasteners:** Buttons, zippers, belt buckles, bra clasps
- **Coins:** Euro coins release measurable nickel
- **Tools and occupational items:** Hand tools, keys, metalworking
- **Dental materials:** Orthodontic wires, crowns, bridges
- **Surgical implants:** Joint prostheses, fracture fixation devices, cardiac stents
- **Electronic devices:** Mobile phones, laptops, tablets (metal casings)

### Exposure Quantification

- **Dimethylglyoxime (DMG) test:** Qualitative screening test for nickel release from objects. A positive result (pink color) indicates nickel release exceeding approximately 0.5 microg/cm2/week
- **Nickel release in artificial sweat:** Quantitative laboratory measurement used for regulatory compliance testing
- **Skin sampling:** Acid-wipe technique to measure nickel deposited on skin from contact with objects

### Skin Penetration

- The stratum corneum is the primary barrier to nickel penetration
- Lag time for penetration through intact skin is approximately 50 hours
- Nickel accumulates preferentially in the upper layers of the stratum corneum
- Damaged or compromised skin barrier significantly increases penetration
- The trivalent nickel ion (Ni3+) may be the immunologically active form, as oxidation of Ni2+ to Ni3+ could occur at the skin surface

### Immunological Mechanism

Nickel allergy is a type IV (delayed-type) hypersensitivity reaction involving both innate and adaptive immunity:

- **Innate immunity:** Nickel directly activates Toll-like receptor 4 (TLR-4) on dendritic cells and keratinocytes, triggering pro-inflammatory cytokine release. This TLR-4 activation is specific to humans (the relevant histidine residues are absent in mice)
- **Antigen presentation:** Dendritic cells (Langerhans cells in epidermis) process and present nickel-protein complexes to T cells in draining lymph nodes
- **T cell response:** Both CD4+ helper T cells and CD8+ cytotoxic T cells are involved. The response is predominantly Th1 and Th17, with production of IFN-gamma, IL-17, and IL-22
- **Elicitation phase:** Upon re-exposure, memory T cells migrate to the skin contact site and drive the inflammatory response that manifests as allergic contact dermatitis

### Diagnosis

- **Patch testing** is the gold standard: 5% nickel sulfate hexahydrate in petrolatum applied under occlusion for 48 hours, read at 48 and 72-96 hours
- Important distinction between **sensitization threshold** (dose needed to induce allergy in a naive individual) and **elicitation threshold** (dose needed to trigger a reaction in an already sensitized individual) - the elicitation threshold is much lower
- False-positive and false-negative results are possible; clinical relevance must be assessed

### EU Regulation History

- **EU Nickel Directive (1994, enforced ~2001):** Limited nickel release from items intended for direct and prolonged skin contact to 0.5 microg/cm2/week, and from post-assembly items inserted into pierced ears to 0.2 microg/cm2/week
- The Directive led to demonstrably decreased sensitization rates in countries with strong enforcement (notably Denmark)
- **REACH Regulation:** The Nickel Directive was subsequently incorporated into the EU REACH framework (Registration, Evaluation, Authorisation and Restriction of Chemicals), extending and strengthening the regulatory approach

### Treatment

- **Primary treatment: Avoidance** of nickel-releasing items, guided by DMG testing of personal items
- **Topical corticosteroids:** First-line pharmacologic treatment for acute flares
- **Topical calcineurin inhibitors** (tacrolimus, pimecrolimus): Second-line, particularly for sensitive skin areas (face, intertriginous zones)
- **Low-nickel diet:** Recommended for patients with systemic contact dermatitis from [[dietary-nickel-exposure]], where ingested nickel triggers or exacerbates skin symptoms
- **Disulfiram:** A nickel chelator that has been used as adjunct therapy; binds nickel and reduces systemic exposure
