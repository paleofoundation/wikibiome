# Effects of neuroactive metabolites of the tryptophan pathway on working memory and cortical thickness in schizophrenia

## Summary

This study measured serum levels of KYNA, 5-HI (5-hydroxyindole), and QUIN (quinolinic acid) in 195 schizophrenia patients and 70 healthy controls, along with cognitive performance (MATRICS) and cortical thickness (MRI). It provides insight into the dynamic roles of [[tryptophan-metabolism]] pathway metabolites on cognition in schizophrenia.

## Key Findings

- Schizophrenia patients had significantly lower serum KYNA (p < 0.001) and QUIN (p = 0.02)
- Increased 5-HI/KYNA ratio (p < 0.001) and QUIN/KYNA ratio (p < 0.001) in schizophrenia
- Working memory positively correlated with serum 5-HI levels (t = 2.10, p = 0.04) but inversely with KYNA (t = -2.01, p = 0.05)
- Patients with high 5-HI and low KYNA had better working memory
- Higher 5-HI levels associated with thicker left lateral orbitofrontal cortex (t = 3.71, p = 2.94 x 10^-4)
- The different effects of 5-HI and KYNA on working memory may reflect opposite receptor-level mechanisms (alpha7nAChR agonism vs antagonism)
- KYNA antagonizes NMDA receptor, contributing to cognitive deficits

## Relevance to Gut-Metal-Microbiome Axis

This study directly links altered [[tryptophan-metabolism]] to cognitive dysfunction and brain structure in schizophrenia. The tryptophan-kynurenine pathway is substantially modulated by gut bacteria, making this a key [[gut-brain-axis]] mechanism. [[dysbiosis]] can shift tryptophan catabolism toward kynurenine and KYNA production, contributing to NMDA receptor hypofunction. Heavy metals, particularly [[iron]] and [[zinc]], are cofactors in tryptophan-degrading enzymes (IDO, TDO), and [[mis-metallation]] could alter pathway flux. The findings support targeting [[tryptophan-metabolism]] through microbiome modulation, potentially via [[probiotics]] that restore healthy tryptophan processing.

## Limitations

- Cross-sectional design
- Serum metabolites may not reflect brain concentrations
- Most patients on antipsychotic medication
- Chinese population
