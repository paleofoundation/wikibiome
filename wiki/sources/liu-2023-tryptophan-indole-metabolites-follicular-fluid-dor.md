# Tryptophan and Indole Metabolites in Follicular Fluid with Diminished Ovarian Reserve

## Key Findings

- **Tryptophan (TRP), indole-3-propionic acid (IPA), and indole-3-acetic acid (IAA) levels** were significantly lower in follicular fluid of women with diminished ovarian reserve (DOR) compared to normal ovarian reserve (NOR) controls (P<0.01) ([[liu-2023-tryptophan-indole-metabolites-follicular-fluid-dor]], cross-sectional)
- **TRP in follicular fluid positively correlated with available embryo rate** in NOR females, suggesting tryptophan availability directly influences oocyte quality
- **IPA, a gut bacteria-derived indole metabolite**, was significantly reduced in DOR follicular fluid, providing a direct link between intestinal microbiome activity and ovarian function
- **Indole metabolites possess antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties** that may protect the ovarian follicular environment from oxidative damage
- **Serum and follicular fluid metabolite levels showed correlation**, suggesting systemic microbial metabolite availability affects the ovarian microenvironment <!-- NEEDS VERIFICATION: strength of serum-FF correlation -->

## Methods

Cross-sectional study comparing tryptophan and its indole metabolite levels in follicular fluid of women with diminished ovarian reserve (DOR) versus normal ovarian reserve (NOR). Metabolite quantification performed by liquid chromatography-mass spectrometry (LC-MS). Correlations analyzed between metabolite levels and IVF/ICSI outcomes (embryo availability, oocyte maturation). <!-- NEEDS VERIFICATION: exact sample sizes per group -->

## Detailed Summary

This study investigated whether tryptophan and its microbially-derived indole metabolites in follicular fluid differ between women with DOR and NOR, and whether these metabolites are associated with reproductive outcomes. Tryptophan is an essential amino acid metabolized by gut bacteria through the indole pathway, producing metabolites including IPA (via Clostridium sporogenes and other commensals) and IAA (via multiple bacterial species).

The significantly lower levels of IPA and IAA in DOR follicular fluid suggest that reduced gut microbial indole production — or impaired transport of these metabolites to the ovarian compartment — may contribute to ovarian reserve depletion. IPA is one of the most potent endogenous antioxidants known, capable of scavenging hydroxyl radicals. Its depletion in the follicular microenvironment may leave oocytes vulnerable to oxidative stress, contributing to poor oocyte quality and reduced embryo viability.

The positive correlation between TRP levels and embryo availability in NOR women further supports the hypothesis that adequate tryptophan and its metabolites are necessary for optimal follicular development.
