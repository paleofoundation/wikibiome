# Overview

Comprehensive review of two specific microbiota-derived host metabolites -- **p-cresol sulfate (pCS)** and **4-ethylphenyl sulfate (4EPS)** -- associated with ASD in both patients and animal models. These metabolites originate from bacterial fermentation of aromatic amino acids by *Clostridioides* species.

# Key Findings

## p-Cresol and 4-Ethylphenol Metabolism

- Both produced through **aromatic amino acid fermentation** by commensal bacteria (primarily *Clostridioides*)
- p-Cresol and 4-ethylphenol produced from tyrosine/phenylalanine metabolism
- Once produced, metabolized by host into **p-cresol sulfate** and **4-ethylphenyl sulfate**
- Can enter bloodstream, pass blood-brain barrier, and affect microglial cells in CNS

## Evidence in ASD

- Multiple studies report **increased *Clostridioides*** in ASD gut microbiota
- *Desulfovibrio*: involved in sulfate-reducing processes, shows negative correlation with PCS
- Fecal microbiota transplant in open-label trial reduced levels of p-cresol, 4-hydroxy phenylacetate, and indole, modifying *Prevotella*, *Bifidobacterium*, and *Desulfovibrio* abundances
- Maternal immune activation (MIA) models: 4EPS levels **4.6 times higher** in offspring than controls

## Toxicity and CNS Effects

- pCS as uremic toxin: reaches high concentrations in uremic patients, causing cardiovascular and neurological damage
- At low concentrations (10, 25 ug/mL): pCS increases phagocytosis and ROS production in macrophages
- At high concentrations (250, 1000 uM): pCS suppresses Th1-type immune responses by increasing IL-10
- Recent finding: high pCS and indoxyl sulfate in cerebrospinal fluid of Parkinson's patients

## Animal Model Evidence

- BTBR mouse ASD model shows altered intestinal microbiota composition including increased *Clostridioides*
- *Bacteroides fragilis* treatment reversed elevated 4EPS and improved ASD-like behaviors
- Maternal high-fat diet increases both ASD risk and *Clostridioides* abundance in offspring
