# Microbiome-Gut-Brain Axis and Dietary Interventions in Parkinson's Disease

## Summary

This review examines dietary interventions that target the microbiome-[[gut-brain-axis]] in [[parkinsons-disease]], covering how specific dietary patterns and supplements can reshape the PD gut microbiome to improve both GI and neurological outcomes.

## Key Findings

- High-fiber diets increase SCFA production and promote beneficial bacteria depleted in PD
- Mediterranean diet components (olive oil, fish, vegetables) have prebiotic and anti-inflammatory effects
- Protein redistribution diets improve levodopa absorption by reducing amino acid competition
- [[probiotics]] supplementation improves constipation and may reduce inflammation in PD
- Coffee consumption correlates with reduced PD risk, possibly through microbiome modulation
- Dietary polyphenols promote growth of *Akkermansia* and *Faecalibacterium*
- Nutritional interventions should be integrated with standard PD pharmacotherapy

## Relevance to Gut-Metal-Microbiome Axis

Dietary interventions are the most accessible lever for modifying the gut-metal-microbiome axis. This review highlights how different dietary strategies address various components of the axis: fiber for [[butyrate]]/[[iron]] regulation; polyphenols for metal chelation in the gut; protein redistribution for optimizing levodopa absorption through DMT1/amino acid transporter competition; and anti-inflammatory foods for reducing [[hepcidin]]-driven [[iron]] trapping. The levodopa-protein interaction is directly relevant to metal transport, as the large neutral amino acid transporters that compete with levodopa are regulated by [[iron]] status. Dietary management that addresses both metal exposure and microbiome composition represents a comprehensive approach to the gut-metal-microbiome axis in [[parkinsons-disease]].

## Limitations

- Review article without primary data
- Dietary interventions difficult to blind and control in clinical trials
- Individual variability in dietary response
- PD-specific dietary guidelines still in development
