# Is Eating Behavior Manipulated by the Gastrointestinal Microbiota?

## Key Findings

- **Evolutionary conflict**: Microbes in the GI tract are under selective pressure to increase their own fitness, sometimes at the expense of host fitness. They may generate **cravings** for foods they specialize on, **induce dysphoria** (via toxin release) until preferred foods are eaten, or **hijack** the vagus nerve and reward pathways to alter host eating behavior [[alcock-2014-microbiota-manipulate-eating-behavior]], expert-opinion.

- **Three manipulation mechanisms**:
  1. **Toxin production**: Microbes release virulence proteins that activate pain signaling when nutrient-deprived; bacterial virulence proteins activate pain receptors; fasting increases nociception via vagal mechanism.
  2. **Receptor alteration**: Germ-free mice have altered taste receptors (more sweet receptors); *L. acidophilus* increases cannabinoid and opioid receptor expression in intestines; gastric bypass alters taste receptor expression and food preferences.
  3. **Neural hijacking**: Microbes produce neurotransmitters (GABA, serotonin, dopamine, norepinephrine); enteric nerve receptors respond to specific bacterial metabolites; vagus nerve is the main gut-brain communication channel [[alcock-2014-microbiota-manipulate-eating-behavior]].

- **Microbial diet selectivity**: A less diverse microbiome is predicted to more strongly manipulate eating behavior because fewer species means less internal microbial competition. Diverse microbiomes have conflicting "preferences" that cancel out. This predicts that **dysbiosis → stronger food cravings** [[alcock-2014-microbiota-manipulate-eating-behavior]].

- **Tractable intervention**: Because the microbiota can be changed within 24 hours by diet (and by prebiotics, probiotics, FMT), changing the microbial community could change cravings and eating behavior — a route to treating obesity without willpower-dependent approaches [[alcock-2014-microbiota-manipulate-eating-behavior]].
