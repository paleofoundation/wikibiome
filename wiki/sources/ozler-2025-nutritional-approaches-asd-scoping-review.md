# Key Claims

- **Multiple dietary models** evaluated for ASD with limited and variable evidence for each
- **No consensus** on an ideal nutritional model for ASD
- **30-84% of ASD children** have food difficulties and food selectivity
- **Zinc, calcium, iron, selenium, iodine, methylcobalamin, vitamin A, thiamine, pantothenic acid, and niacin** are the most common nutrient deficiencies in ASD
- **GI complaints** observed in 9-91% of ASD individuals

# Methods & Findings

**Scoping review** of dietary interventions and nutritional supplements in ASD.

**Dietary interventions reviewed:**

1. **GFCF diet**: Most studied; targets opioid peptide theory. Some meta-analyses show CARS/ATEC improvement; others show no benefit. Casein-free more beneficial than gluten-free.

2. **Ketogenic diet**: Modified KD with MCT improved ADOS-2 and CARS-2; N-acetyllaspartate (NAA) and GABA associated with ASD severity. Long-term maintenance difficult.

3. **Specific carbohydrate diet (SCD)**: Eliminates grains, lactose, sucrose; limited case evidence.

4. **Low FODMAP diet**: May reduce GI and neurological symptoms; no ASD-specific RCTs.

5. **Mediterranean diet**: Omega-3, vitamins B protective; maternal adherence during pregnancy associated with lower NDD risk. Limited direct ASD studies.

6. **Low glutamate diet**: Targets excitotoxicity; limited evidence.

7. **Low oxalate diet**: Reduces serum and urine oxalate in ASD; limited studies.

8. **Feingold/Candida body ecology/GAPS diets**: Minimal evidence.

**Nutritional supplements:**
- Vitamin D: deficiency common; supplementation may reduce ASD risk
- Omega-3 (DHA/EPA): may improve synaptic transmission; limited RCT evidence
- Probiotics: modulate gut-brain axis; strain-dependent effects
- Camel milk: reduces oxidative stress via SOD and GSH-Px
- Phytochemicals: potential neuroprotective effects

# Relevance to Metallomic Framework

- **Micronutrient deficiency profile**: The specific deficiency pattern (zinc, calcium, iron, selenium, iodine) in ASD directly maps to disrupted metal ecology
- **Mediterranean diet-metal axis**: Polyphenol-rich Mediterranean diet provides metal-chelating compounds that modulate iron and copper redox cycling
- **KD-metal considerations**: Long-term KD may deplete selenium (due to reduced grain intake) and exacerbate existing trace element deficiencies
- **Food selectivity as metal vulnerability**: Restricted dietary intake in ASD children limits metal cofactor supply, compounding microbiome-driven metal dyshomeostasis
- **Camel milk SOD**: Direct supply of zinc/copper-dependent superoxide dismutase may bypass disrupted endogenous metalloenzyme production
