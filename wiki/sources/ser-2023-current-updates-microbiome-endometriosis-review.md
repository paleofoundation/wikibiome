## Key Findings

- **Endometriosis patients display distinct microbiome signatures across multiple body sites** — the vaginal, cervical, endometrial, gut, and peritoneal microbiomes all show compositional shifts compared to healthy controls, with consistently reduced [[lactobacillus]] dominance in reproductive tract sites and increased diversity of pathobionts including [[enterobacteriaceae]], [[streptococcus]], [[prevotella]], [[gardnerella]], and [[pseudomonas]] (expert-opinion, narrative review synthesizing ~20 clinical studies).

- **The gut microbiome of endometriosis patients shows an altered Firmicutes/Bacteroidetes ratio**, with most studies (both clinical and animal models) reporting increased Firmicutes and decreased Bacteroidetes, though two studies reported the opposite pattern — likely attributable to sampling methodology differences (fecal pellet vs. cecum segment) (expert-opinion).

- **The estrobolome — the aggregate of bacterial genes capable of metabolizing estrogens — is implicated in endometriosis pathogenesis** through dysbiosis-triggered alterations in beta-glucuronidase activity, which can increase circulating estrogen levels and drive estrogen-dependent pathologies including [[endometriosis]] and endometrial cancer (expert-opinion).

- **Microbial biomarkers may differ by disease stage**: Stage I-II endometriosis is associated with *[[lactobacillus-iners|Lactobacillus jensenii]]*, *Corynbacteriales*, *Porphyromonadaceae*, and *[[ruminococcaceae|Ruminococcaceae]]*, while Stage III-IV is associated with *[[bifidobacterium|Bifidobacterium breve]]*, *[[streptococcus|Streptococcaceae]]* members, and increased *[[prevotella|Prevotella bivia]]* (expert-opinion).

- ***[[lactobacillus-gasseri|Lactobacillus gasseri]]* OLL2809 is the most clinically evaluated probiotic for endometriosis**, with a placebo-controlled RCT demonstrating significant pain reduction (VAS: -3.28 vs. -2.00; VRS: -1.44 vs. -1.03) over three months, and animal model evidence showing inhibition of ectopic endometrial cell development via NK cell activation and IL-12 induction (RCT + animal-model).

## Methods

This is a **narrative review** (not systematic) summarizing recent scientific and clinical findings on the relationship between endometriosis and microbiomes across multiple body sites: the female reproductive tract (vaginal, cervical, endometrial), gastrointestinal tract, and peritoneal cavity. The review also covers animal model studies (mouse and non-human primate) and discusses potential probiotic interventions. The authors searched published literature without specifying formal inclusion/exclusion criteria, PRISMA methodology, or systematic search strings. The review synthesizes findings from approximately 155 cited references spanning prevalence data, diagnostic approaches, clinical microbiome studies (Table 3, covering ~18 studies from 2019-2022), animal model studies (Table 4, covering 6 studies from 2018-2022), and probiotic intervention data.

## Detailed Summary

### Endometriosis Prevalence and Diagnosis

The review opens by establishing that endometriosis affects 6-10% of reproductive-age women globally, with prevalence varying by geography and ethnicity. Asian women had 2.96 times the odds of diagnosis compared to Caucasians (95% CI: 1.65-5.31, p=0.0003). Diagnosis remains challenging — up to 40% of laparoscopies for pelvic pain find no pathology, and 84.1% of women had symptoms prior to diagnosis, indicating significant diagnostic delay. The review summarizes treatment guidelines from ESHRE, TIGE, NICE, OGSM, and ASRM across surgical, pharmacological, and assisted reproduction approaches.

### Vaginal and Cervical Microbiome Changes

The review synthesizes ~18 clinical studies (Table 3) examining microbiome changes in endometriosis patients across multiple body sites. Key patterns in the vaginal/cervical microbiome include:

- **Reduced *Lactobacillus* dominance** — multiple studies from Brazil, China, and Japan found lower abundance of *[[lactobacillus]]* spp. in vaginal samples of endometriosis patients compared to controls.
- **Enrichment of diverse pathobionts** — *[[corynebacterium]]*, *[[enterobacteriaceae]]*, *[[flavobacterium]]*, *[[pseudomonas]]*, and *[[streptococcus]]* were increased in cervical mucus of endometriosis patients.
- **Community state type (CST) shifts** — CST IV (characterized by strictly anaerobic bacteria including *[[prevotella]]*, *Dialister*, *[[atopobium]]*, *[[gardnerella]]*, *Megasphaera*, *Peptoniphilus*, *Sneathia*, *Eggerthella*, *Aerococcus*, *Finegoldia*, and *Mobiluncus*) is linked to vaginal inflammation and dysbiosis.
- **Stage-dependent microbial signatures** — Chang et al. (2022) found that cervical microbiome composition differed between Stage I-II and Stage III-IV, with *Prevotella bivia* abundance associated with Stage III-IV and DIE patients showing increased *Catabacter*, *Dialister micraerophilus*, *Fibrobacter intestinalis*, *Treponema berlinense*, *Prevotella intermedia*, and *Helicobacter macacae*.

### Gut Microbiome Changes

Multiple studies reported gut dysbiosis in endometriosis patients:

- **Altered Firmicutes/Bacteroidetes ratio** — most studies found increased Firmicutes and decreased Bacteroidetes, though inconsistencies exist.
- **Depletion of beneficial taxa** — *[[lachnospiraceae]]* (including *Clostridia Clostridiales*, *Lachnospiraceae Ruminococcus*), *[[ruminococcaceae|Ruminococcaceae]]*, *Sneathia*, *Barnesella*, and *[[gardnerella]]* were decreased in fecal samples.
- **Enrichment of potential pathobionts** — *[[shigella]]*, *[[escherichia-coli|Escherichia]]*, *Eggerthella lenta*, and *Eubacterium dolicum* were increased.
- ***[[ruminococcaceae|Ruminococcaceae]]* depletion** was highlighted as particularly significant given these strictly anaerobic bacteria produce anti-inflammatory short-chain fatty acids including butyrate. *Ruminococcus* has been inversely correlated with the inflammatory cytokine IL-6.
- Svensson et al. (2021) found *[[prevotella]]* correlated with GI symptoms including constipation (r=0.307, p=0.014), bloating/flatulence (r=0.297, p=0.016), and nausea/vomiting (r=0.295, p=0.017).

### Peritoneal Microbiome

The peritoneal cavity, once thought to be sterile, harbors its own microbiome that is altered in endometriosis:

- Yuan et al. (2022) found 276 OTUs in peritoneal fluid of endometriosis patients vs. 211 in controls, with 120 OTUs unique to the endometriosis group.
- Significantly higher abundance of *Acidovorax* (p=0.01), *Devosia* (p=0.03), *Methylobacterium* (p=0.03), *Phascolarctobacterium* (p=0.03), and *[[streptococcus]]* (p=0.04) in endometriosis patients.
- Huang et al. (2021) found increased *Pseudomonadaceae*, *Prevotellaceae*, and *Xanthomonadaceae* and depletion of *Actinomycetales*, *Microbacteriaceae*, *[[lactobacillus-iners|Lactobacillus iners]]*, and *Microbacteriaceae Cryocola* in peritoneal fluid of endometriosis patients.

### Animal Model Evidence

Six animal model studies (2018-2022) are summarized, using mice (C57BL6 and SD rats) and non-human primates (olive baboons, *Papio Anubis*):

- **Yuan et al. (2018)** — C57BL6 mice with intraperitoneal endometrial injection showed higher beta diversity, enrichment of Firmicutes and *[[actinobacteria]]*, and elevated *[[bifidobacterium|Bifidobactericeae]]* and *Alcaligenaceae*.
- **Chadchan et al. (2019)** — critically demonstrated that **metronidazole treatment reduced endometriotic lesion mass and volume**, and that fecal transplant from endometriosis donor mice to metronidazole-pretreated recipients promoted disease progression, establishing a causal role for the gut microbiome.
- **Ni et al. (2020, 2021)** — showed decreased alpha diversity, altered Firmicutes/Bacteroidetes ratios, and increased *[[akkermansia]]*, *Parasutterella*, and *Rikenella* in endometriosis mice. The 2021 study demonstrated fecal microbiota transplant effects.
- **Le et al. (2022)** — the only non-human primate study, using olive baboons over 15 months, showing dynamic microbiome changes including diminished vaginal microbiome post-induction, increased *Succinivibrio*, *[[prevotella]]*, and CF231 during disease progression, and positive correlations between GI tract microbial populations and T regulatory/T helper 17 cells.

### Estrogen-Microbiome Axis

The review highlights the estrogen-microbiome axis as a critical crosstalk mechanism. Dysbiosis-induced alterations in the estrobolome can trigger estrogen-mediated pathologies including endometriosis. Beta-glucuronidase-producing bacteria can deconjugate estrogen metabolites in the gut, increasing circulating free estrogen levels. This directly connects to endometriosis pathogenesis as an estrogen-dependent condition.

### Probiotic Interventions

- ***L. gasseri* OLL2809** — inhibits ectopic endometrial cell development in murine models via NK cell activation and IL-12 induction. A placebo-controlled RCT showed significant pain reduction (VAS and VRS scales) over 3 months. A separate rat study showed significant lesion volume reduction.
- **LactoFem multi-strain probiotic** (10^6 colony of *L. acidophilus*, *L. plantarum*, *L. fermentum*, and *L. gasseri*) — a pilot placebo-controlled RCT in Stage III-IV patients showed significant dysmenorrhea score reduction after 8 weeks (from 6.53 to 3.07 in treatment group vs. 5.60 to 4.47 in placebo, p=0.018).
- The review notes the absence of clinical guidelines supporting standard probiotic use in endometriosis management and calls for more studies on administration routes, dosing, and stability.

## Contradictions / Tensions

- **Firmicutes/Bacteroidetes ratio inconsistency**: Most clinical and animal studies report increased Firmicutes and decreased Bacteroidetes in endometriosis, but Chadchan et al. (2019) and Ni et al. (2020) found the opposite — higher Bacteroidetes and lower Firmicutes. The review attributes this to sampling methodology (fecal pellets vs. cecum segments) but does not resolve the contradiction. This tension should be noted on the [[endometriosis-signature]] page and may affect confidence levels for the taxonomic layer.

- **Cervical microbiome findings are inconsistent**: Huang et al. (2021) reported no significant differences in cervical microbiome between endometriosis and control, while Chang et al. (2022) and Akiyama et al. (2019) found clear differences. This may reflect differences in specimen collection, geographic populations, or disease stage distribution.

- **Hantschel et al. (2019)** found no significant changes at genus or family level between endometriosis and sham groups in mice during early lesion formation, suggesting microbiome changes may be secondary to disease establishment rather than causal — a tension with the Chadchan et al. findings suggesting a causal role.

## Open Questions

1. **Is the microbiome causally involved in endometriosis pathogenesis, or is dysbiosis a consequence of the disease?** The Chadchan et al. fecal transplant study suggests causality, but the Hantschel et al. finding of no early-phase dysbiosis complicates this picture. More time-course studies are needed.

2. **Can microbial biomarkers reliably stage endometriosis non-invasively?** The stage-dependent signatures (Stage I-II vs. III-IV) from Chang et al. are promising but based on small sample sizes and single-site studies.

3. **What is the optimal probiotic formulation, dose, and route of administration for endometriosis?** *L. gasseri* OLL2809 has the strongest evidence but only from small trials. Multi-strain formulations lack adequate testing.

4. **How does the estrobolome specifically drive endometriosis progression?** The beta-glucuronidase mechanism is plausible but direct measurement of estrobolome activity in endometriosis patients — linking specific taxa to estrogen deconjugation to lesion growth — has not been demonstrated.

5. **What role does the peritoneal microbiome play in lesion implantation and growth?** The Le et al. baboon study is the only model examining all microbiome sites simultaneously, and peritoneal microbiome research remains in its infancy.

6. **What is the relationship between metal exposures and the microbiome alterations observed in endometriosis?** This review does not discuss metallomics — a significant gap from the WikiBiome perspective. The connection between heavy metal exposure, microbiome disruption, and endometriosis pathogenesis remains unexplored in this paper.
