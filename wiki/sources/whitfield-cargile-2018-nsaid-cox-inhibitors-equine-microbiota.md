# Key Claims

- Both COX-2 selective (firocoxib) and non-selective (phenylbutazone) NSAIDs significantly alter fecal microbiota composition and reduce diversity in horses
- NSAID-induced [[dysbiosis]] has been associated with multiple intestinal diseases in horses and may be relevant to human IBD
- Microbial diversity decreased and inferred metagenome alterations were most pronounced at day 10 of NSAID administration
- Changes were temporary, with recovery beginning after NSAID cessation
- COX-2 selectivity does not appear to confer microbiome protection over non-selective NSAIDs

# Methods/Findings

- **Study type**: Randomized controlled trial in 25 adult horses; 3 groups (control n=5, phenylbutazone n=10, firocoxib n=10)
- **Duration**: 10 days NSAID administration; fecal samples collected every 5 days for 25 days
- **Methods**: 16S rRNA V4 sequencing; PICRUSt metagenome prediction; QIIME analysis
- **Key findings**: Both NSAIDs reduced alpha diversity; beta diversity shifts by PCoA; altered predicted metagenome pathways
- **Recovery**: Bacterial concentrations and diversity began recovering after NSAID cessation

# Connections

- Demonstrates that NSAID-induced [[dysbiosis]] occurs regardless of COX selectivity, relevant to drug choice in [[crohns-disease]] patients
- Microbiome disruption by NSAIDs may compound existing [[dysbiosis]] in IBD patients
- [[inflammation]] from NSAID-altered microbiota may involve metal-dependent pathways in the [[gut-metal-microbiome]]
- Supports caution with NSAID use in IBD patients due to additive microbiome damage
