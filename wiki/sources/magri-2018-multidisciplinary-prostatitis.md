## Key Findings

- **Enterobacteriaceae, especially *Escherichia coli*, are the predominant pathogens** in acute and chronic bacterial prostatitis (CBP), with an increasing role of *Enterococcus* species; many strains exhibit biofilm formation and multidrug resistance ([[magri-2018-multidisciplinary-prostatitis]], expert opinion).
- **Sexual dysfunction is highly prevalent in CP/CPPS**: erectile dysfunction in 29–49.9% of cases, ejaculatory dysfunction in 59.9% (predominantly burning or ejaculatory pain and premature ejaculation), with 46–92% of patients reporting sexual dysfunction impacting quality of life ([[magri-2018-multidisciplinary-prostatitis]], expert opinion).
- **Clinical chronic prostatitis significantly increases prostate cancer risk**: a meta-analysis component found OR = 1.83 (95% CI: 1.43–2.35, P < 0.00001), converted to a risk-ratio estimate of 1.63 (95% CI: 1.23–2.17) ([[magri-2018-multidisciplinary-prostatitis]], expert opinion citing meta-analysis data).
- **Gut microbiome dysbiosis is associated with CP/CPPS**: reduced alpha diversity with lower *Prevotella* abundance; intestinal symptoms (diarrhea, constipation, abdominal pain, bloating) co-occur in 60% of CBP and 60.5% of CPPS patients ([[magri-2018-multidisciplinary-prostatitis]], expert opinion).
- **Biofilm-producing bacteria are commonly found in CBP patients** and have a significant negative impact on clinical response to antibiotic therapy; biofilms may drive prostate calcifications and symptom persistence ([[magri-2018-multidisciplinary-prostatitis]], expert opinion).

## Methods

This is a **multi-author expert review** compiled from the proceedings of the meeting "Prostatitis: A Multidisciplinary Approach (Issues and Controversies)," held in Milano, Italy, 26–27 October 2018. Each section was authored by a domain specialist. The paper synthesizes clinical experience across urology, microbiology, radiology, infectious disease, gastroenterology, and gynecology. It includes discussion of the NIH-NIDDK classification system, the UPOINT(S) phenotyping algorithm, meta-analytic data on prostatitis-prostate cancer risk, antimicrobial resistance patterns from a 6-year Greek cohort (548 isolates from 1,324 visits), and observational data on gut-prostate axis connections. No new primary data were generated; the paper consolidates existing evidence and expert consensus.

## Detailed Summary

### Classification and Diagnosis

The NIH-NIDDK classification divides prostatitis into four categories: acute bacterial prostatitis (Category I), chronic bacterial prostatitis (Category II), chronic non-bacterial prostatitis/CPPS (Category III, subdivided into inflammatory IIIA and non-inflammatory IIIB), and asymptomatic inflammatory prostatitis (Category IV). The **UPOINT(S) system** further phenotypes patients across domains: Urinary, Psychosocial, Organ-specific, Infection, Neurological/systemic, Tenderness, and Sexuality. The sexuality domain (S) was added because sexual dysfunction affects 46–92% of CP/CPPS patients.

### Physiopathological Aspects

Chronic prostatic inflammation involves reactive astrocytes, activated microglia, adaptive immune system involvement (P < 0.00001 for inflammatory markers), overexpression of chemokines and cytokines, and increased ROS/RNS. The neuroinflammatory component affects surrounding neural tissue at molecular, structural, and functional levels. Clinical observations link chronic inflammation with CP/CPPS, BPH, and increased odds of prostate cancer.

### Prostatitis and Prostate Cancer Risk

A meta-analysis of studies published to July 2012 (updated with data on over 7,000 patients through 2017) found that **clinical prostatitis is moderately associated with prostate cancer**: crude OR = 1.83 (95% CI: 1.43–2.35, P < 0.00001). Pooled data showed considerable heterogeneity (I2 = 91%). Sensitivity analysis excluding extreme outliers yielded OR = 1.55 (95% CI: 1.30–1.85). The trim-and-fill adjusted estimate was 2.12 (95% CI: 1.38–3.22). A notably strong association was observed in Asian patients (OR = 3.54, 95% CI: 2.60–4.82).

### Sexual Dysfunction and Chronic Prostatitis

Prevalence of sexual dysfunction in CP/CPPS: erectile dysfunction 2–40%, decreased interest/desire 15–25%, ejaculatory dysfunction 8–32%, orgasm issues 12–19%, dyspareunia 17%. Among men with CP/CPPS, erectile dysfunction prevalence was 29% overall, increasing with age. Aetiology of ED in CP/CPPS includes vasculogenic, arterial insufficiency, veno-occlusive disease, endocrine, neurogenic, and psychogenic factors. Chronic prostatitis causes **nitric oxide-mediated arterial stiffness** and vascular endothelial dysfunction; 50% of patients show pelvic floor spasm decreasing arterial penile inflow. Hypogonadism is a common comorbid finding.

### Causative Pathogens

**Enterobacteriaceae, especially *E. coli***, are the predominant organisms. *Enterococcus* has an increasing role. Many strains form **biofilm** and exhibit **multidrug resistance** (extended-spectrum beta-lactamases, AmpC beta-lactamases, vanA gene for Enterococci, mecA/mecC for *S. aureus*). Sexually transmitted infection agents include *Chlamydia trachomatis*, *Mycoplasma genitalium*, *Ureaplasma urealyticum*, and *Mycoplasma hominis*. In the authors' experience: *C. trachomatis* isolated in 26.8–33.6%, *U. urealyticum* in 56.3–59.7%, *M. hominis* in 7.3%, *Trichomonas vaginalis* in 1.4–1.8%.

### Biofilm Role

Biofilm-producing bacteria were commonly found in CBP patients and had a **significant negative impact on clinical response to antibiotic therapy**. Symptom relief was inversely related to biofilm production rather than apparent microbiological clearance. Bacterial biofilms may drive **prostate calcifications** — a possible sonographic sign of chronic biofilm harboring difficult-to-treat organisms. The presence of bacterial biofilm represents a chronic inflammatory stimulus that sustains symptoms.

### Antimicrobial Resistance — Greek Cohort

A 6-year retrospective study (2009–2015) of 390 bacterial isolates from 1,324 CBP visits found: most frequent pathogen was *E. coli*, followed by coagulase-negative *Staphylococcus* and *Enterococcus* spp. Gram-negative bacteria predominated. Increasing quinolone resistance was observed. Clinical relapses were most commonly associated with *Enterococcus faecalis*, *Staphylococcus* CoN, and *E. coli*. Mean relapse interval: 13.9 months. Higher *Enterococcus faecalis* resistance was noted in previously treated patients (69% vs 25%). Polymicrobial isolates were significantly more common in previously treated patients (31% vs 19%).

### Antibiotic Treatment

Fluoroquinolones are the first-line agents for CBP (4–6 weeks). An RCT showed minocycline had better microbiological cure rates than cephalexin (45 vs 21% microbiological, 65 vs 46% clinical). Combination treatment with ciprofloxacin + azithromycin showed 64.2% microbiological eradication after one 6-week cycle, reaching 83.9% after a second cycle. Fosfomycin emerged as a promising alternative for quinolone-resistant *E. coli* CBP (clinical cure 50–77%, microbiological eradication >50%). Aminoglycosides showed promise for resistant cases. Local injection of antimicrobials (e.g., amikacin submucosally) showed encouraging but unvalidated results.

### Phytotherapy and Multimodal Treatment

Quercetin (500 mg twice daily) improved urinary symptoms and quality of life vs placebo in CP/CPPS. Cernilton (pollen extract) showed 25% decrease in NIH-CPSI and better pain control vs placebo. Pollen extract (Deprox 500) showed superiority over ibuprofen for early pain relief with better quality-of-life outcomes. PEA (palmitoylethanolamide) + polydatin showed anti-inflammatory and apoptotic effects in a BPH model. Curcumin + Calendula extract improved sexual function (IIEF-5) and PEDT in CP/CPPS type III. **Multimodal phenotype-directed therapy** (UPOINTS) achieved clinically appreciable NIH-CPSI reduction in 77.5% of patients over 6 months.

### Gut Microbiome and Prostatitis (Gut-Prostate Axis)

The association of CP/CPPS with intestinal symptoms is documented in 63.2% of patients with prostatic inflammation. Gut microbiome analysis reveals **reduced alpha diversity** in CP/CPPS patients with **lower *Prevotella* abundance**. The intestinal microbiota can generate, amplify, and maintain a systemic or local inflammatory condition. Dysbiosis with persistence of pathogenic noxae can activate mast cells causing chronic inflammation. **Bacteria belonging to phylum Proteobacteria** produce LPS that increases intestinal permeability via TLR-4 upregulation. Zonulin dysregulation in 22–31% of CBP patients indicates compromised intestinal barrier. Hydrocolontherapy was proposed as a therapeutic option to restore intestinal eubiosis.

### Gynecological Investigation of Partners

Partners of 399 CP/CPPS patients showed the same pathogens isolated in male patients: *Enterobacteriaceae*, *Ureaplasma urealyticum*, *Gardnerella vaginalis*, and *Chlamydia*. The vaginal microbiome is a dynamic ecosystem open to contamination from external and intestinal sources. Partner investigation is recommended for comprehensive management.

## Contradictions / Tensions

- The paper's meta-analysis section on prostatitis-prostate cancer risk shows high heterogeneity (I2 = 91%), weakening confidence in the pooled estimate. The authors acknowledge this but still present the association as meaningful.
- The role of semen culture in CBP diagnosis is debated within the paper itself — EAU guidelines exclude it, but the authors' data suggest 78.6% of CBP patients show positive semen cultures vs none in CP/CPPS, supporting its diagnostic utility.
- The paper advocates hydrocolontherapy for restoring gut eubiosis in prostatitis patients, but presents no controlled trial evidence for this intervention — only mechanistic rationale and case experience.
- Tension between STI pathogens (*Chlamydia*, *Mycoplasma*) as causative agents vs commensal colonization — the paper acknowledges this is "a much debated issue."

## Open Questions

1. What is the specific taxonomic composition of the prostate microbiome in CBP vs healthy controls, beyond the culture-based data presented here? Metagenomic sequencing would clarify.
2. Does the gut dysbiosis (reduced Prevotella, increased Proteobacteria) observed in CP/CPPS patients precede or follow the onset of prostatic inflammation?
3. Are biofilm-producing strains in the prostate metal-dependent in ways that could be targeted via nutritional immunity strategies (e.g., iron or zinc restriction)?
4. Does the zonulin-mediated intestinal permeability in CBP patients share mechanisms with the gut barrier dysfunction seen in other inflammatory conditions (IBD, IBS) that have established microbiome signatures?
5. What is the role of fungal co-colonization in prostate biofilms — the paper mentions *Candida* only in passing for immunocompromised patients, but interkingdom biofilms are well-documented elsewhere.
