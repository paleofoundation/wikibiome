# Association of Combined Effect of Metals Exposure and Behavioral Factors on Depressive Symptoms in Women

## Key Claims

- Combined exposure to heavy metals (Pb, Cd, Hg) and behavioral factors (alcohol, smoking) is associated with increased depression risk in women
- Cadmium emerged as the strongest individual metal contributor to depressive symptoms (conditional PIP = 0.447 via BKMR analysis)
- Total mercury paradoxically showed a negative association with depression, likely due to the protective effect of omega-3 fatty acids from fish consumption
- Cadmium exhibited a non-linear exposure-response: depressive symptoms sharply increased at higher levels before slightly declining, suggesting a threshold effect
- Lead showed a positive but more modest association with depression
- Women are particularly vulnerable to metal-depression effects due to hormonal fluctuations and physiological differences

## Methods/Findings

- **Study design**: Cross-sectional analysis of NHANES 2017-2018 data; n=153 women meeting inclusion criteria
- **Outcome measure**: PHQ-9 (Patient Health Questionnaire-9); mean score 5.94 (mild depression)
- **Metal levels**: Blood Pb mean 1.21 ug/dL, Cd 1.47 ug/L, total Hg 0.80 ug/L
- **Analytical methods**: Descriptive statistics, linear regression, Spearman correlation, Bayesian Kernel Machine Regression (BKMR) with 50,000 MCMC iterations
- **BKMR results**: Metal group PIP = 0.511; behavioral group PIP = 0.367; alcohol conditional PIP 0.565, smoking 0.435
- **Cadmium mechanisms**: Disrupts monoaminergic neurotransmitter system (serotonin, dopamine, norepinephrine); accumulates in CNS; mimics estrogen via ER-alpha/ER-beta binding; disrupts HPA axis
- **Limitations**: Cross-sectional design; small sample size (n=153); self-reported behavioral data; nickel was not measured
