## Key Findings

- **Metallophores as diagnostic biomarkers**: Microbial metallophores detected by mass spectrometry can distinguish invasive infection from colonization — a clinical game-changer ([[patil-2021-infection-metallomics-covid-era]], expert-opinion).
- **Nutritional immunity drives virulence**: Host metal sequestration forces pathogens into secondary metabolism, producing detectable metallophores that serve as infection fingerprints.
- **Gallium as therapeutic decoy**: [[gallium]] mimics Fe³⁺ and disrupts iron-dependent pathogen metabolism, offering a metal-based antimicrobial strategy.
- **Multi-metal landscape of infection**: Reviewed the roles of [[iron]], [[copper]], [[zinc]], [[nickel]], and [[cobalt]] in host-pathogen interactions, establishing that nutritional immunity operates across multiple metals simultaneously.
- **COVID-era relevance**: Discussed how metallomics-based diagnostics could improve infection discrimination in pandemic settings where rapid pathogen identification is critical.

## Detailed Summary

This review synthesizes the field of infection metallomics — the study of how metals shape host-pathogen interactions and how these interactions can be detected by mass spectrometry. The central thesis is that microbial metallophores (siderophores, zincophores, and other metal-chelating molecules) are specific biomarkers for invasive infection, distinguishable from colonization because invasive pathogens face intense nutritional immunity pressure and upregulate metallophore production accordingly. The review covers the metal requirements of major pathogens: **[[pseudomonas-aeruginosa]]** deploys pyoverdine and pyochelin for iron acquisition; **[[aspergillus]]** produces siderophores essential for tissue invasion; **[[staphylococcus-aureus]]** uses staphyloferrin A/B; and **[[mycobacterium-tuberculosis]]** relies on mycobactin. The authors argue that detecting these molecules in patient specimens (blood, urine, bronchoalveolar lavage) could provide rapid, pathogen-specific diagnostics. The review also discusses **[[gallium]]** as a therapeutic Trojan horse — Ga³⁺ is taken up by bacterial iron transport systems but cannot be reduced, poisoning iron-dependent enzymes. The COVID-19 pandemic context highlighted the need for non-culture-based diagnostics, making metallomics approaches particularly timely.
