# A Comparative Study on the Paradoxical Relationship Between Heavy Metal Exposure and Kidney Function

## Key Claims

- In the general Korean population, increased heavy metal levels are associated with decreased eGFR (expected nephrotoxic pattern)
- Paradoxically, in environmentally vulnerable areas, eGFR increased with higher heavy metal levels -- possibly due to reduced renal excretion as kidney function declines
- Urinary cadmium was the most significant contributor: a one-log increase in urinary Cd was associated with 19.9% increase in risk of eGFR decline below 60 in the general population
- In vulnerable areas, higher blood Hg reduced the OR of eGFR decline by 45.3%, and higher urinary Cd reduced it by 23.3% -- the paradoxical direction
- Korean adults have substantially higher metal levels than US, Canadian, and European populations (blood Pb 1.58 vs 0.86 ug/dL; Hg 3.11 vs 0.75 ug/L)

## Methods/Findings

- **Study design**: Comparative analysis of 4th Korean National Environmental Health Survey (KoNEHS, n=2984 general population) and FROM study (n=1157 vulnerable area residents, 13 sites)
- **Metals measured**: Blood Pb, blood Hg, urinary Cd; integrated sum (sigma HM) also calculated
- **eGFR classification**: Normal (>=90), slightly reduced (60-<90), impaired (<60 mL/min/1.73m2) using CKD-EPI 2021
- **General population (KoNEHS)**: Weak negative correlation between eGFR and sigma HM (r = -0.260, p <= 0.01)
- **Vulnerable areas (FROM)**: Weak positive correlation (r = 0.13); in 9 of 13 areas, eGFR increased as sigma HM increased
- **Logistic regression**: In general population (age >=50), only urinary Cd OR significant (1.21 for Cd, adjusted 1.206)
- **Explanation**: In vulnerable areas, chronic kidney damage reduces renal excretion of metals, so blood/urine levels decrease as kidneys fail -- creating apparent inverse association
