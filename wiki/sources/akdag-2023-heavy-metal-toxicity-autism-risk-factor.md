# Heavy Metal Toxicity as Autism Risk Factor

## Key Findings

- **Correlation between autism and heavy metal exposure** is established across multiple independent studies, with mercury, lead, cadmium, and arsenic all implicated ([[akdag-2023-heavy-metal-toxicity-autism-risk-factor]], expert-opinion)
- **Mercury is particularly associated with elevated ASD risk**, with exposure sources including airborne pollutants, dental amalgams, thimerosal in vaccines (historical), and dietary fish consumption
- **Children with autism had approximately double the mercury levels in baby teeth** compared to neurotypical children, indicating higher prenatal and early postnatal exposure
- **Blood mercury levels showed significant correlation** with ASD diagnosis severity in multiple studies
- **Complex interplay between genetic susceptibility and environmental metal exposure** contributes to ASD etiology — children with impaired detoxification capacity (metallothionein polymorphisms, glutathione deficits) may be disproportionately affected

## Methods

Narrative review of the literature on heavy metal toxicity as a risk factor for autism spectrum disorder. Covers epidemiological studies (hair, blood, urine, and deciduous teeth metal levels in ASD vs. controls), mechanistic studies (neurotoxicity pathways), and exposure source analyses. ## Detailed Summary

This review consolidates evidence linking heavy metal exposure to autism spectrum disorder risk. The authors examine four metals with the strongest evidence base: mercury, lead, cadmium, and arsenic. For each metal, they review exposure sources, neurotoxic mechanisms, and epidemiological associations with ASD.

Mercury has the strongest evidence base, with studies showing elevated Hg levels in hair, blood, and deciduous teeth of ASD children. The baby teeth finding is particularly compelling because deciduous teeth accumulate metals during prenatal and early postnatal development, providing a retrospective biomarker of exposure during critical neurodevelopmental windows. Mercury's neurotoxicity involves multiple mechanisms: mitochondrial dysfunction, oxidative stress, disruption of calcium signaling, and interference with neurotransmitter systems.

Lead neurotoxicity operates partly through displacement of zinc and calcium from critical enzymes and receptors (primitive 3: mis-metallation). Cadmium similarly displaces zinc from zinc-finger transcription factors essential for neurodevelopment. Arsenic disrupts thiol-dependent enzymes and depletes glutathione, compromising antioxidant defenses.

The review emphasizes that genetic susceptibility modulates the risk: children with variants in metallothionein genes, glutathione synthesis genes (GSTM1, GSTT1), or metal transporter genes may have reduced capacity to detoxify and excrete these metals.
