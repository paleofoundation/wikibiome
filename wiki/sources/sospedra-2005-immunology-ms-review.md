# Immunology of Multiple Sclerosis

## Summary

This landmark review covers the immunological basis of [[multiple-sclerosis]] (MS), focusing on the role of CD4+ T cells, HLA genetics, and the interplay between the immune system and the CNS. It represents a paradigm shift recognizing that MS is not solely a disease of the immune system but that CNS-intrinsic factors are equally important.

## Key Immunological Concepts

- MS develops in young adults with a complex predisposing genetic trait, likely requiring an environmental insult (e.g., viral infection) to trigger disease
- Activation of CD4+ autoreactive T cells and their differentiation into Th1 phenotype is a crucial initiating event
- CNS damage is mediated by antibodies, complement, CD8+ T cells, and innate immune factors
- Perturbations in immunomodulatory networks (Th2, Tregs, NK cells) may explain relapsing-remitting vs progressive courses

## HLA Gene Complex

Comprehensive table of HLA class I and II allele associations:
- **HLA-DRB1*1501**: strongest risk allele across many populations (Caucasians, Japanese, Tasmanians)
- **HLA-A*02**: protective effect (OR ~0.6)
- Disease-associated HLA-DR and -DQ molecules may preferentially present specific self-peptides (e.g., myelin peptides)
- HLA associations are heterogeneous across ethnic groups and MS subtypes

## Disease Models

- EAE (experimental autoimmune encephalomyelitis): injection of myelin components induces CD4+-mediated autoimmune disease
- EAE can be adoptively transferred by encephalitogenic CD4+ T cells
- Both EAE and MS share pathological features: inflammation, demyelination, axonal loss, gliosis

## CNS-Intrinsic Factors

- MS pathology may depend on processes "other than cell-mediated immunity"
- Demyelinating lesions after bone marrow transplant have few or no T cells, suggesting intrinsic CNS pathological processes
- The "inside out" theory proposes that metabolic dysfunction in the CNS triggers release of inflammatory mediators

## Relevance to Gut-Metal-Microbiome Axis

This foundational immunology review establishes the immune mechanisms through which [[gut-microbiome]] [[dysbiosis]] could influence MS. The Th1/Th17 balance described here is directly modulated by [[short-chain-fatty-acids]] and other [[microbial-metabolites]]. The HLA-DRB1*15:01 association with MS risk may interact with microbiome composition through antigen presentation of microbial-derived peptides. The environmental triggers discussed (viral infections, diet) are increasingly understood to act through the [[gut-brain-axis]].

## Limitations

- Published before the modern era of microbiome research
- Does not address gut-brain axis or microbial influences on neuroimmunity
