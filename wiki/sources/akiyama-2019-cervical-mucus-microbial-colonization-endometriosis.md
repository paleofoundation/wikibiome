# Key Claims

- *Enterobacteriaceae* and *Streptococcus* were more frequently detected in cervical mucus of [[endometriosis]] patients than controls by both NGS and qPCR
- Shannon diversity index was significantly higher in the endometriosis group (P<0.05), indicating more diverse microbial communities
- Distribution of microbiota was similar regardless of menstrual cycle phase in both groups
- *Lactobacillus* prevalence was 20-40x higher than other detected bacteria in both groups but did not differ between groups
- *Corynebacterium*, *Pseudomonas*, and *Flavobacterium* were also significantly present in cervical mucus of endometriosis patients

# Methods/Findings

- **Study design**: Case-control at Kyoto Prefectural University of Medicine
- **Participants**: 30 women with endometriosis (rASRM stage III-IV) and 39 controls
- **Methods**: Gram staining, 16S rRNA NGS (Ion Torrent PGM), quantitative real-time PCR

 - Higher Shannon diversity in endometriosis (P<0.05)
 - No significant beta diversity difference by weighted UniFrac
 - qPCR confirmed significantly higher *Enterobacteriaceae* (P<0.05) and *Streptococcus* (P<0.05) in endometriosis
 - *Pseudomonas* and *Corynebacterium* not significantly different by qPCR
 - No difference in *Prevotella* or *Gardnerella* (BV-associated bacteria)

# Relevance to Wiki

This study provides molecular evidence for increased microbial colonization of cervical mucus in [[endometriosis]], with *Enterobacteriaceae* and *Streptococcus* as the primary candidates. The confirmation by both NGS and qPCR strengthens the evidence. These bacteria may transmigrate from the cervix to the uterine cavity, supporting the [[bacterial-contamination-hypothesis]] where ascending bacterial colonization activates the LPS/TLR4 cascade to promote endometriotic lesion growth and inflammation.
