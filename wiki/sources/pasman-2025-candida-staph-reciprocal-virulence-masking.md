# Key Findings

- **Co-culture of *C. albicans* and *S. aureus* promoted secretion of 7 cytolytic and 11 proteolytic virulence factors**, demonstrating reciprocal virulence enhancement ([[pasman-2025-candida-staph-reciprocal-virulence-masking]], in-vitro).
- **Co-culture simultaneously promoted hypha formation AND beta-glucan masking** — the bacterial partner drives Candida toward its most invasive form while simultaneously hiding it from immune detection.
- **The bacterial partner actively helps the fungus evade the immune system**, providing the most direct evidence that interkingdom cooperation includes immune evasion as a shared benefit.

# Methods

In-vitro co-culture of *C. albicans* and *S. aureus* with proteomic analysis of secreted virulence factors and fluorescence-based beta-glucan exposure quantification.

# Relevance to WikiBiome

- Core evidence for [[candida-albicans]], [[staphylococcus-aureus]], [[biofilm]], and interkingdom relationships. Demonstrates that polymicrobial infections are qualitatively different from single-species infections.
- The simultaneous virulence + masking phenotype explains clinical persistence of mixed fungal-bacterial infections.

# Relevance to Cureva

- **Primitive 6**: The strongest evidence that functional shielding is a bidirectional, active process — not passive co-occurrence. Interventions must target the partnership, not just individual organisms.
- Implies that antimicrobial strategies targeting only one kingdom may inadvertently destabilize the partnership in unpredictable ways.

# Open Questions

- Does this reciprocal enhancement occur with other bacterial species commonly found in Candida biofilms (e.g., *Streptococcus*, *Enterococcus*)?
- What is the iron ecology of the co-culture — does *S. aureus* iron piracy benefit or compete with Candida?
