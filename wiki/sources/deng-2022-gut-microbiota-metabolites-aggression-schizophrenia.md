# Altered gut microbiota and its metabolites correlate with plasma cytokines in schizophrenia inpatients with aggression

## Summary

This study compared serum and stool specimens from 25 schizophrenia inpatients with aggression (ScZ-Ag) and 25 without aggression (NScZ-Ag). It measured systemic inflammation, oxidation, leaky gut biomarkers, gut microbiota by 16S rRNA sequencing, [[short-chain-fatty-acids]] by GC-MS, and neurotransmitters by LC-MS.

## Key Findings

- Significantly higher systemic pro-inflammation, pro-oxidation, and leaky gut biomarkers in ScZ-Ag vs NScZ-Ag (all P < 0.001)
- Alpha-diversity and evenness of fecal bacterial community were lower in ScZ-Ag
- *Prevotella* was significantly increased while *Bacteroides*, *Faecalibacterium*, *Blautia*, *Bifidobacterium*, *Collinsella*, and *Eubacterium_coprostanoligenes* were remarkably reduced in ScZ-Ag
- 6 [[short-chain-fatty-acids]] and 6 neurotransmitters were much lower in ScZ-Ag group
- Positive or negative correlations among altered gut microbiota, SCFAs, systemic pro-inflammation, leaky gut, pro-oxidation, and aggression severity were detected
- Pro-inflammation, leaky gut phenotypes relating to enteric dysbacteriosis and microbial SCFAs feature aggression onset or severity

## Relevance to Gut-Metal-Microbiome Axis

This study demonstrates that aggression in schizophrenia is associated with a more severe form of [[dysbiosis]] characterized by depleted butyrate producers and reduced [[short-chain-fatty-acids]]. The combined elevation of inflammatory, oxidative stress, and gut permeability markers creates a "perfect storm" for metal translocation and [[neuroinflammation]]. The depletion of *Faecalibacterium* and *Bifidobacterium* -- genera known to bind and sequester heavy metals -- may increase systemic metal exposure. The correlation between gut permeability and aggression supports the model where [[immune-balance]] disruption via the [[gut-brain-axis]] contributes to behavioral dysregulation.

## Limitations

- Small sample size (25 per group)
- Cross-sectional design
- Aggression assessment may be subjective
- Inpatient population may not be representative
