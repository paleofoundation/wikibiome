# Microbial Biomarkers for Parkinson's Disease: A Multi-Omics Approach

## Summary

This study integrates multi-omics data (metagenomics, metabolomics, proteomics) to identify robust microbial biomarkers for [[parkinsons-disease]], moving beyond single-omics approaches to capture the functional consequences of microbiome alterations.

## Key Findings

- Multi-omics integration improved biomarker performance compared to single-omics approaches
- Microbial metabolite signatures (bile acids, SCFAs, tryptophan metabolites) complemented taxonomic biomarkers
- Fecal calprotectin and other inflammatory markers correlated with specific microbial features
- The integrated biomarker panel showed promise for early PD detection in prodromal populations
- Cross-validation across independent cohorts demonstrated robustness of the multi-omics signature
- Specific metabolite-microbe correlations revealed mechanistic insights into PD gut dysfunction

## Relevance to Gut-Metal-Microbiome Axis

Multi-omics biomarker integration is essential for capturing the metallomic dimension of [[parkinsons-disease]] gut [[dysbiosis]]. Fecal [[calprotectin]] (already included in inflammatory marker panels) is itself a [[zinc]]/[[manganese]]-sequestering protein of the [[nutritional-immunity]] response. Bile acid alterations affect metal solubility and absorption. Tryptophan metabolite shifts (particularly indoles) are influenced by metal-dependent enzymes. Incorporating fecal metal profiles ([[iron]], [[zinc]], [[manganese]], [[copper]] levels) into the multi-omics framework would add a metallomic layer that could improve both diagnostic specificity and mechanistic understanding.

## Limitations

- Multi-omics studies are expensive and complex
- Sample size limitations when combining multiple data types
- Standardization across omics platforms is challenging
- Clinical validation in large prospective cohorts still needed
