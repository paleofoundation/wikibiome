## Key Findings

- **NAC at oral doses up to 900 mg three times daily** produced significant improvement on the Aberrant Behavior Checklist (ABC) irritability subscale compared to placebo (F=6.80, p<0.01, effect size d=0.96) ([[hardan-2012-nac-pilot-trial-autism-children|this study]], randomized-controlled-trial, n=33).
- **No significant group differences** were observed on other ABC subscales (lethargy, stereotypy, hyperactivity, inappropriate speech) or the Social Responsiveness Scale (SRS).
- **NAC was well tolerated** with gastrointestinal symptoms (constipation, nausea, diarrhea, increased appetite) as the most common side effects; no serious adverse events were reported.
- **Dual mechanism of action proposed**: NAC functions both as a glutathione precursor (addressing oxidative stress) and a glutamatergic modulator (addressing excitotoxicity), two pathways implicated in ASD pathophysiology.

## Methods

- **Design**: 12-week double-blind, randomized, placebo-controlled pilot trial.
- **Population**: 33 children (31 males, 2 females) aged 3.2-10.7 years meeting DSM-IV criteria for autistic disorder.
- **Dosing**: NAC titrated from 900 mg/day (weeks 1-4) to 900 mg twice daily (weeks 5-8) to 900 mg three times daily (weeks 9-12).
- **Outcome measures**: Primary — ABC irritability subscale. Secondary — remaining ABC subscales, SRS, Clinical Global Impression (CGI).
- **Completers**: Follow-up data available for 14 NAC and 15 placebo subjects.
- **Statistical approach**: Mixed-effects repeated-measures analysis; intent-to-treat analysis conducted.

## Detailed Summary

This study was among the first RCTs to evaluate oral NAC specifically in children with autism. The rationale rested on two converging lines of evidence: (1) children with ASD frequently exhibit markers of oxidative stress including reduced glutathione levels, and NAC is the rate-limiting precursor for glutathione synthesis; (2) glutamatergic dysfunction, including elevated peripheral glutamate, has been documented in ASD, and NAC modulates the cystine-glutamate antiporter (system Xc-), which regulates extracellular glutamate levels.

The significant improvement on the ABC irritability subscale (a large effect size of d=0.96) is clinically meaningful, as irritability is one of the most impairing behavioral domains in ASD and one of only two domains with FDA-approved pharmacotherapy (risperidone and aripiprazole). The lack of effect on social or communication measures suggests NAC may target a specific symptom cluster rather than core ASD features.

The favorable safety profile is notable given the pediatric population. The titration schedule allowed dose-related tolerability to be assessed, with most side effects occurring at the highest dose level.

## Open Questions

- Whether longer treatment duration or different dosing would affect core ASD symptoms beyond irritability.
- Whether baseline glutathione or glutamate levels predict NAC response (potential biomarker for patient selection).
- How NAC's effects on the gut microbiome (not measured in this study) may contribute to observed behavioral improvements, given emerging evidence of gut-brain axis involvement in ASD.
- Whether combining NAC with microbiome-targeted interventions would produce additive or synergistic effects.
