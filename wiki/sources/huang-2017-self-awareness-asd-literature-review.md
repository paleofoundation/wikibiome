# Overview

Review of existing research on the **self-system** in individuals with ASD using a three-dimensional approach: experiential, interpersonal, and narrative dimensions. Explores how impairments in the self-system correlate with social and cognitive functioning levels.

# Key Findings

## Self-Awareness in ASD

- High-functioning ASD individuals with higher IQ have **better awareness** of their limitations in social and communication domains
- Impairments are primarily in the **psychological (not physical) self** -- linked to theory of mind deficits
- Some researchers argue ASD involves **selective** rather than **global** impairments in self

## Key Deficits

- **Joint attention, language, and theory of mind**: individuals with ASD are not proficient in using language as a tool for guiding behavior and emotional regulation
- **Friendship**: critical for moral, social, and emotional growth; ASD individuals have difficulty maintaining friendships
- **Abstract reasoning**: difficulty with extraction and manipulation of information about events and concepts not in the immediate environment

## Three Dimensions of Self

- **Experimental Dimension**: examining relationship between perceived self-competence, emotional states, and social adaptation
- **Interpersonal Dimension**: ASD children show difficulty understanding others' perceptions
- **Narrative Dimension**: approach to self collected from narratives providing "heterogeneous aspects of life"

## Clinical Implications

- High-functioning autism (HFA) participants with higher IQ perceived themselves as **less competent** than peers but had better understanding of emotions
- Sleep problems, under-eating, and GI symptoms predicted avoidant behavior in ASD individuals
