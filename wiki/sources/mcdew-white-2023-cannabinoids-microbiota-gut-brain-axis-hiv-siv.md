# Cannabinoids Modulate the Microbiota-Gut-Brain Axis in HIV/SIV Infection

## Summary

This study demonstrates that long-term, low-dose delta-9-tetrahydrocannabinol (THC) positively modulates the microbiota-gut-brain axis (MGBA) in chronically SIV-infected rhesus macaques by reducing [[neuroinflammation]] and [[dysbiosis]] while elevating endocannabinoid and indole-3-propionate levels.

## Key Findings

- THC reduced [[neuroinflammation]] and [[dysbiosis]] in SIV-infected macaques
- Significantly increased plasma endocannabinoid, endocannabinoid-like, glycerophospholipid, and indole-3-propionate levels
- Blocked upregulation of type-I interferon response genes (*NLRC5*, *CCL2*, *CXCL10*, *IRF1*, *IRF7*, *STAT2*, *BST2*), excitotoxicity (*SLC7A11*), and enhanced WFS1/CRYM protein expression (endoplasmic reticulum stress/oxidative stress) in basal ganglia
- THC countered miR-142-3p-mediated suppression of WFS1 protein via cannabinoid receptor-1 mechanism in HCN2 neuronal cells
- Most importantly, THC significantly increased relative abundance of **Firmicutes** and **Clostridia** including:
  - Indole-3-propionate producers (*C. botulinum*, *C. sporogenes*, *C. cadaveris*)
  - Butyrate producers (*C. butyricum*, *Faecalibacterium prausnitzii*, *Butyricicoccus pullicaecorum*)

## Relevance to Gut-Metal-Microbiome Axis

The demonstration that cannabinoids can reverse [[dysbiosis]] and promote growth of [[short-chain-fatty-acids]] and [[indoles]]-producing bacteria has direct implications for [[multiple-sclerosis]]. The specific enrichment of *Faecalibacterium prausnitzii* (depleted in MS) and butyrate/indole-3-propionate producers addresses exactly the metabolic deficiencies documented in MS-associated [[dysbiosis]]. The endocannabinoid system modulates [[gut-microbiome]] composition, and its disruption by [[heavy-metals]] or other environmental factors could represent an upstream mechanism linking metal exposure to [[neuroinflammation]] through the [[gut-brain-axis]].

## Limitations

- SIV/HIV model, not MS
- Rhesus macaque, not human
- Long-term safety of THC in MS not established
- Small group sizes
