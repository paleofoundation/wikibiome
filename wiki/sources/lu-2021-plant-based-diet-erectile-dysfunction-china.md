## Key Findings

- **Higher plant-based diet consumption was negatively associated with ED presence.** After multivariate adjustment for age, lifestyle, and metabolic factors, both PDI (OR 0.869, 95% CI 0.795-0.943, P=0.004) and hPDI (OR 0.784, 95% CI 0.690-0.878, P<0.001) were independently protective against ED ([[lu-2021-plant-based-diet-erectile-dysfunction-china]], case-control, n=184).
- **Dose-response relationship between plant diet and ED severity.** Both PDI and hPDI increased significantly as IIEF-5 scores increased within the ED group (P<0.05), with trend analysis significant across all severity strata (severe, moderate, mild-to-moderate, mild).
- **Endothelial function biomarkers linked to diet pattern.** Nitric oxide was significantly lower in ED patients (30.8 +/- 21.3 vs 42.4 +/- 20.2 umol/L, P<0.001) and E-selectin was significantly elevated (37.3 +/- 12.7 vs 12.9 +/- 4.9 ng/mL, P<0.001). Both were independently associated with ED in multivariate analysis.
- **Metabolic syndrome was significantly more prevalent in ED group** (39.1% vs 21.7%, P=0.010) and was independently associated with ED presence (OR 1.323, P=0.001).
- **Testosterone levels did not differ between groups** (TT 4.4 vs 4.2 ng/mL, P=0.530; FT 86.3 vs 88.4 pg/mL, P=0.366), and plant-based diet was not associated with reduced testosterone, addressing a common concern about plant-based diets.

## Methods

- **Design:** Nested case-control study conducted in Tianjin, China (February 2019 to May 2020).
- **Participants:** 92 men with confirmed organic ED and 92 age-matched male controls. Controls were selected from a follow-up cohort of patients with nonfunctional adrenal adenoma.
- **ED confirmation:** All ED patients underwent nocturnal penile tumescence and rigidity (NPTR) monitoring by Rigiscan for three separate nights to objectively differentiate organic from psychogenic ED. Organic ED defined as IIEF-5 score <22 and failure to meet NPTR criterion (>=60% rigidity at tip lasting >10 min).
- **Dietary assessment:** NHANES 154-item food frequency questionnaire (FFQ) evaluating 12-month intake, converted to plant-diet index (PDI) and healthful plant-diet index (hPDI) using a validated 17-food-category methodology with quintile scoring (1-5).
- **Blood biomarkers:** Fasting samples for total testosterone, free testosterone, metabolic syndrome components (FBG, TG, TC, HDL-C, LDL-C), CRP, nitric oxide, and E-selectin (by ELISA).
- **Statistical analysis:** SPSS 26.0. Univariate and multivariate logistic regression adjusted for age, partner age, duration of partnership, frequency of intercourse, residence, occupational/educational status, income, and lifestyle. Multi-model sensitivity analyses confirmed robustness.
- **Power calculation:** PASS 15.0 confirmed 90% power at alpha=0.05 with effect size 0.50 required n=86 per group.

## Detailed Summary

This study is the first to investigate the association between quantitatively measured plant-based diet consumption and erectile dysfunction in a Chinese population. The investigators recruited 92 men with Rigiscan-confirmed organic ED from two hospitals in Tianjin and matched them with 92 ED-free controls.

**Dietary indices:** Mean PDI was 47.1 +/- 7.5 in ED group versus 58.5 +/- 7.1 in controls (P<0.001). Mean hPDI was 46.8 +/- 8.2 versus 59.4 +/- 8.9 (P<0.001). The overall population means (PDI 52.8, hPDI 53.1) were consistent with prior studies of Chinese dietary composition and slightly higher than American means (PDI 50.4, hPDI 50.8), attributed to lower dairy intake in Chinese diets.

**Endothelial biomarkers:** The ED group showed significantly lower nitric oxide (30.8 vs 42.4 umol/L) and markedly elevated E-selectin (37.3 vs 12.9 ng/mL), both surrogates for endothelial dysfunction. In multivariate regression within the ED group, PDI was positively correlated with NO (beta=0.147, P=0.005) and IIEF-5 scores (beta=0.473, P=0.022), and negatively correlated with E-selectin (beta=-0.129, P=0.001) and number of MetS components (beta=-2.034, P=0.005). Similar results were observed for hPDI.

**Metabolic parameters:** The ED group had significantly higher waist circumference (89.2 vs 86.3 cm, P=0.037), fasting blood glucose (6.4 vs 5.7 mmol/L, P=0.002), CRP (4.9 vs 3.9 mg/L, P=0.030), and MetS prevalence (39.1% vs 21.7%, P=0.010). BMI was independently associated with ED (OR 1.201, P=0.005).

**Lifestyle differences:** The ED group had significantly higher rates of smoking (38.0% vs 22.8%, P=0.025) and alcohol consumption (28.3% vs 13.0%, P=0.011), and lower rates of exercise (31.5% vs 46.7%, P=0.034). These were controlled for in multivariate analysis.

**Individual food categories:** No single food subgroup was significantly correlated with IIEF-5 scores on its own; the protective association emerged only through the composite diet index, supporting a dietary pattern effect rather than a single-nutrient mechanism.

**Limitations noted by authors:** Cross-sectional design limits causal inference; self-reported dietary data subject to recall bias; single testosterone measurement; controls from adrenal adenoma cohort; findings limited to organic ED only.

## Relevance to Cureva

- Supports dietary intervention hypotheses for [[erectile-dysfunction-signature]]: plant-based diet may serve as an ecological intervention that improves the vascular microenvironment (increased NO, decreased E-selectin) without affecting testosterone levels.
- The dose-response relationship (PDI/hPDI increasing linearly with IIEF-5 scores) strengthens the I_to_D edge of a dietary intervention triangle for ED.
- The finding that hPDI (distinguishing healthy from unhealthy plant foods) had a stronger association (OR 0.784 vs 0.869) suggests specificity: whole grains, fruits, vegetables, nuts, and legumes drive the effect, not refined grains or sugar-sweetened beverages.
- Addresses a common patient concern: plant-based diet did NOT reduce testosterone levels (TT P=0.530, FT P=0.366), removing a barrier to dietary counseling.
- MetS as an independent ED risk factor supports the ecological framing: metabolic dysfunction creates the inflammatory, low-NO vascular environment in which ED-associated dysbiosis thrives.

## Contradictions / Tensions

- **Contrasts with Liu et al. 2016:** A previous 4-year prospective study of elderly Chinese men (Mr OS Hong Kong) found no association between fruit/vegetable intake and ED. However, that study had two noted limitations: the population was elderly and sexually inactive, and they did not adjust for comorbidities. The present study addresses both limitations with an active, age-matched population and full MetS adjustment.
- **Individual food categories showed no significant correlation** with IIEF-5 scores, which may seem to contradict the overall diet index finding. The authors interpret this as evidence that the cumulative dietary pattern, not any single food, drives the association.
- **No microbiome data collected.** While the [[erectile-dysfunction-signature]] emphasizes gut-penile axis mechanisms through specific taxa, this study offers no direct microbiome evidence. The endothelial biomarkers (NO, E-selectin) provide an intermediate mechanistic layer but do not connect to the taxonomic signature.

## Open Questions

1. What is the microbiome composition of men with high vs low PDI/hPDI, and does it explain the NO/E-selectin differences observed?
2. Does the plant-based diet association hold in a prospective or RCT design, as the authors themselves recommend?
3. Which specific plant food components (polyphenols, dietary fiber, antioxidants, arginine) mediate the NO-enhancing effect, and do they act through the gut microbiome?
4. Is the MetS-ED-diet triad a single shared pathway (systemic endothelial dysfunction) or do diet and MetS independently affect penile vasculature?
5. Would findings generalize to psychogenic ED or mixed ED populations, given this study only included organic ED?
