# Tapsell 2015 -- Fermented Dairy Food and CVD Risk

## Key Claims

- The PREDIMED trial confirmed that the [[Mediterranean diet]] reduces [[cardiovascular disease]] risk by about 30%, with fermented dairy foods (yoghurt and cheese) being key components
- Yoghurt consumption is negatively associated with weight gain and has a more neutral effect on CVD risk; cheese may be associated with reduced inflammatory biomarkers
- Fermented dairy products may not have the same effect on raising LDL-cholesterol levels as butter with the same saturated fat content
- Population studies from multiple countries show a reduced risk of CVD associated with consumption of fermented dairy foods
- Total dairy intake was inversely associated with risk of [[myocardial infarction]] (HR=0.77, 95% CI 0.63-0.95) in the Swedish Mammography Cohort

## Methods/Findings

- **Type**: Narrative review

 - EPIC-Norfolk cohort (n=4000): low-fat fermented dairy consumption associated with reduced risk of type 2 diabetes (HR 0.76, 95% CI 0.60-0.99) and yoghurt intake specifically (HR 0.72, 95% CI 0.55-0.95)
 - Malmo Diet and Cancer cohort (n=24,495): dairy products overall inversely associated with CVD risk; fermented milk was the only product with a statistically significant negative relationship (risk ~15%)
 - Japanese factor analysis: fermented foods (fish, seaweed, vegetables, fruit, green tea) pattern associated with lower CVD risk
 - Evidence that cheese consumption across various regions is not consistently associated with increased CVD risk
- **Limitations**: Challenge in attributing separate effects to yoghurt vs cheese; people who eat healthier diets may be more likely to consume yoghurt; study design confounders
