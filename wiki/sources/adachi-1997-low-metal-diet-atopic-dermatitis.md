## Key Claims

- Low metal diets and/or dental metal alloy elimination produced marked or moderate improvement in 67% (18/27) of patients with moderate-to-severe atopic dermatitis (AD) who had positive metal patch tests
- Patients with a positive history of metal contact hypersensitivity responded significantly better (78% marked/moderate improvement) than those without such history (p<0.01)
- Both peripheral blood eosinophil counts and serum LDH levels decreased significantly (p<0.05) after 3 months in patients showing marked or moderate improvement
- Restriction of ingested metal allergens combined with dental alloy removal may be useful for managing AD in patients with metal sensitivity

## Methods/Findings

- **Design**: Open clinical study at Kobe University, Japan (1991-1992)
- **Population**: 27 patients (7 males, 20 females; age 9-40, mean 22.8) with moderate-to-severe AD; selected from 67 AD patients with metal hypersensitivity history; all had positive patch tests to at least one metal allergen
- **Metal allergens tested**: Nickel (NiSO4), chromium (K2Cr2O7), cobalt (CoCl2), plus 20+ other metals; most common positives: Cr, Ni, Co
- **Treatment**: Low metal diet (3 months) restricting foods high in nickel, cobalt, chromium, manganese, zinc, copper; 11 patients also had dental metal alloys removed; 4 patients had oral provocation with metal salts (all positive within 3 days)
- **High-nickel foods restricted**: All beans, all nuts, unpolished rice, buckwheat, oatmeal, spinach, pumpkin, lettuce, cabbage, mushroom, oyster, salmon, herring, seaweed, all spices, chocolate, tea, cocoa, wine, tap water, canned goods, cigarettes, herbs
- **Results**: 7 (26%) marked improvement; 11 (41%) moderate improvement; 9 (33%) minimal/no change; 5 patients who had both diet and dental elimination showed flare-up during metal elimination confirming sensitivity
- **AD scoring**: Graded 0-2 (0 = minimal/no change, 1 = moderate improvement 50-75% decrease, 2 = marked improvement >75% decrease)
- **Laboratory changes**: Eosinophils and LDH significantly decreased in responders (p<0.05); no significant changes in non-responders
