# Infectious Spondylitis in a Patient with Chronic Kidney Disease

## Key Findings

- This is the **first reported case** of spondylitis caused by *Campylobacter fetus* subsp. *testudinum*, identified in an 81-year-old male with ESRD on maintenance hemodialysis.
- The organism was identified by **16S rRNA gene sequencing** of blood cultures, as conventional culture methods failed to identify the subspecies.
- Underlying [[chronic-kidney-disease]]/ESRD is associated with immunosuppression (uremia-related immune dysfunction), which likely predisposed this patient to an opportunistic infection by a rarely pathogenic *Campylobacter* subspecies.
- The patient presented with back pain and fever; imaging revealed spondylodiscitis at L4-L5. Treatment with antibiotics (meropenem followed by oral ciprofloxacin) achieved clinical resolution.
- The case highlights the increased susceptibility of CKD/ESRD patients to unusual infectious agents due to uremia-related immunosenescence.

## Methods

**Case report** of a single patient. Blood cultures were obtained during febrile episodes. Initial culture grew gram-negative rods that could not be identified by standard biochemical methods. 16S rRNA gene sequencing was performed to achieve species-level identification. Spinal MRI confirmed spondylodiscitis. The patient was treated empirically and then with targeted antibiotic therapy.

## Detailed Summary

Choi et al. described an elderly male with ESRD secondary to diabetic nephropathy who developed infectious spondylitis caused by *Campylobacter fetus* subsp. *testudinum* — a subspecies previously isolated primarily from reptiles and rarely associated with human disease. The patient had been on maintenance hemodialysis for several years when he presented with progressive lower back pain and intermittent fever.

Blood cultures yielded gram-negative rods, but standard identification methods (API, VITEK) could not classify the organism. 16S rRNA sequencing revealed 99% homology with *C. fetus* subsp. *testudinum*. The authors noted that this subspecies had only been described in humans in a small number of prior cases, none involving vertebral osteomyelitis.

The case illustrates two concepts relevant to the CKD-microbiome axis: (1) uremia-related immunodeficiency — CKD patients have well-documented defects in both innate and adaptive immunity, including impaired neutrophil function, reduced T-cell responses, and chronic inflammation — and (2) the potential for gut-associated organisms to cause extraintestinal infections when barrier function is compromised. While the route of infection was not definitively established, *Campylobacter* species are typically acquired via the gastrointestinal tract.

## Relevance to WikiBiome

This case report illustrates the **immunocompromised state** of CKD/ESRD patients and their vulnerability to opportunistic infections, including by organisms typically considered low-virulence. The CKD-associated immune dysfunction (immunosenescence accelerated by uremia) creates an ecological niche where organisms that would normally be cleared by a competent immune system can establish invasive infections.

From a metallomics perspective, the iron dysregulation and zinc deficiency common in ESRD patients contribute to impaired immune function — connecting microbial metal dependencies and host nutritional immunity to clinical infectious outcomes.

- Would improved nutritional immunity support (e.g., zinc repletion) reduce the incidence of opportunistic infections in ESRD?
