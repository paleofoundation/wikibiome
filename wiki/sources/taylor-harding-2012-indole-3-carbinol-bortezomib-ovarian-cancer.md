## Key Findings

- **Indole-3-carbinol (I3C) synergistically sensitises ovarian cancer cells to bortezomib** (a proteasome inhibitor), with combination treatment more effective than either agent alone
- **Synergistic mechanism involves multiple converging pathways**: ER stress induction, cytoskeletal disruption, cell cycle arrest (G2/M), and apoptosis activation
- **Combination treatment overcomes chemoresistance** to platinum-based agents (cisplatin, carboplatin), which is a major clinical challenge in ovarian cancer
- **In vivo validation**: Co-treatment with I3C and bortezomib significantly inhibited tumor growth in mouse xenograft models, confirming the in vitro synergy translates to a living system
- **I3C is a dietary compound** derived from cruciferous vegetables (broccoli, cabbage, Brussels sprouts) through the breakdown of glucobrassicin by plant myrosinase and gut bacterial metabolism

## Methods

- **Study design**: Combined in vitro (cell line) and in vivo (mouse xenograft) study
- **Cell lines**: Multiple ovarian cancer cell lines including platinum-resistant lines
- **Interventions**: I3C alone, bortezomib alone, and I3C + bortezomib combination at various dose ratios
- **In vitro assays**: Cell viability (MTT/MTS), cell cycle analysis (flow cytometry), apoptosis markers (Annexin V, caspase activation), protein expression (Western blot for ER stress markers, cytoskeletal proteins)
- **In vivo model**: Mouse xenograft with subcutaneous ovarian cancer cell implantation, treatment with I3C and/or bortezomib, tumor volume measurement
- **Synergy assessment**: Combination index (CI) analysis using Chou-Talalay method

## Detailed Summary

This study from the British Journal of Cancer investigates the combination of indole-3-carbinol (I3C), a dietary compound from cruciferous vegetables, with bortezomib, an FDA-approved proteasome inhibitor, for ovarian cancer treatment. Ovarian cancer has the highest mortality rate among gynecological cancers, and platinum resistance is a major clinical challenge. Novel combination strategies that overcome resistance are urgently needed.

I3C is produced from glucobrassicin during the digestion of cruciferous vegetables. Plant myrosinase and gut bacterial myrosinase-like enzymes convert glucobrassicin to I3C, which can then form condensation products including 3,3'-diindolylmethane (DIM) under acidic conditions. I3C and its derivatives have demonstrated anti-cancer properties through multiple mechanisms in preclinical studies.

Bortezomib inhibits the 26S proteasome, leading to accumulation of misfolded proteins and activation of the unfolded protein response (UPR)/ER stress pathway. I3C augments this ER stress through independent mechanisms, creating synergistic cytotoxicity. The combination disrupts multiple survival pathways simultaneously, making resistance more difficult.

The combination index analysis confirms true synergy (CI < 1) rather than merely additive effects. This synergy is observed across multiple ovarian cancer cell lines, including those resistant to platinum-based chemotherapy, suggesting broad applicability.

The in vivo xenograft experiments validate the in vitro findings. Mice bearing subcutaneous ovarian cancer tumors treated with the I3C-bortezomib combination showed significantly reduced tumor growth compared to either agent alone, with acceptable toxicity profiles.

The microbiome connection is relevant because I3C bioavailability depends partly on gut bacterial metabolism. Gut bacteria with myrosinase-like activity convert glucobrassicin to I3C and further metabolize I3C to bioactive derivatives. Individual variation in gut microbiome composition may therefore affect I3C bioavailability and therapeutic efficacy. Additionally, I3C and DIM are aryl hydrocarbon receptor (AhR) ligands that modulate gut immune function.
