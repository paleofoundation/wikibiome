## Key Claims

- Urinary nickel levels show a weak but positive association with diabetes risk (pooled SMD 0.16, 95% CI 0.07-0.25, p<0.01), suggesting elevated urinary nickel as a potential biomarker
- Blood nickel levels do NOT show a significant association with diabetes risk (pooled SMD 0.03, 95% CI -0.20 to 0.27)
- The association between nickel exposure and diabetes may differ at different stages of the disease: blood nickel concentrations appear lower in prediabetic groups than in diabetic groups, suggesting a potentially reversed relationship
- Nickel may promote hyperglycemia through increased hepatic glycogenolysis, heightened pancreatic glucagon release, reduced glucose utilization, and elevated inducible nitric oxide synthase

## Methods/Findings

- **Design**: Updated systematic review and meta-analysis; PROSPERO registered (CRD42024534139); searched PubMed, Web of Science, Wanfang databases through July 2024
- **Included studies**: 20 studies (19 publications); 3 cohort, 2 case-control, 14 cross-sectional; total 46,071 participants; majority from Asia (n=15)
- **Quality assessment**: Newcastle-Ottawa Scale; 9 (47.4%) high-quality, 10 (52.6%) medium-quality; 1 excluded for low quality
- **Urinary nickel results**: 10 data sets; 5 positive significant, 5 non-significant; pooled SMD 0.16 (95% CI 0.07-0.25); I2=86% (high heterogeneity)
- **Blood nickel results**: 15 data sets; 3 positive, 4 negative, 8 non-significant; pooled SMD 0.03 (95% CI -0.20 to 0.27); I2=83%
- **Subgroup analysis by diabetes stage**: Diabetes group SMD 0.51 (-0.08 to 1.10); Pre-diabetes -0.01 (-0.56 to 0.54); Undivided -0.11 (-0.34 to 0.12) - no significant differences between subgroups
- **Sensitivity analysis**: Stable; no individual study substantially impacted overall results
- **Publication bias**: No significant bias for urine (Egger p=0.115, Begg p=0.655) or blood (Egger p=1.23, Begg p=0.240)
- **Proposed mechanisms**: Nickel induces hyperglycemia, insulin overload, glycogenolysis; may elevate inducible nitric oxide synthase and guanine monophosphate; dose-response relationship with fasting blood glucose reported
