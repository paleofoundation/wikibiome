# Microglia and Microbiome in Schizophrenia: Can Immunomodulation Improve Symptoms?

## Key Claims

- Early disturbances of microglial cells and cytokines could lead to weakness of the immunological system in the prodromal phase of [[schizophrenia]], fully manifesting in patients
- Maternal immune activation (MIA) using poly I:C during pregnancy produces schizophrenia-like phenotype in offspring; receptor expression changes are present from postnatal days and remain throughout development
- Microglia exhibit M1 (pro-inflammatory) and M2 (anti-inflammatory) activation states; the transition from M1 to M2 is inhibited in the microenvironment of schizophrenic brains
- Activation of microglia leads to neuronal loss, synaptic plasticity disruption, and was repeatedly found in patients and animal models
- Minocycline (antibiotic crossing blood-brain barrier) shows promising effects on negative symptoms of schizophrenia in some clinical studies
- Anti-inflammatory drug celecoxib holds positive effect when used as adjunctive therapy (meta-analysis evidence)
- Aspirin as add-on therapy shows beneficial effect in some studies but mixed results overall
- Combination of vitamin D and [[probiotics]] to target gut microbiome succeeded in reducing symptoms in one study
- Measurements of microbiome features might allow identifying the prodromal phase of schizophrenia

## Methods/Findings

- **Study type**: Review article
- **Key framework**: Microglial activation + microbiome disruption as dual pathway to [[neuroinflammation]]
- **Therapeutic targets**: Minocycline, aspirin, celecoxib, vitamin D + probiotics
