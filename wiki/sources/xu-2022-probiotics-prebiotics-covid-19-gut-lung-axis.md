## Key Findings

- **COVID-19 dysbiosis impairs lung immunity** — COVID-19 causes gut dysbiosis that disrupts the gut-lung axis communication, impairing respiratory immunity and enabling severe lung pathology. The intestinal microbiota produces SCFA and other metabolites that support mucosal immunity in the respiratory tract.
- **Probiotics and prebiotics restore dysbiosis-impaired immunity** — Beneficial bacteria (Lactobacillus, Bifidobacterium, Faecalibacterium) and their prebiotic substrates (FOS, inulin, fiber) restore SCFA-producing capacity and anti-inflammatory immune signaling.
- **SCFA restores barrier integrity and immune tolerance** — Butyrate and propionate from restored microbiota upregulate tight junction proteins, support regulatory T cells (Tregs), and reduce pro-inflammatory Th17 responses that drive severe COVID-19.
- **Metabolite-dependent immune activation** — Microbiota-derived metabolites (butyrate, secondary bile acids, trimethylamine-N-oxide precursors) activate G-protein coupled receptors (GPR41, GPR43) on innate lymphoid cells and epithelial cells, enhancing barrier function and pathogen resistance.

## Detailed Summary

This review examines how dysbiosis in COVID-19 impairs respiratory immunity via the gut-lung axis, and how probiotics and prebiotics can restore protective microbiota and prevent or alleviate disease severity.

**Mechanistic framework:**

1. **Dysbiosis breaks gut-lung axis signaling** — COVID-19 causes dysbiosis characterized by:
   - Reduced SCFA-producing bacteria (Firmicutes loss)
   - Reduced anti-inflammatory metabolite production
   - Increased intestinal barrier permeability
   - Reduced intestinal IgA and secretory IgA (sIgA) production
   - Impaired systemic and mucosal immune communication

2. **Gut-lung axis normal physiology** — In healthy individuals:
   - Gut microbiota-derived SCFA (butyrate, propionate) maintain intestinal barrier integrity
   - SCFA and other metabolites activate GPR41/43 on intestinal epithelial cells and immune cells
   - Intestinal sIgA protects mucosal surfaces systemically (including respiratory tract)
   - Gut-derived Tregs migrate to respiratory tract mucosa, suppressing excessive Th17 responses
   - Intestinal barrier-tight junctions prevent LPS/PAMP translocation that would trigger systemic inflammation

3. **Dysbiosis-driven respiratory vulnerability** — Loss of SCFA-producing bacteria:
   - Reduces intestinal barrier integrity (reduced tight junction proteins)
   - Reduces sIgA production and respiratory mucosal immunity
   - Reduces intestinal Treg differentiation
   - Allows increased circulating Th17 cells and pro-inflammatory cytokines
   - Enables LPS translocation and TLR4-driven systemic inflammation
   - Impairs aerobic metabolism in respiratory epithelial cells (SCFA depletion)

4. **Probiotics restore protective mechanisms** — Lactobacillus and Bifidobacterium species:
   - Directly produce SCFA and other anti-inflammatory metabolites
   - Restore butyrate and propionate availability
   - Upregulate tight junction proteins
   - Enhance sIgA production via dendritic cell and B cell signaling
   - Differentiate intestinal T cells toward Treg phenotype
   - Produce bacteriocins that inhibit pathogenic bacteria
   - Compete with pathogens for resources and colonization sites

5. **Prebiotics amplify probiotic benefits** — Dietary prebiotic fiber (FOS, inulin, oligofructose):
   - Selectively feeds beneficial Lactobacillus and Bifidobacterium (they preferentially metabolize specific prebiotic structures)
   - Amplifies SCFA production by providing fermentable substrate
   - Enriches populations capable of metabolite production
   - Economical and scalable compared to probiotic supplements

6. **Proposed COVID-19 applications:**
   - **Prevention:** Probiotics + prebiotics before or early in infection to maintain intestinal barrier and respiratory immunity
   - **Treatment:** Probiotics + prebiotics during acute infection to blunt excessive Th17 and inflammatory cascade
   - **Post-COVID:** Microbiota restoration to address persistent dysbiosis-driven symptoms
