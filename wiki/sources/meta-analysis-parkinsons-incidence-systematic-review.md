# The Incidence of Parkinson's Disease: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis

## Summary

This systematic review and meta-analysis aggregates global epidemiological data on [[parkinsons-disease]] incidence and prevalence. It provides updated estimates of PD frequency across different populations, age groups, and geographic regions.

## Key Findings

- Global PD incidence is approximately 10-18 per 100,000 person-years, increasing sharply with age
- PD incidence is higher in males than females (approximately 1.5:1 ratio)
- Incidence varies by geographic region, with higher rates in North America and Europe versus Asia
- Age is the strongest risk factor, with incidence rising exponentially after age 60
- The global burden of PD is increasing due to aging populations
- Environmental and genetic factors contribute to geographic variation
- Methodological differences between studies limit precise global estimates

## Relevance to Gut-Metal-Microbiome Axis

The epidemiological patterns of [[parkinsons-disease]] align with metal exposure patterns. The male predominance parallels higher occupational metal exposure ([[manganese]] in welding, [[lead]] in industrial work, [[nickel]] in metallurgy). The geographic variation (higher in industrialized regions) correlates with environmental metal pollution levels. The exponential age-related increase aligns with cumulative lifetime metal exposure and age-related decline in metal detoxification capacity. The increasing global PD burden coincides with increasing dietary [[nickel]] exposure from processed foods and stainless steel food contact materials. Understanding PD epidemiology provides the population-level context for metal-microbiome interventions, identifying high-risk populations (older males in industrialized regions) who would benefit most from metal-aware [[dysbiosis]] screening.

## Limitations

- Heterogeneity in diagnostic criteria across studies
- Ascertainment bias in different healthcare systems
- Limited data from low-income countries
- Temporal trends confounded by improved diagnosis over time
