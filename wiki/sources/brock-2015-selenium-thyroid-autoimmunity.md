# The Role of Selenium in Thyroid Autoimmunity: A Review

## Key Findings

- **Thyroid tissue contains the highest concentration of selenium in the body**, owing to its crucial role in glutathione peroxidases (GPx), thioredoxin reductases (TRx), and iodothyronine deiodinases (IDD), which protect thyrocytes from H2O2 damage and regulate thyroid hormone metabolism ([[brock-2015-selenium-thyroid-autoimmunity]], expert-opinion, narrative review)
- **Selenium supplementation (200 ug) reduces anti-TPO antibodies** in autoimmune thyroiditis, with the greatest reductions in patients with the highest baseline antibody levels: those with anti-TPO >1200 IU/mL experienced a mean 40% reduction vs 10% in those <1200 IU/mL ([[brock-2015-selenium-thyroid-autoimmunity]], citing Gartner et al. 2002 RCT, n=70)
- **Selenium deficiency is an independent risk factor for Graves' ophthalmopathy (GO) development**; a 2011 double-blind RCT demonstrated 200 ug Se/day for 6 months significantly decreased GO severity, improved quality of life, and decreased disease worsening, with benefits persisting after therapy withdrawal ([[brock-2015-selenium-thyroid-autoimmunity]], citing Marcocci et al. 2011 RCT)
- **Selenium has protective effects against cadmium and mercury thyrotoxicity**: Se binds cadmium and facilitates biliary excretion; Se antagonizes mercury, protecting against Hg-induced TPO inhibition and thyroglobulin iodination impairment ([[brock-2015-selenium-thyroid-autoimmunity]], expert-opinion)
- **Selenium-iodine interaction is critical**: in combined Se and I deficiency, normalizing Se without I supplementation aggravates hypothyroidism by increasing T4 metabolism via selenium-dependent deiodinases; iodine excess induces thyroid damage via selenoprotein inactivation ([[brock-2015-selenium-thyroid-autoimmunity]], expert-opinion)

## Methods

- **Study type**: Narrative review of selenium's role in thyroid autoimmunity
- **Scope**: Covers selenium biochemistry in thyroid tissue, environmental deficiency, selenium-iodine interactions, selenium-heavy metal interactions, Hashimoto's thyroiditis, Graves' disease, Graves' ophthalmopathy, and maternal thyroid health
- **Sources reviewed**: 84 references spanning epidemiological studies, RCTs, animal models, and Cochrane reviews
- **Population focus**: Patients with autoimmune thyroid disease (Hashimoto's and Graves'), pregnant women with anti-TPO antibodies

## Detailed Summary

### Selenium Biochemistry in Thyroid

Selenium is incorporated as selenocysteine into three critical enzyme families in thyroid tissue: **glutathione peroxidases (GPx)** that protect thyrocytes from oxidative damage by degrading H2O2 (required for thyroid hormone production but toxic if not rapidly cleared); **thioredoxin reductases (TRx)** that maintain cellular redox balance; and **iodothyronine deiodinases (IDD)** that convert T4 to T3. Selenium is linked to T-cell functioning, Th1/Th2 balance, and macrophage function, suggesting a multifaceted role in immune regulation. In transient deficiency, selenium is preferentially preserved for thyroid function, demonstrating its physiological priority.

### Selenium and Heavy Metals

**Cadmium** decreases T4 secretion by thyroid follicular cells and alters peripheral T4-to-T3 conversion by deiodinase enzymes. Selenium reduces cadmium levels by binding Cd and facilitating biliary excretion. **Mercury** (both inorganic and organic) inhibits thyroid peroxidase and thyroglobulin iodination, with elevated Hg linked to increased thyroglobulin levels. Selenium demonstrates an antagonistic relationship with mercury, conferring protective effects when Hg is elevated.

### Selenium and Iodine Interactions

In animal models, Se exerts protective effects in cases of excessive iodine ingestion. Human studies show co-administration of Se is important when addressing iodine deficiency to maintain adequate GPx function. Population-based studies demonstrate increased anti-TPO prevalence in areas practicing iodine fortification, with possible protective effect from adequate selenium status. It is hypothesized that iodine excess induces thyroid damage via selenoprotein inactivation, increasing the need for selenium. Critically, in combined Se and I deficiency, normalizing Se without I supplementation aggravates hypothyroidism through increased T4 metabolism via selenium-dependent IDDs.

### Hashimoto's Thyroiditis

A 2003 RCT (n=65, intervention=35, control=30) of 6 months selenium supplementation with levothyroxine showed significant anti-TPO reduction in the intervention group (p<0.05) and improved mood/well-being. A 2002 study (n=70, 200 ug selenite + L-T4 for 6 months) showed the greatest anti-TPO reduction in those with levels >1200 IU/mL (40% mean reduction) versus <1200 IU/mL (10% reduction). However, anti-TPO-positive euthyroid patients supplemented for 6 months with 200 ug Se showed no reduction in anti-TPO, suggesting baseline thyroid function matters. A 2013 Cochrane Review of four studies found significant anti-TPO decreases but concluded insufficient evidence for clinical application due to unclear blinding and absence of quality-of-life scores. The CATALYST trial (NCT02013479, n=472, Denmark) was underway at publication to assess quality of life as primary outcome.

### Graves' Disease and Ophthalmopathy

A population-based study demonstrated low selenium levels in newly diagnosed GD. Selenium status is an indicator of risk for GO development, with Se deficiency potentially an independent risk factor. A 2011 double-blind RCT (Marcocci et al., NEJM) showed 200 ug Se/day for 6 months significantly decreased GO severity, improved quality of life, and prevented disease worsening, with benefits persisting 6 months after withdrawal (conducted in a selenium-deficient region). Selenium may influence GD through GPx/TRx regulation of T3 levels, reduction of oxidative stress from elevated H2O2, and decrease of inflammatory cytokines via NF-kappa-B inhibition. The GRASS trial (NCT01611896, 7 Danish centers) was underway to establish selenium's clinical role in GD.

### Maternal Health

A 2007 study showed selenomethionine 200 ug/day during pregnancy and postpartum in euthyroid women with anti-TPO antibodies decreased risk of permanent hypothyroidism and postpartum thyroiditis vs placebo, with significantly reduced anti-TPO and improved thyroid ultrasound echogenicity. A 2014 study found 60 ug Se from 12 weeks gestation decreased free T4 and TSH only in thyroid-antibody-positive women. A 2013 Cochrane review concluded Se had positive impact on postpartum thyroid function and postpartum thyroiditis rates but no effect on pre-eclampsia or preterm birth.

## Relevance to Cureva

- **Intervention hypothesis**: Selenium 200 ug/day as adjunctive therapy for autoimmune thyroid disease, particularly in selenium-deficient populations
- **STOP signal**: Selenium supplementation in combined selenium-iodine deficiency without iodine correction first may worsen hypothyroidism
- **Triangle evidence**: Se supplementation (I) reduces anti-TPO (f) in Hashimoto's (D); Se (I) reduces GO severity (f) in Graves' disease (D)
- Supports interpretation of low serum selenium as potential nutritional immunity response vs true deficiency (Karen's Brain Primitive 2)

## Contradictions / Tensions

- Anti-TPO reduction in hypothyroid patients but NOT in euthyroid anti-TPO-positive patients creates tension regarding mechanism: is the effect via thyroid function restoration or direct immunomodulation?
- The 2013 Cochrane Review concluded insufficient evidence for clinical recommendations despite multiple positive RCTs, highlighting the gap between laboratory parameter improvement and clinical outcomes
- Benefits of Se in GO were demonstrated in a selenium-deficient region; applicability to selenium-sufficient populations remains unclear

## Open Questions

- Does selenium supplementation improve clinical outcomes (quality of life, disease progression) beyond antibody reduction? (CATALYST trial may answer)
- What is the optimal selenium form (selenomethionine vs sodium selenite) and duration for thyroid autoimmunity?
- Does baseline selenium status determine therapeutic response? No RCTs have stratified by selenium status
- What is the mechanism linking Se deficiency specifically to Graves' ophthalmopathy?
- How does selenium interact with the gut microbiome in the context of thyroid autoimmunity? (not addressed in this review)
