# Gut Microbiota Therapy for CKD

## Key Findings

- **Prebiotics, particularly resistant starch**, lower circulating uremic toxins and reduce systemic inflammation in CKD patients, with the most consistent evidence among microbiome-targeted interventions.
- **Low-protein and gluten-free diets** modulate microbial diversity and improve renal biomarkers, though evidence remains limited to small studies with heterogeneous designs.
- **FMT stabilizes creatinine levels** and shifts gut microbiota toward beneficial saccharolytic taxa in preclinical and early clinical studies, showing promise for CKD progression delay.
- **Emerging phage therapy** offers precision targeting of pathogenic bacteria without disrupting beneficial commensals, addressing a key limitation of broad-spectrum antibiotics in CKD.
- **AI-driven multi-omics integration** is positioned as the next frontier for personalized CKD microbiome therapy, enabling patient-specific intervention design based on individual microbial, metabolomic, and clinical profiles.

## Methods

- **Design**: Mini review synthesizing evidence on gut microbiota modulation strategies for CKD.
- **Scope**: Covers dietary interventions (prebiotics, dietary restriction), biotic therapies (probiotics, synbiotics, FMT), and emerging approaches (phage therapy, AI-guided precision therapy).
- **Journal**: Frontiers in Immunology (2025).

## Detailed Summary

This mini review by Liu et al. provides a comprehensive landscape of microbiome-targeted therapeutic strategies for [[chronic-kidney-disease]]. The authors organize interventions by evidence maturity: dietary approaches (prebiotics, resistant starch, protein restriction) have the most established evidence base; probiotic and synbiotic supplementation show moderate evidence with strain-specific variability; FMT demonstrates proof-of-concept but lacks large clinical trials; and phage therapy and AI-guided approaches represent the investigational frontier. The review emphasizes that the gut microbiome is not merely a bystander in CKD but an active contributor to disease progression through uremic toxin generation, barrier dysfunction, and immune dysregulation. The AI-driven multi-omics perspective is forward-looking — the authors argue that the heterogeneity of CKD microbiome profiles (varying by etiology, stage, dialysis modality, diet, and geography) necessitates personalized rather than one-size-fits-all microbiome interventions.
