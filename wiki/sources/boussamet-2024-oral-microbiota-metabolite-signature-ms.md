# Investigating the Metabolite Signature of an Altered Oral Microbiota as a Discriminant Factor for Multiple Sclerosis

## Summary

This pilot case-control study simultaneously characterized faecal and oral microbiota in French RRMS patients (n=14) and healthy volunteers (n=21) using 16S rRNA sequencing and metabolic inference tools. The study identified a specific oral metabolite signature that could discriminate MS patients from healthy controls and rheumatoid arthritis patients with 92% specificity.

## Key Findings

### Oral Microbiota Alterations
- Decreased commensal bacteria in saliva: *Aggregatibacter* and *Streptococcus* significantly reduced in RRMS
- Enrichment of [[inflammation]]-associated bacteria: *Leptotrichia* and *Fusobacterium* increased in MS saliva, and *Enterobacteriaceae* and *Actinomyces* in faeces
- Oral microbiota alterations appeared more pronounced than faecal changes
- Decreased oral microbial diversity (Shannon index) in RRMS patients

### Faecal Microbiota
- *Oscillospiraceae UCG-002* and *Roseburia* abundances significantly lower in RRMS
- *Akkermansia* tended to increase in RRMS patients (1% HV vs 2.6% RRMS)
- *Fusobacterium* tended to increase in both oral and faecal compartments

### Compartmentalization Breakdown
- In healthy controls, 28 genera were shared between oral and faecal compartments (11.3%)
- In MS patients, the proportion of oral-specific and faecal-specific genera decreased, suggesting breakdown of compartmentalization between oral and gut microbiomes

### Metabolic Pathway Alterations
- Decreased malate dehydrogenase (Krebs cycle enzyme) in oral compartment of RRMS
- Increased holoacyl carrier protein synthase (fatty acid biosynthesis) in RRMS saliva
- Shift from butyrate to acetate production in RRMS patients
- Decreased nitrite reductase and 3'-nucleosidase (stress response enzymes) in RRMS saliva
- Oral metabolite classification model (deoxyadenosine, hypoxanthine, glycine) achieved 92% specificity in independent validation cohort

## Relevance to Gut-Metal-Microbiome Axis

The oral cavity is a primary entry point for dietary [[heavy-metals]] and environmental toxicants. The finding that oral [[dysbiosis]] may be even more pronounced than gut dysbiosis in [[multiple-sclerosis]] suggests that metal exposure via ingestion could initiate microbiome disruption at the oral level before propagating to the gut. The breakdown of oral-gut compartmentalization in MS provides a mechanism by which oral pathobionts (e.g., *Fusobacterium*, *Leptotrichia*) could colonize the gut and amplify [[inflammation]] through the [[gut-brain-axis]]. The shift from butyrate to acetate production mirrors SCFA alterations seen in metal-exposed microbiomes.

## Limitations

- Small pilot study (n=35 total)
- Cross-sectional design
- French cohort; limited generalizability
- 16S rRNA provides limited functional resolution
