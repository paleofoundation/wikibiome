## Key Findings

- **Two courses of bacteriophage therapy achieved complete and durable resolution** of chronic bacterial prostatitis (CBP) in a 26-year-old male who had failed 14 courses of antibiotics over 5 years, including fluoroquinolones, Fosfomycin, trimethoprim, nitrofurantoin, and IV ceftriaxone (case-report, n=1).
- **Phage lysis of the dominant *E. coli* biofilm exposed previously undetected co-infecting species** — *Staphylococcus aureus*, *Enterococcus faecalis*, and *Serratia marcescens* — consistent with polymicrobial biofilm architecture shielding secondary organisms from detection and treatment.
- **Initial *E. coli* load was extremely high** (1 x 10^8 CFU/mL across EPS, semen, and urine), declining to <1 x 10^3 CFU/mL within 2 weeks of phage therapy, with complete clearance after the second treatment course.
- **The patient remained relapse-free for 4 years** post-treatment with no growth of pathogenic bacteria on repeat cultures — a durability that no antibiotic course had achieved.
- **Phage-antibiotic synergy (PAS)** is discussed as a promising combinatorial strategy, since phage and antibiotic resistance mechanisms are unrelated, meaning resistance to one can restore sensitivity to the other.

## Methods

- **Study design:** Single-patient case report.
- **Patient:** 26-year-old Indian male with CBP symptoms from March 2013 to May 2018 (penile pain, ejaculatory burning, pelvic pain, urinary urgency, burning micturition, fever, malaise).
- **Diagnosis:** Semen cultures showing *E. coli* ~1 x 10^5 CFU/mL; digital rectal exam (DRE) revealing boggy, tender prostate.
- **Prior treatment:** 14 courses of antibiotics (4-8 weeks each) over 5 years — ofloxacin, levofloxacin, ciprofloxacin, Fosfomycin, trimethoprim, nitrofurantoin, amoxicillin (oral), and ceftriaxone (IV). All failed to prevent recurrence.
- **Phage therapy protocol:** Bacterial isolates were tested against Eliava Institute standard phage preparations. *E. coli* showed sensitivity to Intesti, Ses, and Enko bacteriophage preparations.
  - **Course 1:** 10 days of Intesti phage (oral, rectal suppository, intravesical) + Ses phage (oral, rectal suppository), followed by 30 days of Intesti + Enko for residual *E. coli*, *S. aureus*, and *E. faecalis*.
  - **Course 2 (9 months later):** Intesti phage targeting *E. faecalis*. *Serratia marcescens* was judged likely commensal and not treated.
- **Outcome measures:** Symptom assessment, bacterial cultures of EPS, semen, and urine at baseline, 2 weeks, and 9 months post-treatment.

## Detailed Summary

The patient presented with a 5-year history of refractory CBP caused by *E. coli*. Despite 14 courses of antibiotics spanning multiple drug classes, the infection recurred after each course. The patient initially presented with daily fevers up to 39.4 degrees C, which persisted as subfebrile temperature (37.8 degrees C) after initial antibiotic therapy, requiring daily acetaminophen.

At the Eliava Phage Therapy Center (EPTC), initial testing revealed *E. coli* at 1 x 10^8 CFU/mL across all three sample types (EPS, semen, urine). No fungal growth or gonococcus was detected. Blood tests (CBC, leukocytes, ESR, CRP, PSA) were normal.

Within 7 days of starting phage therapy, the patient experienced significant symptom reduction, with fever resolving first. At 2-week follow-up, the prostatic massage was no longer painful (previously it had been), and cultures revealed a dramatically altered microbial landscape: *E. coli* had dropped to <1 x 10^3 CFU/mL, but *S. aureus* (5 x 10^5 CFU/mL) and *E. faecalis* (1 x 10^5 CFU/mL) were now detected for the first time. The authors attributed this unmasking to phage-mediated disruption of polymicrobial biofilms — a key observation about biofilm architecture in chronic infections.

Treatment was adapted: Intesti and Enko phages were administered for 30 days targeting all three organisms. *E. faecalis* showed no in-vitro sensitivity to available phage preparations, but was treated empirically. After this course, only mild ejaculatory burning remained.

Nine months later (no antibiotics taken in the interim), repeat cultures showed *Serratia marcescens* (1 x 10^8 CFU/mL in EPS) and *E. faecalis* (1 x 10^6 CFU/mL in semen). *S. marcescens* was judged likely commensal in an immunocompetent host. *E. faecalis* was treated with Intesti phage. After this second course, all chief complaints resolved completely and repeat semen cultures showed no bacterial growth. The patient remained symptom-free 4 years post-treatment.

The authors note that CBP affects an estimated 8.2% of men globally, most under age 50, and that the mental health burden is comparable to diabetes mellitus and chronic heart failure. Antibiotic failure in CBP is attributed to two main factors: bacterial biofilms (which protect colonies from both antibiotics and immune response) and rising antibiotic resistance. Prostatic calcifications can provide favorable environments for biofilm formation. The limited set of antibiotics capable of penetrating the prostatic epithelium further constrains treatment options.

A notable limitation acknowledged is the lack of regular microscopy throughout testing, which would have better differentiated CBP from chronic pelvic pain syndrome (CPPS). The authors also note a conflict of interest: the lead authors are founders of Vitalis Phage Therapy, and co-authors are affiliated with the Eliava Phage Therapy Center.

## Relevance to WikiBiome

- **[[escherichia-coli]]**: Demonstrates *E. coli* as the dominant pathogen in this CBP case, with extremely high bacterial loads (10^8 CFU/mL) and resistance to multiple antibiotic classes. Reinforces *E. coli*'s role as a biofilm-forming, multidrug-resistant uropathogen.
- **[[biofilm]]**: Provides direct clinical evidence for polymicrobial biofilm architecture — phage disruption of the dominant *E. coli* biofilm revealed *S. aureus*, *E. faecalis*, and *S. marcescens* that were previously undetectable. This is consistent with functional shielding within polymicrobial communities (Primitive 6).
- **[[antimicrobial-resistance]]**: Real-world case demonstrating the failure of 14 antibiotic courses across 7 drug classes, supporting the narrative of AMR as a growing clinical crisis. The paper cites 1.27 million deaths attributable to bacterial AMR in 2019.
- **[[staphylococcus-aureus]]**, **[[enterococcus-faecalis]]**: Both unmasked as co-infecting species within the polymicrobial biofilm, relevant to their known roles as opportunistic pathogens in genitourinary infections.

## Relevance to Cureva

- **Phage therapy as an intervention for CBP/erectile dysfunction-associated prostatitis:** This case provides preliminary (case-report level) evidence for bacteriophage therapy as a viable intervention when antibiotics fail. Relevant to the [[erectile-dysfunction]] signature, as CBP is a recognized cause of erectile dysfunction and the patient's symptoms included ED.
- **Biofilm disruption strategy (Primitive 6):** The sequential unmasking of shielded organisms suggests a clinical approach — target the dominant biofilm-forming pathogen first, then reassess the polymicrobial community and adapt treatment. This has implications for any condition involving polymicrobial biofilm infections.
- **Microbial competition (Primitive 5):** Phage therapy represents a targeted ecological intervention — selectively eliminating a dominant pathogen rather than broad-spectrum antibiotic disruption. This aligns with the two-sided ecological engineering primitive: suppress the pathogen without collateral damage to the commensal microbiota.
- **Potential STOP signal:** The repeated failure of antibiotics over 5 years in this case, combined with the biofilm-mediated recurrence pattern, suggests that continued antibiotic monotherapy for biofilm-associated CBP may be counterproductive — selecting for resistance while failing to penetrate biofilms.

## Contradictions / Tensions

- **Conflict of interest:** The lead authors are founders of Vitalis Phage Therapy and co-authors are employed at the Eliava Phage Therapy Center, creating a commercial interest in positive phage therapy outcomes. This does not invalidate the findings but must be noted when citing this source.
- **No metals data:** Unlike most WikiBiome sources, this paper contains no metallomic data. The connection to the metallomics framework is indirect — *E. coli*'s iron and nickel dependencies (known from other sources) are not discussed here. The biofilm and ecological dimensions are the primary contributions.
- **CBP vs. CPPS differentiation:** The authors acknowledge that the lack of regular microscopy (leukocyte counts in prostatic fluid) is a limitation, as it weakens the distinction between CBP and CPPS. Some symptom improvement could theoretically be attributed to phage-mediated immune modulation rather than direct bacterial killing.

## Open Questions

1. **What is the metallomic profile of the CBP-associated biofilm?** Iron and nickel are known cofactors for *E. coli* virulence enzymes — does the prostatic biofilm environment exhibit elevated iron/nickel that could be targeted through nutritional immunity strategies alongside or instead of phage therapy?
2. **Would phage therapy synergize with metal-restriction strategies?** If *E. coli* biofilm persistence in the prostate depends on iron acquisition (siderophores), could lactoferrin or other iron-sequestration approaches complement phage therapy?
3. **What was the full resistome of the *E. coli* isolate?** The paper mentions multidrug resistance but does not characterize the specific resistance genes, which would be relevant for understanding the ESBL status and cross-condition AMR patterns.
4. **How does phage-mediated biofilm disruption interact with the prostate microbiome more broadly?** The unmasking of *S. marcescens* as a possible commensal raises questions about the normal prostatic microbial ecology and whether phage therapy preserves it better than antibiotics.
5. **Is the 4-year relapse-free period durable long-term?** Longer follow-up and larger case series are needed to establish durability of phage therapy outcomes in CBP.
