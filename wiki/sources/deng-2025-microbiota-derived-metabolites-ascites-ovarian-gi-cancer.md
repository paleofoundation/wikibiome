## Key Findings

- **Ovarian cancer stage IV ascites exhibited metabolic profiles similar to gastrointestinal cancers**, while stage II-III OC ascites showed significantly different metabolic signatures — suggesting metabolic convergence in advanced disease ([[deng-2025-microbiota-derived-metabolites-ascites-ovarian-gi-cancer|this study]], cross-sectional).
- **Eleven typically microbiome-derived metabolites** were found at higher levels in stage IV OC ascites compared to earlier stages, indicating increasing microbial metabolite presence with disease progression.
- **3-hydroxyanthranilic acid** (a tryptophan catabolite in the kynurenine pathway), **indole** (a microbial tryptophan metabolite), and **naringenin** (a flavonoid metabolized by gut bacteria) were identified as potential markers of disease progression.
- **4D metabolomics approach** (combining spatial, temporal, and molecular dimensions) provided comprehensive metabolite profiling of the ascites microenvironment.
- The convergence of OC and GI cancer metabolic profiles at advanced stages suggests a shared microbial contribution to the peritoneal tumor microenvironment.

## Methods

- **Study type**: Cross-sectional metabolomic profiling study.
- **Samples**: Ascites fluid from ovarian cancer patients (staged II-IV) and gastrointestinal cancer patients.
- **Platform**: 4D metabolomics using advanced mass spectrometry techniques for comprehensive metabolite identification.
- **Analysis**: Comparison of metabolite profiles between OC stages and between OC and GI cancer ascites; identification of microbiota-derived metabolites using established databases of microbial metabolic products.
- **Metabolite classification**: Metabolites categorized as host-derived, microbiota-derived, or co-metabolized based on known biosynthetic pathways.

## Detailed Summary

This study provides direct evidence that microbiota-derived metabolites are present in the peritoneal cavity of cancer patients and that their abundance increases with disease stage. The peritoneal cavity, historically considered sterile, is now recognized to harbor microbial communities — particularly in advanced cancer with compromised intestinal barriers.

The convergence of stage IV OC and GI cancer metabolic profiles is a key finding. It suggests that as OC progresses, the peritoneal microenvironment becomes increasingly influenced by gut-derived microbial metabolites, possibly due to increased intestinal permeability, tumor-induced barrier disruption, or bacterial translocation. This blurring of metabolic boundaries between cancer types at advanced stages has implications for understanding how the microbiome shapes the tumor microenvironment.

The three highlighted metabolites have specific biological relevance. 3-Hydroxyanthranilic acid is produced via the kynurenine pathway of tryptophan metabolism, which is activated by inflammation and has immunosuppressive properties — contributing to tumor immune evasion. Indole and indole derivatives are classic microbial tryptophan metabolites that signal through the aryl hydrocarbon receptor (AhR), modulating immune responses and barrier function. Naringenin, while also found in citrus fruits, undergoes significant microbial metabolism and has documented anti-inflammatory and anti-proliferative properties.

## Open Questions

- Whether the microbial metabolites in ascites are derived from gut translocation, peritoneal microbial communities, or both.
- How metal status (particularly iron, which modulates indoleamine 2,3-dioxygenase activity in the kynurenine pathway) influences microbial metabolite production in the peritoneal space.
- Whether interventions targeting gut barrier integrity could reduce the microbial metabolite load in early-stage OC and slow progression.
- The functional impact of these microbial metabolites on peritoneal immune cells and tumor biology.
