# EU SCF Opinion on Acute Risks Posed by Tin in Canned Foods

## Key Claims

- **Document type**: Regulatory scientific opinion (adopted 12 December 2001) by the EU Scientific Committee on Food (SCF)
- Only inorganic [[tin]] (the principal form in canned foods) was assessed; organotin compounds are much more toxic but rarely found in food from environmental contamination
- Inorganic tin salts (stannous and stannic forms) are poorly absorbed from the GI tract and mainly excreted in faeces
- Tin salts are corrosive; acute toxicity manifests as gastric irritation, nausea, vomiting, abdominal discomfort, and diarrhoea within 0.5-3.0 hours of ingestion, resolving within 48 hours
- Toxicity depends more on tin concentration in the foodstuff than on total tin intake per body weight
- Adverse reactions reported from canned beverages (100-490 mg/kg tin) and canned tomato juice (140-400 mg/kg tin) in unlacquered or partially lacquered tinplate cans
- The SCF concurs with JECFA that tin levels of 150 mg/kg in beverages or 250 mg/kg in other canned foods may cause gastric irritation in some individuals
- No data available on whether infants are more sensitive than older children and adults

## Methods/Findings

- **Scope**: Assessment of acute risks only, with reference to JECFA opinion (2000)
- **Key limitation**: Circumstances of intake (volume, duration) were not recorded in adverse event cases; tin concentrations came from analysis of juice batches rather than actual consumed drinks
- **No-effect concentration**: Could not be precisely determined from available data
- **Reference**: WHO (2001) Safety Evaluation of Certain Food Additives and Contaminants, WHO Food Additives Series No. 46
