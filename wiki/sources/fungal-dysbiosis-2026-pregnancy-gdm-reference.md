## Key Findings

- **Fungal dysbiosis is relevant to gestational diabetes pathogenesis**, with pregnancy-specific mycobiome shifts that parallel and interact with bacterial microbiome changes
- **[[candida-albicans]] expansion during pregnancy** may be driven by the hormonal and immune changes of gestation that alter fungal-bacterial competition dynamics
- **Pregnancy-specific mycobiome changes** may serve as early predictive biomarkers for GDM development, potentially detectable before conventional metabolic markers (glucose, insulin)
- **Interkingdom interactions** between fungi and bacteria are disrupted during pregnancy, with loss of bacterial competitors allowing fungal overgrowth
- **Iron supplementation during pregnancy** may inadvertently promote fungal expansion, as [[candida-albicans]] possesses sophisticated iron acquisition systems

## Detailed Summary

This article examines the role of fungal dysbiosis in gestational diabetes, an area that has been under-studied relative to bacterial microbiome changes in pregnancy. While bacterial dysbiosis in GDM has received substantial attention, the mycobiome (fungal community) represents an important but often overlooked component of the gestational microbiome.

Pregnancy involves profound physiological changes that affect the microbial ecosystem: progesterone and estrogen surges alter vaginal and gut epithelial environments; immune modulation (Th2 skewing) reduces antifungal defenses; and metabolic changes (increased glucose availability, insulin resistance of late pregnancy) favor saccharolytic organisms including fungi.

[[candida-albicans]] is the most studied fungal organism in this context. Pregnancy-associated hormonal changes promote Candida hyphal morphogenesis, which increases tissue invasiveness and virulence. The shift from yeast to hyphal form is a key pathogenicity switch that is influenced by hormonal signals, nutrient availability, and reduced bacterial competition.

The interkingdom perspective is critical: in a healthy gut and vaginal microbiome, bacterial communities (particularly [[lactobacillus]] species) produce organic acids, bacteriocins, and compete for nutrients in ways that constrain fungal growth. When bacterial communities are disrupted (e.g., by antibiotics, dietary changes, or immune shifts during pregnancy), fungal organisms can expand into the vacated ecological niche. This fungal expansion may contribute to GDM through inflammatory pathways, altered metabolite production, and disruption of gut barrier function.

Iron and zinc dynamics are particularly relevant during pregnancy. Prenatal iron supplementation is universal, but excess iron may promote fungal growth, as [[candida-albicans]] possesses sophisticated iron acquisition systems including a reductive high-affinity iron uptake pathway. Similarly, pregnancy increases zinc demand, and zinc deficiency may impair both antifungal immune responses and the metabolic function of zinc-dependent commensal bacteria.
