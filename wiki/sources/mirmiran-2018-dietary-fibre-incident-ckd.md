# Dietary Fibre Intake and Risk of Incident CKD

## Key Findings

- An **inverse association** was found between total dietary fibre intake and the risk of developing [[chronic-kidney-disease]], with the highest quartile of intake associated with a 53% lower risk compared to the lowest quartile (OR 0.47).
- A **dose-response relationship** was demonstrated: every 5 g/day increase in total fibre intake was associated with an 11% reduction in incident CKD risk.
- **Fibre from vegetables and legumes** showed the strongest protective associations, while fibre from grains showed weaker or non-significant associations — suggesting that the source of fibre matters, not just the quantity.
- The protective effect persisted after adjustment for potential confounders including age, sex, BMI, smoking, physical activity, energy intake, diabetes, and hypertension.
- The findings support the hypothesis that adequate dietary fiber — particularly from plant sources — may protect against CKD through gut microbiome modulation, anti-inflammatory effects, and metabolic benefits.

## Methods

**Population-based prospective cohort study** nested within the Tehran Lipid and Glucose Study (TLGS). A total of 1,630 adult participants free of CKD at baseline were followed for a mean of 6.1 years. Dietary intake was assessed using a validated 168-item food frequency questionnaire. Incident CKD was defined as eGFR decline to below 60 mL/min/1.73m2. Multivariable logistic regression was used to estimate odds ratios across quartiles of fiber intake, adjusting for demographic, dietary, and clinical confounders.

## Detailed Summary

Mirmiran et al. examined whether habitual dietary fiber intake predicts the development of CKD in an Iranian population. The TLGS is a long-running community-based cohort study that has generated substantial data on the relationships between dietary patterns and chronic disease risk in a Middle Eastern population.

The finding that vegetable and legume fiber was most protective — while grain fiber was less so — is noteworthy and has mechanistic implications. Vegetable and legume fibers tend to be more fermentable by gut bacteria (producing SCFAs) and contain more soluble components compared to grain fibers, which are predominantly insoluble cellulose. SCFAs — particularly butyrate — have documented renoprotective effects, including reduction of renal inflammation, improvement of intestinal barrier integrity (reducing bacterial translocation), and modulation of immune responses in the kidney.

The dose-response relationship (11% risk reduction per 5 g/day of fiber) provides a practical target for dietary counseling and public health recommendations. It also suggests that even modest increases in fiber intake — achievable through dietary modification — could meaningfully reduce population-level CKD incidence.

The study is limited by reliance on food frequency questionnaires (subject to recall bias) and by the observational design (unable to establish causation despite the prospective nature). However, the biological plausibility of the fiber-CKD relationship, supported by animal model data and mechanistic studies, strengthens the case for a causal interpretation.

## Relevance to WikiBiome

This study provides **epidemiological evidence** supporting the gut-kidney axis hypothesis: dietary fiber, by feeding beneficial SCFA-producing gut bacteria, may protect against CKD development. The stronger association with vegetable/legume fiber versus grain fiber aligns with microbiome research showing that fermentable fibers produce more SCFAs and support greater microbial diversity.

The fiber-kidney connection also has metallomics relevance — dietary fiber from plant sources provides phytates and polyphenols that can chelate heavy metals in the gut lumen, potentially reducing metal absorption and renal metal burden.

## Open Questions

- Does the protective effect of dietary fiber on CKD risk operate primarily through gut microbiome modulation (SCFA production) or through other mechanisms (glycemic control, anti-inflammatory effects)?
- Do specific types of fermentable fiber (e.g., inulin, resistant starch, pectin) differ in their renoprotective effects?
- Does the fiber-CKD relationship differ in populations with higher environmental metal exposure, where fiber's metal-chelating properties might provide additional benefit?
