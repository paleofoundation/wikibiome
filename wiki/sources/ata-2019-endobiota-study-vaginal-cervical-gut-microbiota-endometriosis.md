## Key Findings

- **Complete absence of *Atopobium*** in both vaginal and cervical microbiota of women with stage 3/4 endometriosis, while present in healthy controls ([[ata-2019-endobiota-study-vaginal-cervical-gut-microbiota-endometriosis]], cross-sectional, n=28).
- **Increased potentially pathogenic genera in cervical microbiota** of endometriosis patients: after excluding *Lactobacillus*, *Gardnerella* comprised 67.7% vs 36.8% in controls (p<0.05), with *Streptococcus*, *Escherichia/Shigella*, and *Ureaplasma* also more abundant.
- ***Prevotella*, *Dialister*, and *Megasphaera* were significantly decreased** in cervical microbiota of endometriosis patients compared to controls.
- **Gut microbiome dominated by *Escherichia/Shigella*** in two endometriosis patients who subsequently required segmental colon resection — suggesting potential gut microbiome biomarker for bowel involvement severity.
- **Overall alpha and beta diversity were not significantly different** between endometriosis and control groups across all three body sites (vaginal adonis p=0.504; cervical p=0.776; stool p=0.635).

## Methods

- **Study design:** Prospective observational cohort (cross-sectional comparison), conducted 2016–2017 at Koc University Hospital, Istanbul, Turkey.
- **Participants:** 14 Caucasian women with histology-proven stage 3/4 endometriosis (American Fertility Society Revised Classification) and 14 Caucasian healthy controls. All nulligravid. Age-matched (median 28.5 vs 27.5 years) and BMI-matched (median 23 vs 21 kg/m2). No oral contraceptives, IUDs, antibiotics, or probiotics within 8 weeks.
- **Sample sites:** Vaginal swabs, endocervical swabs, and stool samples from each participant (84 total samples).
- **Sequencing:** 16S rRNA gene V3–V4 regions, MiSeq 2x300bp paired-end sequencing. 327 genera identified, 6,160,862 total reads, average 73,344 per sample.
- **Bioinformatics:** Quality filtering with prinseq-lite, FLASH for read joining, Bowtie2 for human genome removal, RDP classifier for taxonomy, QIIME 1.9.0 for diversity analyses.
- **Statistics:** Shannon diversity index for alpha diversity; Bray-Curtis dissimilarity with PCoA for beta diversity; Adonis nonparametric ANOVA for group differences; Wilcoxon tests for genus-level comparisons.
- **Controls:** Strict exclusion criteria including dysmenorrhea, dyspareunia, dysschesia, infertility (B&B score >0 excluded from controls), IBD, GI surgery/cancer, abnormal pap smear, BMI >30.
- **Data availability:** Sequences deposited in EBI Short Read Archive under PRJEB26800.

## Detailed Summary

The Endobiota Study compared vaginal, cervical, and gut microbiota between women with severe (stage 3/4) endometriosis and healthy controls, using 16S rRNA gene sequencing. All endometriosis patients had endometriomas, widespread pelvic lesions, deep infiltrating nodules on sacrouterine ligaments, and partially or completely obliterated pouch of Douglas. Two of 14 had muscular/mucosal layer bowel involvement.

**Overall diversity:** No significant differences in alpha diversity (Shannon index) or beta diversity (Bray-Curtis PCoA) between groups at any body site. Fecal samples had the highest diversity, followed by cervical and vaginal samples.

**Vaginal microbiota:** *Gemella* and *Atopobium* were completely absent in the endometriosis group. After excluding *Lactobacillus*, *Gardnerella* comprised a significantly higher proportion in endometriosis (72.9% vs 36.8%, p<0.05). *Escherichia/Shigella* was also more abundant; *Prevotella* and *Dialister* were decreased (non-significant).

**Cervical microbiota:** Dominated by *Lactobacillus* in both groups (84.6% endometriosis, 80.2% controls). *Atopobium* and *Sneathia* were completely absent in the endometriosis group. *Alloprevotella* was significantly increased in endometriosis (p<0.01). After excluding *Lactobacillus*, *Gardnerella* (67.7% vs 36.8%, p<0.05), *Streptococcus*, *Escherichia/Shigella*, and *Ureaplasma* were increased, while *Prevotella*, *Dialister*, and *Megasphaera* were significantly decreased.

**Gut microbiota:** *Sneathia*, *Barnesella*, and *Gardnerella* were significantly decreased in the endometriosis group (p<0.01 for all). Two endometriosis patients had *Escherichia/Shigella*-dominant stool microbiomes; both subsequently required segmental colon resection for deep infiltrating endometriosis — a finding the surgeons were unaware of at the time of microbiome sampling.

**Sensitivity analyses:** The exclusion of *Lactobacillus* from vaginal and cervical data revealed shifts masked by the dominant genus, particularly the increased relative abundance of potentially pathogenic bacteria in endometriosis.

## Relevance to Cureva

- **Signature enrichment:** Supports the taxonomic layer of the [[endometriosis-signature]] with multi-site data. *Gardnerella*, *Streptococcus*, *Escherichia/Shigella*, and *Ureaplasma* enrichment in the cervix aligns with existing signature patterns for pathogen enrichment in endometriosis.
- **Depletion pattern:** Loss of *Atopobium*, *Prevotella*, *Dialister*, and *Megasphaera* from cervical microbiota suggests ecological shifts away from commensal diversity.
- **Potential biomarker:** *Escherichia/Shigella*-dominant gut microbiome may predict severity of bowel involvement, with implications for surgical counseling — this is a clinically actionable finding if validated.
- **Estrobolome connection (Primitive 7):** The increased *Gardnerella* (a beta-glucuronidase producer) in cervical microbiota connects to estrogen recirculation. Combined with the estrogen-dependent nature of endometriosis, this supports the estrobolome pathway as a mechanistic contributor.
- **Ecological engineering (Primitive 5):** The shift from commensals (*Prevotella*, *Dialister*, *Megasphaera*) toward pathobionts (*Gardnerella*, *Streptococcus*, *E. coli*) represents a two-sided ecological disruption — both pathogen enrichment and beneficial function loss.

## Contradictions / Tensions

- **Atopobium paradox:** This study finds *Atopobium* completely absent in endometriosis (a benign condition), while Walther-Antonio et al. (2016) found *Atopobium vaginae* enriched in endometrial cancer. The authors note this contrast — *Atopobium* absence may be associated with benign gynecologic pathology through a "different downstream effect." This needs reconciliation with existing [[atopobium]] content.
- **Gardnerella directionality:** *Gardnerella* was increased in cervical microbiota of endometriosis patients but decreased in stool. This site-specific behavior complicates simple enrichment/depletion classifications on the [[gardnerella]] entity page.
- **No overall diversity difference:** Despite genus-level shifts, overall alpha and beta diversity were not significantly different, which contrasts with studies reporting broad dysbiosis in endometriosis. The small sample size (n=14 per group) limits statistical power.
- **No metals data:** This study does not measure any metal concentrations, which limits its direct contribution to the metallomic signature layer.

## Open Questions

1. **Causality:** Does the microbiome shift precede endometriosis development, or does the disease alter the microbiome? The study explicitly cannot answer this.
2. **Stage dependency:** Are these genus-level shifts specific to severe (stage 3/4) endometriosis, or do they also appear in stage 1/2 disease? No mild-stage patients were included.
3. **Atopobium mechanism:** What is the functional consequence of *Atopobium* absence in the reproductive tract? Is it protective loss or a marker of ecological disruption?
4. **Bowel biomarker validation:** Can *Escherichia/Shigella*-dominant gut microbiome reliably predict bowel involvement requiring resection? Only 2 cases observed — needs larger cohort validation.
5. **Metagenomic depth:** The study used 16S rRNA sequencing only (genus-level resolution). Species-level and functional metagenomic data would clarify whether the *Gardnerella* enrichment involves beta-glucuronidase-producing strains specifically.
6. **Metal connections:** No metal measurements were made. Future work integrating metallomic data with these taxonomic findings would strengthen the endometriosis signature.
