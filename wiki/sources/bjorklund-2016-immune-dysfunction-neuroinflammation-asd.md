# Key Claims

- **Immune dysfunction is frequently present** in individuals diagnosed with ASD
- **Alterations in immune cells** including T cells, B cells, monocytes, natural killer cells, and dendritic cells found in ASD
- **Immunoglobulin alterations and increased autoantibodies** reported in ASD individuals
- **Elevated peripheral cytokines and chemokines** found in a significant portion of ASD individuals
- **Immune dysregulation and inflammation** are important components in ASD diagnosis and treatment

# Methods & Findings

**Comprehensive review** examining the hypothesis that immune dysfunction is frequently present in ASD.

- Reviewed four decades of research on altered immune responses in ASD
- Documented alterations across multiple immune cell types
- Assessed autoantibody profiles in ASD
- Examined cytokine and chemokine elevations
- Concluded that immune dysregulation is a consistent feature of ASD

# Relevance to ASD Framework

- **Immune-mediated pathogenesis** as major component of ASD biology
- **Multiple immune compartments affected** suggesting systemic rather than localized dysfunction
- **Autoimmunity** as potential mechanism driving neuroinflammation in ASD
