## Key Findings

- **Gut microbiome-derived metabolites play multifaceted roles in prostate cancer development.** Short-chain fatty acids (SCFAs), cholesterol derivatives (bile acids, sex steroid hormones), folic acid, trimethylamine, and phenylacetylglutamine all contribute to prostate cancer oncogenesis through distinct mechanisms — SCFAs via anti-neoplastic p53/p21 pathways and histone deacetylase inhibition; bile acids via FXR potentiation and tumor suppression; folic acid via genetic stability and unregulated cell growth; trimethylamine via TMAO-associated cancer lethality ([[trecarten-2025-obesity-diet-microbiome-prostate-cancer]], expert-opinion).

- **The prostate tumor microenvironment (TME) is shaped by microbiome-derived signals.** Inflammation (via NF-kB, TLR pathways), local tissue hypoxia (HIF-1α, VEGF upregulation), and epithelial-mesenchymal transition (EMT, via TGF-β, CAFs, TAMs) are three primary TME mechanisms linking the microbiome to prostate cancer progression. Lipopolysaccharide (LPS) from gut dysbiosis can activate NF-kB signaling in prostate cancer cells, and high-fat diet (HFD)-induced gut permeability elevates serum LPS ([[trecarten-2025-obesity-diet-microbiome-prostate-cancer]], expert-opinion).

- **Obesity and the microbiome interact bidirectionally to promote prostate cancer.** Obesity alters the *Firmicutes*/*Bacteroidetes* ratio, reduces *Akkermansia muciniphila* and *Faecalibacterium prausnitzii*, elevates *Firmicutes*, *Fusobacteria*, and *Proteobacteria*, and produces metabolic endotoxemia. Periprostatic white adipose tissue (PPAT) hypoxia drives HIF-1α potentiation, angiogenesis, and inflammatory cytokine release ([[trecarten-2025-obesity-diet-microbiome-prostate-cancer]], expert-opinion).

- **Dietary interventions alter microbiome composition with implications for prostate cancer.** Mediterranean diet increases butyrate and SCFA producers. Low-carbohydrate diet reduces intestinal permeability and zonulin levels. Green tea (EGCG) enriches *Akkermansia* and enhances butyrate production. Curcumin elevates *Bifidobacterium* and *Lactobacillus*. Soy isoflavones are metabolized by gut microbiota into secondary metabolites with anti-neoplastic potential ([[trecarten-2025-obesity-diet-microbiome-prostate-cancer]], expert-opinion).

- **Testosterone and the estrobolome connect microbiome ecology to hormonal prostate cancer drivers.** Men with higher testosterone show increased *Ruminococcus*, *Acinetobacter*, and *Dorea* abundance. *Ruminococcus* can convert pregnenolone and hydroxypregnenolone to downstream androgens. β-glucuronidase-producing bacteria (e.g., *Bacteroides*, *Faecalibacterium*) deconjugate estrogens, increasing circulating free estrogens that activate ERα in prostate tissue ([[trecarten-2025-obesity-diet-microbiome-prostate-cancer]], expert-opinion).

## Methods

**Study design:** Narrative review. No primary data collection. The authors performed a stepwise literature synthesis covering: (1) the role of gut microbiome-derived metabolites in prostate cancer, (2) microbiome interactions with the prostate TME, and (3) the impact of obesity and dietary interventions on the microbiome-prostate cancer axis.

**Scope:** 197 references spanning preclinical (animal model, in vitro) through clinical (cohort, meta-analysis, RCT) evidence. The review integrates findings across urology, immunology, microbiology, and nutritional epidemiology.

## Detailed Summary

### Gut Microbiome-Derived Metabolites

**Short-chain fatty acids (SCFAs):** Butyrate, propionate, and acetate are produced primarily by *Firmicutes* (butyrate) and *Bacteroidetes* (propionate, acetate). Butyrate inhibits histone deacetylases (HDACs), triggering p53/p21 pathways and apoptosis in prostate cancer cells in vivo. SCFA-producing bacteria including *Rikenellaceae*, *Alistipes*, and *Lachnospira* were more abundant in high-grade prostate cancer patients. SCFA supplementation (acetate, butyrate, propionate) to *Pten*-knockout mice enhanced tumor growth via IGF1 potentiation through MAPK and PI3K pathways — illustrating concentration-dependent dual effects.

**Cholesterol derivatives and bile acids:** Primary bile acids (CA, CDCA) are metabolized by gut bacteria into secondary BAs (DCA, LCA). CDCA inhibits prostate cancer cell proliferation through FXR potentiation. LCA promotes apoptosis, autophagy, and mitochondrial dysfunction. UDCA demonstrates death receptor-mediated apoptosis. There is a bidirectional relationship between BAs and microbiome composition.

**Estrogen metabolism:** β-glucuronidase-producing bacteria (*Bacteroides*, *Faecalibacterium*, absent in *Eubacterium*) deconjugate estrogens, increasing free circulating estrogens. ERα activation leads to prostate neoplasia and osteoblastic tumorigenesis. Shotgun sequencing showed increased *Bacteroides* in prostate cancer patients and increased *Eubacterium* and *Faecalibacterium prausnitzii* in benign tissue.

**Testosterone:** 16S rRNA sequencing of 54 patients showed positive correlation (R²=0.33, p=0.014) between *Firmicutes* abundance and serum testosterone. Men with testosterone >455 ng/dL had higher *Ruminococcus*, *Acinetobacter*, *Dorea*, and *Megamonas* (r=0.46, p=0.009 for *Ruminococcus*). *Ruminococcus* can convert pregnenolone to downstream androgens. Patients with CRPC had increased *Ruminococcus* abundance, and *Prevotella*, if present, was associated with worse prognosis.

**Folic acid:** Meta-analysis of 10 studies (202,000 men) found high dietary folate had no significant impact on prostate cancer risk, but high serum folate levels were associated with increased risk (RR=1.21, 95%CI 1.05–1.39, p=0.008). Colonic bacteria produce significant amounts of folic acid. A case-control study (N=6,875 cases, 8,104 controls) found increased odds of prostate cancer in the highest quintile of serum folate (OR 1.13, 95%CI 1.02–1.26, p=0.018). Prostate-specific membrane antigen (PSMA/folate hydrolase 1) is upregulated during androgen deprivation, increasing folate susceptibility.

**Other metabolites:** Elevated TMAO linked to aggressive prostate cancer (TNM stage III–IV, Gleason ≥8, AJCC ≥3; OR 1.36 in ATBC study). Phenylacetylglutamine (PAGIn) associated with prostate cancer lethality via β2 adrenergic receptor signaling.

### Prostate Tumor Microenvironment

**Inflammation:** Prostatitis is associated with elevated PSA. The Prostate Cancer Prevention Trial (PCPT/SELECT) found increasing mean percentage of inflamed tissue increased odds of prostate cancer (0–<1.8%: OR=1.7; 1.8–<5%: OR=2.39; ≥5%: OR=3.31, p=0.047). 16S rRNA sequencing of urinary microbiome identified *Streptococcus* and *Anaerococcus* enriched in prostate cancer. Mendelian randomization (N=1,859 prostatitis, 72,799 controls) identified causal role for *Fecalibacterium*, *LachnospiraceaeUCG004*, *Sutterella*, and *Gastranaerophilales* in prostatitis risk.

**Hypoxia:** Tissue hypoxia drives HIF-1α degradation impairment, angiogenesis, and treatment resistance. Facultative anaerobes (*Salmonella typhimurium*, *Serratia marcescens*) can trigger apoptosis in prostate cancer cells in hypoxic conditions. Oncolytic viruses can selectively target prostate cancer cells through HIF-1α inhibition.

**EMT:** TGF-β drives EMT through polarization of TAMs (M1→M2) and activation of CAFs. CAFs associated with higher Gleason scores and worse recurrence-free survival. TAMs secrete matrix metalloproteinases (MMP-2, MMP-9) enabling basement membrane destruction. *Akkermansia muciniphila* extracts increased tumor-killing M1-type macrophages and reduced M2-type.

**LPS:** High-fat diet induces gut dysbiosis with elevated serum LPS and increased intestinal permeability. LPS activates NF-kB via TLR4 in prostate cancer cells. *Pten*-knockout mice with HFD showed LPS leakage from gut into bloodstream. Antibiotic-induced gut dysbiosis with enriched *Proteobacteria* elevated serum LPS and increased colonic morphology changes, with NF-kB-IL6-STAT3 axis activation demonstrated both in vitro and in vivo.

### Obesity and Diet

**Obesity-microbiome axis:** Systematic review of 32 studies: obesity elevates *Firmicutes*/*Bacteroidetes* ratio, with higher *Firmicutes*, *Fusobacteria*, *Proteobacteria*, *Mollicutes*, *Lactobacillus reuteri*; and reduced *Verrucomicrobia* (*Akkermansia muciniphila*), *Faecalibacterium prausnitzii*, *Bacteroidetes*, *Methanobrevibacter smithii*, *Lactobacillus plantarum* and *paracasei*. HFD increased tumor growth and M2:M1 macrophage ratio with elevated IL-6 and myeloid-derived suppressor cells. Periprostatic WAT hypoxia drives HIF-1α potentiation, angiogenesis, and inflammatory cytokine release.

**Low-carbohydrate diet:** Post-hoc analysis of biochemical recurrence patients showed LCD improved PSA doubling time (PSADT) vs. controls (30 vs. 13 months, p=0.007). LCD associated with reduced zonulin (intestinal permeability marker), significant weight loss, and improved PSADT.

**Mediterranean diet:** Meta-analysis (10 studies, 33,451 patients) found no relationship to prostate cancer risk or lethality. One retrospective study found higher adherence associated with lower risk of progression on active surveillance. Common microbiome theme: increased butyrate and SCFA-producing bacteria, increased *Bifidobacterium*. HEI-2005 and AHEI scores associated with lower prostate cancer risk in men undergoing PSA testing.

**Omega-3 fatty acids:** Mouse models showed supplementation reduced tumor growth, increased *Ruminococcaceae*, and increased fecal butyrate. However, clinical evidence is conflicting — a phase IIb RCT comparing omega-3 supplements to placebo pre-prostatectomy showed no difference in cancer proliferation (Ki-67 expression).

**Green tea (EGCG):** One study (N=26) found daily Polyphenon E (800mg EGCG) reduced hepatocyte growth factor, VEGF, and PSA pre-prostatectomy. Meta-analysis found significantly reduced pooled risk for prostate cancer (risk-ratio=0.41; 95%CI 0.19–0.86) in HGPIN/ASAP patients. EGCG enriched *Akkermansia* and enhanced butyrate production in mouse models.

**Curcumin and ursolic acid:** Curcumin elevated *Bifidobacterium* and *Lactobacillus* in mouse models, reduced *Firmicutes*/*Bacteroides* ratio. Phase 1 trial found combination curcumin (1,200 mg/day) and UA (300 mg daily) safe with no grade 3+ adverse effects. Curcumin (1,440 mg/day) suppressed PSA in patients on intermittent androgen deprivation (one RCT) but showed no significant improvement in castrate-resistant prostate cancer (phase II RCT).

**Soy isoflavones:** Genistein and daidzein metabolized by gut microbiota glucosidases into secondary metabolites (O-DMA, equol). Daidzein→equol conversion occurs in only 20–35% of Western populations vs. 60% of Asian populations. Meta-analysis of 38 human studies found no significant effect on serum testosterone or estrogen levels. One study showed 6-month soy consumption reduced cancer incidence in HGPIN/ASAP patients.

**Folic acid reduction diet:** PSMA upregulation during androgen deprivation increases folate absorption susceptibility. One retrospective study found 94% of men had increased serum folate on androgen deprivation therapy (p=0.04). Folic acid reduction diet significantly reduced serum folate and red blood cell folate at 12 weeks (P=0.03).

## Contradictions / Tensions

- **SCFAs: tumor-suppressive vs. tumor-promoting.** Butyrate induces apoptosis via HDAC inhibition in prostate cancer cells (in vivo), yet SCFA supplementation (acetate, butyrate, propionate) to *Pten*-knockout mice enhanced tumor growth via IGF1/MAPK/PI3K pathways. The review notes this is likely concentration- and context-dependent but does not resolve the contradiction. This conflicts with the generally positive framing of SCFAs on existing [[short-chain-fatty-acids]] pages.
- **Folic acid: protective vs. harmful.** Dietary folate shows no significant prostate cancer risk association (meta-analysis, 202,000 men), but elevated serum folate is associated with increased risk (RR=1.21). The distinction between dietary intake and serum levels is critical and may reflect microbial folate production as a confounding variable.
- **Omega-3 fatty acids: preclinical promise vs. clinical null.** Mouse models show reduced tumor growth with omega-3 supplementation, but a phase IIb RCT found no difference in cancer proliferation. This tension applies broadly to the dietary intervention space in prostate cancer.
- **Mediterranean diet: epidemiological inconsistency.** Meta-analysis of 10 studies found no relationship to prostate cancer risk, but individual retrospective studies show benefit for progression on active surveillance. The Canary Prostate Active Surveillance cohort found no association with grade reclassification.

## Open Questions

1. **What is the dose-response relationship for SCFAs in prostate tissue?** The dual pro- and anti-neoplastic effects suggest a threshold or tissue-specific mechanism that has not been characterized in humans.
2. **Does the urinary/prostatic microbiome contribute independently of the gut microbiome?** The review focuses primarily on gut-derived metabolites but notes prostatic microbiome data is emerging — the relative contribution is unknown.
3. **Can microbiome-targeted dietary interventions improve prostate cancer outcomes in RCTs?** The review notes that dietary interventions to suppress fructose in prostate cancer have not been undertaken, and most dietary evidence remains preclinical or observational.
4. **What is the role of microbial iron and zinc competition in the prostate TME?** The review discusses siderophore competition and metal-dependent enzymes peripherally but does not map the metallomic signature systematically — a gap WikiBiome's framework could fill.
5. **How does the estrobolome interact with androgen deprivation therapy?** β-glucuronidase activity, estrogen recirculation, and the shift in hormonal milieu during ADT may create a unique ecological state that the review does not fully explore.
