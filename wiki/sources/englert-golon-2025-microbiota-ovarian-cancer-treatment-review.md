## Key Findings

- **Microbiota influence all major OC treatment modalities**: Gut, vaginal, and uterine microbial communities modulate responses to chemotherapy (platinum-based), PARP inhibitors, anti-angiogenic therapy, and immunotherapy ([[englert-golon-2025-microbiota-ovarian-cancer-treatment-review|this study]], expert opinion).
- **Favorable microbiota profiles predict better treatment outcomes**: Patients enriched in Lactobacillus and depleted in pro-inflammatory taxa (Fusobacterium nucleatum, certain Escherichia strains) show improved chemotherapy response rates and reduced treatment resistance.
- **Chemotherapy disrupts the microbiome**: Platinum-based chemotherapy causes significant dysbiosis that can promote secondary infections, reduce treatment efficacy through altered drug metabolism, and increase side effects.
- **Microbiota as treatment biomarkers**: Pre-treatment microbiota composition may predict which patients will respond to immunotherapy (checkpoint inhibitors), enabling personalized treatment selection.
- **Multi-compartment microbiome matters**: The review emphasizes that gut, vaginal, and intra-tumoral microbiota all contribute to OC treatment outcomes through distinct mechanisms.

## Methods

- **Study design**: Narrative review synthesizing evidence on microbiota-treatment interactions across all major OC treatment modalities.
- **Treatment modalities covered**: Platinum-based chemotherapy (cisplatin, carboplatin), PARP inhibitors (olaparib, niraparib), anti-angiogenic agents (bevacizumab), immune checkpoint inhibitors (PD-1/PD-L1 antibodies).
- **Microbiome compartments reviewed**: Gut (fecal), vaginal, uterine/endometrial, and intra-tumoral.
- **Limitations**: Expert opinion level. Most evidence is from preclinical studies or small clinical cohorts. ## Detailed Summary

Englert-Golon et al. provide a comprehensive overview of how the microbiome interacts with ovarian cancer treatment across multiple therapeutic modalities.

**Chemotherapy.** Platinum-based agents (the backbone of OC treatment) are affected by gut bacteria in two ways: (1) certain bacteria can metabolize platinum drugs, reducing their bioavailability and efficacy; (2) chemotherapy-induced mucositis and neutropenia create conditions for bacterial translocation and infection. Gut dysbiosis after chemotherapy is characterized by loss of Lactobacillus and Bifidobacterium and expansion of pathogenic Enterobacteriaceae — a pattern that may worsen treatment outcomes.

**PARP inhibitors.** The review discusses emerging evidence that microbiota may influence PARP inhibitor efficacy through effects on DNA repair pathways and inflammatory signaling. Bacteria that produce reactive oxygen species or genotoxic metabolites may create conditions that enhance or impair PARP inhibitor activity. <!-- NEEDS VERIFICATION: whether specific microbiome-PARP inhibitor interaction studies exist in OC -->

**Immunotherapy.** The strongest evidence for microbiome-treatment interaction comes from checkpoint inhibitor studies in melanoma and lung cancer, where specific gut bacteria (particularly Akkermansia muciniphila, Faecalibacterium prausnitzii, and Bifidobacterium species) predict immunotherapy response. The review extrapolates these findings to OC, proposing that pre-treatment microbiome profiling could identify patients likely to benefit from immunotherapy.

**Anti-angiogenic therapy.** Bevacizumab targets VEGF-mediated angiogenesis. The microbiome may influence angiogenic signaling through production of metabolites that affect VEGF expression, hypoxia-inducible factor activation, and endothelial cell function.

The iron connection operates through chemotherapy-induced inflammation and the iron ecology of the tumor microenvironment. Platinum chemotherapy triggers massive cell death, releasing intracellular iron that can fuel pathogenic bacterial growth in the gut (through siderophore-mediated iron acquisition by Enterobacteriaceae) and in the tumor microenvironment. This chemotherapy-iron-microbiome axis may be a treatable driver of treatment resistance.
