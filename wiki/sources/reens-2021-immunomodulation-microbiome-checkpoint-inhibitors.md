# Reens 2021 -- Immunomodulation by Microbiome During Immune Checkpoint Therapy and Vaccination

## Key Claims

- The microbiome significantly influences host response to immune-targeted interventions including cancer immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICIs) and vaccines; overall microbial diversity is a key determinant of ICI responsiveness
- Table 1 compiles clinical associations: *Faecalibacterium prausnitzii*, *Ruminococcus bromii*, and high microbial diversity baseline associated with longer progression-free survival in anti-PD-1 melanoma patients; *Akkermansia muciniphila* associated with enhanced ICI response
- FMT from ICI-responder patients elicits antitumor immunity; *Bifidobacterium* restored ICI response by stimulating antigen presentation by DCs and CTL activation; *B. pseudolongum* promoted ICI response via adenosine A2A receptor stimulation on T cells
- Butyrate supplementation reduced ICI efficacy in preclinical studies by inducing anti-inflammatory Tregs; DCs isolated from butyrate-supplemented mice showed reduced surface expression of costimulatory molecules on APCs
- Table 2 summarizes preclinical ICI studies: antibiotic cocktail exposure decreased ICI efficacy; colonization with *B. fragilis* potentiated anti-CTLA-4 via TLR2/TLR4 host signaling

## Methods/Findings

- **Type**: Review focusing on reciprocal interactions between microbiome, immune system, and outcomes of immune interventions
- **Figure 1**: Cellular players in immune checkpoint inhibitor-mediated tumor killing and vaccine-induced immunity
- **Key insight**: ICIs can also change the microbiome, raising questions about bidirectional effects; anti-PD-1/PD-L1 drugs appear to have minimal impact on microbiome, while anti-CTLA-4 may increase gastrointestinal adverse events
