# The Potential Prebiotic Berberine Combined With Methimazole Improved the Therapeutic Effect of Graves' Disease Patients Through Regulating the Intestinal Microbiome

## Key Findings

- **Berberine + methimazole was superior to methimazole alone**: both groups restored FT3 to healthy levels by 6 months, but only the combined group restored TSH (to 4.2 IU/L healthy threshold) and shifted TRAb closer to healthy threshold (1.75 IU/mL) ([[han-2022-berberine-methimazole-graves-microbiome]], quasi-experimental, n=18)
- **Methimazole alone failed to modulate gut microbiota** (no significant change in Shannon/Simpson diversity or Bray-Curtis distance), while berberine addition significantly altered microbiota structure (Bray-Curtis P=0.013 at 6 months) ([[han-2022-berberine-methimazole-graves-microbiome]], quasi-experimental, n=18)
- **Berberine increased beneficial Lactococcus lactis and Enterococcus hirae** while decreasing pathogenic Enterobacter hormaechei and Chryseobacterium indologenes ([[han-2022-berberine-methimazole-graves-microbiome]], quasi-experimental, n=18)
- **Enterobactin biosynthesis was significantly upregulated by berberine**, which is essential for iron uptake by the host and bacteria; iron is a key micronutrient for thyroid hormone synthesis (Fe-dependent TPO) ([[han-2022-berberine-methimazole-graves-microbiome]], quasi-experimental, n=18)
- **Faecalibacterium prausnitzii and Lactococcus lactis were negatively correlated with FT3, FT4, and TRAb** but positively correlated with TSH, indicating their role in thyroid homeostasis ([[han-2022-berberine-methimazole-graves-microbiome]], quasi-experimental, n=18)

## Methods

- **Study type**: 6-month controlled clinical trial with shotgun metagenomic sequencing
- **Population**: GD patients from Hainan Provincial People's Hospital (2018-2019). MI group (methimazole alone, n=8); Combined group (MI + berberine, n=10)
- **Dosing**: MI group: Methimazole 20mg, 1 tablet once daily. Combined group: MI (same) + Berberine tablets 0.3g per tablet, 3 tablets each time, 3 times daily (=2.7g/day berberine)
- **Sampling**: Serum and stool at baseline, 3 months, and 6 months; thyroid parameters and shotgun metagenomics at each timepoint
- **Sequencing**: Shotgun metagenomic sequencing on Illumina HiSeq 2500; MAGs constructed using MetaBAT; GTDB-TK (V1.40) for taxonomic annotation; HUMAN2 for metabolic pathway analysis
- **Statistics**: Kruskal-Wallis test for thyroid indicators between timepoints; Bray-Curtis distance for microbiota structure; Spearman correlation for strain-pathway-thyroid index networks
- **Ethics**: Reviewed and approved by Hainan Provincial People's Hospital; sequence data deposited in NCBI (PRJNA784925)

## Detailed Summary

### Thyroid Function Outcomes

Both groups significantly reduced FT3 by 6 months (to healthy threshold of 5.7 pmol/L). However, TSH recovery differed markedly: the combined group reached the 4.2 IU/L healthy threshold, while MI alone did not. FT4 decreased in both groups. TRAb approached the 1.75 IU/mL healthy threshold only in the combined group. The differences between the two groups were statistically significant for TSH trajectory.

### Methimazole Alone Effects on Microbiome

MI alone did not significantly change Shannon or Simpson diversity indices at any timepoint. Bray-Curtis distance from baseline was not significantly different at 6 months. Significant metabolic pathway changes at 6 months included L-phenylalanine degradation IV, polymyxin resistance, S-adenosyl-L-methionine cycle I, mannosylglycerate biosynthesis I, and flavin biosynthesis I. At the strain level, some changes occurred (Blautia sp., Faecalimonas nexilis, Erysipelatoclostridium ramosum, Anaerobutyricum hallii) but without significant structural reorganization.

### Berberine + Methimazole Effects on Microbiome

The combined treatment significantly altered Bray-Curtis distance from baseline at 6 months (P=0.013). More metabolic pathways were significantly changed, including: ureide biosynthesis, TCA cycle (VII and IV), superpathways of L-isoleucine biosynthesis, glyoxylate bypass and TCA, L-arginine and L-ornithine degradation, enterobactin biosynthesis, 3-phenylpropanoate and 3-(3-hydroxyphenyl)propanoate degradation. Key strain changes: Blautia wexlerae, Enterococcus hirae, Ruminococcus sp., Faecalibacterium prausnitzii significantly altered; Enterobacter hormaechei and Chryseobacterium indologenes decreased; Lactococcus lactis increased.

### Correlation Network

TSH was positively correlated with Faecalibacterium prausnitzii, Lactococcus lactis, and enterobactin biosynthesis pathways. FT3 and FT4 were negatively correlated with Faecalibacterium prausnitzii. TRAb was negatively correlated with Faecalibacterium prausnitzii and Lactococcus lactis. Vitamin K2 biosynthesis pathways (menaquinol-6, -8, -9 biosynthesis) were correlated with the treatment response. Creatinine degradation was negatively correlated with FT4 and F. prausnitzii.

### Iron-Enterobactin Connection

Enterobactin is a siderophore essential for iron uptake. Iron (Fe) is a key micronutrient for thyroid function: it is required for thyroid peroxidase (TPO) activity, and iron deficiency can cause thyroid dysfunction. The significant upregulation of enterobactin biosynthesis by berberine may improve iron availability, thereby supporting thyroid hormone synthesis and recovery.

## Relevance to Cureva

- **Intervention — validated**: Berberine (2.7g/day) + methimazole combination for GD with evidence of superior thyroid function recovery and microbiome modulation
- **Triangle evidence**: Berberine (I) increases beneficial bacteria and enterobactin biosynthesis (f) in GD (D) with clinical thyroid function improvement
- **Mechanism**: Berberine acts as a prebiotic, not directly antimicrobial, reshaping the gut ecosystem to favor SCFA producers and iron-acquiring commensals
- **STOP consideration**: Methimazole alone may be suboptimal for gut-mediated disease modification

## Contradictions / Tensions

- Very small sample size (n=8 and n=10) limits statistical power and generalizability
- Non-randomized allocation (patients chose or were assigned to groups); potential selection bias
- Berberine dose (2.7g/day) is high and may cause GI side effects not reported here
- The enterobactin-iron-thyroid connection is mechanistically plausible but the causal chain is not directly demonstrated in this study

## Open Questions

- Would a randomized, double-blind trial confirm berberine's superiority over methimazole alone?
- Is the enterobactin upregulation beneficial long-term, or could it favor iron-pirating pathogens?
- What is the optimal berberine dose and duration for GD adjunctive therapy?
- Do the vitamin K2 pathway changes contribute to thyroid function improvement, and by what mechanism?
- How does berberine compare to other prebiotics (e.g., inulin, FOS) for GD microbiome modulation?
