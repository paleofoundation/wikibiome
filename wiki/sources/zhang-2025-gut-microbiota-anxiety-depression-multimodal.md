## Key Findings

- **Depression and anxiety affect approximately 3.1% and 4.8%** of the world's population respectively, making them among the most prevalent neuropsychiatric conditions ([[zhang-2025-gut-microbiota-anxiety-depression-multimodal]], expert-opinion).
- **Gut microbiota disruptions may trigger or exacerbate symptoms** via four interconnected pathways: vagal nerve signaling, HPA axis dysregulation, immune responses (pro-inflammatory cytokines), and SCFA-related metabolic processes.
- **Multimodal interventions** reviewed: probiotics, prebiotics, postbiotics, synbiotics, dietary modifications, FMT, fecal virome transplantation (FVT), and traditional Chinese medicine (TCM) — representing the most comprehensive survey of microbiome-targeted psychiatric interventions to date.
- **Inflammasome activation in depression** can exacerbate neuroinflammation, leading to reduced microbial diversity and gut microbiota imbalance — establishing a bidirectional neuroinflammation-dysbiosis cycle.
- **Mineral cofactors** ([[zinc]], [[iron]], [[copper]], [[magnesium]]) are essential for neurotransmitter synthesis, and their availability is modulated by gut microbiota composition.

## Detailed Summary

This 2025 Frontiers in Microbiology review provides a comprehensive synthesis of the gut-brain axis in depression and anxiety, integrating mechanistic evidence with therapeutic applications. The authors describe four major communication pathways between gut microbiota and the brain: (1) **Neural** — gut bacteria produce neuroactive metabolites (GABA, serotonin precursors, dopamine) that signal via vagal afferents; dysbiosis disrupts this signaling. (2) **Immune** — gut barrier dysfunction from SCFA depletion allows bacterial endotoxin (LPS) translocation, activating the NLRP3 inflammasome and driving neuroinflammation; inflammatory cytokines (IL-1beta, IL-6, TNF-alpha) cross the blood-brain barrier and promote depressive behavior. (3) **Endocrine** — dysbiosis activates the HPA axis through inflammatory signaling, elevating cortisol and creating a stress-dysbiosis feedback loop. (4) **Metabolic** — SCFAs maintain gut barrier integrity and have direct neuroprotective effects; their depletion in depression is both cause and consequence of dysbiosis. The review uniquely covers **mineral cofactors** for neurotransmitter synthesis: **[[zinc]]** (essential for glutamate/GABA balance), **[[iron]]** (dopamine and serotonin synthesis), **[[copper]]** (norepinephrine synthesis via dopamine beta-hydroxylase), and **[[magnesium]]** (NMDA receptor modulation). Gut microbiota influence the bioavailability of these metals through pH modulation, chelation, and competitive absorption. The therapeutic review covers evidence for multiple intervention modalities, with the strongest evidence for specific probiotic strains (Lactobacillus rhamnosus, Bifidobacterium longum) and dietary interventions (Mediterranean diet). Novel approaches including fecal virome transplantation (FVT) — transferring only the viral component of fecal matter — are discussed as emerging options.
