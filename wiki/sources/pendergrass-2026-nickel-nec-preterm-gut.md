# Nickel as a Catalytic Driver of Necrotizing Enterocolitis

> **Note:** This is a preprint by the WikiBiome founder, not independently peer-reviewed journal literature.

## Key Claims

- Dietary nickel from infant formula (especially soy-based) may be a critical but overlooked contributor to necrotizing enterocolitis (NEC) pathogenesis in preterm infants
- Key NEC-associated pathogens (E. coli, Klebsiella, Enterobacter, Citrobacter, Ureaplasma) all rely on [[nickel-dependent enzymes]]: urease, [NiFe]-hydrogenase, and glyoxalase I
- Soy-based formula contains ~10x more nickel than cow's milk formula (0.45 vs 0.03 mg/L) and orders of magnitude more than human breast milk (0.005-0.016 mg/L)
- Human breast milk is naturally nickel-poor, potentially as an evolved mechanism of [[nutritional-immunity|nutritional immunity]] -- starving Ni-dependent pathogens of their essential cofactor
- Excess dietary nickel creates a positive feedback loop: Ni-fueled urease raises gut pH, favoring Proteobacteria over acid-producing commensals like Lactobacillus
- The host's calprotectin and lactoferrin attempt to sequester nickel, but formula feeding may overwhelm this defense, enabling pathogen virulence
- Nickel chelation therapy (e.g., aspergillomarasmine A) represents a potential anti-virulence strategy that disarms pathogens without killing them

## Methods/Findings

- **Study type**: Hypothesis-driven synthesis integrating microbiome ecology, enzymatic biochemistry, and infant nutrition data
- **Table 1**: Comprehensive mapping of NEC-enriched taxa to their nickel-dependent enzyme profiles (urease, glyoxalase, [NiFe]-hydrogenase status for each pathogen)
- **Urease function**: Generates ammonia from urea, raises local pH, damages intestinal epithelium, promotes bacterial translocation across mucosal barrier
- **[NiFe]-hydrogenase function**: Enables bacteria to use H2 as energy source in anaerobic environments, providing competitive advantage in the inflamed preterm gut
- **Glyoxalase I function**: Ni-activated enzyme detoxifying methylglyoxal (a toxic glycolysis byproduct), helping pathogens survive under stress; E. coli GlxI specifically requires Ni2+
- **Formula vs. breast milk**: Formula provides the exact metal cofactor that pathogenic gut bacteria require; breast milk's protective effect may partly derive from nickel limitation
- **Proposed biomarkers**: Fecal urease activity, ammonia levels, and stool nickel content could serve as early NEC risk indicators
