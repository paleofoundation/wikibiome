# Oxidative Stress in Parkinson's Disease: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis

## Summary

This meta-analysis quantifies oxidative stress markers in [[parkinsons-disease]] by aggregating data from multiple studies measuring lipid peroxidation products, antioxidant enzyme activities, and DNA damage markers in PD patients versus controls.

## Key Findings

- Malondialdehyde (MDA, lipid peroxidation marker) is significantly elevated in PD blood
- 8-hydroxydeoxyguanosine (8-OHdG, DNA oxidation marker) is elevated in PD
- Superoxide dismutase (SOD) activity shows mixed results across studies
- Glutathione levels are consistently reduced in PD, particularly in the substantia nigra
- Glutathione peroxidase (GPX) activity is decreased in PD
- Catalase activity shows variable results
- The magnitude of oxidative stress markers correlates with disease severity

## Relevance to Gut-Metal-Microbiome Axis

This meta-analysis provides quantitative confirmation that [[ferroptosis]]-relevant oxidative damage is elevated in [[parkinsons-disease]]. The elevated MDA (lipid peroxidation) and reduced glutathione/GPX are hallmarks of [[ferroptosis]] -- the [[iron]]-dependent cell death pathway. The depleted glutathione in PD removes the substrate for GPX4, the master regulator of anti-ferroptotic defense. [[Iron]] and [[manganese]] catalyze the Fenton reactions that generate the ROS responsible for MDA elevation and 8-OHdG formation. The gut-metal-microbiome axis contributes to this oxidative stress burden through: (1) [[dysbiosis]]-driven reduction in [[butyrate]], which normally upregulates antioxidant enzymes; (2) increased intestinal [[iron]] absorption from loss of HIF-2alpha regulation; (3) translocation of metal-laden gut bacteria and LPS through a leaky gut. This meta-analysis quantifies the downstream consequences of the gut-metal-microbiome disruption in PD.

## Limitations

- Heterogeneity in oxidative stress measurement methods across studies
- Blood markers may not reflect brain oxidative stress levels
- Medication effects on oxidative stress markers not fully controlled
- Some included studies had small sample sizes
