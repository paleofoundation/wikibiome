# Stewart 2013 — Preterm Gut Microbiome Development in Twins at Risk of NEC and Sepsis

## Key Findings

- **Related twins shared significantly more similar gut microbiomes** than unrelated preterm infants (PLS-DA, weighted UniFrac clustering), demonstrating that host genetic or maternal environmental factors shape the preterm gut microbiome even in the highly controlled NICU setting ([[stewart-2013-preterm-gut-microbiome-twins-nec-sepsis]], prospective cohort, n=27)
- **Escherichia sp. dominance began increasing ≥5 days before NEC diagnosis** in the NEC-affected twin (Twin 139), with a corresponding diversity decrease — but not in the healthy co-twin (Twin 140) despite identical NICU care, feeding, and antibiotic exposure ([[stewart-2013-preterm-gut-microbiome-twins-nec-sepsis]])
- **Post-antibiotic treatment for NEC**: Klebsiella sp. bloom emerged alongside Parascardovia (Bifidobacteriaceae) — suggesting antibiotic pressure selects for resistant Enterobacteriaceae and paradoxically also promotes some Bifidobacteriaceae ([[stewart-2013-preterm-gut-microbiome-twins-nec-sepsis]])
- **Skin flora confirmed in gut**: Staphylococcus, Corynebacterium, and Propionibacterium — classic skin commensals — were detected in stool samples, confirming **skin contact as a gut colonization route** in NICU infants ([[stewart-2013-preterm-gut-microbiome-twins-nec-sepsis]])
- **Core preterm NICU microbiome**: Enterobacteriaceae, Enterococcaceae, and Staphylococcaceae dominated across all infants, contrasting with the Bifidobacterium-dominant core of healthy full-term infants ([[stewart-2013-preterm-gut-microbiome-twins-nec-sepsis]])
- **Expressed breast milk (EBM) microbiome** clustered with the respective infant's stool microbiome — maternal milk as a dynamic microbiome-seeding vector ([[stewart-2013-preterm-gut-microbiome-twins-nec-sepsis]])

## Methods

**Design:** Longitudinal prospective cohort. Serial stool sampling from admission to discharge or 35 weeks corrected gestational age.

**Population:** 27 preterm infants <32 weeks gestational age from 12 twin pairs and 1 triplet set; Royal Victoria Infirmary NICU, Newcastle upon Tyne, April 2010–February 2012. 5 developed definite NEC (Bell's criteria), 5 developed late-onset sepsis.

**Samples:** 173 stool samples + 18 expressed breast milk samples from 4 mothers.

**Methods:** PCR-DGGE (Denaturing Gradient Gel Electrophoresis) for all samples; 454 pyrosequencing for a representative subset to achieve species-level resolution.

**Key limitation:** Subset pyrosequencing means some samples are DGGE-only (band-pattern level resolution). Twin concordance analysis is limited to 12 pairs — sample size for genetic effect is modest. PCR-DGGE is now considered lower-resolution than modern short-read or long-read sequencing.

## Detailed Summary

This study's central contribution is the **within-twin comparison design**, which provides a near-perfect natural experiment: co-twins share gestational age, delivery mode, feeding regimen, antibiotic exposure, and NICU physical environment. Microbiome differences between co-twins therefore cannot be attributed to these environmental variables — they reflect host genetic or in-utero microbiome seeding differences.

**The NEC prediction finding** is clinically significant: Escherichia sp. dominance increasing ≥5 days before clinical signs appeared in Twin 139, but not in Twin 140 who received identical care. This establishes that the NEC-preceding Enterobacteriaceae bloom is detectable before clinical diagnosis in individual infants — not just in population-level studies — and that a healthy co-twin can maintain microbiome stability under the same conditions that permit Escherichia expansion in the affected twin.

The **post-antibiotic Klebsiella bloom** is a recurring pattern across NEC microbiome studies: antibiotics targeting Escherichia (aminoglycosides, ampicillin) do not eliminate Klebsiella, which is intrinsically resistant to many first-line NICU antibiotic regimens. This antibiotic-driven succession from Escherichia to Klebsiella is clinically important because Klebsiella carries additional virulence mechanisms (hypermucoviscous capsule, siderophores) and is associated with late-onset NEC and NICU outbreaks.

**Skin colonization of the gut** via caregiving contact is confirmed by the presence of classic skin commensals (Staphylococcus, Corynebacterium, Propionibacterium) in infant stool — a finding with implications for NICU hand hygiene, kangaroo care, and the role of skin-to-skin contact in microbiome seeding.

**EBM microbiome clustering** with infant stool (per respective mother-infant pair) reinforces that breast milk is not just a nutrient and immunological vehicle but an active microbiome seeding intervention — with each mother providing a personalized microbial inoculum.

## Relevance to Cureva

- **STOP signal**: Antibiotic treatment of NEC may eliminate Escherichia but promote Klebsiella bloom — a potentially more resistant and dangerous successor. Current antibiotic selection for NEC should consider this succession risk
- **NEC prediction opportunity**: Serial microbiome monitoring in NICU twin pairs could detect pre-NEC Escherichia bloom 5+ days before clinical signs — supports a prospective surveillance protocol
- **Skin-to-gut colonization route**: NICU protocols (gloves, kangaroo care guidance) affect microbiome seeding — potentially modifiable

## Contradictions / Tensions

- Twin 140 (healthy co-twin) maintained microbiome stability despite identical NEC risk factors as Twin 139 — implies a host-intrinsic (genetic or epigenetic) component to NEC susceptibility that is not captured by microbiome surveillance alone
- Klebsiella bloom post-NEC antibiotic treatment conflicts with the expectation that antibiotic treatment resolves dysbiosis; it instead replaces one pathobiont with another

## Open Questions

- What host-genetic factors in Twin 139 permitted Escherichia dominance when Twin 140 resisted it?
- Is the Escherichia-to-Klebsiella succession a universal antibiotic side effect in NEC or dependent on specific antibiotic regimen?
- Does expressed breast milk microbiome composition predict which infants will have protective Bifidobacterium colonization vs Enterobacteriaceae dominance?
