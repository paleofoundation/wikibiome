# Overview

First meta-analysis examining **p-cresol** (a bacterial-derived aromatic metabolite) as a potential biomarker for ASD. Systematic review of 15 articles; 6 used for meta-analysis. Urinary p-cresol levels were **significantly higher** in ASD children, though fecal levels showed no significant differences.

# Key Findings

## Urinary p-Cresol

- Meta-analysis of 3 urine studies: pooled effect **p = 0.46** (moderate) with **low heterogeneity** (I-squared = 0.00%)
- Urinary total p-cresol: ASD 98.8 +/- 17.3 ug/mL vs controls 52.0 +/- 7.8 ug/mL
- p-cresol sulfate (PCS), p-cresol glucuronide (PCG), and free p-cresol all elevated in ASD
- Levels up to **40% higher** in ASD population, especially in children aged **2-7 years** (critical neurodevelopmental period)

## Fecal p-Cresol

- Subgroup meta-analysis: pooled effect 0.20 (CI -0.16 to 0.57), p = 0.471 -- **not significant**
- Higher fecal levels reported in some studies but inconsistent across populations

## Biological Significance

- p-Cresol produced by *Clostridioides* bacteria via tyrosine fermentation
- Inhibits dopamine beta-hydroxylase, blocking conversion of dopamine to norepinephrine
- Can cross the **blood-brain barrier** -- relevant to CNS effects
- At least 55 bacterial species identified as p-cresol producers, including significantly elevated *Clostridioides* in ASD
- Possible link to motor stereotypies, delayed walking, and compulsive/repetitive behaviors

## Limitations

- Low number of included studies with varied detection methods
- Confounders: sex, genetics, dietary factors, medication, lifestyle, and bacterial species composition
- Need for standardization of detection methods and normality limits
