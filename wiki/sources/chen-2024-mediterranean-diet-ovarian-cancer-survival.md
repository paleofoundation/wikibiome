# Mediterranean Diet and Ovarian Cancer Survival

## Key Findings

- **High Alternate Mediterranean Diet (AMED) score associated with 41% reduced mortality** (HR=0.59, 95% CI 0.38-0.90) in ovarian cancer patients ([[chen-2024-mediterranean-diet-ovarian-cancer-survival]], prospective-cohort).
- **Decreased AMED intake from pre- to post-diagnosis** linked to significantly worse overall survival, suggesting that dietary deterioration after diagnosis is an independent risk factor.
- **Maintaining consistently high AMED intake** through diagnosis and treatment associated with the best survival outcomes — dietary consistency matters, not just baseline diet.

## Methods

Prospective cohort study using data from a large-scale cancer epidemiology consortium. AMED score calculated from food frequency questionnaires assessing intake of fruits, vegetables, whole grains, legumes, nuts, fish, monounsaturated-to-saturated fat ratio, red and processed meat, and moderate alcohol consumption. Both pre-diagnosis and post-diagnosis dietary data collected, enabling analysis of dietary change trajectories. Overall survival (OS) as primary endpoint. Cox proportional hazards regression adjusted for age, stage, histological subtype, BMI, and treatment received. <!-- NEEDS VERIFICATION: exact cohort name, total n, and follow-up duration -->

## Detailed Summary

Chen et al. examine whether adherence to the Mediterranean dietary pattern — measured by the Alternate Mediterranean Diet (AMED) score — influences survival after ovarian cancer diagnosis.

**Pre-diagnosis diet and survival.** Women with the highest AMED scores before their ovarian cancer diagnosis had significantly better overall survival than those with the lowest scores. The hazard ratio of 0.59 (95% CI 0.38-0.90) indicates a 41% reduction in mortality risk. This is a clinically meaningful effect size, comparable to some pharmacological interventions.

**Dietary trajectory matters.** The most novel aspect of this study is the analysis of dietary change from pre- to post-diagnosis. Women who decreased their AMED intake after diagnosis had worse survival than those who maintained or increased it. This suggests that the common post-diagnosis dietary deterioration (due to treatment side effects, psychological stress, appetite changes) may itself contribute to worse outcomes — not merely reflect sicker patients eating less.

**Consistency as the key.** The best survival outcomes were observed in women who maintained consistently high AMED intake through diagnosis and treatment. This finding has intervention implications: supporting dietary quality during cancer treatment may be as important as pre-treatment dietary patterns.

**Potential mechanisms.** While this study does not directly investigate mechanisms, several pathways connect Mediterranean diet to cancer outcomes through the WikiBiome framework: (1) Mediterranean diet promotes microbiome diversity and SCFA-producing bacteria, potentially counteracting cancer-associated [[dysbiosis]] (see [[alhilli-2025-dietary-fat-gut-microbiome-ovarian-cancer-mouse]] for contrasting high-fat diet effects); (2) high antioxidant and polyphenol intake may modulate oxidative stress and metal-mediated damage; (3) anti-inflammatory dietary components (omega-3 fatty acids, olive oil polyphenols) may reduce the chronic inflammation that supports tumor progression; (4) lower intake of processed and red meat reduces exposure to heme iron, which is associated with oxidative damage and cancer risk.

## Open Questions

- Which specific components of the Mediterranean diet drive the survival benefit — is it the anti-inflammatory fats, the fiber/prebiotic content, the reduced processed meat, or the overall pattern?
- Does the Mediterranean diet's microbiome-modulating effect mediate the survival benefit, or are systemic metabolic/inflammatory pathways more important?
- How does the AMED survival benefit interact with ovarian cancer histological subtype and molecular classification?
- Can a dietary intervention trial demonstrate causal benefit, or is this association confounded by overall health-seeking behavior?
