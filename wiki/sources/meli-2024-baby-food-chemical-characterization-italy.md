## Key Findings

- Among toxic elements, **antimony (Sb) and mercury (Hg) were detectable in 100% of samples**; strontium in 96%, tin in 68%, aluminum in 36%, nickel in 16%, arsenic in 12%. Lead, cadmium, thallium, barium, tellurium, thorium, and uranium were below LOD in all samples.
- Estimated dietary intakes never exceeded 86% of the Provisional Tolerable Weekly Intake (PTWI) for any toxic element.
- Essential elements (Ca, K, Fe, Mg, Mn, P, S, Si, Zn) detected in 68-100% of samples. Commercially available baby food provided ~70% of infant adequate intake (AI) for essential elements.
- Method: EPA 3050B digestion + ICP-AES, ICP-MS (for trace/toxic), and AAS for Hg (EPA 7470). LOD for Hg = 0.0001 mg/kg; LOD for Cd = 0.005 mg/kg.

## Methods

25 baby foods from Italian/European producers: 9 powdered milks, 13 homogenized products (meat, fish, cheese, fruit), 3 cereal creams. Sample dissolution followed EPA 3050B 1996; multi-element analysis by ICP-AES/ICP-MS; Hg by thermal decomposition AAS. Daily intake calculated using EFSA reference body weight (8.6 kg for 7-11 month infant).

## Relevance to Cureva

- Baseline data for estimating infant/toddler metal burden from commercial baby food in European populations.
- Contrast with U.S. baby food data (Jackson 2012, Bradman etc.) which report higher detection rates for heavy metals.

## Contradictions / Tensions

- Finding that Pb, Cd are below detection contrasts with U.S./Asian studies showing frequent Cd and Pb detection in baby foods. Possible reasons: lower LODs elsewhere; stricter EU sourcing; small sample of 25 products.

## Open Questions

- Whether cumulative exposure from multiple food sources (including water, air) exceeds safety limits even when individual foods are compliant.
- Sb detection in 100% of samples — source (packaging, ingredients) not identified.
