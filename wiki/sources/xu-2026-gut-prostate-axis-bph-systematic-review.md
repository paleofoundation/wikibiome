## Key Findings

- **Elevated Firmicutes/Bacteroidetes (F/B) ratio** is the most consistent microbial marker of BPH across both clinical and preclinical evidence, confirmed in 5 preclinical and 3 clinical studies ([[xu-2026-gut-prostate-axis-bph-systematic-review]], systematic review).
- **Prevotella abundance is significantly increased** in BPH patients and animal models, proposed as a specific gut biomarker for BPH risk or progression ([[xu-2026-gut-prostate-axis-bph-systematic-review]], systematic review).
- **Ruminococcus enrichment** is consistently observed in BPH, with potential roles in hormone metabolism regulation ([[xu-2026-gut-prostate-axis-bph-systematic-review]], systematic review).
- **Lactobacillus is consistently depleted** in BPH across animal and clinical studies, with its reduction linked to loss of anti-inflammatory and barrier-protective functions ([[xu-2026-gut-prostate-axis-bph-systematic-review]], systematic review).
- **Four mechanistic pathways** link gut dysbiosis to BPH pathogenesis: (1) promotion of growth factors through hormone metabolism, (2) upregulation of 5-alpha reductase via NADPH increase, (3) ghrelin-mediated suppression of apoptosis through F/B ratio elevation, and (4) DNA damage and epithelial-mesenchymal transition via NF-kB activation ([[xu-2026-gut-prostate-axis-bph-systematic-review]], systematic review).

## Methods

- **Study design:** Systematic review following PRISMA guidelines, preregistered on PROSPERO (CRD420250632333).
- **Databases searched:** PubMed, MEDLINE, Web of Science from establishment through October 7, 2025.
- **Search terms:** "Gastrointestinal Microbiome" OR "Gastrointestinal Microbiomes" AND "Prostatic Hyperplasia" OR "Hyperplasia, Prostatic."
- **Inclusion criteria:** Clinical/preclinical original studies on gut microbiota and BPH composition/correlation/intervention. Excluded reviews, Mendelian randomization studies, and non-English articles.
- **Screening:** 128 records identified, 79 duplicates removed, 49 screened, 34 excluded (12 reviews, 22 irrelevant), 15 studies included in qualitative synthesis (10 preclinical, 6 clinical; one study contributed to both categories).
- **Quality assessment:** Newcastle-Ottawa scale for clinical studies — two scored 8/6, three scored 6/6, one scored 5/6 (generally high quality).
- **Populations:** 413 BPH patients and 338 healthy controls across 6 clinical studies from Poland, Taiwan, Japan, and China. Five different BPH mouse models used in preclinical studies.

## Detailed Summary

### Microbiota Diversity

Beta-diversity showed significant differences between BPH and controls (5 preclinical, 3 clinical studies). Alpha-diversity findings were inconsistent — one clinical study found reduced alpha-diversity, but preclinical studies showed divergent results (decreased, unchanged, or increased). The authors attribute this to community composition shifts (PCoA) rather than within-individual diversity reductions.

### Core Microbial Changes

**Phylum level:** Increased F/B ratio consistently observed. This is regarded as a pathological marker of BPH.

**Genus level — consistently enriched in BPH:**
- **[[prevotella]]** — enriched in both animal and clinical studies. Involved in glycolysis and gluconeogenesis. Its Pcopi species may upregulate 5-alpha reductase activity via NADPH production, promoting testosterone-to-DHT conversion.
- **[[ruminococcus]]** — enriched in animal and clinical studies. May regulate hormone metabolism affecting BPH progression.
- **[[escherichia-coli]]** — can trigger DNA damage and EMT process in prostate epithelial cells by activating NF-kB signaling.

**Genus level — consistently depleted in BPH:**
- **[[lactobacillus]]** — depleted across multiple animal and clinical studies. Associated with inhibiting prostate apoptosis when absent. Supplementation with specific probiotic strains (Bifidobacterium longum BL-G1, Bifidobacterium psychraerophilum Q5) can reduce pro-inflammatory cytokines (TNF-alpha, IL-1, IL-6) and increase anti-inflammatory IL-10.

**Other notable taxa:**
- **[[akkermansia-muciniphila]]** — may modulate the gut microbiota-prostate axis and metabolic syndrome. Shown to decrease pro-inflammatory cytokines.
- **[[bacteroides]]** — part of the BPH-associated enterotype dominated by Blautia, Bacteroides, and Streptococcus.
- Enterococcus, Streptococcus, Clostridium, and Turicibacter also reported in some studies.

### Mechanistic Pathways (Fig. 2)

The review identifies four key pathways linking gut dysbiosis to BPH:

1. **Hormone metabolism:** [[lactobacillus]] and [[ruminococcus]] influence BPH through growth factor promotion via hormone metabolism. Prevotella's Pcopi species promotes testosterone conversion.
2. **NADPH/5-AR pathway:** Prevotella Pcopi upregulates 5-alpha reductase (5-AR) activity by increasing NADPH, driving DHT production and BPH progression.
3. **Ghrelin/apoptosis:** Elevated F/B ratio increases ghrelin levels in prostate tissue, activating JAK2/STAT3 pathway and suppressing BAX/Caspase-3 apoptosis while upregulating Cyclin D1 — promoting proliferation.
4. **DNA damage/EMT:** Specific microbiota (particularly [[escherichia-coli]]) trigger DNA damage and epithelial-mesenchymal transition in prostate epithelial cells via NF-kB signal transduction.

### Inflammatory Mediators

- **IL-6** is elevated in BPH patients. Negatively correlated with hexanoate and butyrate, positively correlated with acetate.
- **IL-18** exhibits moderate positive correlation with isocaproate.
- Alterations in short-chain fatty acid (SCFA) profiles mediate the microbiota's effect on prostate inflammation.
- Intestinal barrier dysfunction (tight junction proteins ZO-1 and claudin-1) contributes to systemic LPS translocation and prostate inflammation.

### Metabolic Pathways (KEGG Analysis)

Significantly enriched pathways in BPH models: (1) steroid hormone biosynthesis, (2) unsaturated fatty acid biosynthesis, (3) pyrimidine metabolism, (4) NLR signaling, (5) estrogen signaling, and (6) PI3K-Akt signaling.

### Therapeutic Prospects

- **Dietary patterns:** Mediterranean diet (increased fruits, vegetables, reduced red meat) demonstrates potential ameliorative effects.
- **Probiotics:** Bifidobacterium and [[akkermansia-muciniphila]] supplementation shows promise in modulating intestinal tight junction proteins and metabolic parameters.
- **Natural compounds:** EGCG (green tea polyphenol) alleviates BPH progression by inhibiting IGF-1/PI3K/AKT pathway. Grape polyphenols reduce TNF-alpha, IL-6, and LPS after gut bacteria metabolism.
- **FMT:** Fecal microbiota transplantation has demonstrated potential in animal studies to remotely influence prostate remodeling.
- **SCFAs:** Butyrate and acetate can inhibit inflammation in mesangial cells induced by high glucose and LPS, potentially reducing BPH occurrence.

## Contradictions / Tensions

- **Alpha-diversity inconsistency:** Clinical and preclinical studies disagree on alpha-diversity changes in BPH. One clinical study shows significant reduction; preclinical studies show variable results. The authors resolve this by arguing that community composition changes (beta-diversity) are more informative than alpha-diversity indices.
- **Lactobacillus directionality:** While the majority of studies show Lactobacillus depletion in BPH, one study reported upregulation of Lactobacillus in the BPH group, contradicting the prevailing pattern. The review notes this but does not fully resolve it.
- **Methodological heterogeneity:** Studies used different sequencing technologies (16S rRNA, gas chromatography, NGS, metagenome sequencing) and different BPH induction methods in animal models (testosterone, high-fat diet, testosterone propionate), making direct comparisons challenging.
- **Cross-sectional limitation:** All clinical evidence is cross-sectional, preventing causal inference. The review acknowledges this explicitly but frames the mechanistic pathways from animal models as supporting causality.

## Open Questions

1. **Metal involvement unclear:** The review does not address heavy metal exposure or metallomic signatures in BPH. Given that Prevotella and E. coli have known metal dependencies, what is the metallomic signature of BPH? Do zinc, iron, or nickel levels in prostate tissue correlate with the gut dysbiosis pattern?
2. **Causal direction:** Does gut dysbiosis cause BPH, or does BPH (via hormonal changes) alter the gut microbiome? Prospective cohort studies and Mendelian randomization (excluded from this review) are needed.
3. **Strain-level specificity:** Which specific Prevotella, Ruminococcus, and Lactobacillus species drive the BPH association? The review operates mostly at genus level.
4. **SCFA dose-response:** What SCFA concentrations are therapeutic vs. pathogenic in the prostate context? The butyrate/acetate anti-inflammatory evidence is from in vitro mesangial cell models, not prostate tissue.
5. **Biofilm role:** Is there biofilm formation in BPH-associated gut dysbiosis? The review does not address this ecological feature despite its relevance to other WikiBiome signatures.
6. **Fungal involvement:** The review is bacteria-focused. Are there interkingdom (fungal-bacterial) interactions in the gut-prostate axis?
