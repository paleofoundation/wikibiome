# Elevated Levels of Circulating DNA in Cardiovascular Disease Patients: Metagenomic Profiling of Microbiome in the Circulation

## Summary

This study quantified circulating cell-free DNA (circDNA) and profiled the circulating microbiome by deep shotgun sequencing in 80 CVD patients (24 valvular heart disease, 30 ischemic heart disease, 26 congenital heart disease) versus 40 healthy controls from Tamil Nadu, India.

## Key Findings

### Circulating DNA Levels
- circDNA was significantly higher in all CVD patient groups compared to controls (p<0.0001)
- 16S rRNA and beta-globin gene copy numbers were significantly higher in CVD patients
- AUROC for circDNA discriminating CVD patients: 0.925-0.989 depending on subtype
- circDNA, 16S rRNA, and beta-globin levels were statistically dependent on cardiovascular status

### Circulating Microbiome Composition
- CVD patients had higher bacterial diversity than controls
- At phylum level: Actinobacteria dominated in CVD samples; Proteobacteria dominated in controls
- Actinobacteria:Proteobacteria ratio significantly increased in CVD (p=0.035)
- Proteobacteria:Actinobacteria ratio significantly increased in controls (p=0.028)
- Family Propionibacteriaceae significantly increased in CVD with parallel reduction in Pseudomonadaceae

### Virome Differences
- Higher proportion of archaeal DNA in CVD (0.14%) vs controls (0.01%)
- Lower fraction of viral DNA in CVD (2.2%) vs controls (6.7%)
- CVD virome enriched with *Propionibacterium* phages and *Rhizobium* phages
- Controls enriched with *Pseudomonas* phages and eukaryotic viruses (Lymphocystis virus, Torque Teno viruses)

## Relevance to Gut-Metal-Microbiome Axis

This study contributes to the [[gut-metal-microbiome]] model by:

1. **Bacterial translocation biomarker** - Elevated circDNA and 16S rRNA in blood provides measurable evidence of increased bacterial translocation in CVD, a process that may be exacerbated by [[lead]] and [[cadmium]] exposure through intestinal barrier disruption
2. **Circulating [[dysbiosis]]** - The shift from Proteobacteria to Actinobacteria dominance in CVD blood suggests a disease-specific blood microbiome signature
3. **[[inflammation]] link** - Increased Actinobacteria and decreased Proteobacteria in CVD circulation may reflect differential bacterial translocation through a compromised gut barrier, directly relevant to systemic [[inflammation]]
