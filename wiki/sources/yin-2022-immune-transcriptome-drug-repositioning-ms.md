# Immune Cells Transcriptome-Based Drug Repositioning for Multiple Sclerosis

## Summary

This bioinformatics study uses GEO transcriptome data from [[multiple-sclerosis]] immune cells to identify candidate drugs for repositioning. It analyzes differentially expressed genes (DEGs) in CD4+ T cells (Fingolimod-treated), plasmacytoid dendritic cells, and PBMCs (IFN-beta-treated), then identifies hub target genes and candidate drugs through CMap, Drugbank, and KEGG databases.

## Key Findings

- **50 hub target genes** for CD4+ T cells, 15 for pDCs, 7 for PBMCs
- **6 candidate drugs** targeting 2+ hub targets: Fostamatinib, Copper, Artenimol, Phenethyl isothiocyanate, Aspirin, and Zinc
- Target pathways primarily belong to immune system and viral infectious disease pathways
- Drugs targeting both "PI3K-Akt signaling pathway" and "Chemokine signaling pathway" (e.g., Nemiralisiband Umbralisib) or tyrosine kinase inhibitors (e.g., Fostamatinib) may be potential MS therapies
- **69 candidate drugs** targeting 2+ pathways were identified

## Notable Candidates

- **Copper** and **Zinc** identified as hub target compounds -- directly relevant to metal homeostasis
- **Aspirin** (an NSAID) identified as targeting MS immune cell pathways
- **Fostamatinib** (SYK inhibitor) -- already approved for immune thrombocytopenia

## Relevance to Gut-Metal-Microbiome Axis

The identification of copper and zinc as candidate repositioning agents for MS is striking in the context of the [[gut-metal-microbiome]] axis. Both metals are essential trace elements whose homeostasis is regulated by the [[gut-microbiome]], and their dysregulation by [[heavy-metals]] exposure could drive immune dysfunction in MS. The finding that metal compounds can modulate MS-relevant immune pathways supports the hypothesis that [[heavy-metals]]-induced disruption of trace metal balance contributes to MS pathogenesis. [[Aspirin]]'s identification reinforces the [[NSAIDs]]-microbiome connection.

## Limitations

- Purely computational study without experimental validation
- Limited to transcriptomic data; proteome-level validation only partial
- Drug efficacy predictions need clinical testing
