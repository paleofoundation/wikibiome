# Beneficial Effects of a Low-Nickel Diet on Relapsing IBS-Like and Extraintestinal Symptoms of Celiac Patients during a Proper Gluten-Free Diet

Borghini et al. (2020). *Nutrients* 12:2277.

## Overview

A pilot study demonstrating that celiac disease patients on a gluten-free diet (GFD) experience symptom relapse attributable to increased [[dietary-nickel-exposure]] from gluten-free substitute foods, and that adding a low-nickel diet (LNiD) to the GFD significantly improves both gastrointestinal and extraintestinal symptoms. The study highlights the overlap between high-nickel foods and common gluten-free staples.

## Key Findings

### Nickel Allergic Contact Mucositis (Ni ACM)

- Nickel allergic contact mucositis is a condition where dietary [[nickel]] triggers IBS-like gastrointestinal symptoms in predisposed individuals through a local allergic response in the gut mucosa
- Estimated prevalence of Ni ACM exceeds 30% in the general population, based on epicutaneous patch test positivity rates
- Diagnosis uses the oral mucosa patch test (omPT), which applies nickel sulfate directly to the oral mucosa

### Study Design

- 102 consecutive celiac disease patients on GFD were initially screened
- 20 patients were ultimately enrolled (all female), selected based on persistent or relapsing symptoms despite proper GFD adherence
- All 20 patients tested positive on the Ni oral mucosa patch test, indicating 100% Ni ACM prevalence in this symptomatic subgroup
- Three time points assessed:
 - **T1:** Initial celiac diagnosis (before GFD)
 - **T2:** After prolonged GFD (symptom relapse period)
 - **T3:** After adding low-nickel diet to the GFD

### Paradoxical Symptom Relapse on Gluten-Free Diet

- A critical and counterintuitive finding: prolonged adherence to GFD caused symptom relapse rather than sustained improvement
- From T1 to T2: 83.4% of tracked symptoms worsened, with 41.7% reaching statistical significance
- The authors attribute this relapse to the increased nickel content of gluten-free substitute foods
- Patients replacing wheat with corn, rice, buckwheat, and legume-based products were inadvertently increasing their daily nickel intake substantially

### Improvement with Low-Nickel Diet

- Adding a low-nickel diet to the GFD (T2 to T3) improved 83.4% of tracked symptoms, with 41.7% reaching statistical significance
- Both gastrointestinal and extraintestinal symptoms responded:
 - **GI symptoms improved:** Abdominal pain, bloating, nausea, loose stools
 - **Extraintestinal symptoms improved:** Dermatitis, headache, chronic fatigue, muscle and joint pain
- The degree of improvement on LNiD roughly mirrored the degree of worsening that had occurred during prolonged GFD

### High-Nickel and Gluten-Free Food Overlap

The paper presents a Venn diagram showing substantial overlap between foods that are high in nickel and foods commonly used in gluten-free diets. Key overlapping items include:

- Corn and corn-based products (a primary GF staple)
- Chickpeas, lentils, beans, and other legumes
- Buckwheat
- Certain nuts and seeds
- Chocolate and cocoa products

This overlap creates a particular vulnerability for celiac patients who must avoid gluten: the foods they rely on as substitutes are often the same foods that deliver high nickel doses to the gut.

### Nickel as a Metalloestrogen

- The paper notes that [[nickel]] can act as a metalloestrogen, binding to estrogen receptors and mimicking estrogenic activity
- This property may explain the strong female predominance observed in both Ni ACM and [[nickel-allergy]] more broadly
- All 20 enrolled patients in this study were female, consistent with the known sex disparity

## Clinical Significance

This study has practical implications for managing celiac patients with persistent symptoms despite strict GFD adherence. It suggests that:

1. Ni ACM should be considered in the differential diagnosis when celiac patients experience symptom relapse on GFD
2. Oral mucosa patch testing for nickel sensitivity may be warranted
3. A combined GFD + low-nickel diet can provide significant relief
4. Clinicians should be aware that gluten-free substitute foods may introduce new dietary triggers
