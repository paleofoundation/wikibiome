# Why This Paper Matters

This review provides the most comprehensive synthesis of how [[iron]], [[zinc]], [[manganese]], and [[copper]] shape the three-way competition between the vertebrate host, enteric pathogens, and commensal gut microbiota. For the [[mis-metallation]] framework, the paper establishes that excess metals are cytotoxic specifically "through mismetallation of essential proteins and production of chemical species that cause oxidative damage." The review documents how the host exploits this narrow window between metal deficiency and toxicity through [[nutritional-immunity]] -- both withholding metals (via calprotectin, lactoferrin, transferrin) and flooding pathogens with toxic metal concentrations (via neutrophil/macrophage Cu and Zn mobilization). Critically, the paper reveals that gut commensal bacteria possess their own metal competition strategies, including xenosiderophore piracy and metallophore production (yersiniabactin), that provide [[probiotics|colonization-resistance]] against pathogens.

# Key Claims

- **Excess metals are cytotoxic through mismetallation** of essential proteins and oxidative damage production -- explicitly named as a toxicity mechanism
- **Calprotectin** comprises 40-50% of neutrophil protein content, can reach >1 mg/mL at infection sites, and binds both Zn and Mn (also Fe) to starve pathogens
- **Both zinc deficiency and excess disrupt gut microbiota**: deficiency depletes Verrucomicrobia and promotes Enterobacteriaceae; excess causes dysbiotic Enterobacteriaceae bloom
- **Siderophore competition** drives pathogen-commensal metal warfare: Salmonella uses salmochelin (stealth siderophore evading lipocalin-2), while commensals like B. thetaiotaomicron use xenosiderophore piracy via XusB
- **Ferrosomes** are membrane-bound Fe storage organelles in C. difficile, representing the first bacterial organelle for iron storage
- **Zinc toxicity in macrophages** reaches levels sufficient to intoxicate engulfed pathogens; ZnO supplementation reduces Clostridium spp. and Enterobacteriaceae in pigs
- **Manganese is weaponized by host** through calprotectin-mediated Mn restriction at abscess sites, rendering S. aureus virtually devoid of Mn
- **Copper is mobilized to phagolysosomes** at >500 uM concentrations to kill engulfed bacteria

# Methods & Findings

## Iron Competition in the Gut

| Strategy | Organism | Mechanism |
|----------|---------|-----------|
| Siderophore (enterobactin) | E. coli, Salmonella | Highest Fe-binding affinity; TonB-dependent import |
| Stealth siderophore (salmochelin) | Salmonella | Glycosylated enterobactin evades lipocalin-2 |
| Xenosiderophore piracy (XusB) | B. thetaiotaomicron | Captures enterobactin via outer membrane vesicles |
| Ferrosomes | C. difficile | Membrane-bound Fe storage organelle (FezA/FezB) |
| Iron storage (Dps/ferritin) | Salmonella, E. coli | Intracellular Fe sequestration for oxidative stress defense |
| Heme acquisition | B. anthracis, C. difficile | Hemophores (IsdX1/X2) extract heme from hemoglobin |

## Zinc in Gut Health and Disease

- 10^5-10^6 ions of Fe required per bacterial cell; similar requirements for Zn
- Zn deficiency in pregnant mice: depletes Verrucomicrobia/Proteobacteria, increases gut permeability and neuroinflammation
- Zn-deficient children: lower Bifidobacterium, higher pro-inflammatory metabolites
- Excess Zn (ZnO in pigs): decreased Clostridium spp. and Enterobacteriaceae
- Chronic Zn excess in mice: dysbiotic Enterobacteriaceae bloom, reduced SCFA-producing Ruminococcus

## Host Metal Weaponization

| Metal | Host Strategy | Mechanism | Target |
|-------|-------------|-----------|--------|
| Fe | Sequestration | Lactoferrin, transferrin, lipocalin-2 | Starve pathogens of Fe |
| Zn | Sequestration + Toxicity | Calprotectin restriction; macrophage Zn flooding | Dual approach |
| Mn | Sequestration | Calprotectin at abscess sites | Impair SOD-dependent antioxidant defense |
| Cu | Intoxication | Phagolysosomal Cu mobilization (>500 uM) | Mismetallation of Fe-S clusters |

## Commensal Colonization Resistance via Metals

- E. coli Nissle (probiotic): possesses yersiniabactin metallophore, resists calprotectin-induced Zn sequestration better than pathogenic Salmonella
- Lactobacillus plantarum: increases Fe absorption in Fe-deficient humans better than Fe supplementation alone
- C. jejuni: znuABC zinc uptake essential for colonization; znuABC inactivation prevents infection
