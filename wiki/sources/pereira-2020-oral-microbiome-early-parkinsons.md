# The Oral Microbiome of Early Stage Parkinson's Disease and Its Relationship with Functional Measures

## Summary

This study characterizes the oral [[gut-microbiome|microbiome]] in early-stage [[parkinsons-disease]] and correlates oral microbial features with both motor and non-motor symptom measures, providing functional context for oral [[dysbiosis]] in PD.

## Key Findings

- The oral microbiome is significantly altered in early-stage PD
- Oral microbial composition correlated with motor symptom severity, cognition, and depression scores
- Functional metagenomic analysis revealed altered metabolic pathways in the PD oral microbiome
- Oral-gut microbial correlations suggest coordinated dysbiosis across body sites
- Salivary microbiome changes may precede gut microbiome alterations
- Non-motor symptoms (depression, cognitive impairment) showed stronger correlations with oral microbiome than motor symptoms

## Relevance to Gut-Metal-Microbiome Axis

The oral microbiome represents the upstream origin of gut microbial communities -- oral bacteria are continuously swallowed and can colonize the gut. In [[parkinsons-disease]], oral [[dysbiosis]] may initiate the cascade of gut [[dysbiosis]] and metal disruption. The oral cavity is the first site where dietary metals encounter the microbiome. Periodontal pathogens enriched in PD (such as *P. gingivalis*) have extensive heme-[[iron]] acquisition systems. The correlation between oral dysbiosis and non-motor symptoms supports the body-first PD model where peripheral microbiome disruption precedes central neurodegeneration.

## Limitations

- Cross-sectional design
- Early-stage PD definition varies across studies
- Oral hygiene practices and dental status are confounders
- Oral microbiome studies are less standardized than gut studies
