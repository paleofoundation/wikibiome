## Key Findings

- **Specific bacterial strains present in the gut microbiome at age one** were exclusive to children who later developed T1D, celiac disease, hypothyroidism, Crohn's disease, juvenile idiopathic arthritis (JIA), or autism — suggesting infant dysbiosis as a shared precursor across these conditions ([[turpin-2021-infant-gut-bacteria-adolescent-autism-autoimmune|this study]], prospective-cohort, n=1741).
- **96 out of 1,741 Swedish children** followed for up to 18 years developed one of these six conditions, enabling identification of early microbial predictors.
- **Class II HLA haplotypes** appear to function as "bacterial gatekeepers" that influence which bacteria can successfully colonize the infant gut, potentially explaining why genetically at-risk individuals harbor distinct microbiome profiles from infancy.
- **Infant gut dysbiosis and associated inflammation** may represent a common biological precedent to both autoimmune diseases and autism, supporting a shared pathogenic pathway.
- **Dietary patterns in infancy** interacted with HLA genotype to influence microbiome composition, suggesting that both genetic and environmental factors shape the early microbial community.

## Methods

- **Design**: Prospective birth cohort study from the All Babies in Southeast Sweden (ABIS) cohort.
- **Population**: 1,741 Swedish children enrolled at birth and followed for up to 18 years; stool samples collected at age 1.
- **Outcomes**: Clinical diagnoses of T1D, celiac disease, hypothyroidism, Crohn's disease, JIA, or autism ascertained through Swedish national health registries.
- **Microbiome analysis**: 16S rRNA gene sequencing of stool samples collected at approximately 1 year of age.
- **Genetic analysis**: HLA class II haplotype genotyping from cord blood or neonatal dried blood spots.
- **Statistical approach**: Comparison of microbial taxa between future disease cases and healthy controls, with HLA stratification.
- **Note**: This is a preprint (medRxiv) and has not undergone peer review as of the cited version.

## Detailed Summary

This study leveraged the large Swedish ABIS birth cohort to ask whether the infant gut microbiome at age one contains signals predictive of disease diagnoses that occur years or even decades later. The key insight is that autism and five autoimmune conditions — which share epidemiological associations but are conventionally treated as distinct diseases — may share a common early-life microbial antecedent.

The HLA gatekeeping hypothesis is particularly compelling. HLA molecules are primarily known for their role in antigen presentation to T cells, but this study suggests they also influence which bacteria establish in the infant gut. This creates a pathway from genetics (HLA haplotype) through microbiome composition (altered colonization) to disease risk (autism or autoimmunity). The concept parallels the nutritional immunity framework: just as the host uses metal sequestration to shape microbial ecology, HLA-mediated immune surveillance may selectively permit or exclude specific bacterial colonizers during the critical window of microbiome establishment.

The finding that dietary patterns interact with HLA to shape the microbiome adds a modifiable variable to this framework — suggesting that early-life dietary interventions, if appropriately timed and targeted, could potentially alter disease trajectories even in genetically susceptible individuals.
