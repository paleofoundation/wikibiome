# Key Claims

- Urinary [[arsenic]] significantly associated with female infertility (Q4 OR 2.76, 95% CI 1.33-5.70, p for trend = 0.045 in fully adjusted model)
- Urinary [[cadmium]] associated with infertility in crude and partially adjusted models (Q3 OR 3.77, 95% CI 1.52-9.35) but attenuated after full adjustment
- Blood [[lead]] (Q3 OR 2.62, 95% CI 1.19-5.77) and urinary Pb associated with infertility in ages 35-44 and BMI >= 25
- Blood [[mercury]] NOT significantly associated with infertility
- Urinary Cd and As significantly higher in infertile women vs controls (p < 0.05)
- Age and BMI are important effect modifiers of the metal-infertility relationship

# Methods/Findings

- **Data**: NHANES 2013-2018, n = 838 women aged 20-44 (112 infertile, 726 controls)
- **Metals**: Blood Cd, Pb, Hg; urinary Cd, Pb, As by ICP-MS
- **Three models**: Crude, Model 1 (age-adjusted), Model 2 (+ ethnicity, education, marital status, PIR), Model 3 (+ BMI, menstrual periods, pelvic infection, smoking)
- Subgroup analysis: metal-infertility associations mainly significant in ages 35-44 (blood Pb OR 1.52, urinary Pb OR 1.68, urinary As OR 1.02)
- BMI >= 25 subgroup: blood Pb (OR 1.67), urinary Pb (OR 1.54) significantly associated
- Urine tests may be more reliable than blood for assessing tissue metal stores
