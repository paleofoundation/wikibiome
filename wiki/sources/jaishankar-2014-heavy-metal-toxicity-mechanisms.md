## Summary

A comprehensive review covering the sources, toxicity mechanisms, and health effects of major heavy metals including arsenic, lead, mercury, cadmium, chromium, aluminum, and iron. The review details how these metals generate oxidative stress through free radical formation, displace essential metals from binding sites, and interfere with cellular processes.

## Key Findings

- Arsenic: biomethylation is a detoxification process; MMA(III) is the most toxic intermediate; chronic exposure causes arsenicosis affecting skin, lungs, bladder, liver, and kidney
- Lead: causes toxicity by following ionic mechanisms similar to Ca2+, Mg2+, Fe2+; affects cell adhesion, signaling, protein folding, apoptosis, and neurotransmitter release; blood lead levels >10 ug/dL affect IQ
- Mercury: the most toxic heavy metal; methyl mercury is 95-100% absorbed in intestinal tract and readily crosses blood-brain and placental barriers; estimated 8-10% of American women have mercury levels that could induce neurological disorders in children
- Cadmium: accumulates in proximal tubular cells of kidney; interacts with calcium and inhibits zinc, leading to osteoporosis and renal dysfunction; 50% absorbed in lungs; tobacco is a major source
- Chromium: Cr(VI) is highly toxic and carcinogenic; Cr(III) is an essential nutritional supplement for glucose metabolism
- Aluminum: third most abundant element in earth's crust; poorly absorbed (<0.1% orally); neurotoxicity is the primary concern; linked to Alzheimer's-like changes in animal models
- Iron: essential but toxic in excess; free iron generates hydroxyl radicals via Fenton reaction; iron overload damages heart, liver, and brain

## Relevance to Wiki

Provides foundational toxicological reference for the mechanisms by which heavy metals cause cellular damage through oxidative stress and metal displacement, applicable to understanding the health effects of dietary metal exposure.
