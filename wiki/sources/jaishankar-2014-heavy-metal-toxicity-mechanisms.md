# Toxicity, Mechanism and Health Effects of Some Heavy Metals

## Overview

Foundational review of the toxicological mechanisms of heavy metals in human health, covering [[arsenic]], [[lead]], [[mercury]], [[cadmium]], [[chromium]], aluminum, and [[iron]]. The paper establishes that despite their chemical diversity, heavy metals converge on several common toxicity pathways: generation of reactive oxygen species (ROS), depletion of [[glutathione]] and other antioxidant defenses, [[mis-metallation]] (displacement of essential metals from enzyme active sites), and direct binding to sulfhydryl groups on proteins and DNA. These mechanisms produce organ-specific damage patterns depending on the metal's pharmacokinetics, valence states, and tissue distribution.

## Unifying Mechanisms of Metal Toxicity

### Reactive Oxygen Species (ROS) Generation

- **Fenton and Fenton-like reactions**: Redox-active metals ([[iron]], [[copper]], [[chromium]], cobalt, vanadium, [[nickel]]) directly catalyze conversion of H2O2 to hydroxyl radicals via the reaction: Me(n) + H2O2 -> Me(n+1) + OH* + OH-
- **Indirect ROS generation**: Non-redox metals ([[arsenic]], [[cadmium]], [[mercury]], [[lead]]) generate [[oxidative-stress]] indirectly by:
  - Depleting [[glutathione]] (GSH) through direct binding to thiol groups (Hg2+, Cd2+, As3+ have extremely high affinity for -SH)
  - Inhibiting glutathione reductase and glutathione peroxidase, preventing GSH recycling
  - Disrupting mitochondrial electron transport chain, causing superoxide leakage
  - Inactivating catalase and superoxide dismutase
- **DNA damage pathways**: ROS cause base modifications, strand scission, DNA-protein crosslinks, and depurination. Cd and As additionally inhibit DNA repair enzymes (nucleotide excision repair, mismatch repair), allowing oxidative lesions to accumulate and drive carcinogenesis.

### [[mis-metallation]] and Metal Displacement

- Heavy metals displace essential metals from their native binding sites in enzymes and structural proteins:
  - [[lead]] mimics Ca2+, Mg2+, Fe2+, and Zn2+ -- displaces them from binding sites in enzymes, ion channels, and signaling proteins
  - [[cadmium]] displaces [[zinc]] from zinc-finger transcription factors and metallothioneins, and [[iron]] from iron-sulfur clusters
  - [[mercury]] and [[arsenic]] bind cysteine residues with higher affinity than native zinc or iron, displacing essential metals from catalytic sites
  - [[chromium]](III) can substitute for [[iron]](III) in transferrin binding
- Mis-metallation disrupts protein folding, enzyme catalysis, and transcriptional regulation. This is mechanistically distinct from oxidative damage but often co-occurs with it.

### Thiol Binding and Enzyme Inhibition

- Mercury, cadmium, arsenic, and lead all show high affinity for protein sulfhydryl (-SH) groups.
- This directly inactivates enzymes that depend on free cysteine residues for catalysis, including delta-aminolevulinic acid dehydratase (ALAD, inhibited by [[lead]]), pyruvate dehydrogenase (inhibited by [[arsenic]]), and multiple selenoenzymes (inhibited by [[mercury]]).

## Metal-by-Metal Toxicity Mechanisms

### [[arsenic]]

**ROS pathways**: Arsenite [As(III)] is the more toxic oxidation state. Biomethylation -- once considered purely detoxifying -- generates monomethylarsonous acid (MMA(III)), which is the **most toxic arsenical intermediate**, more cytotoxic than inorganic arsenic. As(III) depletes [[glutathione]] by direct thiol binding (As-SG conjugation) and inhibits glutathione reductase, collapsing the GSH/GSSG ratio. Arsenic also generates superoxide during its oxidation from As(III) to As(V).

**Enzyme inhibition**: Arsenite binds vicinal dithiols in pyruvate dehydrogenase and alpha-ketoglutarate dehydrogenase, blocking the TCA cycle. Arsenate [As(V)] mimics phosphate and can substitute for phosphate in glycolysis (arsenolysis), uncoupling substrate-level phosphorylation.

**Organ-specific toxicity**:
- **Skin**: chronic exposure causes arsenicosis -- hyperpigmentation, keratoses, Bowen's disease, and squamous cell carcinoma. Skin is a major accumulation site.
- **Lung**: inhalation exposure causes lung cancer (IARC Group 1 carcinogen). Arsenic inhibits DNA repair in bronchial epithelium.
- **Liver**: hepatic angiosarcoma, cirrhosis, and non-cirrhotic portal hypertension with chronic exposure.
- **Kidney**: proximal tubular damage; arsenic accumulates in renal cortex.
- **Bladder**: transitional cell carcinoma; one of the strongest epidemiological associations for any environmental carcinogen.
- **Peripheral nervous system**: sensorimotor neuropathy with chronic low-level exposure.

### [[lead]]

**ROS pathways**: Lead is not redox-active but generates [[oxidative-stress]] indirectly by: (i) depleting [[glutathione]] via binding to -SH groups; (ii) inhibiting ALAD (delta-aminolevulinic acid dehydratase), causing accumulation of ALA, which auto-oxidizes to generate superoxide and hydroxyl radicals; (iii) inhibiting ferrochelatase, causing accumulation of protoporphyrin IX which photosensitizes to produce singlet oxygen; (iv) stimulating NADPH oxidase activity. Lead also substitutes for [[zinc]] in zinc-finger proteins and for Ca2+ in calmodulin and protein kinase C signaling.

**Enzyme inhibition**:
- **ALAD**: Lead inhibits this heme biosynthesis enzyme by displacing [[zinc]] from its active site (IC50 ~5 uM). This is the basis of the zinc protoporphyrin (ZPP) blood test for lead exposure.
- **Ferrochelatase**: insertion of Fe2+ into protoporphyrin IX is blocked, causing microcytic anemia.
- **Pyrimidine-5'-nucleotidase**: inhibition causes basophilic stippling of erythrocytes (pathognomonic finding).

**Organ-specific toxicity**:
- **Brain/nervous system**: Lead crosses the blood-brain barrier (especially in children). Disrupts neurotransmitter release (mimics Ca2+ at synaptic terminals), inhibits NMDA receptor function, damages oligodendrocytes (demyelination). Blood lead levels >10 ug/dL are associated with reduced IQ in children; no safe threshold has been established.
- **Kidney**: chronic lead nephropathy with proximal tubular dysfunction, hyperuricemia, and progressive renal failure.
- **Bone**: lead substitutes for calcium in hydroxyapatite; bone serves as a long-term reservoir (half-life 20-30 years). Mobilization during pregnancy, lactation, or osteoporosis releases stored lead.
- **Hematopoietic system**: microcytic hypochromic anemia from ALAD and ferrochelatase inhibition.
- **Reproductive**: reduced sperm count and motility; increased miscarriage risk.

### [[mercury]]

**ROS pathways**: Mercury is described as the "most toxic heavy metal." Methylmercury (MeHg) is 95-100% absorbed in the intestinal tract and readily crosses the blood-brain barrier and placental barrier. Hg2+ binds thiol groups with the highest affinity of any heavy metal, depleting cellular [[glutathione]] pools and inactivating selenoenzymes (glutathione peroxidase, thioredoxin reductase). Mercury also disrupts mitochondrial function by binding to inner membrane thiol groups, causing electron transport chain leakage and superoxide generation.

**Enzyme inhibition**:
- **Glutathione peroxidase/thioredoxin reductase**: Hg displaces selenium from selenocysteine active sites (Se-Hg affinity exceeds Se-protein affinity).
- **Na+/K+-ATPase**: mercury binding to membrane -SH groups disrupts ion transport.
- **Tubulin**: methylmercury depolymerizes microtubules, disrupting axonal transport.

**Organ-specific toxicity**:
- **Brain**: methylmercury concentrates in the brain at 5-6x blood levels. Targets cerebellar granule cells and visual cortex. Causes Minamata disease: ataxia, sensory disturbances, visual field constriction, hearing loss. Prenatal exposure causes cerebral palsy-like syndrome. An estimated 8-10% of American women have mercury levels sufficient to cause neurological disorders in offspring.
- **Kidney**: inorganic mercury (Hg2+) accumulates in proximal tubules, causing membranous nephropathy and nephrotic syndrome.
- **Cardiovascular**: methylmercury promotes atherosclerosis and hypertension by inactivating paraoxonase-1 and endothelial nitric oxide synthase.
- **Immune**: mercury is a potent immunomodulator; can trigger autoimmune responses (anti-nuclear antibodies) at low doses.

### [[cadmium]]

**ROS pathways**: Cadmium is non-redox-active but generates [[oxidative-stress]] by: (i) displacing [[iron]] from ferritin and iron-sulfur proteins, releasing free Fe2+ that then catalyzes Fenton reactions ([[ferroptosis]]-like mechanism); (ii) depleting [[glutathione]] through direct Cd-SG binding; (iii) inhibiting catalase and SOD. Cadmium also interferes with calcium signaling by competing for Ca2+ binding sites, activating calcium-dependent endonucleases that contribute to apoptosis.

**Enzyme inhibition**:
- Displaces [[zinc]] from metallothionein (initially protective, but chronic exposure overwhelms MT capacity).
- Inhibits DNA repair enzymes (OGG1, XPA), causing accumulation of 8-oxo-dG lesions.
- Blocks heme biosynthesis at multiple steps.

**Organ-specific toxicity**:
- **Kidney**: the primary target organ. Cadmium accumulates in proximal tubular cells (biological half-life 10-30 years). Causes Fanconi-like syndrome: glucosuria, aminoaciduria, phosphaturia, and beta-2-microglobulinuria. Cd-induced renal damage is **irreversible** once proteinuria is established.
- **Bone**: cadmium causes osteomalacia and osteoporosis by interfering with calcium metabolism and vitamin D activation. Itai-itai disease (Japan) combined renal failure with severe osteomalacia.
- **Lung**: inhalation exposure (primarily occupational and from tobacco smoke -- each cigarette contains 1-2 ug Cd, of which 50% is absorbed via lungs) causes emphysema and lung cancer (IARC Group 1).
- **Liver**: hepatocellular damage and disrupted lipid metabolism.
- **Reproductive**: testicular necrosis at high doses; endocrine disruption at low doses (estrogenic activity via estrogen receptor binding).

### [[chromium]]

**ROS pathways**: Cr(VI) is the toxic and carcinogenic form (IARC Group 1). Cr(III) is an essential trace element for glucose metabolism (glucose tolerance factor). The critical distinction is that Cr(VI) exists as chromate (CrO4^2-), which enters cells via anion channels (phosphate/sulfate transporters) due to structural similarity to phosphate and sulfate. Inside the cell, Cr(VI) undergoes stepwise reduction: Cr(VI) -> Cr(V) -> Cr(IV) -> Cr(III), with each step generating free radicals:
  - Cr(VI) is reduced by ascorbate, [[glutathione]], cysteine, NADPH, and ribose/fructose
  - Cr(V) and Cr(IV) intermediates react with H2O2 via Fenton-like chemistry to produce hydroxyl radicals
  - The final Cr(III) product forms stable DNA adducts and DNA-protein crosslinks

**Enzyme inhibition**: Cr(VI) reduction consumes cellular reductants (GSH, ascorbate, NADPH), depleting antioxidant capacity. Cr(III)-DNA adducts block replication fork progression and inhibit DNA repair.

**Organ-specific toxicity**:
- **Lung**: primary target for inhalation exposure. Cr(VI) causes lung cancer, pneumonia, and chromium-induced asthma. Chrome holes (painless ulcerating skin lesions) on hands and nasal septum perforation are classic occupational findings.
- **Kidney**: proximal tubular necrosis from Cr(VI) exposure.
- **Liver**: hepatocellular damage with Cr(VI); Cr(III) is relatively non-toxic via oral route.
- **Skin**: allergic contact dermatitis (Cr is one of the most common contact allergens); hexavalent chromium compounds cause skin ulceration.

### Aluminum

- Not a classical heavy metal (density 2.7 g/cm3) but included for its neurotoxicity.
- Poorly absorbed orally (<0.1%), but accumulates in brain, bone, and kidney with chronic exposure.
- Neurotoxicity: promotes neurofibrillary tangle-like changes in animal models, interferes with calcium-dependent processes, and disrupts cholinergic and glutamatergic neurotransmission. Linked to dialysis dementia in renal patients and investigated as a potential cofactor in Alzheimer's disease, though causality remains debated.
- Competes with magnesium for ATP binding and with calcium in second messenger systems.

### [[iron]]

- Essential but toxic in excess. Free (non-transferrin-bound) [[iron]] catalyzes hydroxyl radical production via the Fenton reaction: Fe2+ + H2O2 -> Fe3+ + OH* + OH-
- Iron overload damages the heart (cardiomyopathy), liver (cirrhosis, hepatocellular carcinoma), and endocrine organs (diabetes from pancreatic iron deposition).
- Hereditary hemochromatosis and repeated transfusions are primary causes of pathological iron loading.
- [[ferroptosis]]: a recently characterized iron-dependent form of regulated cell death driven by lipid peroxidation, directly linking iron toxicity to a specific programmed death pathway.
- Iron toxicity is generally protected against by: transferrin/ferritin binding (preventing free iron), chelation of ferrous ion, and maintenance of the Fe3+ (non-reactive) redox state.

## Chelation Therapy

- **For [[lead]]**: Succimer (DMSA, dimercaptosuccinic acid) is first-line for children with blood lead >45 ug/dL. CaNa2-EDTA for severe cases. D-penicillamine as oral alternative. All chelators mobilize lead from soft tissues for renal excretion but do not effectively access bone stores.
- **For [[mercury]]**: DMSA and DMPS (unithiol) for inorganic mercury. BAL (dimercaprol) for acute inorganic mercury poisoning. No effective chelator for methylmercury in the brain; selenium supplementation may be partially protective by forming inert Hg-Se complexes.
- **For [[arsenic]]**: BAL (dimercaprol) for acute arsenicosis. DMSA for chronic exposure. Chelation is less effective once arsenic has been methylated and distributed to tissues.
- **For [[cadmium]]**: EDTA can mobilize cadmium but paradoxically **increases** renal damage by redistributing Cd to the kidneys. Chelation is therefore **contraindicated** in chronic cadmium exposure. Treatment is primarily supportive.
- **For [[iron]]**: Deferoxamine (parenteral), deferasirox (oral), and deferiprone (oral) are used for transfusional iron overload. Deferoxamine binds Fe3+ with extremely high affinity (Kd ~10^-31).
- **For [[chromium]]**: No specific chelator. Treatment involves removal from exposure and supportive care. Ascorbic acid (vitamin C) has been proposed to reduce Cr(VI) to less toxic Cr(III) extracellularly before cellular uptake.

## Cross-Cutting Themes

### The [[glutathione]] Connection

- Nearly every toxic heavy metal depletes GSH, either by direct conjugation (As, Hg, Cd) or by inhibiting GSH recycling enzymes (Pb, Cr). GSH depletion is the **single most common early event** in metal toxicity, preceding overt oxidative damage. This explains why individuals with genetically lower GSH synthetic capacity (glutathione S-transferase polymorphisms) are more susceptible to metal toxicity.

### Organ Vulnerability Patterns

- **Kidney**: target organ for Cd, Hg (inorganic), Pb, As, Cr -- reflects the kidney's concentrating function and high blood flow
- **Brain**: target for Pb, MeHg, Al, Mn -- reflects lipophilicity of organometal forms and blood-brain barrier permeability
- **Liver**: target for As, Fe, Cd, Cu -- reflects first-pass exposure from portal circulation
- **Lung**: target for Cr(VI), Cd, Ni, As -- reflects inhalation exposure route
- **Bone**: reservoir for Pb (substitutes for Ca) and Cd (displaces Ca), creating long-term internal exposure sources

### Interactions Between Metals

- [[zinc]] supplementation is protective against cadmium toxicity (Zn competes for MT binding sites and intestinal absorption).
- [[iron]] deficiency increases lead and cadmium absorption (shared DMT1/NRAMP2 intestinal transporter).
- Selenium is protective against mercury toxicity (Hg-Se complex formation).
- These interactions have significant implications for populations with multiple concurrent metal exposures and micronutrient deficiencies.
