# Key Claims

- Extra-neuronal factors including nutrition, metabolic disorders, and gut microbiota may affect brain physiology and predispose to neurodegenerative diseases
- Obesity and type 2 diabetes increase the risk of AD and PD after midlife; Mediterranean diet shows protective effects
- The gut microbiota has emerged as a novel factor engaging strong interactions with neurons and glia, deeply affecting function and behavior in NDDs
- Gut microbes are involved in the seeding of prion-like proteins and their spreading to the CNS
- Dysbiosis has been robustly associated with metabolic disorders and affects multiple aspects of health

# Methods/Findings

- **Design**: Systematic search on PubMed and Google Scholar (through September 2020); 387 peer-reviewed articles from experimental and clinical data
- **AD and Metabolic Disorders**:
  - Obesity during midlife associated with up to 74% increased risk of dementia
  - T2D leads some to define AD as "type-3 diabetes" with up to 50% higher risk
  - APOE4 genotype modifies the relationship between metabolism and neurodegeneration
  - High total and LDL-C during midlife most consistently associated with future dementia
  - MAFLD associated with reduced cognitive function and lower brain volume
- **AD and Diet**:
  - omega-3 PUFAs (DHA) have anti-amyloidogenic effects through indirect mechanisms
  - Mediterranean diet associated with substantial AD risk reduction
  - Caloric restriction protects against neurodegeneration in experimental models
  - Vitamin E is a powerful antioxidant supporting mitochondrial function
- **AD and Microbiota**:
  - AD patients show reduced Bacteroidetes diversity and decreased Firmicutes; increased Gram-negative bacteria
  - LPS enhances amyloid-beta fibrillization and triggers NF-kB signaling
  - Bacterial amyloids (Curli from E. coli) cross-seed with alpha-synuclein and cerebral amyloid-beta
  - FMT from WT to AD mice alleviated cognitive impairment and reduced amyloid-beta plaque burden
  - SCFAs are reduced in AD; sodium butyrate curbs cholesterol-induced neuronal amyloidosis
- **PD and Microbiota**:
  - Robust evidence for dysbiosis in PD: higher Verrucomicrobiaceae, Lactobacillaceae; lower SCFA-producing bacteria
  - Active gut-to-brain transport of alpha-synuclein aggregates along vagus nerve (Braak hypothesis)
  - Vagotomy associated with lower PD risk in epidemiological studies
  - Endotoxemia disrupts BBB, increases alpha-synuclein deposition
- **ALS**:
  - Hypermetabolism is a prominent feature; lower BMI at diagnosis predicts shorter survival
  - Omega-3 PUFAs reduced ALS risk (RR 0.66) in large prospective cohorts
  - Limited evidence for microbiota involvement but emerging
