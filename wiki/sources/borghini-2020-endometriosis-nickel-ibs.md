# Key Claims

- Alimentary [[nickel]] may cause allergic contact mucositis (Ni ACM) presenting with IBS-like and extra-intestinal symptoms, and these symptoms are frequent in [[endometriosis]]
- 90.3% of endometriosis patients with GI symptoms tested positive for Ni ACM via the nickel oral mucosa patch test (Ni omPT)
- A 3-month [[low-nickel-diet]] produced statistically significant improvement in all gastrointestinal (15 symptoms), extra-intestinal (7 symptoms), and gynecological symptoms (dysmenorrhea, dyspareunia, pelvic pain) in endometriosis patients
- [[Nickel-allergy]] (Ni ACM) should be considered as a cause or contributing factor of intestinal and extra-intestinal symptoms in women with endometriosis
- [[nickel]] may act as a "metalloestrogen" -- heavy metals can interfere with estrogenic activity, and Ni can bind estrogen receptors and induce proliferation of ERa+ breast cancer cells

# Methods/Findings

- **Study design**: Open-label pilot study at Sapienza University of Rome
- **Participants**: 83 patients recruited (Oct 2016 -- Feb 2018); 51 met inclusion criteria (>=3 GI symptoms with score >=5 on modified GSRS); 4 excluded (celiac/wheat allergy); 16 dropped out; 31 completed the study (age range 19-46, mean 33.5 years)
- **Inclusion criteria**: Fertile age, endometriosis diagnosis by imaging/laparoscopy, >=3 gastrointestinal symptoms with score >=5 on modified GSRS
- **Ni omPT method**: 5-mm filter paper disk with 5% Ni-sulfate in Vaseline applied to upper lip mucosa for 2 hours; induces type IV hypersensitivity reactions (TLR4-dependent innate immune response)
- **Results -- Ni omPT**: 28/31 (90.3%) tested positive for Ni ACM; 3/31 (9.7%) tested negative
- **Results -- GI symptoms**: All 15 gastrointestinal symptoms (abdominal pain, heartburn, acid regurgitation, bloating, nausea, borborygmus, swelling, belching, flatulence, increased/decreased evacuations, loose/hard stools, urgent defecation, oral ulcers) showed statistically significant decrease after 3 months of low-Ni diet (p <0.05)
- **Results -- Extra-intestinal symptoms**: All 7 symptoms (dermatitis, headache, foggy mind, fainting, numbness of limbs, joint/muscle pain, fatigue) showed statistically significant reduction (p <0.05)
- **Results -- Gynecological symptoms**: Dysmenorrhea, dyspareunia, and pelvic pain all showed significant improvement (p <0.005)
- **High-nickel foods avoided**: Herring, mackerel, salmon, shellfish, tuna; fresh/dried legumes, chickpeas, lentils, peanuts, peas, red kidney beans, soy; garlic, green leafy vegetables, spinach, onion, raw carrots, tomatoes; almonds, hazelnuts, walnuts; buckwheat, maize, millet, oat, rye, whole grain, whole wheat; beer, coffee, initial tap water, red wine, tea; baking powder, canned foods, cocoa, chocolate, foods cooked in stainless steel
- **Limitations**: Open-label, single-center, small sample size, high dropout rate (34%), no accurate biomarkers for Ni content measurement, potential overlap with low-FODMAP diet effects

# Connections

- [[nickel]] -- identified as a ubiquitous metal whose dietary intake causes low-grade GI inflammation in susceptible individuals
- [[nickel-allergy]] -- Ni ACM prevalence of 90.3% in symptomatic endometriosis patients is remarkably high; prevalence in general population estimated >=30%
- [[dietary-nickel-exposure]] -- main source of Ni for humans is food (0.1-0.5 mg/kg); levels depend on soil Ni content, irrigation water, fertilizers, industrial processing
- [[low-nickel-diet]] -- demonstrated efficacy in reducing GI, systemic, and gynecological symptoms; pioneering study linking dietary Ni restriction to endometriosis symptom relief
- [[oxidative-stress]] -- Ni exposure linked to inflammatory and estrogenic effects that may promote endometriosis progression
- [[endocrine-disruption]] -- Ni classified as a "metalloestrogen" or xenoestrogen that can bind ERs and interfere with sex hormones
- [[inflammation]] -- Ni ACM represents low-grade gastrointestinal inflammation with systemic manifestations
