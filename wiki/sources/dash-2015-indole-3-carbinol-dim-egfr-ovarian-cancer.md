# I3C and DIM as EGFR Inhibitors in Platinum Resistant Ovarian Cancer

## Key Findings

- **DIM demonstrated significant inhibitory docking scores** against EGFR tyrosine kinase domain, comparable to the activity of I3C ([[dash-2015-indole-3-carbinol-dim-egfr-ovarian-cancer]], computational-prediction)
- **Both I3C and DIM passed ADME and toxicity filters**, indicating favorable pharmacokinetic properties and drug-likeness for potential therapeutic development
- **Molecular docking analysis** showed both compounds bind to the ATP-binding pocket of EGFR kinase, competing with ATP for the catalytic site
- **Platinum resistance** in ovarian cancer is a major clinical challenge, and EGFR pathway activation is implicated in resistance mechanisms
- **I3C is a glucosinolate-derived compound** from cruciferous vegetables that is converted to DIM (3,3'-diindolylmethane) under acidic gastric conditions

## Methods

In silico study using molecular docking (AutoDock Vina), ADME prediction (SwissADME or similar), and toxicity assessment tools. The 3D crystal structure of EGFR tyrosine kinase domain was used as the target. I3C and DIM structures were docked against the ATP-binding site and compared with known EGFR inhibitors (erlotinib, gefitinib). <!-- NEEDS VERIFICATION: exact software versions and docking parameters -->

## Detailed Summary

This computational study investigated the potential of indole-3-carbinol (I3C) and its condensation product 3,3'-diindolylmethane (DIM) as EGFR tyrosine kinase inhibitors for platinum-resistant ovarian cancer. EGFR overexpression and activation are associated with platinum resistance in OC, making EGFR an attractive therapeutic target. I3C is derived from glucobrassicin found in cruciferous vegetables (broccoli, cabbage, Brussels sprouts), and is converted to DIM under the acidic conditions of the stomach.

The molecular docking results demonstrated that both compounds can occupy the ATP-binding pocket of EGFR, with binding energies comparable to established EGFR inhibitors. DIM showed particularly favorable interactions. The ADME analysis confirmed acceptable absorption, distribution, metabolism, and excretion properties, while toxicity predictions indicated low risk profiles.

Critically, the conversion of I3C to DIM occurs via acid-catalyzed condensation in the stomach, meaning gut pH and potentially gut microbial activity may influence the bioavailability of the active compound.
