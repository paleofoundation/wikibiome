## Summary

A case-control study from central India investigating associations between heavy metals (As, Cd, Pb, Cr) and chronic kidney disease of unknown cause (CKDu), comparing CKDu patients (n=60), CKD patients (n=62), and healthy controls (n=54). Both blood and urine metal levels were measured as biomarkers of exposure.

## Key Findings

- Blood and urinary levels of Cd, Pb, and Cr were significantly higher in CKD and CKDu patients compared to healthy controls
- Blood arsenic was significantly higher in CKDu (91.97 mcg/Lt) compared to CKD (4.5 mcg/Lt) and healthy subjects (39.01 mcg/Lt)
- Blood arsenic was independently associated with CKDu on multinomial regression (OR 1.013, 95% CI 1.003-1.024, p=0.014)
- Surface water use was independently associated with CKDu (OR 3.178, p=0.045)
- Urinary Cd, Pb, and Cr were undetectable in healthy subjects but significantly higher in CKDu and CKD
- Strong correlations found between blood Cd, Pb, and Cr in CKDu patients (rho=0.68-0.88)
- Urine-to-blood ratios differed between healthy and kidney-impaired subjects, reflecting altered metal excretion
- CKDu patients had significantly higher pesticide use and surface water consumption

## Methods

- Case-control study at a tertiary hospital, December 2019 to June 2022
- Blood and urine metals measured by ICP-OES
- CKDu defined by eGFR <60, albumin-to-creatinine ratio >30 mg/g, without diabetes or known causes
- Multinomial regression adjusted for age, gender, and confounders

## Relevance to Wiki

Demonstrates the role of multiple heavy metals (especially arsenic) in CKDu pathogenesis in an Indian cohort, with surface water and pesticide exposure as key environmental risk factors, supporting the broader connection between agricultural contamination and kidney disease.
