## Key Findings

- **Bile acids at supraphysiological concentrations are pro-carcinogenic**, but at physiological reference concentrations, certain bile acids (particularly UDCA and lithocholic acid) possess anticancer activity — creating a dose-dependent duality ([[rezen-2022-bile-acids-carcinogenesis-review]], expert-opinion).
- **Ursodeoxycholic acid (UDCA) has almost purely anticancer properties** across multiple cancer types reviewed, making it a strong drug repurposing candidate. UDCA is already FDA-approved for primary biliary cholangitis.
- **Differences in cancer responses to bile acids** are determined by differential expression of bile acid receptors: FXR (farnesoid X receptor, generally tumor-suppressive) vs. TGR5 (G protein-coupled bile acid receptor 1, context-dependent) vs. PXR, CAR, VDR, and muscarinic receptors CHRM2/3.
- **The gut microbiome converts primary bile acids to secondary bile acids** through deconjugation (bile salt hydrolase activity), dehydroxylation (7-alpha-dehydroxylase), and other transformations — modulating cancer risk across multiple cancer types including colorectal, breast, and ovarian cancers.
- The review covers bile acid involvement in **8+ cancer types**, establishing bile acid-mediated carcinogenesis as a cross-condition phenomenon.

## Methods

**Comprehensive narrative review** covering the role of bile acids in carcinogenesis across multiple cancer types. The authors synthesized evidence from in-vitro studies, animal models, epidemiological data, and clinical observations to construct a framework for understanding bile acid-cancer interactions. Key bile acid receptors (FXR, TGR5, PXR, CAR, VDR, LXR, CHRM2/3) were systematically reviewed for their roles in each cancer type. The review also addressed the gut microbiome as the primary modifier of bile acid composition and cancer risk.

## Detailed Summary

Rezen et al. provided a comprehensive review of bile acid involvement in carcinogenesis, revealing a nuanced picture where the same molecules can be either pro-carcinogenic or anticancer depending on concentration, receptor expression, and tissue context.

**Pro-carcinogenic mechanisms of bile acids** include: (1) direct DNA damage through reactive oxygen species generation (particularly deoxycholic acid, which generates ROS through mitochondrial perturbation); (2) activation of NF-kB-mediated inflammatory signaling, creating a chronic inflammatory microenvironment; (3) promotion of cell proliferation through muscarinic receptor (CHRM2/3) activation; (4) disruption of epithelial barrier integrity through detergent-like membrane effects; and (5) epigenetic modifications including altered DNA methylation patterns.

**Anticancer mechanisms** include: (1) FXR activation, which suppresses cell proliferation and induces apoptosis in many epithelial cancers — FXR loss is common in advanced tumors; (2) UDCA-mediated anti-inflammatory effects and suppression of DCA-induced DNA damage; (3) lithocholic acid activation of VDR (vitamin D receptor), which has established tumor-suppressive functions; and (4) bile acid-induced differentiation in certain cancer cell types.

The **gut microbiome connection** is central: the balance between primary bile acids (synthesized by the liver) and secondary bile acids (produced by gut bacteria through deconjugation and dehydroxylation) determines the overall carcinogenic vs. protective bile acid profile. Gut dysbiosis — particularly loss of 7-alpha-dehydroxylase-producing bacteria and altered bile salt hydrolase activity — shifts this balance. Importantly, different cancers are affected by different aspects of bile acid metabolism: colorectal cancer is primarily influenced by luminal bile acid concentrations, while breast and ovarian cancers are influenced by systemic bile acid levels through enterohepatic circulation.

The **UDCA drug repurposing opportunity** is particularly compelling: UDCA has an established safety profile (decades of clinical use in liver disease), oral bioavailability, and demonstrated anticancer activity across multiple cancer types in preclinical models. Its primary anticancer mechanism appears to involve displacement of toxic hydrophobic bile acids from cell membranes and FXR-mediated transcriptional changes.

The [[iron]] connection is relevant through bile acid transport: iron is required for bile acid synthesis (cytochrome P450 enzymes CYP7A1 and CYP27A1 are heme-iron dependent), and bile acid recycling involves iron-dependent transporters. Disrupted iron homeostasis in cancer may alter bile acid synthesis and composition.

## Relevance to WikiBiome

This review establishes [[bile-acid-metabolism]] as a critical microbiome-mediated pathway in carcinogenesis across multiple cancer types. The gut microbiome's role as the primary modifier of bile acid composition connects microbial ecology to cancer risk in a mechanistically well-defined way. The [[iron]]-bile acid synthesis connection links this to the metallomics framework — iron-dependent CYP enzymes control the rate of bile acid production, and altered iron homeostasis in cancer may dysregulate this pathway.

The cross-condition nature of bile acid-mediated carcinogenesis (affecting colorectal, breast, ovarian, and other cancers) makes this a concept that unifies multiple disease entities through a shared microbial mechanism.
