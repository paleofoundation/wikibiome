# Gut Microbiota and Thyroid Diseases: A Comprehensive Review of Mechanisms and Clinical Implications

## Key Claims

- Gut microbes not only influence thyroid hormone synthesis and metabolism through metabolites such as [[SCFAs]], [[LPS]], secondary [[bile acids]], and indoles, but also play critical roles in immune homeostasis, contributing to [[Graves disease]], [[Hashimotos thyroiditis]], thyroid dysfunction, and thyroid cancer
- In HT, Firmicutes and Bacteroidetes are the main dominant microbiota; their proportion is related to disease susceptibility, which may reflect the ecosystem of the gastrointestinal tract in the disease status of HT
- Gut microbiota impacts thyroid pharmacotherapy: [[Lactobacillus]] and [[Bifidobacterium]] lower intestinal LT4 absorption efficiency; [[Bacteroides]] enrichment indirectly influences drug solubility and bioavailability through alterations in bile acid metabolism
- [[Fecal microbiota transplantation]] (FMT) has been explored as an emerging strategy, with animal experiments indicating FMT can restore SCFA levels, modulate immune responses, and rebalance gut-thyroid axis signaling pathways
- [[Probiotics]] (Lactiplantibacillus plantarum and Bifidobacterium longum) have been demonstrated to improve thyroid function, shorten TSH recovery time, and enhance drug efficacy
- In thyroid cancer, Prevotella abundance was significantly correlated with thyrotropin receptor antibody (TRAb) levels, and Clostridium correlated with lymph node metastasis in papillary thyroid carcinoma

## Methods/Findings

- **Study type**: Comprehensive review (6 pages with 44 references)
- **Mechanistic framework** (Figure 1): Gut microbiota influences thyroid disease through: (1) immune regulation (Treg/Th17 balance, IL-6, TNF-alpha), (2) nutrient metabolism (iodine, selenium utilization for thyroid hormones), (3) drug handling (LT4 absorption, antithyroid drug metabolism), (4) barrier and inflammation (LPS-TLR4, mucosal integrity), and (5) microbial metabolites (SCFAs, secondary bile acids, indoles)
- **Figure 2**: Mechanistic overview showing gut pathogens (LPS), impaired gut barrier, SCFAs, bile acids, and selenium interactions with thyroid disease through Th1/Th2/Th17/Treg immune pathways
- **Hashimoto's thyroiditis**: Firmicutes and Bacteroidetes dominant; increased Firmicutes/Bacteroidetes ratio, Prevotella copre, and Bacteroides; high Ruminococcus, decreased Butyricimonas and Lactobacillus; loss of SCFA-producing bacteria
- **Graves' disease**: Prevotella abundance correlated with TRAb; Bacteroidetes reduction; Prevotella, Roseburia, and Lactobacillus depletion
- **Thyroid cancer**: Elevated Neisseria and Streptococcus linked to tumorigenesis; reduced SCFAs and elevated lipopolysaccharides (LPS); Clostridium correlated with lymph node metastasis
- **Drug interactions**: Dysbiosis may reduce LT4 metabolic stability and weaken therapeutic effect; antithyroid drug (methimazole, propylthiouracil) metabolism influenced by gut microbes; probiotic interventions show clinical potential
- **Clinical applications**: Microbial features for risk stratification; SCFA/LPS profiling; interventions (probiotics, diet, FMT); synergy with standard treatments (RAI, antithyroid drugs, levothyroxine)
- **Challenges**: Causal relationships remain unclear; disease heterogeneity and population differences compromise reproducibility; low-biomass sample biases; need for standardized protocols and multi-omics integration
