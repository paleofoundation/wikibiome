# Overview

Case-control study measuring **21 chemical elements in hair** of 181 children (57 controls, 53 mild-to-moderate ASD, 71 severe ASD) aged 1-16 years in southeastern China using ICP-MS. Found that ASD severity correlates with accumulation of specific [[heavy metals]] and depletion of essential minerals, with notable **sex-specific differences**.

# Key Findings

## Heavy Metals Elevated in Severe ASD

- **Vanadium (V)**: control 0.029 vs severe 0.044 ug/g (p = 0.007)
- **Cobalt (Co)**: control 0.010 vs severe 0.019 (p = 0.028)
- **Nickel (Ni)**: control 0.186 vs severe 0.248 (p = 0.014)
- **Arsenic (As)**: control 0.072 vs severe 0.116 (p < 0.001)
- **Cadmium (Cd)**: control 0.027 vs severe 0.069 (p = 0.001)
- **Lead (Pb)**: control 0.881 vs severe 1.778 (p < 0.001)

## Essential Minerals

- **Copper (Cu)**: only essential mineral significantly elevated in ASD (p < 0.001)
- No significant differences in [[zinc]], [[iron]], [[selenium]], [[magnesium]], or calcium between groups
- Girls with severe autism had significantly lower **potassium (K)** levels

## Sex-Specific Patterns

- **Boys with severe ASD**: higher levels of Cu, As, Cd, and Pb vs controls
- **Girls with severe ASD**: lower K levels; higher Pb levels
- Boys showed more pronounced heavy metal accumulation across severity grades

## Age-Dependent Patterns

- Children aged 1-6 years: elevated Cu, V, Ni, Cd, Pb in ASD
- Children aged 7-16 years: higher Cu and As in severe vs mild-to-moderate ASD
- Severe ASD children under 7: significantly increased Mn, Cu, V, Co, Ni, As, Cd, Pb

## Premature Birth Association

- Severe ASD group had significantly higher premature delivery rate (21.13%) vs controls (10.53%) (p = 0.014)

# Mechanistic Implications

- [[lead]] disrupts blood-brain barrier, causes [[neuroinflammation]]
- [[arsenic]] and [[cadmium]] induce [[oxidative stress]] and mitochondrial damage
- [[copper]] excess may reflect impaired [[ceruloplasmin]] metabolism or [[Wilson disease]]-like phenotype
- [[manganese]] neurotoxicity during critical developmental periods
- Heavy metal accumulation may compound genetic susceptibility to ASD

# Relevance to Metallomic Framework

**Very high relevance** — provides severity-stratified metallomic data in ASD:

- Demonstrates **dose-response relationship** between heavy metal burden and ASD severity
- Cu as only essential mineral elevated — suggests specific [[copper]] dyshomeostasis in ASD
- Sex-specific metal patterns align with ASD sex ratio (male predominance)
- Hair analysis captures chronic exposure (weeks to months)
- Supports [[nutritional immunity]] framework: metal dyshomeostasis as both cause and consequence

# Methodological Notes

- Reasonable sample size (n = 181) with severity stratification
- ICP-MS provides reliable multi-element quantification
- Hair as biomarker captures chronic but not acute exposure
- Cross-sectional design — cannot determine causality
- Excluded children on metal chelation agents, mineral supplements
- Chinese population — may not generalize to other regions
