# Gut Virome Alterations in Parkinson's Disease

## Summary

This study characterizes the gut [[virome]] in [[parkinsons-disease]] patients using metagenomic sequencing, revealing significant alterations in bacteriophage communities. The viral component of the gut microbiome has been largely overlooked in PD research despite phages being the primary regulators of bacterial community composition.

## Key Findings

- PD patients showed distinct gut virome composition compared to healthy controls
- Bacteriophages targeting [[lactococcus]] and other lactic acid bacteria were enriched in PD
- Phage-driven lysis of beneficial bacteria may contribute to the bacterial [[dysbiosis]] observed in PD
- *Lactococcus* phage enrichment correlated with depletion of *Lactococcus* bacteria in PD
- The phageome showed greater variability in PD patients than controls
- Virome alterations may precede or amplify bacterial community shifts in the gut

## Relevance to Gut-Metal-Microbiome Axis

Bacteriophage-mediated killing of beneficial bacteria represents a previously unrecognized mechanism for driving [[dysbiosis]] in [[parkinsons-disease]]. Phage predation on lactic acid bacteria (which are metal-binding and SCFA-producing commensals) removes a key component of the gut's metal buffering capacity. The resulting expansion of metal-tolerant opportunists further disrupts [[iron]] and [[zinc]] homeostasis. Phage activity is itself influenced by metal availability -- many phage enzymes require divalent cations for DNA injection and replication, connecting the virome to the metallomic framework.

## Limitations

- Cross-sectional design
- Virome characterization is technically challenging with high proportion of uncharacterized viral sequences ("viral dark matter")
- Small cohort
- Cannot distinguish cause from consequence
- Medication effects on virome not fully addressed
