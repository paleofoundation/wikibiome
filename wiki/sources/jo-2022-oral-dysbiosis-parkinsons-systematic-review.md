# Association Between Oral Dysbiosis and Parkinson's Disease: A Systematic Review

## Summary

This systematic review examines the emerging evidence for oral [[microbiome]] [[dysbiosis]] in [[parkinsons-disease]], extending the focus beyond gut bacteria to include the oral microbial community as a potential contributor to PD pathogenesis.

## Key Findings

- Multiple studies report altered oral microbiome composition in PD patients
- *[[prevotella]]* and *[[streptococcus-pneumoniae]]* species show altered abundance in the PD oral microbiome
- Periodontal pathogens are enriched in PD oral samples
- Oral dysbiosis may precede gut dysbiosis as oral microbes are continuously swallowed
- Periodontal disease is more prevalent in PD and may contribute to systemic inflammation
- The oral-gut-brain axis represents an extended pathway for microbiome-driven neurodegeneration

## Relevance to Gut-Metal-Microbiome Axis

Oral [[dysbiosis]] in [[parkinsons-disease]] adds an upstream dimension to the metallomic-microbiome framework. The oral cavity is the first site of dietary metal exposure, and oral bacteria play a role in metal speciation and bioavailability before metals reach the gut. Periodontal pathogens like *Porphyromonas gingivalis* have extensive [[iron]]-acquisition systems (hemolysins, hemin-binding proteins) and their enrichment in PD reflects a metal-favoring oral environment. Oral microbes continuously seed the gut, potentially transplanting metal-tolerant pathobionts that contribute to downstream gut [[dysbiosis]].

## Limitations

- Systematic review of heterogeneous studies
- Oral microbiome studies in PD are still limited in number
- Different sampling sites (saliva vs. plaque vs. tongue) yield different profiles
- Medication effects on oral microbiome not well characterized
- Causality between oral dysbiosis and PD not established
