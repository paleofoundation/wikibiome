# Circulating Saturated Fatty Acids and Incident CKD

## Key Findings

- This de-novo meta-analysis examined whether **circulating saturated fatty acid levels** are associated with the risk of developing [[chronic-kidney-disease]] in initially healthy individuals.
- Data were drawn from **multiple international prospective cohorts** participating in the Fatty Acids and Outcomes Research Consortium (FORCE), providing large sample sizes and diverse populations.
- The study used **biomarker-based fatty acid measurement** (circulating levels in plasma/serum or red blood cell membranes) rather than dietary questionnaires, providing more objective exposure assessment than self-reported dietary intake data.
- The prospective design allows assessment of temporal relationships between fatty acid profiles and subsequent CKD development.

## Methods

**De-novo meta-analysis of prospective cohort investigations** from the FORCE (Fatty Acids and Outcomes Research Consortium) consortium. FORCE is a collaborative research initiative that harmonizes individual-level data from multiple prospective cohort studies worldwide to examine relationships between circulating and tissue fatty acids and health outcomes. Individual saturated fatty acids were measured in blood compartments (plasma, serum, or erythrocyte membranes) using gas chromatography or related methods. Incident CKD was defined by study-specific criteria (typically eGFR decline below 60 mL/min/1.73m2 or development of albuminuria). Meta-analytic methods (likely random-effects models) were applied to pool results across cohorts.

## Detailed Summary

Lidgard et al. leveraged the FORCE consortium infrastructure to examine a question with significant public health implications: whether the type of circulating saturated fatty acids — reflecting both dietary intake and endogenous metabolism — predicts the development of CKD. Unlike dietary questionnaire-based studies, which are subject to recall bias and imprecise exposure assessment, biomarker-based studies measure actual circulating fatty acid concentrations, providing a more reliable exposure metric.

The FORCE consortium approach — harmonizing individual-level data across multiple cohorts — provides statistical power and population diversity that single-cohort studies cannot achieve. This is particularly important for CKD, where incident cases may be relatively few in any single general-population cohort.

The focus on individual saturated fatty acids (rather than total saturated fat) is scientifically important because different saturated fatty acids have distinct metabolic effects. For example, even-chain saturated fatty acids (palmitic, stearic acid) primarily reflect de novo lipogenesis and dietary intake of animal fats, while odd-chain saturated fatty acids (pentadecanoic, heptadecanoic acid) are biomarkers of dairy fat intake and may have different health implications.

## Relevance to WikiBiome

This study informs WikiBiome's understanding of the **metabolic and dietary risk factors** for CKD. Circulating fatty acid profiles are influenced by both diet and gut microbial metabolism — gut bacteria modify dietary fatty acids through biohydrogenation, conjugation, and chain elongation. Altered gut microbiome composition in pre-CKD states could therefore influence circulating fatty acid profiles, potentially creating a link between dysbiosis and CKD risk.

The study's focus on circulating biomarkers rather than dietary intake also connects to the broader metabolomics approach to CKD biomarker discovery, complementing studies on uremic toxins and amino acid metabolites.
