# Emerging Role of the Host Microbiome in Neuropsychiatric Disorders: Overview and Future Directions

## Key Claims

- The host microbiota encompasses oral, nasal, lung, gut, skin, bladder, and vaginal communities, all potentially influencing neuropsychiatric disorders
- Oral microbiota imbalance and periodontitis increase risk for neuropsychiatric disorders; postmortem brain samples detected widespread oral bacteria in brains of Alzheimer's patients
- Salivary microbiome in drug-naive first-episode [[schizophrenia]] patients showed higher alpha-diversity, lower beta-diversity, and H2S-producing bacteria with disease-stage-specific enrichment correlating with symptom severity
- Periodontal disease significantly higher in patients with MCI than healthy controls; oral [[bacteroides]] enrichment in Alzheimer's patients
- Gut microbiota of patients with periodontal disease showed Bacteroidetes enrichment consistent with the oral-gut axis
- Nasal microbiota may influence the brain through olfactory nerve pathways (transcellular, paracellular, and intracellular routes)
- Microbial density varies dramatically across GI locations: stomach (10^1/g) to colon (10^12/g)

## Methods/Findings

- **Study type**: Expert review (Molecular Psychiatry)
- **Scope**: Comprehensive coverage of host microbiome across body sites in relation to neuropsychiatric disorders
- **Key organisms discussed**: Oral bacteria in brain tissue, salivary microbiome in schizophrenia, nasal microbiota and olfactory pathway
