# Ovarian Cancer and Body Size: Individual Participant Meta-Analysis

## Key Findings

- **OC risk increased significantly with height** (RR 1.07 per 5 cm, p<0.001), independent of BMI and other confounders ([[collaborative-2012-ovarian-cancer-body-size-meta-analysis]], systematic-review-meta-analysis, n=25,157 cases)
- **BMI positively associated with OC risk in never-HRT users** (RR 1.10 per 5 kg/m2), but this association was attenuated in ever-HRT users
- **BMI-OC association was limited to non-serous histological subtypes**, suggesting distinct etiological pathways for different OC histologies
- **47 epidemiological studies** were pooled in this individual participant data meta-analysis, representing the largest analysis of body size and OC risk
- **HRT use modified the BMI-OC relationship**, indicating that exogenous hormone exposure interacts with adiposity-related cancer risk

## Methods

Individual participant data meta-analysis pooling data from 47 epidemiological studies worldwide (including both case-control and cohort designs). Included 25,157 women with ovarian cancer and 81,311 controls. Analyses stratified by HRT use, histological subtype, menopausal status, and other potential confounders. Relative risks estimated per unit increase in height and BMI using logistic regression with study as a random effect. <!-- NEEDS VERIFICATION: exact statistical model details -->

## Detailed Summary

This landmark individual participant data meta-analysis examined the relationship between body size measures (height, BMI, weight) and ovarian cancer risk using harmonized data from 47 epidemiological studies. The analysis found that both height and BMI were positively associated with OC risk, though the BMI association was notably modified by HRT use status. In women who had never used HRT, each 5 kg/m2 increase in BMI was associated with approximately 10% increased OC risk. However, this association was attenuated or absent in HRT users, suggesting that the hormonal pathway linking adiposity to OC is overshadowed or modified by exogenous hormone exposure.

The histological subtype analysis revealed that the BMI-OC association was primarily driven by non-serous subtypes, indicating that adiposity may promote ovarian carcinogenesis through pathways distinct from those driving serous tumors. This has implications for understanding the metabolic and hormonal microenvironment of different OC subtypes.

- Why is the association limited to non-serous subtypes — does this reflect distinct microenvironmental or metabolic differences?
