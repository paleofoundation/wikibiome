# Intestinal Fungi and Heart Failure Progression in CKD

## Key Findings

- **Ascomycota and Candida-Lodderomyces clade fungi were significantly enriched** in [[chronic-kidney-disease]] patients with concurrent heart failure (CKD+HF), compared to CKD patients without heart failure and healthy controls.
- **CKD mice receiving dual fungal and uremic toxin treatment** showed decreased left ventricular ejection fraction (LVEF) and left ventricular fractional shortening (LVFS), demonstrating that intestinal fungi can directly contribute to cardiac dysfunction in the CKD context.
- **Intestinal fungi are associated with CKD-HF progression**, providing evidence for a gut-heart axis mediated by fungal dysbiosis in kidney disease.
- **Gut fungi represent a novel therapeutic target** for CKD-associated heart failure, with antifungal or mycobiome-modulating strategies potentially addressing the cardiorenal syndrome.
- The study combines clinical mycobiome profiling with experimental animal validation, strengthening the causal inference.

## Methods

- **Design**: Translational study combining clinical cross-sectional mycobiome profiling with experimental mouse model validation.
- **Clinical cohort**: CKD patients, CKD+HF patients, and healthy controls with 18S rRNA gene sequencing of fecal samples for fungal community profiling.
- **Animal model**: C57BL/6J mice with CKD induction, treated with intestinal fungi and uremic toxins. Cardiac function assessed by echocardiography (LVEF, LVFS).
- **Analysis**: Differential abundance of fungal taxa across clinical groups, echocardiographic outcomes in mouse model.

## Detailed Summary

Heart failure is the leading cause of death in CKD patients, yet the mechanisms linking kidney and cardiac dysfunction remain incompletely understood. This study investigated whether intestinal fungi contribute to the cardiorenal syndrome. Clinical mycobiome profiling revealed that Ascomycota — the largest fungal phylum, including Candida and related genera — were enriched specifically in CKD patients who also had heart failure, not just in CKD alone. This suggests that the mycobiome shift is associated with the cardiac complication rather than kidney disease per se. The mouse model experiments provided mechanistic support: when CKD mice were exposed to both intestinal fungi and uremic toxins, cardiac function declined more severely than with either insult alone, indicating a synergistic interaction between fungal [[dysbiosis]] and uremia in driving cardiac damage. The Candida-Lodderomyces clade enrichment is particularly relevant because these organisms produce metabolites (including candidalysin and other cytolysins) that can activate systemic inflammatory pathways implicated in cardiomyopathy.

## Relevance to WikiBiome

Establishes a mycobiome link in the cardiorenal syndrome, extending the gut-kidney axis to include a gut-heart component mediated by fungi. Relevant to [[chronic-kidney-disease]] and [[candida-albicans]] entity pages. Supports the interkingdom relationships concept — fungal contributions to CKD complications are mechanistically distinct from bacterial contributions. The LVEF/LVFS data provide hard outcome measures linking mycobiome changes to organ function.

## Open Questions

- Which specific fungal metabolites mediate the cardiac damage observed in the CKD+fungi mouse model.
- Whether antifungal therapy (systemic or gut-targeted) could improve cardiac outcomes in CKD patients with heart failure.
- How bacterial-fungal interactions in the CKD gut influence the cardiac effects — whether bacteria facilitate or antagonize fungal pathogenicity.
