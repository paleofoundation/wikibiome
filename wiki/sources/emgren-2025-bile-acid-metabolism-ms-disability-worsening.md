# Bile Acid Metabolism in Multiple Sclerosis Is Perturbed and Associated with the Risk of Confirmed Disability Worsening

## Summary

This large population-based study from the Swedish EIMS cohort examined serum [[bile-acids]] in 907 newly diagnosed MS patients and 907 matched controls, with clinical follow-up data from the Swedish MS Registry (median 10.5 years). It is the largest study to date linking bile acid levels to MS disability progression.

## Key Findings

### Bile Acid Alterations in MS
- Lower concentrations of glycochenodeoxycholic acid (GCDCA) in MS (log2 FC=-0.29, p=0.009) compared to controls
- In RRMS specifically: lower primary conjugated BAs (log2 FC=-0.30, p=8.40E-5), secondary conjugated BAs (log2 FC=-0.18, p=0.007), and total BAs (log2 FC=-0.22, p=2.99E-4)
- In PMS group: no significant changes detected

### Sex-Specific Differences
- **Males showed more substantial BA alterations** than females
- In men: significantly lower total BAs, primary conjugated BAs, and primary non-conjugated BAs compared to male controls
- In female pwMS with RRMS: lower concentrations found only for GLCA (glycolithocholic acid, p=0.002)
- These sex differences may partially explain the different MS disease course between sexes

### Bile Acids and Disability Worsening
- Elevated total BA levels associated with increased risk of confirmed disability worsening (CDW): HR 1.22, 95% CI 1.08-1.39
- Primary conjugated BAs (HR 1.19, 95% CI 1.06-1.33) and secondary conjugated BAs (HR 1.21, 95% CI 1.08-1.39) both associated with CDW risk
- For reaching EDSS 4.0: GDCA (HR 1.19, 95% CI 1.05-1.36, p=0.008) was significant after multiple comparison adjustment
- In male MS subjects: TCA (HR 1.22), GHDCA (HR 1.23), and TDCA (HR 1.22) associated with risk of reaching EDSS 4.0

## Relevance to Gut-Metal-Microbiome Axis

This study provides the strongest population-level evidence linking [[bile-acids]] to MS disability progression:

1. **Secondary bile acids require gut bacterial metabolism** - their depletion in MS reflects [[dysbiosis]] affecting bile acid-transforming bacteria (Clostridia, *Bacteroides*), the same taxa sensitive to [[heavy-metals]]
2. **Sex-specific BA alterations** (more prominent in males) may reflect sex differences in metal metabolism, gut microbiome composition, and bile acid conjugation
3. **Conjugated BA elevation predicts disability worsening** - this suggests that impaired bacterial deconjugation (a function of metal-sensitive gut bacteria) creates a harmful bile acid profile
4. **Consistent with Bhargava et al. 2020** findings of altered bile acid metabolism in MS, now validated in a much larger cohort with longitudinal disability data

## Limitations

- Serum BA levels reflect hepatic and gut bacterial metabolism combined
- Gut microbiome not directly measured
- Dietary habits not comprehensively assessed
- Treatments during follow-up may confound disability worsening analysis
