# Brain Virome Dysbiosis in Parkinson's Disease and Multiple System Atrophy

## Summary

This pilot study provides the first evidence of a brain virome in the human putamen and suggests a potential link between virome [[dysbiosis]] and neurodegenerative disease. Whole-genome sequencing data from 32 post-mortem putamen samples (PD n=10, MSA n=10, healthy controls n=12) were analyzed using MetaPhlAn4 for virome profiling.

## Key Findings

- Neurodegenerative samples exhibited significantly higher virome alpha diversity compared to controls (Shannon p<0.001, Simpson p<0.001)
- Significant beta diversity differences (Bray-Curtis p=0.001, Jaccard p=0.001)
- **Nine viral species enriched** in neurodegenerative group: *Pestivirus A*, *Pestivirus Giraffe-1*, *Woolly monkey sarcoma virus*, *Abelson murine leukemia virus*, *Murine osteosarcoma virus*, *Human endogenous retrovirus K*, *Salmonella virus SP6*, *Taterapox virus*, and *Saccharomyces cerevisiae killer virus M1*
- *Alcelaphine gammaherpesvirus 1* was more abundant in controls
- Core virome composition profiles differed markedly between disease and healthy groups

## Relevance to Gut-Metal-Microbiome Axis

While focused on Parkinson's disease and MSA rather than [[multiple-sclerosis]], this study establishes that the brain harbors a virome that is dysbiotic in neurodegenerative disease. This extends the [[gut-brain-axis]] concept to include viral communities. The enrichment of endogenous retroviruses (HERV-K) in disease is particularly relevant to MS, where HERV activation has been implicated in pathogenesis. The [[gut-microbiome]] includes a vast phageome (viral component) that influences bacterial composition and can translocate viral elements systemically, potentially contributing to brain virome [[dysbiosis]].

## Limitations

- Very small sample size (32 total)
- Post-mortem tissue only
- Focused on PD/MSA, not MS
- Cannot determine if virome changes are cause or consequence of neurodegeneration
