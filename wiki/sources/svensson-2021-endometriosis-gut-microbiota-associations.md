# Key Claims

- Both alpha and beta diversity of gut microbiota are significantly higher in healthy controls compared to [[endometriosis]] patients
- 12 bacteria at genus level (after FDR correction) differed significantly between 66 endometriosis patients and 198 controls, belonging to classes Bacteroidia, Clostridia, Coriobacteriia, Bacilli, and Gammaproteobacter
- *Prevotella* abundance correlated with constipation (R=0.307, p=0.014) and vomiting/nausea (R=0.295, p=0.017)
- No significant differences in microbiota were found within the endometriosis cohort based on disease localization, symptoms, or hormonal treatment after FDR adjustment
- Fecal calprotectin correlated with *Ruminococcus* abundance (R=0.260, p=0.038)

# Methods/Findings

- **Study design**: Case-control study at Skane University Hospital, Malmo, Sweden
- **Participants**: 66 women with laparoscopy/laparotomy-confirmed endometriosis matched with 198 controls (3:1) by sex, age, BMI, and smoking from Malmo Offspring Study
- **Methods**: 16S rRNA V1-V3 sequencing of stool samples, VAS-IBS questionnaire

 - 86.4% of endometriosis patients had GI symptoms in prior 2 weeks
 - 19 bacteria differed before FDR correction; 12 survived correction
 - *Paraprevotella*, *Bacteroides*, and *Oscillospira* were among differentially abundant genera
 - Patients with hormonal treatment had altered *Blautia*, *Ruminococcus*, and *Butyricimonas*

# Relevance to Wiki

This is the largest human study of gut microbiota in [[endometriosis]] at time of publication, providing evidence that the disease is associated with reduced gut microbial diversity. The high prevalence of GI symptoms and their correlation with specific bacterial taxa supports the concept of a gut-reproductive axis in endometriosis. The findings complement animal studies showing [[dysbiosis]] in endometriosis and raise questions about whether microbial changes drive disease or vice versa.
