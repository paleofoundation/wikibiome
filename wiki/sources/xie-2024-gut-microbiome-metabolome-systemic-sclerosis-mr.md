# The Effect of Gut Microbiome and Plasma Metabolome on Systemic Sclerosis: A Bidirectional Two-Sample Mendelian Randomization Study

## Summary

This bidirectional MR study examines causal relationships between 196 gut microbiome taxa, 1,400 plasma metabolites, and systemic sclerosis (SSc) using data from MiBioGen (n=18,340), GWAS Catalog, and a European SSc meta-analysis (9,095 cases, 17,584 controls).

## Key Findings

**Risk factors for SSc:**
- *Victivallaceae* (family): OR = 1.469 (p = 0.009)
- *LachnospiraceaeUCG004* (genus): OR = 1.548 (p = 0.04)
- Pregnenediol disulfate (C21H34O8S2) levels: OR = 1.164 (p = 0.041)

**Protective factors for SSc:**
- *Prevotella7* (genus): OR = 0.759 (p = 0.048)
- Sphingomyelin levels: OR = 0.821 (p = 0.045)

**Reverse MR:** No causal effect of SSc on gut microbiome or metabolites was found, suggesting unidirectional causality.

## Relevance to Gut-Metal-Microbiome Axis

While focused on systemic sclerosis rather than [[multiple-sclerosis]], this study demonstrates causal links between specific gut taxa and autoimmune disease through MR methodology. The protective role of *Prevotella7* parallels findings in MS where *Prevotella* depletion is consistently associated with disease. The *Lachnospiraceae* risk association is noteworthy as this family includes both [[short-chain-fatty-acids]]-producing beneficial species and potentially pathogenic members. The metabolite findings (pregnenediol, sphingomyelin) suggest that [[gut-microbiome]]-derived metabolic signals influence autoimmune susceptibility, a principle likely shared with MS.

## Limitations

- SSc is a distinct autoimmune condition from MS
- European ancestry only
- Reverse MR may be underpowered
