# Oral fungal dysbiosis and systemic immune dysfunction in Chinese patients with schizophrenia

## Summary

This cross-sectional study enrolled 118 Chinese schizophrenia (SZ) patients and 97 age-matched healthy controls to evaluate the oral fungal microbiota from tongue coating samples using ITS1 amplicon sequencing and assess host immunity via multiplex immunoassays. It identified distinct fungal clusters (mycotypes) associated with schizophrenia and correlated them with systemic [[neuroinflammation]] markers.

## Key Findings

- SZ patients exhibited reduced fungal richness and significant differences in beta-diversity compared to HC
- Two distinct fungal clusters identified: increased *Malassezia* and decreased *Candida* levels in SZ
- *Malassezia* mycotype correlated positively with peripheral pro-inflammatory cytokines (IL-6, TNF-alpha, MCP-1)
- *Candida* mycotype exhibited negative correlation with pro-inflammatory cytokines
- Key functional oral fungi may serve as potential diagnostic biomarkers for SZ
- SZ patients displayed signs of immunological dysfunction characterized by elevated pro-inflammatory cytokines
- Altered oral fungal communities and systemic immune dysfunction demonstrated for the first time in Chinese SZ patients

## Relevance to Gut-Metal-Microbiome Axis

This study extends the concept of [[dysbiosis]] beyond bacteria to include the oral mycobiome in schizophrenia. The enrichment of *Malassezia* and its correlation with inflammatory cytokines suggests a fungal contribution to [[neuroinflammation]]. Fungal species are known to sequester metals, particularly [[iron]], [[copper]], and [[zinc]], and altered fungal communities could disrupt metal homeostasis in the oral-gut axis. The [[immune-balance]] disruption evidenced by cytokine profiling supports systemic inflammatory pathways connecting oral [[dysbiosis]] to brain dysfunction via the [[gut-brain-axis]].

## Limitations

- Cross-sectional design; cannot determine causality
- Oral microbiome may not directly reflect gut fungal communities
- Medication effects not fully accounted for
- Chinese population may limit generalizability
