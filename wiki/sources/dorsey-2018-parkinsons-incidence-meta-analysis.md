# The Incidence of Parkinson's Disease: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis

## Summary

This systematic review and meta-analysis provides comprehensive estimates of [[parkinsons-disease]] incidence worldwide, examining trends over time and geographic variation in disease occurrence.

## Key Findings

- Global PD incidence is estimated at 10-18 per 100,000 person-years overall
- Incidence increases sharply with age, peaking in the 70-79 age group
- Men have ~1.5x higher incidence than women
- Geographic variation exists, with higher rates in North America and Europe than Asia
- PD incidence may be increasing over time, even after accounting for aging populations
- Industrialized regions show higher rates, suggesting environmental contributing factors

## Relevance to Gut-Metal-Microbiome Axis

The epidemiological patterns of [[parkinsons-disease]] incidence are consistent with the metallomic-microbiome hypothesis. The age-dependent rise matches cumulative metal exposure and progressive microbiome deterioration with aging. The male predominance may reflect higher occupational metal exposure and lack of estrogen's metal-binding protective effects. Geographic variation correlating with industrialization suggests environmental metal pollution as a contributing factor. The potential temporal increase in PD incidence parallels increased dietary metal contamination from industrial food systems and antibiotic-driven [[dysbiosis]].

## Limitations

- Meta-analysis of heterogeneous epidemiological studies
- Case ascertainment methods vary across studies and regions
- Diagnostic criteria and awareness differ by geography and time period
- Environmental exposure data not integrated into incidence estimates
