## Key Findings

- **40 distinct viruses** have been detected in human semen, spanning families including *Papillomaviridae*, *Herpesviridae*, *Retroviridae*, *Flaviviridae*, *Hepadnaviridae*, *Coronaviridae*, and others. Each virus has distinct effects on semen parameters and reproductive health.
- **Three primary mechanisms** of viral damage to male fertility are identified: (A) genital tract inflammation (leukocyte infiltration, cytokine release, fibrosis), (B) direct viral replication in Leydig cells, Sertoli cells, germ cells, and macrophages, and (C) systemic effects including oxidative stress, hypothalamic-pituitary-gonadal axis perturbation, and fever-induced spermatogenic impairment.
- **Virus-bacteria and virus-fungus co-infections** in semen are common; lower viral diversity in semen samples is significantly positively correlated with successful pregnancy rates. The microbiota is "highly associated with viral infections (such as HIV and HPV) and disease states," suggesting that considering viruses alone is insufficient.
- **HCV infection** is specifically associated with decreased semen quality, abnormal hormone levels, and **erectile dysfunction** (the only direct ED mention in the review).
- **Oxidative stress** is a shared downstream mechanism: chronic viral infections increase reactive oxygen species (ROS) in testicular tissue, damaging sperm DNA integrity, motility, and morphology.

## Methods

- **Study design:** Narrative literature review (not a systematic review or meta-analysis — no PRISMA protocol, no structured search strategy reported beyond PubMed/Embase/Web of Science keyword searches).
- **Scope:** Consolidation of findings on 40 viruses detected in human semen, their characteristics, modes of transmission, effects on the reproductive and endocrine systems, pathogenic mechanisms, and treatment prospects.
- **Databases searched:** PubMed, Embase, Web of Science. Search terms included "semen," "sperm," "virus," and "sexual transmission." A secondary search used "Virus" and "semen" to identify evidence of sexual transmission. 4,815 results screened; abstracts and full-text articles assessed.

## Detailed Summary

### Virus Families and Reproductive Effects

The review catalogs 40 viruses in a comprehensive table (Table 1), organized by family, genus, genome type, clinical presentation, sexual transmission status, and effect on male reproductive health. Key virus groups:

**HPV (Papillomaviridae):** Prevalence in semen ranges from 6% to 46.2%, averaging 17.1%. HPV in semen is associated with abnormal semen parameters, increased sperm DNA fragmentation index, formation of anti-sperm antibodies, placental dysfunction, and pregnancy loss. HPV DNA on sperm surface can be removed by hyaluronidase-based washing. ESHRE guidelines recommend HPV testing before ART.

**Herpesviruses (Herpesviridae):** HSV-1/2 detected at rates of 7.5–22.9% in semen. HSV-1 associated with decreased sperm count; HSV-2 with hematospermia. CMV prevalence 6–56.9% in semen; associated with decreased sperm count and motility. EBV prevalence 0.4–45% in semen; may be associated with leukocytospermia. VZV, HHV-6, HHV-7, HHV-8 also discussed with limited evidence.

**Hepatitis viruses:** HBV found in semen; can integrate into sperm chromosomes leading to chromosomal aberrations and hereditary effects via vertical transmission. HBV triggers cell apoptosis, sperm DNA fragmentation, and reduced fertilization capacity. HCV in semen correlates with decreased semen quality, abnormal hormone levels, and **erectile dysfunction**. HDV detected in follicular fluid and semen. HEV and HGV have limited evidence.

**HIV (Retroviridae):** Abnormal sperm morphology, increased semen pH, decreased sperm motility, decreased CD4+ T-cell counts in semen. HIV-associated changes include testicular morphology deterioration, decreased testosterone, destruction of spermatogenic tubules, and testicular injury. Sperm washing procedures can reduce risk of vertical transmission.

**SARS-CoV-2 (Coronaviridae):** Conflicting evidence on semen detection. Some studies detect viral RNA in semen; others do not. ACE2 and TMPRSS2 expression in testes suggests susceptibility. Associated with decreased sperm concentration, motility, testosterone, and increased sperm DNA fragmentation. Vaccination does not negatively affect fertility.

**Zika virus (Flaviviridae):** Can persist in semen for more than six months. Causes testicular damage, impaired spermatogenesis, and potential sexual transmission. DNA-based vaccination and attenuated live vaccines show promise.

**Other viruses:** Ebola (shedding persists for extended periods in semen), polyomaviruses (JCPyV and BKPyV most relevant), mumps (orchitis causing infertility), adenovirus (AAV), monkeypox, HTLV, West Nile, Dengue, Chikungunya, Coxsackie, Marburg, Rift Valley fever, adenovirus, Chapare, Toscana, Lassa, SFTSV, Nipah, and Yellow fever viruses.

### Pathogenic Mechanisms (Figure 1)

Three primary pathways:

1. **Genital tract inflammation:** Leukocyte infiltration leads to fibrosis in testicular tissues. Cytokines released during inflammation trigger germ cell apoptosis and disrupt cell functions.
2. **Viral replication in testicular cells:** Leydig cells (altered hormone production), Sertoli cells (disrupted nourishment and development of germ cells), germ cells (direct damage to sperm quality and DNA integrity), macrophages (local inflammatory responses).
3. **Systemic effects:** Oxidative stress (chronic infections increase OS, harming sperm function), hypothalamic-pituitary-gonadal axis perturbation (altered hormone release, affecting testosterone and inhibin B secretion), fever (acute infections raise testicular temperature, impairing spermatogenesis).

### Microbiota-Virus Interactions

The review notes that "the microbiota is also highly associated with viral infections (such as HIV and HPV) and disease states" and that "considering viruses as the sole basis for disease is not entirely accurate." The authors emphasize complex interactions between different pathogens, resident microbiota, and immune responses in the semen microenvironment. Lower viral diversity in semen correlates with higher pregnancy success. Virus-bacteria co-infections, virus-fungus co-infections, and even virus-fungus-bacteria co-infections are documented in semen.

### Limitations

The authors acknowledge the review primarily relies on published literature and known data, which may overlook some viral infections. The lack of large-scale clinical studies necessitates more experiments and clinical data to validate findings.

## Contradictions / Tensions

- **SARS-CoV-2 in semen:** The review documents significant conflicting evidence — some studies detect SARS-CoV-2 RNA in semen while others find no viral presence. The review does not fully resolve this tension, noting it requires further large-scale studies.
- **HPV and fertility:** Some studies find HPV in semen has no impact on fertility outcomes (spontaneous fertility, IVF outcomes) while others find significant impairment. The review acknowledges this inconsistency.
- **CMV and infertility:** Some studies suggest CMV presence in semen is not related to male infertility, while others associate it with decreased sperm count and motility.

## Open Questions

1. **What is the role of semen microbiota in modulating viral pathogenicity?** The review flags virus-microbiota interactions as important but provides no mechanistic detail — this is a gap that could connect to WikiBiome's ecological framework if metal dependencies of semen-associated bacteria are identified.
2. **Does chronic viral infection in the reproductive tract alter the local metal microenvironment?** Inflammation-driven nutritional immunity responses (calprotectin, lactoferrin) in the genital tract are plausible but unaddressed by this review.
3. **Are the oxidative stress effects of semen viral infections mediated through metal-dependent pathways?** ROS generation in testicular tissue may involve iron-catalyzed Fenton reactions, but this review does not explore that connection.
4. **Can the HCV-erectile dysfunction association be explained through microbiome or metallomic mechanisms?** The review cites the association without mechanistic explanation.
