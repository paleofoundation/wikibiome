# Key Claims

- **Testable model proposed**: diet high in fat/gluten and low in resistant starch may be primary driver of gut [[dysbiosis]]
- **Dysbiosis causes lack of butyrate production** by gut bacteria, leading to permeable gut followed by autoimmunity
- **Bacteroides genus** (especially B. dorei) strongly associated with T1D in Finnish cohort
- **DNA methylation analysis** of gut microbiome may be warranted for thorough epigenetic understanding
- **Differences in microbiome composition** between autoimmune and healthy children not consistent across all studies due to strong environmental influences

# Methods & Findings

Review describing model for gut microbiome role in T1D:

- NOD mice and BioBreeding diabetes-prone rats have distinctly different gut microbiomes vs healthy rodents
- Treating diabetes-prone rodents with [[probiotics]] or antibiotics prevents disease development
- Diet and geography are confounding factors of microbiome composition

# Relevance to Metallomic Framework

- **[[zinc]]**: Butyrate production by gut bacteria requires zinc-dependent enzymes -- zinc deficiency creates butyrate deficit driving [[intestinal-permeability]]
- **[[iron]]**: Bacteroides species are iron-dependent organisms; iron availability in the gut selects for their dominance
- **[[selenium]]**: Selenium-dependent antioxidant enzymes protect gut epithelium from damage during butyrate deficiency
- **[[gut-metal-microbiome]]**: The dietary model proposed directly connects to metal availability -- resistant starch fermentation requires metal cofactors
- **[[dysbiosis]]**: Metal-nutrient interactions are upstream of the dysbiosis-butyrate-permeability-autoimmunity cascade
- **[[intestinal-permeability]]**: Zinc and selenium are structural components of tight junctions disrupted in the proposed model
