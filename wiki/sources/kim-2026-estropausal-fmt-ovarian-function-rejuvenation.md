# Estropausal Gut Microbiota Transplant Improves Ovarian Function

## Key Findings

- **Heterochronic FMT from young mice remodeled the ovarian transcriptome** in aged recipients, reducing inflammation-related gene expression and upregulating genes associated with healthy ovarian function ([[kim-2026-estropausal-fmt-ovarian-function-rejuvenation]], animal-model).
- **FMT induced transcriptional signatures consistent with ovarian rejuvenation** — the ovarian gene expression profile of aged FMT recipients shifted toward a younger pattern.
- **Enhanced ovarian health and increased fertility** were observed in recipient mice, providing functional evidence that gut microbiota directly influence ovarian biology.
- **Establishes a causal gut-ovarian axis** — the microbiome is not merely correlated with ovarian aging but actively drives aspects of it.

## Methods

Heterochronic fecal microbiota transplant: fecal material from young ("estropausal" pre-menopausal-equivalent) female mice transplanted into aged (post-reproductive) female mice. Ovarian transcriptomics (RNA-seq) performed on recipient ovaries to assess gene expression changes. Shotgun metagenomic sequencing of gut microbiome composition pre- and post-FMT. Fertility assessed by mating trials and litter counts. Inflammatory markers and ovarian histology evaluated. Controls received age-matched (isochronic) FMT. <!-- NEEDS VERIFICATION: exact mouse ages, strain, FMT protocol details, and sample sizes per group -->

## Detailed Summary

Kim et al. demonstrate that the age-related decline in ovarian function is partially reversible through gut microbiome rejuvenation — a finding with profound implications for understanding the gut-reproductive axis.

**The estrobolome connection.** This study sits at the intersection of two WikiBiome frameworks: the estrobolome (Primitive 7 — beta-glucuronidase activity and estrogen recirculation) and ecological engineering (Primitive 5 — two-sided restoration of microbial function). As the gut microbiome ages, its composition shifts — typically losing diversity and SCFA-producing species while gaining inflammatory pathobionts. The estrobolome component of the microbiome (bacteria expressing beta-glucuronidase that deconjugate estrogens for reabsorption) may also decline, potentially reducing estrogen recirculation and contributing to menopausal hormone changes.

**Transcriptomic rejuvenation.** RNA-seq of ovaries from aged mice that received young-donor FMT showed significant remodeling of the transcriptome compared to controls. Inflammation-related gene sets were downregulated, while genes associated with follicle development, steroidogenesis, and cellular repair were upregulated. The overall expression pattern shifted toward that of younger ovaries — not completely, but significantly.

**Functional outcomes.** The transcriptomic changes translated to functional improvements: FMT recipients showed enhanced ovarian health by histological assessment and, remarkably, increased fertility in mating trials. This demonstrates that the microbiome's influence on ovarian function is not merely molecular but has physiologically meaningful consequences.

**Causal demonstration.** The FMT experimental design provides strong causal evidence for the gut-ovarian axis. Because the only intervention is microbial transplantation (no dietary change, no hormone supplementation, no drug), the ovarian improvements must be mediated by the transplanted microbiome. This parallels the causal evidence for gut-brain and gut-immune axes established by similar FMT approaches.

**Possible mediators.** While the study does not definitively identify the mediating molecules, candidate mechanisms include: (1) altered estrogen metabolism via estrobolome modulation; (2) reduced systemic inflammation through restoration of SCFA production and barrier function; (3) changes in bile acid metabolism affecting ovarian steroid signaling; (4) modulation of tryptophan metabolism and serotonin signaling.

## Open Questions

- Which specific microbial taxa or metabolites mediate the ovarian rejuvenation effect? Is it the estrobolome specifically, or broader anti-inflammatory effects?
- Does the FMT effect persist long-term, or does the aged host environment eventually reshape the transplanted microbiome back to an aged configuration?
- Can the effect be replicated with defined bacterial consortia rather than whole FMT — and if so, which species are essential?
- How does this finding translate to human menopause — is the mouse "estropause" model sufficiently analogous?
- Whether metal homeostasis changes during aging (e.g., iron accumulation, zinc depletion) interact with the microbiome aging trajectory.
