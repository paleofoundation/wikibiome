## Key Findings

- **Oxidative stress (OS) is a central mechanism in male infertility**, driving sperm DNA fragmentation (SDF), lipid peroxidation of PUFA-rich sperm membranes, and mitochondrial dysfunction. ROS at physiological levels are essential for capacitation, hyperactivation, and acrosome reaction, but overproduction from both endogenous (leukocytes, immature spermatozoa, mitochondria) and exogenous (toxic metals, pollution, lifestyle) sources tips the balance toward pathology ([[kurhaluk-2025-oxidative-stress-gut-microbiota-male-fertility]], narrative review).

- **Toxic metals amplify oxidative damage to sperm.** Cadmium exposure significantly increases infertility risk by elevating OS biomarkers (8-OHdG, 8-isoPGF2-alpha). Arsenic, lead, aluminium, and boron disrupt ion channels via ion mimicry, interfere with cell signalling (MAPK, Nrf2, NF-kB, PI3K), induce DNA methylation changes, and compromise the blood-testis barrier ([[kurhaluk-2025-oxidative-stress-gut-microbiota-male-fertility]], narrative review).

- **Gut microbiota dysbiosis impairs male fertility** through multiple pathways: endotoxaemia disrupts the blood-testis barrier and hypothalamic-pituitary-testicular axis; microbial metabolites (SCFAs, TMAO) modulate inflammation and hormone levels; beneficial taxa (*Lactobacillus*, *Bifidobacterium*) support anti-inflammatory states and testosterone regulation, while pathogens (*Escherichia coli*, *Enterococcus*) increase systemic OS and damage sperm DNA ([[kurhaluk-2025-oxidative-stress-gut-microbiota-male-fertility]], narrative review).

- **The Nrf2, AMPK/PGC-1-alpha, and NF-kB pathways** are key molecular regulators linking OS to male infertility. Nrf2 activation upregulates SOD, CAT, and GPx; AMPK/PGC-1-alpha enhances mitochondrial biogenesis; NF-kB dysregulation drives chronic testicular inflammation. Antioxidant-rich diets modulate all three pathways simultaneously ([[kurhaluk-2025-oxidative-stress-gut-microbiota-male-fertility]], narrative review).

- **Synergistic multi-target interventions** combining probiotics (*L. rhamnosus*, *L. reuteri*, *B. longum*, *B. bifidum*), prebiotics (inulin, FOS, GOS), antioxidants (selenium, zinc, CoQ10, vitamin C, vitamin E, N-acetylcysteine), adaptogens (ashwagandha, maca, Korean ginseng, *Tribulus terrestris*), and omega-3 fatty acids are proposed as superior to single-agent approaches for restoring male fertility ([[kurhaluk-2025-oxidative-stress-gut-microbiota-male-fertility]], narrative review).

## Methods

This is a **comprehensive narrative review** based on literature searches across the Cochrane Library, Medline, Embase, SciSearch, PubMed, Web of Science, Scopus, and Google Scholar. Search terms included "male infertility," "oxidative stress," "sperm quality," "gut microbiota," and "lifestyle factors and male reproductive health." Studies published between 2014 and 2024 were prioritised. Peer-reviewed clinical trials, systematic reviews, and meta-analyses were included; conference abstracts, non-peer-reviewed articles, and expert opinion without empirical data were excluded. A total of 247 references are cited.

## Detailed Summary

### Oxidative Stress and Sperm Quality

The review establishes that **approximately 50% of infertility cases** involve male factors, with oxidative stress identified as a critical contributor ([[kurhaluk-2025-oxidative-stress-gut-microbiota-male-fertility]], narrative review). Over 72 million people worldwide are affected by erectile dysfunction, which shares endothelial dysfunction and OS as underlying mechanisms with male infertility.

**Reactive oxygen species** are produced by sperm mitochondria (primary source) and plasma membrane NADPH oxidase. At physiological levels, ROS drive capacitation via cAMP/PKA signalling, tyrosine phosphorylation, and acrosome reaction. However, overproduction causes:

- **Sperm DNA fragmentation** — single- and double-strand breaks, 8-OHdG formation, chromatin damage. SDF below 15% correlates with better pregnancy outcomes.
- **Lipid peroxidation** — targets PUFA-rich sperm membranes, producing MDA and 4-hydroxynonenal, disrupting membrane integrity, fluidity, and motility.
- **Mitochondrial dysfunction** — electron transport chain leakage amplifies ROS in a vicious cycle, reducing ATP for motility.

The **antioxidant defence system** in seminal plasma includes enzymatic components (SOD, catalase, GPx — especially GPx4 for mitochondrial sheath integrity) and non-enzymatic components (vitamin C, alpha-tocopherol, pyruvate, glutathione, L-carnitine, taurine, hypotaurine, urate, albumin). The total radical-trapping antioxidant potential (TRAP) of seminal plasma is approximately 10 times higher than blood plasma.

### Toxic Metal Mechanisms

Environmental pollutants, particularly **toxic metals**, amplify OS through multiple mechanisms ([[kurhaluk-2025-oxidative-stress-gut-microbiota-male-fertility]], narrative review):

1. **Ion mimicry** — Cd, Pb displace essential cofactors (Zn, Ca) via calcium channels, disrupting protein folding and enzyme activity critical for spermatogenesis.
2. **Cell signalling disruption** — Toxic metals alter MAPK, Nrf2, NF-kB, and PI3K pathways, impairing cellular responses vital for reproduction.
3. **ROS generation** — Metals catalyse Fenton/Haber-Weiss reactions (Fe, Cu + H2O2 produce hydroxyl radicals), initiating lipid peroxidation cascades.
4. **Epigenetic modification** — Changes in DNA methylation and histone acetylation alter gene expression profiles critical for reproductive cell function.
5. **Blood-testis barrier disruption** — Toxic metals compromise tight junctions and adhesion molecules, allowing harmful substances to enter the testes.
6. **Mitochondrial pathway activation** — Metals trigger apoptosis and unfolded protein response in germ cells.

Research on Polish men found that aluminium, boron, cadmium, and arsenic concentrations, combined with genetic polymorphisms (MTHFR, IL-4), indirectly influence reproductive potential. Cadmium exposure was specifically identified as significantly increasing infertility risk via elevated MDA levels.

### Molecular Pathways: Nrf2, AMPK/PGC-1-alpha, NF-kB

Three key pathways link OS to male reproductive function ([[kurhaluk-2025-oxidative-stress-gut-microbiota-male-fertility]], narrative review):

- **Nrf2** (nuclear factor erythroid 2-related factor 2): Central regulator of antioxidant response. Keap1 releases Nrf2 during redox dysfunction; Nrf2 translocates to the nucleus and activates SOD, CAT, GPx, HO-1, NQO1 expression. Nrf2 and GPX4 protein levels are significantly reduced in oligospermia patients. Nrf2 knockout mice show reduced sperm concentration, motility, and fertility. Disruption leads to ferroptosis in spermatogenic cells.
- **AMPK/PGC-1-alpha**: Energy sensor pathway crucial for mitochondrial biogenesis in sperm. Activated by resveratrol and quercetin; enhances mitochondrial function and reduces ROS. Works synergistically with Nrf2.
- **NF-kB**: Inflammatory master regulator. Excessive activation leads to chronic testicular inflammation, spermatogenic cell apoptosis, and suppression of testosterone biosynthesis via StAR inhibition. Curcumin, vitamin E, and green tea polyphenols inhibit NF-kB activation.

### Gut Microbiota and Male Fertility

The review identifies multiple pathways through which gut dysbiosis impairs male fertility ([[kurhaluk-2025-oxidative-stress-gut-microbiota-male-fertility]], narrative review):

- **Gut-brain-testis axis**: The vagus nerve mediates bidirectional gut-brain communication. Microbiome imbalance activates the HPA axis (cortisol release), disrupts the vagovagal reflex (cholinergic anti-inflammatory pathway), and impairs hormonal regulation of testicular function.
- **Endotoxaemia**: Overgrowth of pathogens (*E. coli*, *Enterococcus*) increases LPS production, disrupting the blood-testis barrier, reducing testosterone, and increasing systemic inflammation.
- **Sex hormone metabolism**: Gut bacteria modulate sex hormone-related enzymes (aromatase, 5-alpha reductase) and influence enterohepatic circulation of sex hormones via the hypothalamic-pituitary-testicular axis.
- **Metabolite signalling**: SCFAs from beneficial bacteria support gut barrier integrity and modulate immune responses. TMAO overproduction from dysbiosis is associated with reduced fertility.
- **Beneficial taxa**: *Lactobacillus* and *Bifidobacterium* produce SCFAs, enhance gut barrier integrity, modulate immune responses, regulate the HPG axis, and support anti-inflammatory states conducive to spermatogenesis.

Key biomarkers linking gut health to fertility include: CRP (systemic inflammation), IL-6 (chronic inflammation), TNF-alpha (dysbiosis-associated), IgG (autoimmune responses from dysbiosis), T regulatory cells (immune tolerance), and interferon-gamma (gut health-associated immune responses).

### Synergistic Intervention Approaches

The review proposes a comprehensive multi-component strategy ([[kurhaluk-2025-oxidative-stress-gut-microbiota-male-fertility]], narrative review):

- **Probiotics**: *L. rhamnosus*, *L. reuteri*, *B. longum*, *B. bifidum* — reduce systemic inflammation, influence testosterone levels, support gut health.
- **Prebiotics**: Inulin, FOS, GOS — promote beneficial bacteria growth, reduce OS, balance hormones.
- **Antioxidants**: Selenium, zinc, CoQ10, vitamins C and E, N-acetylcysteine — protect sperm from oxidative damage.
- **Adaptogens**: Ashwagandha (inhibits lipid peroxidation, regulates HPA axis), maca (enhances sperm count/motility via NF-kappaB inhibition), Korean ginseng (reduces OS, improves immune function), *Tribulus terrestris* (stimulates LH release, enhances testosterone), shilajit (fulvic acid, trace minerals), fenugreek, saw palmetto, epimedium (icariin), red clover (phyto-oestrogens).
- **Omega-3 fatty acids**: Reduce inflammation, improve sperm membrane integrity.
- **Amino acids**: L-carnitine (mitochondrial energy), L-arginine (nitric oxide, blood flow).
- **Mediterranean diet pattern**: Associated with improved semen concentration, total sperm count, and motility in subfertile couples.

### Lifestyle Factors

The review catalogues lifestyle factors affecting sperm quality: psychological stress (HPA axis activation, cortisol elevation), sleep deprivation (reduced testosterone, oxidative damage), heat exposure (hyperthermia inhibits spermatogenesis, increases ROS), sedentary behaviour (obesity, hormonal imbalance), smoking (free radical introduction), alcohol (hormonal disruption, DNA fragmentation), and environmental toxins (pesticides, endocrine disruptors, heavy metals).

## Contradictions / Tensions

- The review frames the relationship between the gut microbiota and male fertility as "emerging" and requiring further investigation, yet presents substantial evidence for the gut-brain-testis axis. The strength of the evidence for direct microbiota-fertility links (as opposed to microbiota-inflammation-fertility links) varies considerably.
- The review discusses iron and copper primarily in the context of Fenton chemistry generating ROS, but does not address the nutritional immunity framework — whether elevated hepcidin or lactoferrin in the male reproductive tract represents host defence against iron-dependent pathogens rather than simple toxicity. This gap limits direct applicability to the WikiBiome metallomic interpretation.
- Some of the adaptogens discussed (red clover containing phyto-oestrogens) may conflict with the estrobolome framework if gut bacteria further metabolise these compounds. The review does not address this interaction.
- The evidence for synergistic multi-component interventions is largely theoretical; no RCTs testing the full proposed combination are cited.

## Open Questions

1. **Does gut dysbiosis drive male infertility independently of systemic inflammation?** The review presents both pathways but cannot distinguish direct microbiota-testis signalling from inflammation-mediated effects.
2. **What is the role of nutritional immunity in the male reproductive tract?** Are elevated zinc-binding proteins (calprotectin) or iron-sequestering proteins (lactoferrin) in seminal fluid markers of host defence against metal-dependent pathogens?
3. **How do toxic metals interact with gut microbiota composition?** The review discusses metals and microbiota separately but does not address whether Cd/Pb/As exposure reshapes gut ecology in ways that compound fertility damage.
4. **What are the optimal dosing and combination protocols for the proposed synergistic intervention?** The multi-component approach lacks clinical validation as a combined regimen.
5. **Do adaptogens modulate the gut microbiome?** If ashwagandha, maca, or ginseng alter microbial composition, this could be a key mechanism underlying their fertility benefits — and a connection to the ecological engineering primitive.
