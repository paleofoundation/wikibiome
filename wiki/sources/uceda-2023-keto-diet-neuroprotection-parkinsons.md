# Ketogenic Diet and Neuroprotection in Parkinson's Disease

## Summary

This review examines the neuroprotective mechanisms of the ketogenic diet in [[parkinsons-disease]], focusing on how beta-hydroxybutyrate (BHB) and other ketone bodies may protect dopaminergic neurons from degeneration.

## Key Findings

- BHB provides an alternative energy substrate for neurons with impaired glucose metabolism in PD
- Ketone bodies inhibit histone deacetylases (HDACs), upregulating neuroprotective gene expression
- The ketogenic diet reduces neuroinflammation by suppressing NLRP3 inflammasome activation
- BHB has direct antioxidant properties, scavenging hydroxyl radicals
- Mitochondrial function improves with ketone body utilization, bypassing Complex I deficiency in PD
- The ketogenic diet alters gut microbiome composition, though the PD-specific effects are unclear
- Animal models show reduced dopaminergic neuron loss on ketogenic diets

## Relevance to Gut-Metal-Microbiome Axis

The ketogenic diet's neuroprotective effects intersect with the metal axis at several points. BHB's HDAC inhibition mimics [[butyrate]]'s epigenetic effects, potentially compensating for the SCFA deficit in PD [[dysbiosis]]. The antioxidant activity of BHB directly counteracts [[iron]]- and [[manganese]]-catalyzed hydroxyl radical generation (Fenton chemistry). NLRP3 inflammasome suppression would reduce IL-1beta-driven [[hepcidin]] upregulation, normalizing systemic [[iron]] distribution. However, the ketogenic diet's very low fiber content would further deplete gut [[butyrate]] production and worsen the gut-metal-microbiome axis -- creating a paradox where systemic BHB benefits compete with local gut [[short-chain-fatty-acids]] depletion. Modified ketogenic diets with supplemental fiber may be needed to capture the neuroprotective benefits without exacerbating gut [[dysbiosis]] and metal dysregulation.

## Limitations

- Most evidence from animal models
- Human ketogenic diet trials in PD are small and short-term
- The gut microbiome effects of ketogenic diet in PD are understudied
- Long-term safety and adherence concerns
- Potential worsening of constipation and gut dysbiosis
