## Key Findings

- **Statins** were investigated as a novel therapeutic for preventing preterm delivery, based on their pleiotropic anti-inflammatory properties beyond cholesterol lowering ([[whitaker-2021-statins-preterm-delivery-prevention|this study]], animal-model).
- **Animal model evidence** demonstrated that statins could reduce inflammation-driven preterm labor in preclinical settings.
- **A Phase II feasibility trial** was conducted to assess the practicality and safety of administering statins during pregnancy for preterm delivery prevention.
- **The anti-inflammatory mechanism** is the primary therapeutic rationale — statins suppress NF-kB signaling, reduce pro-inflammatory cytokine production, and modulate immune cell function, all relevant to infection- and inflammation-driven preterm birth.

## Methods

- **Study type**: PhD thesis combining two study components.
- **Component 1**: Animal model of infection-induced preterm labor treated with statins; assessed anti-inflammatory effects and pregnancy outcomes.
- **Component 2**: Phase II clinical feasibility trial assessing the practicability, recruitment, and safety of statin administration during pregnancy in women at risk for preterm delivery.
- **Institution**: University of Edinburgh MRC Centre for Reproductive Health.

## Detailed Summary

This PhD thesis explores statins as a drug repurposing opportunity for preterm delivery prevention. Preterm birth (before 37 weeks gestation) remains a leading cause of neonatal morbidity and mortality worldwide. A significant proportion of preterm births are associated with intrauterine infection and inflammation, making anti-inflammatory agents a logical therapeutic target.

Statins (HMG-CoA reductase inhibitors) have well-documented anti-inflammatory effects independent of their cholesterol-lowering action. These include suppression of NF-kB pathway activation, reduction of pro-inflammatory cytokines (IL-6, TNF-alpha, IL-1beta), modulation of T-cell responses, and enhancement of endothelial function. These properties make them candidates for repurposing in inflammatory conditions of pregnancy.

The animal model component tested whether statins could prevent or delay preterm labor in an infection-driven model. The feasibility trial component addressed the practical challenges of conducting a statin trial in pregnant women, including recruitment, adherence, and safety monitoring.

The relevance to microbiome science lies in multiple connections: (1) statins are known to alter gut microbiome composition; (2) intrauterine infection — the driver of inflammation-related preterm birth — involves ascending vaginal and gut microbiota; (3) bile acid metabolism, which statins affect through cholesterol pathway modulation, is a key gut microbial function; (4) preterm delivery alters the infant's initial microbial colonization, with downstream effects on immune development and neurodevelopment.
