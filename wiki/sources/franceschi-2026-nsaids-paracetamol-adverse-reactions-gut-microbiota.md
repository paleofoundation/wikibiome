## Key Findings

- **NSAIDs are more commonly associated with GI bleeding, allergic reactions, and renal failure** in a retrospective analysis of 897 adverse drug reaction (ADR) patients seen over a 6-year period (2015-2021)
- **Gut microbiota composition and function modulate drug metabolism**, potentially determining individual susceptibility to ADRs through pharmacomicrobiomics mechanisms
- **Paracetamol hepatotoxicity** may be influenced by microbial metabolism of the drug and its reactive metabolites (NAPQI), with certain gut bacteria capable of biotransforming paracetamol
- **NSAID-induced GI damage** is partly mediated through disruption of the gut microbiome, creating a vicious cycle where drug-induced dysbiosis worsens GI mucosal integrity
- **Renal failure** as an NSAID adverse effect is relevant to [[chronic-kidney-disease]] management, where patients may already have compromised renal function and altered drug metabolism

## Methods

- **Study design**: Retrospective cohort analysis of patients presenting with adverse drug reactions to NSAIDs or paracetamol
- **Setting**: Hospital emergency department, 2015-2021
- **Sample size**: 897 patients with documented ADRs
- **Data collected**: Type of ADR (GI bleeding, allergic reaction, renal failure, hepatotoxicity), drug involved, patient demographics, comorbidities
- **Microbiome analysis**: Discussed mechanistically rather than directly measured in all patients

## Detailed Summary

This retrospective study examines adverse drug reactions to NSAIDs and paracetamol in 897 patients, with a focus on the potential role of gut microbiota in modulating drug safety. The pharmacomicrobiomics perspective recognizes that the gut microbiome is a major metabolic organ that processes drugs before and after they enter systemic circulation, potentially influencing both efficacy and toxicity.

NSAIDs cause GI damage through both direct (COX inhibition reducing protective prostaglandins) and indirect (microbiome disruption) mechanisms. NSAID-induced changes in gut microbiota composition reduce barrier-protective organisms and may promote expansion of gram-negative bacteria, increasing endotoxin exposure and worsening mucosal inflammation. This creates a feedback loop: the drug damages the microbiome, the damaged microbiome reduces mucosal protection, and the more vulnerable mucosa sustains greater drug-induced injury.

Paracetamol undergoes hepatic metabolism to the reactive intermediate NAPQI, which is normally detoxified by glutathione conjugation. Gut bacteria can metabolize paracetamol through several pathways, potentially altering the fraction of drug reaching the liver and the metabolite profile. Individual variation in gut microbiome composition may therefore explain part of the variability in paracetamol hepatotoxicity risk.

The relevance to CKD is direct: CKD patients frequently use analgesics, have impaired drug clearance, and have uremia-altered gut microbiomes. The combination of altered drug metabolism, impaired renal excretion, and dysbiotic microbiome may create heightened vulnerability to analgesic ADRs. Iron metabolism is relevant because GI bleeding from NSAIDs causes iron loss, while iron supplementation for resulting anemia may further alter the gut microbiome.
