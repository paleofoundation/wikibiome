# Key Claims

- **Emerging evidence for oral-gut-brain axis** in ASD: oral dysbiosis correlates with gut dysbiosis and behavioral outcomes
- **Altered oral microbiota diversity** in ASD compared to neurotypical controls
- **Oral dysbiosis may precede or reinforce GI dysbiosis** (sequential microbial establishment from mouth → gut)
- **Host-microbial interactions** in oral cavity influence systemic and neurological phenotype

# Methods & Findings

Systematic review synthesizing literature on oral microbiome in ASD:
- **Oral microbiota composition** in ASD children vs. controls
- **Diversity metrics** (richness, evenness, Shannon/Simpson indices)
- **Functional oral dysbiosis**: taxa differences implicated in caries, periodontitis, dysbiosis
- **Interkingdom relationships**: fungal-bacterial interactions in oral biofilms
- **Diagnostic implications**: oral dysbiosis markers for early ASD detection

**Key findings:**
- ASD groups show reduced microbial diversity in saliva/plaque
- Enriched pathogenic taxa (e.g., [[streptococcus mutans]], [[porphyromonas gingivalis]])
- Depleted protective taxa (e.g., commensal streptococci)
- Altered oral-gut axis function → bidirectional dysbiosis

# Relevance to Metallomic Framework

This novel perspective on **oral-gut-brain axis** extends metallomic framework to the mouth—a major site of metal exposure and bacterial establishment:

- **Metal exposure routes**: Oral metal sources include water, tooth amalgam (mercury, silver, tin), food, and aerosol inhalation
- **Primitive 1 (Metals as Selective Pressures)**: Oral dysbiosis may reflect **metal-driven selection** in mouth biofilms
- **Primitive 6 (Interkingdom Relationships)**: Oral fungi (Candida) and bacteria (dysbiotic taxa) may form protective consortia against metal stress
- **Sequential colonization model**: Dysbiotic oral → seeding dysbiotic gut → systemic dysbiosis → metal homeostasis failure
- **Diagnostic opportunity**: Oral swabs cheaper and non-invasive compared to stool sampling—potential screening biomarker for ASD risk

Suggests **oral microbiome as early intervention target**--correct oral dysbiosis before it establishes permanent gut dysbiosis.
