## Summary

A literature review examining the effects of lead, cadmium, and copper on male and female infertility, synthesizing evidence from articles published 1982-2020. The review covers mechanisms of reproductive toxicity, epidemiological associations, and the impact of these metals on fertility parameters in both sexes.

## Key Findings

- Lead exposure causes reduced sperm count, motility, and morphological changes; disrupts hormonal regulation via the hypothalamic-pituitary axis
- Lead affects ovarian function, disrupts estrogen and progesterone production, and is associated with delayed conception and increased miscarriage risk
- Cadmium accumulates in the reproductive system; even a single cadmium dose can affect oocyte maturation in vitro
- Cadmium is classified as a Group 1 carcinogen by IARC and is linked to testicular damage and impaired spermatogenesis
- Copper at excessive levels has destructive effects on the reproductive system; exposure to mercury and lead can disrupt normal sperm motility and activity
- Environmental pollution and heavy metal exposure contribute to rising global infertility rates (estimated 10-35% prevalence worldwide)
- Cadmium damages testes through oxidative stress, disrupts calcium metabolism, and depletes zinc in reproductive tissues
- Lead exposure at 40 ug or 25 ug/dL decreases fertility and increases miscarriage risk

## Relevance to Wiki

Provides a comprehensive overview of how dietary and environmental heavy metal exposure (particularly lead, cadmium, and copper) impairs fertility in both sexes, connecting environmental contamination to reproductive health outcomes.
