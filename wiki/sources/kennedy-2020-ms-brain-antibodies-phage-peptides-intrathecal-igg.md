# Recombinant Antibodies from MS Brain Plasma Cells Identified Phage Peptides for Characterizing Intrathecal IgG Response

## Summary

Using laser capture microdissection, this study isolated single CD38+ plasma cells from lesion areas in two autopsy [[multiple-sclerosis]] brains and generated recombinant antibodies (rAbs). Phage-displayed random peptide libraries were used to identify antigenic targets of these MS brain antibodies.

## Key Findings

- 25 high-affinity phage peptides identified from panning MS brain rAbs, of which 5 are unique
- Database searches revealed sequence homologies with **Epstein-Barr nuclear antigens 4 and 6** as well as other viral proteins
- These peptides were recognized by intrathecal IgG and oligoclonal IgG bands in other MS patients
- Functional recombinant antibodies can be generated from clonally expanded plasma cells in MS brain lesions
- rAbs have potential for determining the targets of intrathecal IgG and oligoclonal bands

## Relevance to Gut-Metal-Microbiome Axis

The identification of EBV nuclear antigen homologies among MS brain antibody targets reinforces the viral trigger hypothesis for MS. This connects to the [[gut-brain-axis]] because EBV establishes persistent infection in B cells that traffic through mucosal-associated lymphoid tissue including the gut. The [[gut-microbiome]] modulates B cell maturation and antibody production, and [[dysbiosis]] may impair the immune system's ability to control latent EBV, potentially contributing to the molecular mimicry that drives MS autoimmunity.

## Limitations

- Only two autopsy MS brains examined
- Phage display identifies peptide mimotopes, not necessarily native antigens
- Small number of rAbs generated
