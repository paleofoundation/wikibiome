# A Comparative Study of Blood Levels of Manganese, Some Macroelements and Heavy Metals in Obese and Non-Obese PCOS Patients

## Key Claims

- Blood lead and cadmium levels are significantly higher in both obese and non-obese PCOS patients compared to their control groups (P < 0.01)
- Serum magnesium, calcium, and manganese are significantly lower in both PCOS subgroups compared to controls (P < 0.001)
- Positive correlation between blood Pb and serum TSH in non-obese PCOS (r=0.384, p=0.048), suggesting lead may affect thyroid function
- Positive correlation between Cd and total testosterone in obese PCOS (r=0.465, p=0.015), linking cadmium to hyperandrogenism
- Negative correlation between serum magnesium and LH in non-obese PCOS (r=-0.43, p=0.027)
- No significant differences between obese and non-obese PCOS groups in levels of measured elements

## Methods/Findings

- **Study design**: Case-control study at Kamal Al-Samarrai Hospital (IVF center), Baghdad, Iraq; Oct 2016 - April 2017
- **Participants**: 54 PCOS patients (27 obese BMI >=30, 27 non-obese BMI <30) and 28 controls (14 obese, 14 non-obese)
- **PCOS diagnosis**: Revised Rotterdam criteria (2003) -- at least 2 of: hyperandrogenism, oligo/anovulation, polycystic ovaries on ultrasound
- **Metals measured**: Pb, Cd (whole blood by AAS); Mg, Ca, Mn (serum by AAS)
- **Hormones measured**: FSH, LH, prolactin, total testosterone, TSH, fasting glucose (ELFA, chemiluminescence)
- **Key findings**: Obese PCOS Pb 22.48 ug/dl, non-obese PCOS 23.00 ug/dl vs controls ~14.29 ug/dl; Obese PCOS Cd 0.317 ug/dl, non-obese 0.318 vs controls ~0.14 ug/dl
- **Mn results**: PCOS groups (0.086, 0.084 ug/dl) significantly lower than controls (0.225, 0.236)
- **Mg and Ca**: Both significantly reduced in PCOS vs controls; low magnesium may contribute to insulin resistance through hypoglycemia-catecholamine feedback
