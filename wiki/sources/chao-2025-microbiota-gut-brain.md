## Key Findings

- **Axis integration**: Perinatal depression involves dysregulation of the microbiota-gut-brain axis, integrating dysbiosis, intestinal barrier dysfunction, immune dysregulation, and CNS changes (expert opinion review level).
- **Perinatal-specific mechanisms**: Pregnancy and postpartum periods involve unique microbiota alterations and immunological remodeling distinct from non-perinatal depression (expert opinion level).
- **Treatment opportunities**: Microbiota-targeted interventions (probiotics, prebiotics, dietary modifications, fecal microbiota transplantation) offer novel treatment approaches for perinatal depression (expert opinion level).

## Methods

Narrative review synthesizing evidence on the microbiota-gut-brain axis in perinatal depression, integrating recent mechanistic research with clinical treatment perspectives. ## Detailed Summary

The review establishes the microbiota-gut-brain axis as central to perinatal depression:

- **Dysbiosis patterns**: Pregnancy and postpartum involve characteristic microbiota shifts; dysbiotic states include reduced Faecalibacterium prausnitzii, reduced Akkermansia muciniphila, increased proteobacteria
- **Barrier function**: Dysbiosis impairs tight junction integrity, increases zonula occludens-1 (ZO-1) disruption, leading to increased intestinal permeability ("leaky gut")
- **Immune dysregulation**: Dysbiosis reduces beneficial bacteria production of short-chain fatty acids (butyrate), reduces AhR ligand production, impairs regulatory T cell (Treg) differentiation
- **Microbial translocation**: Increased lipopolysaccharide (LPS) crossing compromised barrier stimulates TLR4 signaling, driving systemic and CNS inflammation
- **Neuroimmune axis**: Elevated LPS activates microglia, impairs blood-brain barrier, drives neuroinflammation and microglia-mediated synaptic pruning
- **Monoamine dysregulation**: Dysbiosis impairs microbial production of GABA, reduces tryptophan metabolite production for AhR signaling
- **Estrobolome dysbiosis**: Reduced beta-glucuronidase activity impairs estrogen deconjugation and recirculation, affecting estrogen-dependent mood and immune regulation

The review highlights that targeting the microbiota-gut-brain axis offers mechanistically sound interventions for perinatal depression prevention and treatment.
