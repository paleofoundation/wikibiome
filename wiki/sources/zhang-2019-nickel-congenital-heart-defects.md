# Metal Nickel Exposure Increase the Risk of Congenital Heart Defects Occurrence in Offspring

## Study Design

Case-control study conducted in China (2010-2013) examining the association between maternal [[nickel]] exposure and congenital heart defects (CHDs) in offspring. Enrolled 490 controls and 399 CHD cases. Nickel concentrations were measured by ICP-MS in maternal hair and fetal placental tissue.

## Key Findings

### Hair Nickel Levels

- Median hair nickel was significantly elevated in CHD cases: **0.629 ng/mg** vs **0.443 ng/mg** in controls (P<.001)
- Logistic regression showed the highest tertile of hair nickel had an adjusted odds ratio (aOR) of **1.326** (95% CI 1.003-1.757, P<.05) for total CHDs

### Placental Nickel Levels

- Placental nickel was also elevated in cases: **0.178 ng/mg** vs **0.148 ng/mg** in controls (P=.039)
- For the "other heart defects" subtype, fetal placental nickel showed a dramatically elevated aOR of **11.280** (95% CI 1.621-78.512), though confidence intervals were very wide

### CHD Subtype Associations (Hair Nickel, Highest Tertile)

Significant associations were found for specific CHD subtypes:

- **Septal defects**: aOR 1.443
- **Conotruncal defects**: aOR 1.376
- **Right ventricular outflow tract obstruction**: aOR 1.543
- **Left ventricular outflow tract obstruction**: aOR 1.549

## Mechanisms

Nickel crosses the placental barrier and can cause embryotoxicity through multiple pathways:

- **Reactive oxygen species (ROS)** generation leading to [[oxidative-stress]]
- **Epigenetic changes** including altered DNA methylation and histone acetylation
- Direct interference with embryonic cardiac development during critical windows

## Limitations

- No established normal reference range for nickel in hair or placental tissue
- Cross-sectional design limits causal inference
- Single time-point measurements may not capture exposure variation across pregnancy

## Connections

This study provides epidemiological evidence linking maternal nickel exposure to birth defects, contributing to the broader understanding of [[nickel-reproductive-toxicity]]. The placental transfer findings are relevant to [[nickel]] toxicokinetics and complement the work by [[ding-2021-nickel-pregnancy-complications|Ding et al. (2021)]] on nickel and pregnancy complications.
