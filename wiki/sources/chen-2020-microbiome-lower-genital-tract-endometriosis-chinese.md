# Key Claims

- *Atopobium* is significantly more abundant in adenomyosis-endometriosis (AMEM) patients compared to other groups
- *Lactobacillus* is the predominant genus in the female lower genital tract across all disease groups
- No significant differences in alpha diversity were found between endometriosis, adenomyosis, AMEM, and control groups
- *Coriobacteriaceae* was significantly higher in the AMEM group at the family level
- PICRUSt functional analysis identified metabolism/lipid metabolism and ketone body degradation as significantly different between endometriosis and adenomyosis groups

# Methods/Findings

- **Study design**: Case-control pilot study at Peking Union Medical College Hospital
- **Participants**: 68 patients (19 adenomyosis, 20 endometriosis, 7 AMEM, 36 controls), 134 cervical/vaginal/uterine samples
- **Methods**: 16S rRNA V3-V4 sequencing (Illumina HiSeq2500), LEfSe, PICRUSt

 - 7.35 million raw sequences from 67 cervical and 65 posterior fornix samples
 - No significant alpha diversity differences between disease groups
 - *Campylobacter*, *Ezakiella*, *Faecalibacterium*, and *Escherichia/Shigella* higher in AMEM group
 - Beta diversity by Bray-Curtis showed AMEM significantly different from other groups
 - Six bacterial genera with OTU abundance >1% were identified in circular plots

# Relevance to Wiki

This study examines the cervicovaginal microbiome across the spectrum of endometriosis and adenomyosis, finding that the combined AMEM phenotype shows the most distinct microbial profile. The prominence of *Atopobium* in adenomyosis-endometriosis and differences in lipid/ketone metabolism pathways suggest metabolic consequences of [[dysbiosis]] in these conditions. The findings support the concept that cervicovaginal microbial shifts may serve as non-invasive [[biomarkers]] for [[endometriosis]] diagnosis.
