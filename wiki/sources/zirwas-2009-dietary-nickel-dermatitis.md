# Dietary Nickel as a Cause of Systemic Contact Dermatitis

Zirwas & Molenda (2009). *J Clin Aesthet Dermatol* 2(6):39-43.

## Overview

A clinically focused paper presenting three case reports and a practical review of systemic contact dermatitis caused by [[dietary-nickel-exposure]], including dose-response data, a simplified low-nickel diet, and strategies to reduce nickel absorption. Aimed at practicing dermatologists managing patients with [[nickel-allergy]] whose dermatitis persists despite avoidance of external nickel contact.

## Key Findings

### Case Reports

Three patients are presented who experienced persistent or recurrent dermatitis despite appropriate avoidance of external nickel sources. In each case, dietary modification (low-nickel diet) produced significant clinical improvement, confirming dietary nickel as the causative trigger for their systemic contact dermatitis.

### Dose-Response Relationship

Oral challenge studies with nickel sulfate hexahydrate in nickel-sensitive individuals demonstrated a clear dose-response:

| Nickel dose | Percentage with cutaneous reaction |
|---|---|
| 0.3 mg | 40% |
| 1.0 mg | 40% |
| 4.0 mg | 70% |

These data show that even very low oral nickel doses can provoke cutaneous flares in sensitized individuals, and that a substantial proportion of nickel-allergic patients react to amounts easily obtained through normal dietary intake.

### Dietary Nickel Intake

A typical daily diet can readily exceed the threshold for eliciting symptoms. Example daily intake calculation:

- Oatmeal: 0.22 mg
- Banana: 0.04 mg
- Chicken: 0.01 mg
- Broccoli: 0.02 mg
- Peanuts: 0.22 mg
- **Total: 0.58 mg**

This total of 0.58 mg exceeds the 0.3 mg dose that triggered reactions in 40% of nickel-sensitive subjects, demonstrating that a completely ordinary diet can deliver clinically relevant nickel doses.

### Simplified Low-Nickel Diet

The authors provide a practical, simplified low-nickel diet for patient counseling. Foods to **avoid**:

- **Whole grains:** Whole wheat, oats, millet, buckwheat
- **Legumes:** Beans, lentils, peas, soybeans, chickpeas
- **Nuts and seeds:** All types, especially peanuts, almonds, cashews
- **Chocolate and cocoa:** All forms
- **Canned foods:** Nickel leaches from cans into food
- **Black tea**

The simplified approach was designed to maximize patient compliance by focusing on the highest-impact food categories rather than providing exhaustive lists.

### Non-Dietary Sources of Oral Nickel

Beyond food, other sources contribute to oral nickel intake:

- **Stainless steel cookware:** Nickel leaches into food during cooking, especially with acidic foods (tomato sauce, citrus). First use of new stainless steel cookware releases the most nickel
- **Tap water from faucets:** Nickel-plated faucets can release nickel into water, particularly from first-draw water that has sat in contact with the fixture overnight

### Strategies to Reduce Nickel Absorption

- **Vitamin C supplementation:** Ascorbic acid acts as a competitive inhibitor of nickel absorption in the gastrointestinal tract. Taking vitamin C with meals can meaningfully reduce the amount of dietary nickel that enters the systemic circulation
- **High-iron diet:** Iron competes with nickel for absorption via shared divalent metal transporters (DMT1). Maintaining adequate or high iron intake reduces nickel absorption
- **Disulfiram:** A nickel chelator (also used in alcohol aversion therapy) that binds nickel and promotes its excretion. Can be used as adjunct pharmacologic therapy in patients with severe or refractory systemic contact dermatitis from dietary nickel

### Clinical Presentations

The authors identify two clinical situations where dietary nickel should be considered as a causative factor:

1. **Vesicular hand dermatitis (dyshidrotic eczema)** in a patient with a positive nickel patch test, especially when the dermatitis persists despite avoidance of external nickel contact
2. **Widespread dermatitis or generalized pruritus** in a patient with a positive nickel patch test, particularly when conventional treatments provide incomplete relief

In both scenarios, a trial of low-nickel diet (with or without vitamin C supplementation) is a reasonable and low-risk diagnostic and therapeutic intervention.

## Clinical Significance

This paper provides practical, actionable guidance for a common clinical scenario. Many nickel-allergic patients are counseled only about avoiding external metal contact (jewelry, belt buckles) without being informed about dietary nickel as a systemic trigger. The simplified diet and absorption-reduction strategies make dietary management accessible for both clinicians and patients.
