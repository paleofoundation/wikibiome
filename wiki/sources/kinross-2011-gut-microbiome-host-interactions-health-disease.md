# Kinross 2011 -- Gut Microbiome-Host Interactions in Health and Disease

## Key Claims

- The gut microbiome represents a 'super-organism' where part of host metabolic regulation is devolved to microbial symbionts, with gut microbiota influencing drug metabolism, dietary caloric bioavailability, immune conditioning, and post-surgical recovery
- Colon cancer is directly influenced by gut microbial metabolism: diets high in red meat and animal fat lead to low SCFA/butyrate, high fecal fats, low vitamin absorption, increased 7-alpha-dehydroxylating bacteria converting cholic acid to deoxycholic acid (co-carcinogen), and low H2S-metabolizing bacteria
- A 'core microbiome' exists at the functional (gene) level rather than the taxonomic (species) level; a minimal core of 57 species was found in 90% of a study cohort, with 18 species at 1% coverage found in all individuals
- The gut microbiome exhibits stability over a 2-year interval, with individuals conserving >60% of phylotypes; phase II metabolic response of the host to metabolites generated by the microbiome has direct impact on drug metabolism
- Key metagenomic approaches reviewed: 16S rRNA, shotgun metagenomics, metabolomics (NMR, mass spectrometry); each provides complementary information

## Methods/Findings

- **Type**: Comprehensive review with summary table of human metagenomic studies (Gill 2006, Kurokawa 2007, Turnbaugh 2009, Qin 2010)
- **Figure 1**: Maps diseases influenced by gut microbial metabolism including colon cancer, IBD, obesity, hypertension, asthma, autism
- **Drug metabolism**: p-cresol generated by gut bacteria competes with acetaminophen for sulfonation, reducing effective systemic capacity; gut microbiota directly modify drug bioavailability
