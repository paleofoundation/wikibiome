# HRT Reverses Gut Microbiome and Serum Metabolome in POI

## Key Findings

- **[[eggerthella]] abundance was significantly increased** in POI patients and was reversed by hormone replacement therapy (HRT), establishing a direct link between estrogen status and this genus ([[jiang-2021-hrt-gut-microbiome-premature-ovarian-insufficiency]], animal-model).
- **POI patients had altered serum metabolic signatures** and increased TGF-beta1 (a pro-fibrotic cytokine), both reversed by HRT — connecting gut microbiome changes to systemic metabolic and fibrotic pathways.
- **Estrogen ameliorated ovarian fibrosis induced by *Eggerthella lenta*** in a mouse model, providing causal evidence that this bacterium drives ovarian pathology and that estrogen is protective.
- **Bidirectional estrogen-microbiome axis**: HRT not only improved ovarian function but also restored gut microbial composition, suggesting that estrogen status and gut microbiome health are mutually reinforcing.

## Methods

- **Study design**: Combined human observational study and animal model validation
- **Population**: Women with premature ovarian insufficiency (with and without HRT) and healthy controls; germ-free or gnotobiotic mice for causal validation
- **Key measurements**: Gut microbiome profiling (16S rRNA or shotgun sequencing), serum metabolomics, TGF-beta1 quantification, ovarian histopathology in mice
- **Distinguishing feature**: Rare combination of clinical observation and mechanistic animal model validation in the same study

## Detailed Summary

This study investigated the relationship between gut microbiome composition, serum metabolome, and ovarian function in premature ovarian insufficiency (POI), using both human samples and a mouse model. In POI patients, **[[eggerthella]]** (particularly *E. lenta*) was significantly enriched compared to healthy controls. Eggerthella is a Gram-positive, obligate anaerobe known for its ability to metabolize steroids and other host compounds — its enrichment in a condition characterized by estrogen deficiency is mechanistically coherent. The study then demonstrated that HRT reversed the Eggerthella enrichment, normalized serum metabolomic profiles, and reduced TGF-beta1 levels. The mouse model provided causal evidence: colonization with *E. lenta* induced ovarian fibrosis (mediated by TGF-beta1), and estrogen treatment ameliorated this fibrosis. This establishes a pathogenic cascade: estrogen depletion → Eggerthella overgrowth → TGF-beta1 elevation → ovarian fibrosis → further ovarian decline — a self-reinforcing cycle that HRT can interrupt. The serum metabolome changes suggest that Eggerthella's metabolic activity alters circulating metabolites beyond steroids, potentially affecting systemic inflammation and tissue remodeling.
