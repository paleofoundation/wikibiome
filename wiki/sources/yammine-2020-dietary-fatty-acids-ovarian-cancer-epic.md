# Dietary and Circulating Fatty Acids and Ovarian Cancer Risk in EPIC

## Key Findings

- **No significant association was observed** between total dietary fat intake or major fatty acid subclasses (saturated, monounsaturated, polyunsaturated) and overall ovarian cancer risk in this large prospective cohort.
- **Circulating fatty acid biomarkers** (measured in a nested subset) were used alongside dietary questionnaire data, providing objective exposure measurement that reduces recall and reporting bias.
- The study included approximately **1,486 incident ovarian cancer cases** from the EPIC cohort of over 521,000 participants across 10 European countries, making it one of the largest prospective investigations of dietary fat and OC risk.
- Subgroup analyses by histological subtype, menopausal status, and oral contraceptive use did not reveal consistent significant associations.

## Methods

- **Design**: Prospective cohort (nested case-control for circulating biomarkers) within the European Prospective Investigation into Cancer and Nutrition (EPIC).
- **Population**: Women from 23 centers in 10 European countries, followed from enrollment (1992-2000) through cancer incidence registries.
- **Exposure assessment**: Validated country-specific dietary questionnaires for fatty acid intake estimation; gas chromatography of plasma/serum phospholipid fatty acids in a biomarker subset.
- **Statistical analysis**: Cox proportional hazards models adjusted for total energy intake, BMI, parity, oral contraceptive use, menopausal status, and other confounders.

## Detailed Summary

This study systematically evaluated whether dietary and circulating fatty acids influence ovarian cancer risk using data from the EPIC prospective cohort. Dietary fat intake was estimated from validated food frequency questionnaires, while circulating fatty acid composition was measured objectively via gas chromatography of phospholipid fractions. The null findings for total fat and major fatty acid classes suggest that gross dietary fat composition is not a major modifiable risk factor for ovarian cancer at the population level. The strength of this conclusion derives from the prospective design, large sample size, geographic and dietary diversity across 10 countries, and the complementary use of biomarker and questionnaire data. The study did not specifically examine individual fatty acids at granular resolution or fatty acid ratios (e.g., omega-6:omega-3), leaving open the possibility that specific lipid species may have effects not captured by class-level analysis.

## Relevance to WikiBiome

Relevant as a null-result reference for the ovarian cancer disease entity page — establishes that gross dietary fat categories are unlikely drivers of OC risk. This negative finding is important for framing the WikiBiome narrative: dietary fat per se is not the exposure of concern for ovarian cancer, directing attention instead toward other exposures (metals, endocrine disruptors, microbial metabolites). The EPIC cohort design serves as a methodological benchmark for other dietary-cancer investigations.

## Open Questions

- Whether specific individual fatty acids (e.g., arachidonic acid, specific omega-3 species) or fatty acid ratios have effects masked by class-level analysis.
- Whether fatty acid-microbiome interactions (e.g., microbial conversion of dietary lipids to pro- or anti-inflammatory metabolites) modify ovarian cancer risk in ways not captured by this study.
- Whether fatty acid effects differ by molecular subtypes of ovarian cancer beyond the histological subtypes examined.
