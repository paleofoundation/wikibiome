# Key Claims

- Acute pancreatitis patients have significantly different fecal fungal microbiota compared to healthy controls, with decline in alpha diversity (Shannon p=0.006, Simpson p=0.019)
- Candida was the most dominant fungal genus in AP patients (61.34%), followed by Aspergillus (15.18%), Penicillium (5.98%), and Apiotrichum (5.23%)
- AP patients had more fungal co-abundance network interactions and mostly positive correlations, with a strong positive link between Aspergillus and WBC counts
- This is the first empirical evidence that AP patients have different fecal fungal microbiota, supporting mycobiota involvement in AP etiology

# Methods/Findings

- **Study design**: Case-control; 11 AP patients and 15 healthy controls; fecal ITS rRNA gene sequencing
- **Sequencing**: 1,916,597 raw reads; 678 OTUs identified (173 shared, 140 unique to AP, 365 unique to controls)
- **Alpha diversity**: Shannon and Simpson indices significantly different (p<0.05); Chao, Ace, Sobs indices showed trends (p=0.07-0.08)
- **Beta diversity**: PCoA showed substantial distinction (Adonis R2=0.1361, p=0.016; ANOSIM R=0.1664, p=0.017)
- **Taxonomic composition**: AP group dominated by Ascomycota (92.3%) and Basidiomycota (7.2%); healthy controls had more balanced Eurotimycetes (45.30%), Saccharomycetes (23.97%)
- **AP-specific shifts**: Candida dominant in AP; healthy controls dominated by Aspergillus, Ganoderma, Penicillium
- **Clinical correlations**: Strong positive correlation between Aspergillus abundance and WBC counts; unclassified Rozellomycota linked to IL-6 levels
