# Overview

Review of the mechanisms and interrelationships among **gut microbiota, immune system, and nervous system** in ASD, covering the gut-brain axis, metabolic pathways (SCFAs, serotonin, GABA), and neuroimmune interactions. Discusses intervention strategies including probiotics, prebiotics, and FMT.

# Key Findings

## Gut-Brain Axis in ASD

- Bidirectional communication via autonomic nervous system, enteric nervous system, neuroendocrine system, and immune system
- Dysbiosis in ASD: abnormal *Bacteroidetes/Firmicutes* ratio, decreased *Bifidobacterium*, *Prevotella*, and *Lactobacillus*
- Transplanting ASD microbiota into germ-free mice induces ASD-like behaviors

## Metabolic Pathways

- **SCFAs**: *Clostridium* produces large amounts of propionate (toxic to brain at high levels); acetate, butyrate cross BBB and affect neural processes
- **Serotonin (5-HT)**: produced from tryptophan by enterochromaffin cells; ASD patients show increased peripheral 5-HT but decreased brain levels
- **GABA**: produced by *Bacteroides*, *Lactobacillus*, *Bifidobacterium*, and *Parabacteroides*; significantly influenced by environmental pH

## Immune System Interactions

- T lymphocyte dysregulation: abnormal Th1/Th2/Th17/Treg ratios; systemic defects in regulatory T cells
- **Th17 cells**: play central role in ASD pathogenesis through IL-17A release
- Elevated IL-6, IL-1beta, IL-17A, IL-12p40 in ASD blood; decreased IL-10
- **Microglia**: chronically activated in ASD brain across lifespan; proinflammatory activation of monocytes

## Nervous System Pathways

- Vagus nerve: directly connecting gut to brain; stimulated by microbes and neuroendocrine mediators
- HPA axis: gut microbiota dysbiosis elevates cortisol levels
- Epigenetic regulation: microbial metabolites may influence gene expression through histone modification
