# FMT from PCOS Patients Induces Metabolic Disorders and Ovarian Dysfunction

## Key Findings

- **FMT from PCOS patients reproduced the PCOS phenotype** in germ-free mice, including disrupted ovarian function, insulin resistance, and an obese-like phenotype, establishing a causal role for the gut microbiome in PCOS pathogenesis ([[huang-2024-fmt-pcos-metabolic-ovarian-dysfunction]], animal-model).
- **PCOS donor microbiota was enriched in [[phocaeicola]], [[mediterraneibacter]], [[oscillospiraceae]], and Lawsonibacter**, demonstrating a transferable dysbiotic signature that persists after transplantation.
- **The gut microbiome may govern the metabolic set point** of PCOS-bearing individuals, suggesting that microbial composition is not merely a consequence of hormonal disruption but a driver of it.
- **Germ-free recipients developed metabolic syndrome features** including altered glucose homeostasis and body composition changes, linking gut dysbiosis to the systemic metabolic component of PCOS.

## Methods

- **Study design**: Animal model (germ-free mice receiving FMT)
- **Population**: Fecal samples from PCOS patients and healthy controls transplanted into germ-free C57BL/6 mice
- **Key measurements**: Body weight, glucose tolerance, insulin sensitivity, ovarian morphology, estrous cyclicity, and 16S rRNA sequencing of cecal contents post-transplant

## Detailed Summary

This study used fecal microbiota transplantation from PCOS patients into germ-free mice to test whether the PCOS-associated gut microbiome is causally linked to metabolic and ovarian dysfunction. Recipients of PCOS microbiota developed hallmark features of the syndrome: disrupted estrous cycles (indicative of ovarian dysfunction), impaired insulin signaling, and increased adiposity. Taxonomic profiling of the transplanted communities revealed enrichment of **[[phocaeicola]]**, **[[mediterraneibacter]]**, **Oscillospiraceae**, and **Lawsonibacter** in PCOS-derived communities compared to healthy donor controls. The persistence of these taxa after transplantation and their association with the disease phenotype in a controlled gnotobiotic setting strengthens the case for a microbiome-driven component of PCOS beyond correlational human studies. The findings align with the broader concept that the gut microbiome acts as an endocrine organ, influencing sex hormone metabolism and systemic metabolic regulation.

## Open Questions

- Which specific metabolites produced by the enriched taxa (Phocaeicola, Mediterraneibacter) mediate the ovarian and metabolic effects?
- Does the transferred microbiome alter sex hormone levels (testosterone, estradiol) in recipient mice, or are the effects downstream of metabolic changes?
- Would FMT from healthy donors reverse an established PCOS phenotype, or only prevent onset?
