# Key Claims

- [[cadmium]], [[lead]], and [[arsenic]] all primarily affect the proximal convoluted tubule (PCT) cells of the kidney
- Even low Cd levels previously considered non-toxic have nephrotoxic effects; up to 7% of exposed populations suffer kidney injury
- Pb at "normal" levels is associated with higher morbidity and mortality; there is no established safe threshold
- [[arsenic]] exposure linked to decreased renal function (OR 1.11, 95% CI 1.09-1.13 for higher As levels)
- Combination of different metals has cumulative nephrotoxic effects
- [[Metallothionein]] (MT) plays central role in Cd/Pb metabolism and toxicity; MT knockout mice are more susceptible
- No effective treatment exists for Cd nephrotoxicity; prevention is key

# Methods/Findings

- **Type**: Narrative review of epidemiology, physiopathology, and clinical manifestations of metal-induced kidney injury
- **Cadmium**: Absorbed via DMT-1 transporter; binds MT in liver; Cd-MT complex filtered at glomerulus and reabsorbed by PCT via megalin; causes mitochondrial dysfunction, free radical formation, caspase activation, apoptosis
- **Lead**: 99% binds proteins in blood; bone is main reservoir; causes mitochondrial damage, GSH depletion, NF-kappaB activation, renin-angiotensin system activation; EDTA chelation therapy can help
- **Arsenic**: Enters cells via aquaglyceroporins AQ3 and AQ9; GSH-mediated methylation in liver; causes GSH depletion and oxidative activity in PCT cells
- Tubular damage markers: alpha-1-microglobulin, beta-2-microglobulin, NGAL, KIM-1
- Clinical manifestations: low molecular weight proteinuria, aminoaciduria, phosphaturia, glycosuria
