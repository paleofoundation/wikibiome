# Glutathione Supplementation in Autism Spectrum Disorders — Clinical Trial

## Key Findings

- **Oral lipoceutical glutathione significantly increased plasma reduced glutathione (GSH)** in ASD children but not whole-blood glutathione, suggesting compartment-specific effects ([[kern-2011-glutathione-supplementation-asd-clinical-trial]], case-series, n=26).
- **Both oral and transdermal glutathione improved transsulfuration metabolites**: plasma sulfate, cysteine, and taurine levels increased significantly in both treatment groups.
- **ASD children have significantly lower baseline glutathione levels** than typically developing children, supporting the hypothesis of impaired antioxidant capacity and metal buffering in ASD.
- **Open-label design without control group** limits causal inference — this is preliminary evidence requiring confirmation by RCT.

## Methods

Open-label clinical trial with two treatment arms: oral lipoceutical glutathione (n=13) and transdermal glutathione (n=13), in children aged 3-13 years with diagnosed ASD. No placebo control group. Treatment duration and dosing details not fully confirmed. <!-- NEEDS VERIFICATION: exact dosing, treatment duration, and washout period --> Outcomes measured: plasma reduced glutathione (GSH), oxidized glutathione (GSSG), GSH:GSSG ratio, whole-blood glutathione, and transsulfuration metabolites (sulfate, cysteine, taurine, methionine). Blood samples collected at baseline and post-treatment.

## Detailed Summary

Kern et al. investigate whether glutathione supplementation can correct the documented glutathione deficit in children with autism spectrum disorder — a deficit that has been replicated across multiple studies and is considered one of the most consistent biochemical findings in ASD.

**The glutathione deficit in ASD.** Multiple independent studies have documented that children with ASD have significantly lower levels of reduced glutathione (GSH), higher levels of oxidized glutathione (GSSG), and a lower GSH:GSSG ratio compared to typically developing children. This imbalance indicates chronic oxidative stress and impaired antioxidant capacity. In the WikiBiome framework, glutathione serves a dual role: it is both the primary non-enzymatic antioxidant and the major intracellular buffer for toxic metals ([[begg-2015-cadmium-toxicity-transition-metal-pneumococcus]]). Glutathione depletion therefore simultaneously increases vulnerability to oxidative damage AND reduces the capacity to buffer heavy metals like [[mercury]], [[lead]], and [[cadmium]] — metals that are elevated in many ASD studies ([[ogrady-2025-metal-dyshomeostasis-asd]]).

**Oral lipoceutical glutathione.** The oral treatment group received a lipoceutical (lipid-encapsulated) formulation designed to improve oral bioavailability of glutathione, which is normally poorly absorbed due to gastrointestinal degradation. This group showed significant increases in plasma reduced GSH, demonstrating that the formulation successfully delivered glutathione to the systemic circulation. However, whole-blood glutathione did not increase, suggesting the supplemented GSH remained in the plasma compartment and did not fully replenish erythrocyte or tissue stores.

**Transdermal glutathione.** The transdermal group also showed improvements in transsulfuration metabolites (sulfate, cysteine, taurine), though plasma GSH changes were less pronounced than the oral route. The improvement in transsulfuration metabolites suggests that glutathione supplementation by either route feeds the broader sulfur metabolism pathway.

**Transsulfuration pathway significance.** The increases in sulfate, cysteine, and taurine are significant because the transsulfuration pathway converts homocysteine to cysteine (a GSH precursor) and produces sulfate and taurine. Impairment of this pathway is a hallmark of ASD biochemistry. The finding that exogenous glutathione stimulates transsulfuration output suggests a bottleneck upstream of glutathione that supplementation bypasses.

**Limitations.** The open-label design without placebo control is the major limitation. The small sample size (n=26 total, split between two arms) limits statistical power. No behavioral or clinical outcome measures were reported — only biochemical endpoints.

## Open Questions

- Does glutathione supplementation improve behavioral outcomes in ASD, or only biochemical markers?
- Can the plasma GSH increase be translated to tissue-level GSH restoration (brain, gut mucosa)?
- Is the glutathione deficit in ASD a cause of symptoms (via impaired metal buffering and oxidative defense) or a consequence of upstream metabolic disruption?
- Would N-acetylcysteine (NAC), a GSH precursor, be more effective than direct glutathione supplementation by supporting endogenous synthesis?
- How does the gut microbiome interact with glutathione metabolism in ASD — do dysbiotic bacteria consume or produce GSH?
