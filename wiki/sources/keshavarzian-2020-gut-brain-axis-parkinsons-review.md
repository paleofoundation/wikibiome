# The Gut-Brain Axis and Its Relation to Parkinson's Disease: A Review

## Summary

This review comprehensively examines the [[gut-brain-axis]] in [[parkinsons-disease]], covering the bidirectional communication between the enteric nervous system and the CNS. It synthesizes evidence for the "body-first" hypothesis of PD, where alpha-synuclein pathology begins in the gut and propagates to the brain via the vagus nerve.

## Key Findings

- Alpha-synuclein aggregates are found in the enteric nervous system years before motor symptom onset
- Vagotomy (surgical cutting of the vagus nerve) is associated with reduced PD risk in epidemiological studies
- Intestinal permeability ("leaky gut") is increased in PD patients, detectable by serum zonulin and lactulose-mannitol tests
- Gut [[dysbiosis]] in PD precedes motor symptoms, supporting it as an early disease feature
- Constipation is one of the earliest prodromal symptoms of PD, reflecting ENS dysfunction
- The enteric nervous system shows Lewy body pathology similar to the substantia nigra
- Gut inflammation may trigger alpha-synuclein misfolding and propagation along the vagus nerve

## Relevance to Gut-Metal-Microbiome Axis

The vagal propagation model is directly relevant to metal-mediated PD pathogenesis. [[iron]], [[manganese]], and other metals that cross the compromised gut barrier could catalyze alpha-synuclein aggregation in the enteric nervous system, initiating the bottom-up pathological cascade. The "leaky gut" documented in PD creates a portal for dietary metals to enter both systemic circulation and local enteric neurons. [[manganese]] in particular promotes alpha-synuclein fibrillation in vitro. The body-first hypothesis suggests that [[dysbiosis]]-driven gut barrier breakdown and metal exposure may be among the earliest events in PD pathogenesis, preceding [[neuroinflammation]] and nigral dopaminergic cell death by years or decades. This positions the [[gut-brain-axis]] as a key interface between environmental metal exposure and neurodegenerative disease.

## Limitations

- Review article synthesizing existing literature
- The body-first hypothesis remains debated; a brain-first subtype may exist
- Vagotomy studies are observational with potential confounding
- Mechanisms of alpha-synuclein propagation along the vagus nerve are not fully elucidated
