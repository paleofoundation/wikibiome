# Why This Paper Matters

This mini-review is a critical bridge between [[mis-metallation]] theory and practical antimicrobial strategy. Sullivan et al. catalog the transport-independent mechanisms bacteria use to resist [[copper]] and [[zinc]] toxicity -- small molecules like glutathione, cysteine, histidine, and branched-chain amino acids that buffer or bypass metal poisoning. Crucially, it also documents compounds that **enhance** metal toxicity (Cu-bisthiosemicarbazones, disulfiram, PBT2, staphylopine), revealing how the host's [[nutritional-immunity]] arsenal can be augmented pharmacologically. The paper demonstrates that Cu kills bacteria by destroying Fe-S clusters in metabolic enzymes (IPMI, fumarase, GOGAT), causing branched-chain amino acid auxotrophy and glutamate starvation -- a direct mechanistic link between [[copper]] intoxication and [[mis-metallation]] of [[iron]]-sulfur enzymes.

# Key Claims

- **Cu toxicity operates primarily through Fe-S cluster destruction**: Cu binds to and destroys solvent-accessible Fe-S clusters in enzymes like isopropylmalate isomerase (IPMI), fumarase A, and GOGAT, causing BCAA auxotrophy and glutamate depletion
- **Cu-dependent growth inhibition in S. pyogenes** occurs via mis-metallation of GapA (GAPDH), reducing glycolytic flux and dysregulating Zn and Mn management
- **Glutathione** buffers free Cu by forming tetranuclear Cu4GS6 clusters; its depletion during Cu stress marks the onset of intoxication
- **Cysteine and histidine** rescue Cu-stressed bacteria through metal chelation; histidine transport (hisMJP) is part of the Cu "resistome" in S. agalactiae
- **BCAAs partially rescue Cu toxicity** in E. coli by bypassing Fe-S cluster-dependent BCAA synthesis enzymes
- **Zn intoxication displaces Mn** from PsaA (the Mn importer) in S. pneumoniae, starving cells of Mn needed for SOD
- **Ornithine (not arginine)** rescues S. agalactiae from Zn intoxication through the ADI pathway
- **PBT2** (zinc ionophore, originally for Alzheimer's) re-sensitizes antibiotic-resistant bacteria by disrupting Zn homeostasis and peptidoglycan synthesis
- **Disulfiram** (FDA-approved) synergizes with Cu to kill M. tuberculosis and S. aureus without increasing intracellular Cu
- **Staphylopine** (S. aureus metallophore) normally sequesters Zn but can inadvertently facilitate Cu uptake, becoming toxic

# Methods & Findings

## Cu Toxicity Mechanisms

- Cu destroys Fe-S clusters in IPMI (leucine biosynthesis) and fumarase A -- damage proceeds even without oxygen
- GOGAT (glutamate synthase, contains 4Fe-4S cluster) is inactivated by Cu, causing glutamate depletion
- Glutamate supplementation rescues Cu-stressed E. coli at pH 7 by bypassing GOGAT; BCAAs rescue at pH 7 but not pH 5
- In S. pyogenes, Cu likely mis-metallates GapA, reducing GAPDH activity

## Zn Toxicity and Mn Displacement

- High Zn displaces Mn from PsaA in [[streptococcus-pneumoniae]], blocking Mn import
- Mn supplementation rescues Zn-intoxicated cells; the Zn:Mn ratio determines toxicity severity
- In vivo, altered dietary Zn affects S. pneumoniae infection outcome in mice
- arcABDC locus (ADI pathway) is among the most strongly upregulated genes under Zn stress in S. agalactiae

## Antimicrobial Metal-Enhancing Compounds

- **Cu(btsc)**: Cu-bisthiosemicarbazones are membrane-permeable Cu ionophores, originally anti-cancer, now showing enhanced toxicity against N. gonorrhoeae
- **PBT2 + Zn**: Combination breaks antibiotic resistance in S. pyogenes, S. aureus, E. faecalis; reduces MICs of erythromycin, methicillin, vancomycin
- **Disulfiram + Cu**: Penetrates cell envelope in a porin-independent manner; potentiates Cu stress without increasing intracellular Cu; effective against M. tuberculosis and S. aureus
