# Postmortem Brain Tissue in Autism Science

## Key Findings

- **Postmortem brain tissue is essential** for understanding the biological processes affecting brain biology in ASD, as in vivo brain sampling is not feasible ([[natowicz-2024-postmortem-brain-tissue-autism-science]], expert-opinion)
- **The etiological basis for an individual's autism is rarely determined**; underlying molecular and cellular mechanisms remain poorly understood despite genetic advances
- **Many genetic variants associated with ASD** involve chromatin remodeling and synapse organization/function, but gene expression and protein studies require brain tissue
- **Current brain banks face severe shortages** of ASD postmortem tissue, limiting the scope and statistical power of neuropathological studies
- **Brain tissue enables multi-omics approaches** (transcriptomics, proteomics, metabolomics, epigenomics) not possible with peripheral samples or imaging alone

## Detailed Summary

This perspective article highlights a critical bottleneck in autism neuroscience: the scarcity of postmortem brain tissue for research. While genetic studies have identified hundreds of ASD-associated variants, understanding how these variants affect brain development and function at the molecular and cellular level requires direct examination of brain tissue.

The authors argue that postmortem brain studies have already yielded important insights, including altered cortical minicolumn organization, neuroinflammatory markers, and synaptic density changes in ASD. However, the small number of available specimens has limited statistical power and the ability to account for the enormous heterogeneity within ASD.

The article emphasizes that the brain is the primary organ system affected in ASD, and that peripheral biomarkers (blood, urine, stool) — while valuable for clinical screening — cannot substitute for direct brain tissue analysis when investigating pathophysiology. Advances in single-cell transcriptomics, spatial transcriptomics, and proteomics make brain tissue more valuable than ever, yet donation rates remain inadequate.
