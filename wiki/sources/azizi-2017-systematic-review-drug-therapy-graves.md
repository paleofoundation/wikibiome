# A systematic review of drug therapy for Graves' hyperthyroidism

## Key Claims

- Antithyroid drugs (ATDs), particularly methimazole and propylthiouracil, remain the first-line treatment for [[Graves' disease]] hyperthyroidism in most countries, with remission rates of 40-60% after 12-18 months of standard therapy
- Long-term low-dose methimazole therapy (>18 months, up to 60-120 months) achieves significantly higher remission rates (up to 70-80%) compared to standard 12-18 month courses, with minimal side effects
- Methimazole is preferred over propylthiouracil due to longer half-life (once-daily dosing), fewer hepatotoxic side effects, and more consistent efficacy; propylthiouracil is reserved for first trimester pregnancy and thyroid storm
- Predictors of relapse after ATD withdrawal include persistent TRAb positivity, large goiter size, high T3/T4 ratio, male sex, smoking, and young age at diagnosis
- [[Selenium]] supplementation (200 mcg/day) as adjunct to ATDs may improve biochemical response and reduce thyroid antibody levels, though evidence remains mixed

## Methods/Findings

- **Study type**: Systematic review of randomized controlled trials and observational studies on drug therapy for GD
- **Methimazole vs PTU**: Methimazole preferred for most patients; PTU associated with higher rates of hepatotoxicity, vasculitis, and requires multiple daily doses
- **Standard duration**: 12-18 months conventional recommendation; relapse rate 40-60% within 1-2 years of discontinuation
- **Extended therapy**: Studies from Japan and Iran show long-term low-dose methimazole (2.5-5 mg/day for 5-10 years) achieves 70-80% sustained remission with excellent safety profile
- **Adjunctive therapies**: Selenium (200 mcg/day sodium selenite) showed benefit in some RCTs for reducing TPOAb and improving quality of life; vitamin D supplementation under investigation
- **TRAb as predictor**: TRAb normalization at end of ATD course is the strongest predictor of sustained remission; persistent TRAb >1.5 IU/L associated with >80% relapse risk
- **Side effects**: Agranulocytosis (0.1-0.5%, most serious); hepatotoxicity (PTU > methimazole); arthralgia; skin reactions; these are generally reversible with drug discontinuation
- **Combination approaches**: Block-and-replace regimen (high-dose ATD + levothyroxine) does not improve remission rates but reduces hypothyroid episodes during treatment
