## Key Findings

- **Deferiprone reduced intracellular [[iron]] by 33.1%** and corresponding bacterial load by 78% in vitro, demonstrating direct linkage between iron availability and pathogen survival ([[caruffo-2020-deferiprone-nutritional-immunity-piscirickettsia]], animal-model).
- **In vivo fish mortality reduced by 34.9%** with deferiprone treatment, confirming therapeutic efficacy of pharmacological iron chelation.
- **"Assisted nutritional immunity"** — the concept that chelation amplifies the host's own iron-withholding defense rather than directly killing bacteria — validated as a host-directed therapeutic strategy.

## Methods

In-vitro cell infection model: fish cell lines (SHK-1 macrophage-like cells) infected with *Piscirickettsia salmonis*, treated with deferiprone (3,4-dimethyl-1-hydroxypyridin-2-one) at multiple concentrations. Intracellular iron measured by colorimetric assay. Bacterial load quantified by qPCR and colony-forming units. In-vivo challenge: Atlantic salmon infected with *P. salmonis* and treated with deferiprone administered via feed supplementation. ## Detailed Summary

Caruffo et al. introduce the concept of "assisted nutritional immunity" — using pharmacological iron chelation not as a direct antimicrobial but as an amplifier of the host's own innate metal-withholding defense.

**The host-directed therapy rationale.** Conventional antimicrobials target bacterial processes directly. Host-directed therapies instead modify the host environment to make it inhospitable to the pathogen. Iron chelation occupies a unique position: it does not kill bacteria directly but deprives them of an essential nutrient, mimicking and strengthening the host's natural [[nutritional-immunity]] response (hepcidin-mediated iron sequestration, lactoferrin, transferrin).

**In-vitro quantification.** In SHK-1 macrophage-like cells infected with *P. salmonis* (an obligate intracellular pathogen), deferiprone treatment reduced intracellular iron by 33.1% and bacterial load by 78%. The disproportionate effect — a modest iron reduction causing a dramatic bacterial reduction — suggests that *P. salmonis* operates near the threshold of iron sufficiency, and even partial iron restriction pushes the pathogen below its survival requirement. This is consistent with the "Achilles' heel" concept (Primitive 4): pathogens dependent on a specific metal are exquisitely sensitive to restriction of that metal.

**In-vivo validation.** When administered to Atlantic salmon challenged with *P. salmonis*, deferiprone reduced mortality by 34.9%. The effect was achieved without direct antibacterial activity — deferiprone has no intrinsic antimicrobial properties. Instead, the chelator reduced the bioavailable iron pool that the pathogen requires for intracellular replication, essentially turning the host's own iron economy against the invader.

**The "assisted" distinction.** The authors emphasize that deferiprone does not replace the host's nutritional immunity — it assists it. The host is already restricting iron (via hepcidin upregulation, which is elevated during infection). Deferiprone amplifies this existing defense by chelating iron that the host's own mechanisms miss, particularly intracellular iron pools that hepcidin cannot access.

## Relevance to WikiBiome

Key proof-of-concept for Primitive 2 (nutritional immunity as interpretive constraint) applied therapeutically. Demonstrates that pharmacological chelation mimics and enhances the host's innate metal-sequestration response. Informs [[nutritional-immunity]] and [[iron]] entity pages. The "assisted nutritional immunity" concept is a candidate for its own concept page — it represents a therapeutic paradigm that bridges metal biology and clinical intervention. Also relevant to the [[siderophores]] concept page, since pathogens that use siderophores to overcome host iron restriction would be targetable by chelation that competes with siderophore-mediated iron acquisition.

## Relevance to Cureva

Deferiprone as a candidate intervention for conditions where intracellular iron-dependent pathogens drive disease. The "assisted nutritional immunity" framework is directly applicable to human conditions with elevated hepcidin and calprotectin — chelation supports, rather than replaces, host defense. Important caveat from [[kashyap-2014-pgrps-kill-bacteria-metal-stress]]: chelation must be calibrated to avoid depleting the iron needed for host immune metal weaponization. The distinction between intracellular chelation (supporting immunity) and extracellular chelation (potentially impairing it) is clinically critical.

## Open Questions

- Can the "assisted nutritional immunity" framework translate from fish to mammalian systems — do human macrophages show similar iron-dependent control of intracellular pathogens?
- What is the optimal deferiprone dosing that restricts pathogen iron without impairing host iron-dependent immune functions (e.g., myeloperoxidase, NADPH oxidase)?
- Whether deferiprone or similar chelators could assist nutritional immunity against gut-resident pathogens (where the iron ecology is fundamentally different from intracellular compartments).
- How siderophore-producing pathogens respond to deferiprone — can they outcompete the chelator for iron?
