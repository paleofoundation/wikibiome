# Metals and Alzheimer's Disease: Mechanistic Review

## Key Claims

- **Document type**: Preprint review (NOT peer-reviewed), posted 30 January 2025 on Preprints.org
- Reviewed 46 original mechanistic research articles (2014-2024): 6 on arsenic, 9 on manganese, 21 on lead, 10 on cadmium
- Heavy metals share overlapping AD-relevant pathways: [[oxidative-stress|oxidative stress]], [[mitochondrial dysfunction]], protein aggregation, neuroinflammation, autophagy dysfunction, and tau hyperphosphorylation
- **Arsenic**: Disrupts S-nitrosylation (SNO) signaling in striatum and hippocampus; increases tau phosphorylation via GSK3-beta and ERK1/2 activation; chronic exposure in 3xTg-AD mice increases amyloid plaques, impairs ATP production, disrupts circadian rhythms
- **Manganese**: Impairs autophagy at low concentrations; Drp1 inhibition protects against Mn-induced autophagic impairment; acute Mn exposure increases cortical GLAST expression and seizure susceptibility in APP/PSEN1 mice; astrocytic REST deletion worsens Mn toxicity
- **Lead**: 21 studies reviewed (most of any metal); Pb induces blood-brain barrier disruption; associated with neuroinflammation and glutamate excitotoxicity
- **Cadmium**: 10 studies reviewed; Cd induces BBB disruption, calcium signaling disruption, mitochondrial dysfunction; associated with AD pathology via multiple mechanisms
- Metal-specific effects: Pb and Cd induce BBB disruption; As and Mn associated with neuroinflammation and glutamate excitotoxicity; As uniquely affects amyloid precursor protein processing and nitric oxide signaling

## Methods/Findings

- **Search strategy**: PubMed database, August-September 2024; keywords: "[Metal] AND Alzheimer's Disease"; restricted to English-language mechanistic studies from the last 10 years
- **Screening**: 391 articles identified, 125 unique, 78 full-text reviewed, 46 included
- **Study types included**: Only cell and animal model studies (mechanistic); excluded epidemiological, in silico, reviews, mixed-metal studies
- **Arsenic results (Table 1)**: 6 studies using mouse models (3xTg-AD, Wistar rats) and SH-SY5Y cells; exposures primarily via drinking water at 1-10 ppm; key outcomes include increased A-beta production, elevated BACE1 activity, RAGE levels up 220-fold, tau hyperphosphorylation at Ser262
- **Manganese results (Table 2)**: 9 studies using mouse models and primary astrocytes; key findings include Drp1-mediated autophagy impairment, glutamate dyshomeostasis, REST deletion worsening neurotoxicity
- **Dose-dependency**: Arsenic showed dose-dependent tau phosphorylation (significant at 10 ppm but not 1 ppm)
