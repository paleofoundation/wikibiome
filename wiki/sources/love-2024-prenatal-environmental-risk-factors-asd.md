# Overview

Narrative review assessing evidence for **potentially modifiable prenatal environmental risk factors** for ASD and their underlying mechanisms. Focuses on maternal infection, obesity/gestational diabetes, SSRI use, antibiotic use, and exposure to toxicants, with emphasis on the role of the **gut microbiome**.

# Key Findings

## Maternal Infection During Pregnancy

- Maternal immune activation (MIA) increases placental and fetal inflammation
- Rubella in first trimester: **2.6-fold increase** in ASD risk; influenza with fever: **3.0-fold increase**
- IL-17a blocking in MIA models prevents ASD-like behaviors in offspring
- MIA drives altered offspring mitochondrial function and increased intestinal permeability

## Maternal Obesity and GDM

- GDM associated with **1.5-1.7-fold increase** in ASD risk
- Maternal metabolic hormones (leptin, insulin) and proinflammatory cytokines transmitted to fetus
- Both conditions alter maternal gut microbiome, increasing LPS translocation and systemic inflammation

## Maternal SSRI Use

- 1.5-4.5-fold increase in ASD risk associated with prenatal SSRI use
- SSRIs cross placenta, alter offspring serotonin production and SCFA release
- Association strongest during **first trimester** -- coincides with critical neurodevelopmental window

## Maternal Antibiotic Use

- Antibiotics increase maternal intestinal permeability, alter SCFA release, and increase LPS translocation
- Maternal immune activation (IL-17 increase) from antibiotic-driven dysbiosis contributes to ASD risk

## Summary Table of Mechanisms

- **Inflammation**: common to all risk factors (maternal cytokines, placental immune activation)
- **Mitochondrial dysfunction**: altered morphology and function in offspring across exposures
- **Gut microbiome**: permeability changes, altered SCFA/serotonin production, LPS translocation
- **Hormonal disruption**: fetal testosterone disruption, neuroestrogen production changes
- **Oxidative stress**: elevated in offspring across multiple exposures
