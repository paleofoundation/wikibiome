# Chen 2021 — Alterations of vaginal microbiota in women with infertility and Chlamydia trachomatis infection

**Citation:** Chen H, Wang L, Zhao L, Luo L, Min S, Wen Y, Lei W, Shu M, Li Z. *Alterations of vaginal microbiota in women with infertility and Chlamydia trachomatis infection.* Frontiers in Cellular and Infection Microbiology. 2021;11:698840. DOI: [10.3389/fcimb.2021.698840](https://doi.org/10.3389/fcimb.2021.698840)

## Key Findings

- **L. iners replaces L. crispatus in CT-positive tubal infertility.** Women with tubal infertility + C. trachomatis (CT-P) showed L. iners-dominated vaginal microbiota (accounting for >70% of all vaginal bacteria), whereas healthy controls and CT-negative infertile women more commonly had L. crispatus-dominance. L. iners lacks histone-deacetylase-4 regulatory capacity and does not produce D-lactic acid, failing to restrict C. trachomatis proliferation — consistent with Edwards et al. 2019 and van Houdt et al. 2018.
- **Diversity loss.** Shannon α-diversity significantly lower in CT-P (1.32±1.29) vs. CT-C healthy (2.34±1.44, p<0.01) and CT-N infertile (1.82±1.14, p<0.05). Observed species, Simpson, Chao1 consistent with the Shannon pattern.
- **Cytokine profile is Th1/regulatory.** CT-P showed elevated IFN-γ (mean ~80 pg/mL vs ~40 in controls, p<0.01) and elevated IL-10 (p<0.05). TNF-α and IL-6 did not differ significantly across groups. This matches intracellular pathogen response (Chlamydia is an obligate intracellular bacterium triggering Th1 response).
- **Azithromycin-associated restoration, incomplete.** CT-PT (post-azithromycin) samples showed Lactobacillus increased from 48.8% to 70.0%; Acidobacteria decreased from 34.4% back toward baseline. Recovered species included L. crispatus, L. gasseri, L. jensenii, L. reuteri, L. aviaries, Bifidobacterium breve, Prevotella bivia, Atopobium vaginae. However, L. iners remained unusually high, and the C. trachomatis genome was still detectable — suggesting azithromycin resistance or persistent infection.
- **L. iners does not suppress C. trachomatis.** Lactobacillus negatively correlated with S. aureus in companion studies, but L. iners was not protective against C. trachomatis. L. crispatus specifically was shown in prior work to suppress CT adhesion (Nardini et al. 2016); its absence in CT-P patients plausibly contributes to CT persistence.

## Methods

30 women aged 20–49 recruited from PEC (physical examination center) and ART (assisted reproductive technology center), Chenzhou No.1 People's Hospital (2019–2020). Groups: CT-C (healthy + CT-negative, n=7), CT-N (tubal infertile + CT-negative, n=8), CT-P (tubal infertile + CT-positive pre-azithromycin, n=6), CT-PT (post-1g azithromycin, n=4). 16S rRNA V3–V4 Illumina NovaSeq sequencing. Species-specific qPCR for 17 dominant taxa. Cytokines by ELISA (TNF-α, IL-6, IFN-γ, IL-10). α-diversity via Shannon, Simpson, Chao1, ACE; β-diversity via Bray-Curtis, weighted/unweighted UniFrac, NMDS.

## Contradictions / Tensions

- The intracellular Chlamydia paradigm (azithromycin as gold standard) is partially contradicted by the persistence of CT genome in CT-PT samples. Biofilm and persistent reticulate body states may explain this — consistent with functional-shielding primitive (Karen's Brain #6).
- L. iners high frequency in CT-PT after azithromycin suggests iners may have selective survival advantage under macrolide pressure; azithromycin-resistant L. iners reported in Edwards 2019 and Tamarelle 2020.
- Small sample (n=25) — findings need replication.

## Open Questions

- Whether prophylactic L. crispatus delivery to women with L. iners-dominance prevents CT acquisition or recurrence.
- Whether the persistent CT genome in CT-PT represents viable organism or residual DNA from cleared infection.
- Whether tubal pathology reverses if L. crispatus dominance is restored.
