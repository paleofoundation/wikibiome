# Dysbiosis in the Gut Microbiota of Patients with Multiple Sclerosis, with a Striking Depletion of Species Belonging to Clostridia XIVa and IV Clusters

## Summary

This foundational study from Japan compared the gut microbiota of 20 relapsing-remitting MS patients with 40 healthy controls and 18 additional longitudinal healthy subjects using 16S rRNA gene pyrosequencing. It is one of the first studies to identify specific clostridial species depleted in MS and their connection to Treg cell induction.

## Key Findings

### Species-Level Alterations
- 21 species showed significantly different relative abundance between MS and controls
- 14 of these belonged to the clostridial clade: 12 in *Clostridia* cluster XIVa and 2 in cluster IV
- All 14 clostridial species were less abundant in MS patients
- These clostridial species are phylogenetically close to the 17 chloroform-resistant, spore-forming Treg-inducing strains described by Atarashi et al.
- Sequence similarity between the 14 MS-depleted clostridial species and the 17 Treg-inducing species was less than or equal to 95%

### Specifically Altered Taxa
**Increased in MS:**
- *Streptococcus thermophilus* (or *S. salivarius*) - significant increase
- *Eggerthella lenta* - significant increase
- *Bifidobacterium* - tended to increase

**Decreased in MS:**
- *Bacteroides* (*B. stercoris*, *B. coprocola*, *B. coprophilus*)
- *Prevotella* (*P. copri*)
- *Sutterella* (*S. wadsworthensis*)
- *Faecalibacterium*, *Anaerostipes*
- Multiple species from *Clostridia* clusters XIVa and IV

### Diversity and Community Structure
- OTU/species numbers were slightly lower in MS (126.9) vs HC (172.8), though not statistically significant
- Shannon diversity was not significantly different
- UniFrac analysis showed significant differences in overall community structure (both weighted and unweighted, P<0.05)
- Higher inter-individual variability in MS patients compared to controls

### Longitudinal Validation
- Differences in species abundance were validated using longitudinal samples (158 samples from 18 healthy subjects over ~3 months)
- The consistency of differences over time confirmed the biological significance

## Relevance to Gut-Metal-Microbiome Axis

This study is critical for the [[gut-metal-microbiome]] hypothesis because:

1. **Clostridia clusters XIVa and IV** are the primary Treg-inducing, [[short-chain-fatty-acids]]-producing bacteria in the human gut, and they are among the most metal-sensitive taxa known
2. **The striking depletion of these specific Clostridia** in MS directly parallels the effects of [[cadmium]], [[lead]], and [[nickel]] exposure on gut microbiota in animal models
3. **Treg deficiency** resulting from clostridial depletion removes a key brake on autoimmune [[inflammation]], enabling the autoreactive T cell responses characteristic of MS
4. **Japanese cohort** - Japan has experienced rising MS incidence coinciding with westernization and changing environmental exposures, supporting environmental (including metal) triggers
5. The increase in *Streptococcus* and *Eggerthella* may represent metal-tolerant opportunists that expand when metal-sensitive Clostridia are depleted

The depletion of these specific clostridial species represents one of the most reproducible findings across MS microbiome studies worldwide, suggesting a fundamental mechanism linking [[dysbiosis]] to [[multiple-sclerosis]] through impaired Treg induction.

## Limitations

- Relatively small Japanese cohort; limited ethnic diversity
- 16S rRNA pyrosequencing (older technology vs. current methods)
- Cross-sectional design
- Only included relapsing-remitting MS phenotype
