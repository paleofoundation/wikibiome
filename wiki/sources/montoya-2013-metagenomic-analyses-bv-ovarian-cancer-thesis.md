## Key Findings

- **Diverse microbial population observed in ovarian cancer transcriptome data**, including organisms not previously associated with the reproductive tract or ovarian tissue
- **Novel metagenomic pipeline** applied to both BV vaginal samples and ovarian cancer transcriptome data revealed organisms that standard culture-based methods miss
- **[[debaryomyces]]** (a fungal genus) was detected in ovarian cancer-associated microbial communities, suggesting interkingdom interactions in the tumor microenvironment
- **Bacteriophage sequences** were identified alongside bacterial communities, indicating complex viral-bacterial dynamics within the reproductive tract microbiome
- **Bacterial vaginosis and ovarian cancer** may share aspects of reproductive tract microbial dysbiosis, with loss of [[lactobacillus]] dominance as a common feature

## Methods

- **Study type**: MSc thesis applying novel metagenomic approaches to two female genital tract diseases
- **Approach**: Next-generation sequencing (NGS)-based metagenomic analysis of vaginal samples (BV) and ovarian cancer transcriptome data
- **Bioinformatic pipeline**: Custom metagenomic pipeline for identifying microbial sequences within human tissue RNA-seq data (subtraction-based approach)
- **Novelty**: Application of metagenomics to ovarian cancer tissue was relatively uncommon at time of publication (2013)
- **Limitation**: MSc thesis, not peer-reviewed in the traditional journal sense; relatively small sample sizes expected

## Detailed Summary

This MSc thesis from the University of British Columbia applies metagenomic approaches to characterize microbial communities associated with two female genital tract diseases: bacterial vaginosis (BV) and ovarian cancer. While BV is a well-characterized polymicrobial condition, the existence of a tumor-associated microbiome in ovarian cancer was a relatively novel concept at the time of this work (2013).

For the BV component, the metagenomic pipeline characterizes the polymicrobial communities that replace [[lactobacillus]]-dominant vaginal flora, identifying both well-known BV-associated organisms ([[gardnerella]], [[prevotella]], Atopobium) and less commonly reported taxa. The NGS approach provides culture-independent community profiling with higher sensitivity than traditional methods.

For the ovarian cancer component, the thesis applies a subtraction-based metagenomic approach to ovarian cancer transcriptome (RNA-seq) data. By subtracting human sequences, the remaining reads can be mapped to microbial reference genomes to identify organisms present in tumor tissue. This revealed a diverse microbial population including bacteria, fungi ([[debaryomyces]]), and bacteriophages.

The detection of [[debaryomyces]] in ovarian cancer-associated communities is noteworthy because this fungal genus has subsequently been implicated in other cancer contexts (colorectal cancer) and is known to affect inflammatory responses. The interkingdom perspective -- bacteria, fungi, and phages coexisting in tumor-associated communities -- suggests complex ecological dynamics that may modulate tumor behavior.

The conceptual connection between BV and ovarian cancer is epidemiologically supported: chronic pelvic inflammatory disease and upper genital tract infections are risk factors for ovarian cancer. The ascending infection hypothesis proposes that pathogenic organisms from the vaginal microbiome can colonize the fallopian tubes and ovarian surface epithelium, contributing to chronic inflammation and carcinogenesis.
