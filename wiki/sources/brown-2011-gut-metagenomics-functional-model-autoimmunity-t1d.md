# Key Claims

- **Functional model proposed**: autoimmune subjects have functionally aberrant microbiome with higher proportion of butyrate-producing and mucin-degrading bacteria in controls vs cases
- **Key rate-limiting step**: butyrate synthesis is more abundant in controls; autoimmune subjects show higher proportion of bacteria producing non-butyrate SCFAs
- **Consortium of lactate- and butyrate-producing bacteria** in healthy gut maintains gut integrity through mucin synthesis
- **Increased adhesion and flagella synthesis** in autoimmune subjects may trigger T1D-associated autoimmune response
- **Over 30 billion nucleotide bases** analyzed from matched T1D case-control stool samples

# Methods & Findings

Shotgun metagenomic analysis of 4 matched T1D case-control pairs:

- 3,849 functions identified; 144 and 797 statistically more prevalent in cases and controls respectively
- Genes involved in carbohydrate metabolism, adhesions, motility, phages, stress responses more prevalent in cases
- DNA and protein metabolism, aerobic respiration, amino acid synthesis genes more common in controls

# Relevance to Metallomic Framework

- **[[zinc]]**: Butyrate synthase requires zinc as cofactor -- zinc deficiency may explain the butyrate deficit in autoimmune subjects
- **[[iron]]**: Iron-dependent adhesion factors increased in autoimmune subjects may promote bacterial translocation through compromised barrier
- **[[selenium]]**: Selenium-dependent antioxidant genes (more common in controls) may protect against autoimmune-triggering oxidative damage
- **[[gut-metal-microbiome]]**: The functional metabolic differences between cases and controls may reflect distinct metal environments
- **[[dysbiosis]]**: Functional [[dysbiosis]] (not just taxonomic) is the key driver -- metal availability shapes metabolic capacity
- **[[intestinal-permeability]]**: Loss of butyrate-mucin synthesis consortium directly causes barrier dysfunction
