# Key Claims

- CD microbiota [[dysbiosis]] involves bacteria, fungi (mycobiome), viruses (virome), and archaea (archaeome), requiring multi-kingdom therapeutic approaches
- [[faecalibacterium-prausnitzii]] depletion with increased adherent-invasive [[escherichia-coli]] is the hallmark bacterial dysbiosis in CD
- Fungal dysbiosis in CD includes reduced Saccharomyces cerevisiae diversity and increased Candida albicans
- Medications commonly used in CD management (aminosalicylates, immunomodulators, biologics, antibiotics, PPIs) directly or indirectly affect the gut microbiome
- FMT, probiotics, prebiotics, synbiotics, and personalized diets represent microbiome-targeted therapeutic strategies

# Methods/Findings

- **Study type**: Comprehensive review of microbiota-targeted therapies for CD
- **Bacterial dysbiosis**: Decreased Firmicutes (especially F. prausnitzii, Roseburia), increased Proteobacteria, increased AIEC
- **Mycobiome**: Increased Candida, decreased S. cerevisiae; Basidiomycota/Ascomycota ratio altered
- **Virome**: Decreased phage diversity; increased phages targeting inflammatory bacteria
- **Archaeome**: Increased Methanosphaera stadtmanae (immunogenic); decreased methanogens
- **Drug effects**: Antibiotics, PPIs, immunosuppressants, and biologics each distinctly alter microbiome composition

# Connections

- Comprehensive framework for understanding [[dysbiosis]] correction in [[crohns-disease]]
- [[probiotics]] (Lactobacillus, Bifidobacterium, E. coli Nissle) show strain-specific effects
- [[bacteroides-fragilis]] and its polysaccharide A play immunomodulatory roles relevant to CD therapy
- Antibiotic-induced [[dysbiosis]] may paradoxically worsen CD despite treating infections
- Metal availability affects fungal-bacterial interactions in the [[gut-metal-microbiome]], relevant to multi-kingdom dysbiosis
