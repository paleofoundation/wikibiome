# Nickel Contact Dermatitis in Children

## Overview

Nickel is the most commonly detected cutaneous allergen on patch testing in US children. This review covers epidemiology, pathogenesis, clinical presentations, exposure sources, and treatment of [[nickel-allergy]] in pediatric populations.

## Epidemiology

- Sensitization prevalence: **14-20% of women**, **2-4% of men** in the US, with increasing incidence
- Children with atopic dermatitis show markedly higher sensitization: **28%** vs **2%** as the leading allergen on patch testing
- [[nickel]] allergy is the most common contact allergy identified in children

## Clinical Classifications

1. **Local**: Dermatitis at the direct contact site (e.g., under belt buckle, earring)
2. **Ectopic**: Dermatitis transferred from contact site (e.g., face touched by nickel-contaminated fingers)
3. **Idiopathic ("id" reaction)**: Auto-eczematization at flexural areas without direct nickel contact
4. **Systemic**: Dermatitis triggered by dietary nickel intake, linking to [[dietary-nickel-exposure]]

## Pathogenesis

- Dendritic cells take up nickel ions and present them to T cells (Th1/Th17 pathways)
- Clonal T-cell expansion leads to cytokine release
- Nickel-loaded keratinocytes undergo apoptosis via perforin-dependent mechanism
- **TLR-4 activation** is a key step in the innate immune response to nickel
- Genetic susceptibility may involve T-cell receptor mutations in CDR (complementarity-determining region) loops

## Modern Exposure Sources

Beyond traditional sources (jewelry, belt buckles), newer exposures relevant to children include:
- Cell phone cases
- Tablets and laptops
- Wrist-worn devices (fitness trackers, smartwatches)
- Metal components in clothing fasteners

## Nickel-Rich Foods Relevant to Children

- Oatmeal
- Beans
- Chocolate
- Canned foods
- Shellfish
- Soy products

These dietary sources are significant for children with systemic nickel allergy, connecting to [[dietary-nickel-exposure]].

## Treatment Approaches

- **Primary**: Avoidance of nickel-containing items
- **DMG (dimethylglyoxime) spot test**: For screening household items and personal objects for nickel release
- **Low-nickel diet**: Intake of 100-150 μg/day shown to be beneficial for sensitized individuals
- **Topical corticosteroids**: First-line pharmacotherapy
- **Calcineurin inhibitors**: Steroid-sparing alternative (tacrolimus, pimecrolimus)
- **UVB phototherapy**: For refractory cases

### Notable Finding

A nickel-free diet can enhance **H. pylori eradication** therapy, suggesting systemic effects of dietary nickel reduction beyond dermatological outcomes.
