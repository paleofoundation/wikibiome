# Fatty Acid Profiles and Gut Microbiome in Parkinson's Disease

## Summary

This study characterizes fatty acid profiles in [[parkinsons-disease]] patients and their relationship to gut microbiome composition. It examines both [[short-chain-fatty-acids]] (microbially produced) and long-chain fatty acids (dietary and host-derived), connecting lipid metabolism to PD pathophysiology.

## Key Findings

- Fecal short-chain fatty acid levels ([[butyrate]], propionate, acetate) are reduced in PD
- Plasma long-chain fatty acid profiles are altered in PD, with shifts in omega-3/omega-6 ratios
- Specific gut bacteria correlate with distinct fatty acid profiles
- SCFA-producing bacteria (Faecalibacterium, Roseburia) are depleted alongside reduced butyrate
- Altered lipid metabolism in PD may reflect both microbiome changes and mitochondrial dysfunction
- Polyunsaturated fatty acid levels correlate with oxidative stress markers
- Fatty acid profiles may serve as biomarkers for PD stage and progression

## Relevance to Gut-Metal-Microbiome Axis

Fatty acid metabolism is centrally connected to [[ferroptosis]] -- the [[iron]]-dependent cell death pathway implicated in PD dopaminergic neuron loss. Polyunsaturated fatty acids (PUFAs) in neuronal membranes are the substrates for [[iron]]-catalyzed lipid peroxidation, the terminal step in ferroptosis. The PD-associated shift toward pro-oxidant lipid profiles increases vulnerability to [[iron]]-dependent cell death. Reduced [[butyrate]] removes the epigenetic brake (HDAC inhibition) that upregulates anti-ferroptotic defenses (GPX4, system Xc-). The omega-3/omega-6 ratio alteration affects [[neuroinflammation]] through eicosanoid pathways that are regulated by [[iron]]-containing lipoxygenases. This study connects gut microbial [[short-chain-fatty-acids]] depletion to the lipid peroxidation vulnerability that underlies [[ferroptosis]] in PD.

## Limitations

- Cross-sectional study
- Fecal SCFAs may not reflect tissue-level concentrations
- Dietary fatty acid intake not fully controlled
- Complex interplay between host and microbial lipid metabolism difficult to disentangle
