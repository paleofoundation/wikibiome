# Key Claims

- Dietary factors have important effects on both estrogenic activity and inflammatory processes central to [[endometriosis]] pathophysiology
- Consumption of trans fats, palmitic acid, and red meat is associated with increased risk of endometriosis
- Plant-based foods, particularly fiber, antioxidants, and vitamin D, may have helpful effects for endometriosis prevention and treatment
- Reducing dietary fat and increasing dietary fiber can reduce circulating estrogen concentrations by 10-25%, which is clinically relevant for this estrogen-dependent disease
- Seaweed may have estrogen-modulating properties relevant to endometriosis

# Methods/Findings

- **Study type**: Mini review of current evidence on nutrition and endometriosis

 - Palmitic acid (from meat and dairy) and trans fat associated with increased endometriosis risk (2010 prospective study)
 - NIH-AARP Diet and Health Study: highest total fat intake had 28% greater risk for ovarian cancer; only animal-derived fats showed significant positive correlation
 - Omega-3 consumption (70,709 premenopausal women) associated with lower endometriosis risk

 - Nurses' Health Study II: >2 servings red meat/day = 56% greater risk of endometriosis (RR 1.56, 95% CI 1.22-1.99)
 - Increased poultry intake also associated with higher risk (RR 1.14, 95% CI 0.88-1.49, not significant for specific categories)
 - Red meat consumption raises estradiol and estrone sulfate levels

 - 8-week study: strict vegan diet produced less inflammation than animal-product diet
 - Randomized crossover trial: low-fat vegan diet increased sex-hormone binding globulin and reduced estrogen activity, reducing dysmenorrhea severity and duration
 - Low-Glycemic Index foods associated with reduced endometriosis risk (NHS II, 81,961 women)
- **Vitamin D**: Low levels associated with increased endometriosis risk; 50,000 IU every 2 weeks for 12 weeks reduced pelvic pain by 1.12 points and reduced hs-CRP
- **Antioxidants**: Women with endometriosis had 30% lower vitamin C intake and 40% less vitamin E; supplementation with vitamins C and E significantly reduced endometriosis symptoms vs. placebo
- **Seaweed**: Inverse dose-response relationship between seaweed consumption and serum estradiol in postmenopausal women; may reduce estradiol in premenopausal women
