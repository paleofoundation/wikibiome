# Key Claims

- **Gut microbial diversity and composition** differ significantly between PPD patients and healthy controls
- **Firmicutes phyla** relative abundance was lower in [[postpartum-depression]] patients
- **Reduced levels** of *Faecalibacterium*, *Phascolarctobacterium*, *Butyricicoccus*, and *Lachnospiraceae* in PPD patients, with increased *Enterobacteriaceae*
- **Correlation** between levels of *Phascolarctobacterium*, *Lachnospiraceae*, *Faecalibacterium*, and *Tyzzerella.3* and the severity of depressive symptoms
- Various bacteria associated with levels of **sex hormones**, suggesting gut microbiota-based biomarkers may aid PPD diagnosis

# Methods & Findings

- **Design**: Case-control study; 57 participants (28 PPD, 16 healthy controls); fecal samples analyzed by 16S rRNA gene sequencing
- **Setting**: Fourth Clinical Medical College, Guangzhou University of Chinese Medicine, Shenzhen, China

 - Reduced SCFA-producing taxa in PPD suggests impaired [[gut-brain-axis]] signaling
 - Increased pro-[[inflammation]]atory *Enterobacteriaceae* in PPD patients
 - Sex hormone levels correlated with specific bacterial taxa, linking [[dysbiosis]] to hormonal disruption postpartum

# Connections

- Supports [[dysbiosis]] as a feature of PPD, paralleling findings in major depressive disorder
- SCFA-producing taxa depletion relevant to [[gut-brain-axis]] communication via vagal and immune pathways
- Hormonal-microbiome interactions relevant to [[developmental-metal-vulnerability]] in the perinatal period
- Biomarker potential connects to trace element studies in [[zinc]], [[iron]], [[copper]], and [[magnesium]] deficiency in PPD
