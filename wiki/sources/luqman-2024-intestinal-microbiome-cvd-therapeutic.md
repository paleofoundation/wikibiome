# Key Claims

- Intestinal [[dysbiosis]] promotes CVD risk factors including heart failure, hypertension, and atherosclerosis through altered metabolite production and immune dysregulation
- [[tmao]] levels in the bloodstream encourage lipid accumulation in arteries, contributing to [[atherosclerosis]]; elevated circulating TMAO is associated with increased CVD risk and mortality
- In CVD patients, >90% of gut bacteria are *Bacteroidetes* and *Firmicutes*, and the Firmicutes/Bacteroidetes (F/B) ratio is a crucial indicator linked to obesity and low-grade inflammation
- Heart failure patients show decreased butyrate-producing bacteria (*Eubacterium hallii*, *Lachnospiraceae*) and the absence of these beneficial microbes is associated with higher mortality and disease severity
- A comprehensive table documents CVD-specific microbial shifts: in atherosclerosis/CAD, *Bactericides* and *Prevotella* decrease while *Streptococcus* and *Escherichia* increase; in hypertension, *Prevotella* and *Klebsiella* increase; in heart failure, *Blautia*, *Collinsella* and Ruminococcaceae decrease
- TMAO could be utilized as a biomarker to predict poor outcomes in HFrEF patients; the higher TMAO precursor trimethyl lysine (TML) is linked to progressive reduction in fatty acid oxidation
- Extracellular vesicles (EVs) from gut bacteria (e.g., *B. fragilis*) may serve as atherosclerosis indicators

# Methods/Findings

- **Study type**: Comprehensive narrative review
- **Coverage**: TMAO metabolite pathway, gut microbiota composition/diversity, heart failure mechanisms, atherosclerosis pathogenesis, and therapeutic options (prebiotics, probiotics, antibiotics, [[fecal-microbiota-transplant]])
- **Key metabolic pathways**: Choline/phosphatidylcholine to TMA to TMAO (via hepatic FMO3); SCFAs from dietary fiber; secondary bile acids; uremic toxins
- **Therapeutic interventions**: Reducing dietary TMAO precursors; pre/probiotics and antibiotics; fecal microbiota transplantation; TMAO reduction strategies
