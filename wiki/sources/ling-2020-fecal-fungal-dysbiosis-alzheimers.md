# Fecal Fungal Dysbiosis in Chinese Patients with Alzheimer's Disease

## Summary

This study characterizes the fecal fungal communities in Chinese patients with [[alzheimers-disease]] compared to healthy controls using ITS sequencing. While focused on AD, the findings are directly relevant to [[parkinsons-disease]] given the shared neurodegenerative mechanisms and overlapping [[mycobiome]] disruptions.

## Key Findings

- AD patients showed significantly altered gut fungal community composition
- *[[candida-albicans]]* was enriched and *[[saccharomyces]]* was depleted in AD patients
- Fungal diversity metrics differed between AD and controls
- Fungal community shifts correlated with clinical cognitive measures
- Several fungal genera showed associations with inflammatory markers
- The pattern of fungal dysbiosis parallels findings in other neurodegenerative conditions including PD

## Relevance to Gut-Metal-Microbiome Axis

The *Candida*-enriched, *Saccharomyces*-depleted pattern found in AD mirrors emerging findings in [[parkinsons-disease]], suggesting a shared [[mycobiome]] signature across neurodegenerative diseases. *Candida* species are metabolically versatile opportunists with extensive metal acquisition systems -- their expansion in the dysbiotic gut reflects and reinforces [[iron]] and [[zinc]] dyshomeostasis. *Saccharomyces* depletion removes a genus with known probiotic properties and metal-binding capacity, further destabilizing the gut metal environment.

## Limitations

- Focused on AD, not PD directly -- cross-disease extrapolation needed cautiously
- Chinese cohort may not generalize to other populations
- ITS sequencing provides limited functional information
- Small sample size
- Cross-sectional design
