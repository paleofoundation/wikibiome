# Hepatitis C Virus and Parkinson's Disease Comorbidity

## Summary

This study examines the epidemiological and biological links between hepatitis C virus (HCV) infection and [[parkinsons-disease]] risk, exploring whether chronic viral hepatitis contributes to neurodegeneration through inflammatory and metabolic pathways.

## Key Findings

- HCV infection is associated with increased risk of PD in epidemiological studies
- Chronic HCV drives systemic inflammation that may reach the CNS
- HCV-associated liver dysfunction alters metal metabolism, particularly [[iron]] homeostasis
- HCV patients frequently develop iron overload, a known PD risk factor
- Antiviral treatment of HCV may reduce subsequent PD risk
- The HCV-PD link may be mediated through neuroinflammation, iron dysregulation, or both

## Relevance to Gut-Metal-Microbiome Axis

The HCV-PD association provides strong evidence for the viral-immune-metal axis in [[parkinsons-disease]]. HCV disrupts hepatic [[iron]] regulation (causing [[hepcidin]] suppression and iron overload) while simultaneously driving chronic [[inflammation]] and gut [[dysbiosis]]. HCV patients have well-documented gut microbiome alterations that overlap with PD [[dysbiosis]] patterns. The triple hit of viral inflammation, iron overload, and gut dysbiosis creates the precise conditions predicted by the metallomic-microbiome framework to drive neurodegeneration.

## Limitations

- Epidemiological association may be confounded by shared risk factors
- HCV treatment era effects (interferon vs. direct-acting antivirals) complicate analysis
- Sample sizes for PD outcomes in HCV cohorts are limited
- The specific neural mechanisms of HCV-PD connection remain hypothetical
