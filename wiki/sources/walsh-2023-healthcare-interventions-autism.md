# Overview

Systematic review of 31 studies evaluating interventions to improve **healthcare experiences and access in autistic persons**. Identifies patterns in intervention design, evaluation methods, and outcomes.

# Key Claims

- Autistic individuals experience **increased morbidity and mortality** compared to neurotypical controls
- Report **significant barriers to accessing healthcare** and receiving appropriate care
- Interventions most commonly **patient-focused (58.1%)** — supporting autistic individuals to engage with or tolerate medical procedures
- Fewer **provider-focused (48.4%)** or **organization-focused (6.5%)** interventions
- Outcomes predominantly **positive (80.6%)** but with significant methodological heterogeneity
- Most studies measure **reactions (45.2%)** or **behavior (48.4%)** rather than clinical outcomes

# Intervention Categories

**Patient-focused**: Communication aids, sensory accommodations, preparation/anticipation strategies, desensitization

**Provider-focused**: Training in autism characteristics, communication modification, sensory management

**Organization-focused**: Policies for scheduling flexibility, sensory-friendly environments

# Methodological Gaps

- Limited participant diversity
- Heterogeneous outcome measures
- Lack of standardization in intervention reporting
- Need for more provider and organizational intervention studies
- Insufficient measurement of what actually matters to autistic community

# Relevance to Metallomic Framework

**Minimal direct relevance** — this paper addresses healthcare access and provider communication rather than microbiome signatures or metal-based pathophysiology. However, it underscores the practical barriers to **rigorous clinical investigation** of biomarker-driven interventions in autistic populations, which impacts recruitment and retention in microbial/metallomic studies.
