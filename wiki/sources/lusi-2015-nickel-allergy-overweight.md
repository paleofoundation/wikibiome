## Key Claims

- Nickel allergy prevalence in overweight females (BMI >26) is 59.7%, dramatically higher than the 12.5% prevalence in the general female population (p<0.001)
- In overweight cases with metabolic syndrome and liver steatosis, nickel allergy prevalence is even higher at 61.1%
- A normocaloric [[low-nickel-diet]] significantly reduced BMI (mean decrease 4.2, p<0.001), body fat (decrease 5.1%, p<0.001), and waist circumference (decrease 11.7 cm, p<0.001) in 24 allergic overweight females over 6 months
- Nickel may have pleiotropic roles in determining overweight through immunological, biochemical, and microbiological pathways, including effects on insulin, glycogenolysis, and gut microbiota

## Methods/Findings

- **Design**: Pilot cross-sectional analysis at Italian Red Cross Allergy Unit, Rome
- **Population**: 87 overweight subjects (72 females, 15 males; BMI >26 kg/m2; mean age 54.2; mean BMI 32.0)
- **Nickel allergy diagnosis**: Patch test with 5% nickel sulfate (Lofarma Diagnostic); 45/87 (51.7%) were nickel allergic
- **Prevalence findings**: 43/72 (59.7%) overweight females allergic vs 12.5% general population (p<0.001); 2/15 (13.3%) males allergic vs 2% general population
- **Diet intervention**: Normocaloric balanced diet low in nickel (80-100 ug/daily); permitted: milk, eggs, meat, fish, refined flour, low-nickel vegetables/fruit; excluded: legumes, soy, whole-grain, tomatoes; some vegetables restricted (cauliflower, carrots, onions, spinach, lettuce)
- **Anthropometric results (24 women completing 6-month follow-up)**: BMI 31.6 to 27.4; body fat 44.8% to 39.6%; waist 113.8 to 102.2 cm; all p<0.001
- **Proposed mechanisms**: Nickel induces insulin-like actions, glycogenolysis, hyperglycemia in animal models; IL-17 (produced by nickel-specific T cells) is upregulated in obesity; estrogen deficiency in menopause may amplify nickel-mediated Th17 responses; H. pylori uses nickel for colonization and a low-nickel diet enhances its eradication

## Expanded Mechanistic Discussion

The association between nickel allergy and overweight likely operates through multiple converging pathways:

**IL-17/Th17 inflammatory pathway.** Nickel-specific T cells produce IL-17, a hallmark cytokine of the Th17 inflammatory axis. IL-17 is independently upregulated in obesity and drives chronic low-grade [[inflammation]] that disrupts insulin signaling, promotes adipogenesis, and impairs metabolic homeostasis. In nickel-allergic individuals, repeated dietary nickel exposure may sustain a Th17-dominant inflammatory state that contributes to [[metabolic-syndrome]]. Estrogen deficiency during menopause further amplifies Th17 responses, potentially explaining the high prevalence of both nickel allergy and overweight in postmenopausal women.

**Nickel as a metalloestrogen.** Nickel is classified among the [[metalloestrogens]] -- metals that can activate estrogen receptors and mimic estrogenic signaling. This hormonal mimicry may promote adipose tissue accumulation, alter fat distribution patterns, and disrupt the hypothalamic-pituitary-gonadal axis. The estrogen-like effects of dietary nickel could contribute to the sex-specific prevalence pattern (59.7% in overweight females vs 13.3% in males).

**Gut microbiota disruption.** Dietary nickel alters the composition and metabolic activity of the [[gut-metal-microbiome]]. Nickel-induced dysbiosis can shift microbial metabolism toward increased energy harvest, disrupt short-chain fatty acid profiles, and impair gut barrier function -- all mechanisms that promote weight gain and metabolic dysfunction. The authors note that H. pylori, a nickel-dependent gastric pathogen, is also associated with metabolic disturbances, and a low-nickel diet enhances its eradication (see [[campanale-2014-nickel-free-diet-h-pylori]]).

**Insulin-like effects of nickel.** Animal models show that nickel induces insulin-like actions, promotes glycogenolysis, and causes hyperglycemia -- direct metabolic effects that could contribute to weight gain and insulin resistance independent of the immune and microbial pathways.

The dramatic weight loss results (BMI decrease of 4.2, body fat decrease of 5.1%, waist circumference decrease of 11.7 cm) on a normocaloric low-nickel diet suggest that removing the nickel trigger -- rather than caloric restriction -- was the primary driver of metabolic improvement, consistent with the multi-pathway model above.
