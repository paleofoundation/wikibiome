# Heavy metal exposures in aerodigestive clinic cohort of infants with reflux or dysphagia

## Key Claims

- Infants (<1 year) with reflux or oropharyngeal dysphagia using rice/oatmeal cereal thickeners showed no significant difference in urinary [[arsenic]] levels between thickener groups, with all levels below ATSDR toxicity limits
- Only [[molybdenum]] showed significant difference between thickener groups after Bonferroni correction (p=0.0012), with oatmeal/rice cereal thickener associated with higher urinary molybdenum (p=0.00198)
- Alternative arsenic dietary servings were the only significant predictor of urinary arsenic exposure (p=0.0012), not the thickener type itself
- Analysis of 21 urinary elements revealed statistically significant differences for 9 elements (beryllium, manganese, iron, molybdenum, [[cadmium]], tin, antimony, [[mercury]], and [[lead]]) but none exceeded ATSDR toxic levels
- Four infants showed high element exposure across 16 of 21 elements measured, with no common demographic characteristic
- 57% of families had consumed at least one serving of food that may contain dietary arsenic (applesauce, brussels sprouts, carrots, sweet potato puree)

## Methods/Findings

- **Study type**: Single-center cross-sectional study at Boston Children's Hospital
- **Population**: 56 infants (<1 year) with reflux or oropharyngeal dysphagia; 27 control (Gelmix/purees), 19 Enfamil AR, 10 oatmeal/rice cereal
- **Period**: December 2021 to December 2023
- **Biomarker**: Urinary arsenic (spot urine from cotton pad in diaper); ICP-MS at Dartmouth Trace Element Analysis Core for 22 elements
- **Dietary assessment**: 3-day dietary recall; alternative arsenic sources quantified
- **Arsenic levels**: Median 1.78 ug/L (oatmeal/rice), 0.96 ug/L (Enfamil AR), 0.76 ug/L (controls); all below 100 ug/L ATSDR limit
- **Key limitation**: Cross-sectional design captures only short-term exposure; cumulative effects unknown
