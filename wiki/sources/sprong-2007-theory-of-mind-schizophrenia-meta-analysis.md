# Theory of Mind in Schizophrenia: Meta-Analysis

## Key Claims

- Twenty-nine studies of mentalising in [[schizophrenia]] (combined n = 1,518) were included, published between January 1993 and May 2006
- The estimated overall effect size was large and statistically significant (d = -1.255, P < 0.0001), confirming substantial theory of mind impairment
- Effect size was not significantly affected by sample characteristics
- All symptom subgroups showed significant mentalising impairment, but participants with symptoms of disorganisation were significantly more impaired than other subgroups (P < 0.01)
- Patients in remission were also impaired, favouring the notion that mentalising impairment represents a possible trait marker of schizophrenia
- The paranoid subgroup showed impaired theory of mind relative to controls, but did not differ significantly from non-paranoid patients
- Types of mentalising tasks included false belief/deception tasks, intention-inferring tasks, and indirect speech comprehension tasks

## Methods/Findings

- **Study type**: Meta-analysis using weighted regression-analysis techniques
- **Studies included**: 29 empirical studies in peer-reviewed journals
- **Effect size**: Large (d = -1.255), stable across subgroups
- **Task types**: First-order false belief, second-order false belief, intention-inferring, indirect speech
