# Metabolomics of Parkinson's Disease

## Summary

This metabolomics study identifies plasma metabolite signatures associated with [[parkinsons-disease]], using untargeted metabolomic profiling to characterize disease-associated metabolic alterations. The study links gut microbiome-derived metabolites to systemic metabolic changes detectable in blood.

## Key Findings

- Distinct plasma metabolomic signatures differentiate PD patients from healthy controls
- [[tryptophan-metabolism]] pathways are significantly altered in PD, with shifts toward the kynurenine pathway
- Bile acid profiles are altered, reflecting changes in gut microbial bile acid metabolism
- Lipid metabolism perturbations include changes in sphingolipids and glycerophospholipids
- Several microbiome-derived metabolites (indoles, phenols) are altered in PD plasma
- Metabolomic signatures correlate with disease severity and motor symptoms
- Multi-metabolite panels show potential as diagnostic biomarkers

## Relevance to Gut-Metal-Microbiome Axis

The metabolomic alterations in PD plasma provide a readout of the gut-metal-microbiome axis. Altered [[tryptophan-metabolism]] in PD connects to both microbiome composition (tryptophan-metabolizing bacteria are shifted in PD [[dysbiosis]]) and metal effects ([[iron]] is a cofactor for tryptophan hydroxylase and indoleamine 2,3-dioxygenase). The kynurenine pathway shift produces quinolinic acid, a potent [[iron]]-dependent neurotoxin that chelates Fe2+ and generates hydroxyl radicals. Altered bile acid profiles reflect both gut microbial changes and hepatic [[iron]] status, as bile acid synthesis is regulated by FXR signaling that is iron-sensitive. These metabolomic fingerprints may serve as accessible biomarkers for monitoring the gut-metal-microbiome axis disruption in PD.

## Limitations

- Cross-sectional study design
- Plasma metabolites reflect systemic changes, not exclusively gut-derived signals
- Medication effects on metabolome not fully controlled
- Validation in independent cohorts needed
