# Key Claims

- Endometriosis induction in mice did NOT significantly alter the fecal bacterial composition during the acute phase (days 7 and 21)
- Fecal microbiota composition remained very stable throughout the experiment in both endometriosis and sham-transplanted mice
- Alpha and beta diversity analyses revealed no significant differences between groups at any timepoint
- The most abundant genera were *Bacteroidales* S24-7 group, *Lactobacillus*, *Prevotellaceae* UCG-001, and *Lachnospiraceae* NK4A136
- This contrasts with two earlier studies that found gut microbiota changes with endometriosis in mice

# Methods/Findings

- **Study design**: Prospective, randomized mouse experiment with GFP+ uterine tissue transplantation
- **Model**: C57BL/6-TgN (ACTB-EGFP) donor tissue into C57BL/6 wild-type recipients; sham controls
- **Methods**: Fecal collection at days -3, 7, and 21; 16S rRNA V4-V5 sequencing (Illumina MiSeq)

 - All transplanted tissue developed into endometriotic lesions with typical histomorphology
 - Take rate: 23.8% +/- 4.2%; lesion size: 5.6 +/- 1.2 mm2
 - 3,703 OTUs from 101 genera, 35 families, 19 orders identified
 - No significant p-values at genus or family level after FDR correction
 - Strict experimental design with standardized tissue punch sizes and GFP tracking

# Relevance to Wiki

This negative-result study is important for the [[endometriosis]]-microbiome field, as it demonstrates that early-phase endometriotic lesion formation may not immediately alter gut microbial composition. The discrepancy with positive studies (Chadchan et al., Yuan et al.) may reflect differences in experimental duration (21 vs 42 days), mouse strains, diet, or methodology. This highlights that [[dysbiosis]] associated with endometriosis may require longer-term disease persistence and more advanced inflammatory processes to manifest.
