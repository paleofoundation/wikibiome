# Key Findings

- **Lead, cadmium, and alcohol** had the strongest impact on depressive symptoms in women, while total mercury had a negative association ([[ogundare-2024-metals-behavioral-factors-depression-women]], cross-sectional, NHANES 2017–2018).
- **Bayesian Kernel Machine Regression (BKMR)** posterior inclusion probabilities identified **cadmium (PIP=0.447)** and **alcohol (PIP=0.565)** as the most significant contributors to depressive symptoms.
- **Combined exposure analysis**: higher quantile levels of all metals and behavioral factors together were associated with increased depression risk — supporting a mixture-effects model.
- Women are disproportionately affected by metal exposure and mood disorders, likely due to physiological and hormonal differences.
- Mean blood levels: lead 1.21 µg/dL, cadmium 1.47 µg/L, total mercury 0.80 µg/L.

# Methods

- Cross-sectional analysis of NHANES 2017–2018 data (women only).
- Depression assessed via PHQ-9 (Patient Health Questionnaire-9).
- Blood levels of lead, cadmium, and mercury measured.
- Statistical methods: linear regression, BKMR, univariate and bivariate analyses.
- Behavioral factors: alcohol consumption, smoking.

- Mercury showed negative association with depression — counter to some neurotoxicity literature. May reflect confounding (fish consumption as mercury source but also source of omega-3s).
