## Summary

This paper outlines design principles for an independent staged-compliance certification framework for heavy metals in infant and child foods, arguing that current regulatory frameworks are insufficient. The framework aims to measurably reduce contamination while generating defensible evidence of due diligence for participating brands, with three objectives: limiting litigation exposure, ensuring governance rules are traceable to toxicological science, and structuring commercial incentives for ongoing reduction.

## Key Findings

- FDA action levels exist only for lead in baby foods and rice cereal, and arsenic in rice cereal and apple juice; levels for cadmium and mercury remain at various stages of development
- Baby Food Products Liability Litigation (MDL No. 3101) had ~345 pending claims as of late 2025
- Exposure mathematics for infants are particularly concerning: a day's consumption of 800 mL formula at 5 ppb lead, plus dry cereal at 20 ppb, one fruit puree at 10 ppb, and one vegetable puree at 20 ppb could yield ~3.8 micrograms of lead per day
- Risk-tiered metal classification: carcinogenic metals (lead, cadmium, inorganic arsenic, methylmercury) warrant zero tolerance for exceedance; metals with established tolerable daily intakes may allow transitional compliance
- Staged compliance architecture uses percent-of-limit scoring, with transitional, conditional, and full compliance tiers
- Exposure-based testing frequencies scale with risk: high-risk categories (baby cereals, dry goods) tested most frequently
- Measurement uncertainty decision rules incorporate conservative two-sided approaches to protect both brands and consumers
