# A Systematic Review and Meta-analysis of Optical Coherence Tomography in Multiple Sclerosis

## Summary

This systematic review and meta-analysis examines optical coherence tomography (OCT) as a tool for measuring neurodegeneration in [[multiple-sclerosis]] via retinal nerve fibre layer (RNFL) thinning. It includes 35 studies suitable for meta-analysis of RNFL thickness.

## Key Findings

- RNFL thinning of **20 micrometers** (95% CI 18-23, n=2063) following MS optic neuritis (MSON)
- RNFL thinning of **7 micrometers** (95% CI 6-9, n=3154) in MS without MSON
- Estimated RNFL thinning exceeds what is expected from normal aging
- Likely cause: retrograde trans-synaptic degeneration and progressive loss of retinal ganglion cells
- RNFL thickness correlated with visual and neurological functioning and paraclinical data

## Clinical Utility

- OCT provides a non-invasive biomarker of axonal loss in MS
- Can distinguish between optic neuritis-related damage and subclinical neurodegeneration
- Spectral/Fourier-domain OCT technologies offer improved resolution
- Potential for monitoring disease progression and treatment response

## Relevance to Gut-Metal-Microbiome Axis

While OCT is primarily a diagnostic imaging tool, RNFL thinning as a biomarker of neurodegeneration connects to the [[gut-brain-axis]] through the mechanisms by which [[gut-microbiome]] [[dysbiosis]] and [[short-chain-fatty-acids|microbial-metabolites]] influence neuronal health. Reduced [[short-chain-fatty-acids]] and altered [[tryptophan-metabolism]] metabolism (both consequences of [[dysbiosis]]) impair neuroprotective pathways. OCT could serve as an objective outcome measure in clinical trials of [[gut-microbiome]]-targeted interventions in MS, detecting whether microbiome modulation slows neurodegenerative processes.

## Limitations

- Only time-domain OCT data analyzed (spectral-domain OCT was emerging)
- Heterogeneity in inclusion criteria across studies
- Cannot differentiate between inflammatory and degenerative components of axonal loss
