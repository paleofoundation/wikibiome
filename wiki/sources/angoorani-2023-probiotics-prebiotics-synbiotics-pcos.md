# Key Claims

- An overview of 8 systematic reviews and meta-analyses (published 2018-2023) confirms that [[probiotics]], [[prebiotics]], and [[synbiotics]] have beneficial effects on some PCOS-related parameters including BMI, fasting plasma glucose (FPG), and lipid profiles
- [[probiotics]] were more effective than [[synbiotics]] on anthropometric indices; synbiotics showed less effect on these parameters
- Evidence for effects on hormonal outcomes (testosterone, SHBG, DHEAS) remains limited and inconsistent
- High heterogeneity across studies makes it difficult to identify optimal probiotic strains, types, duration, and doses

# Methods/Findings

- **Design**: Overview of systematic reviews; systematic search of PubMed, Web of Science, Scopus through September 2021
- **Included**: 8 SRs/meta-analyses with sample sizes ranging from 406 to 1,049 participants; 6-17 RCTs per review; intervention duration 8-12 weeks
- **Quality assessment**: AMSTAR-2 tool; 4 SRs rated high quality, 2 low, 1 critically low
- **Key findings by category**:
  - **Anthropometrics**: Probiotics significantly decreased BMI in 2 of 5 meta-analyses; prebiotics decreased BMI, WC, and HC
  - **Lipids**: Probiotics significantly decreased TG in all 4 evaluating MAs; VLDL-C decreased; no effect on HDL-C; synbiotics decreased TG and LDL-C in some
  - **Glucose homeostasis**: Probiotics significantly decreased FPG (range MD: -0.26 to -6.33 mg/dL) in 6 MAs; significant effects on FBI and QUICKI; synbiotics decreased FPG and HOMA-IR
  - **Inflammation/antioxidant**: Significant effects on GSH, MDA, NO, TAC, CRP
  - **Hormones**: SHBG increased; FAI decreased; total testosterone results mixed
  - **Prebiotics alone**: Decreased BMI, WC, HC, FPG more effectively than probiotics alone
