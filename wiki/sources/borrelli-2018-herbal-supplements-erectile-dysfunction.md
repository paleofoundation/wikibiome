## Key Findings

- **Ginseng (Panax ginseng) significantly improved erectile function** as measured by IIEF-5 score in a meta-analysis of 3 RCTs (n=140 ginseng, n=96 placebo; SMD 0.43; 95% CI 0.15–0.70; P < 0.01; I2 = 0), with positive effects across erectile function, sex desire, maintaining erection, and overall satisfaction domains. However, ginseng did not significantly alter serum testosterone levels (SMD 0.26; 95% CI -0.10–0.61; P = 0.15; I2 = 53%).
- **Saffron (Crocus sativus) produced mixed results** across 3 RCTs (n=80 total in the two placebo-controlled studies). One large crossover trial (n=317) found saffron ineffective compared to sildenafil, while two smaller parallel-group RCTs found improvements in IIEF-EF and IIEF-15 scores, including in antidepressant-induced and diabetic ED.
- **Pinus pinaster (Pycnogenol)** showed improvement in IIEF-5 in one small RCT (n=21), and the combination product Prelox (Pycnogenol + L-arginine + roburin + L-citrulline) showed positive results across multiple RCTs for mild-to-moderate ED.
- **Tribulus terrestris** showed inconsistent results: one larger RCT (n=172) found benefit for IIEF-15 and GEQ, while a smaller study (n=30) found no difference from placebo.
- **Lepidium meyenii (maca)** showed a small but significant effect on IIEF-5 in one RCT (n=50).
- **Median methodological quality was low** (Jadad score 3.5/5), with only 7 of 24 trials at predominantly low risk of bias. Adverse events were recorded in 19 of 24 trials with no significant differences between verum and placebo.

## Methods

- **Study design:** Systematic review and meta-analysis following PRISMA guidelines.
- **Databases searched:** PubMed/MEDLINE, Google Scholar, Scopus, Web of Science, Cochrane Library (inception to June 2017).
- **Inclusion criteria:** RCTs evaluating herbal dietary supplements (monopreparations or formulations) in patients with ED of any severity and etiology, with outcomes including IIEF-15, IIEF-5, IIEF-EF, or patient satisfaction.
- **Exclusion criteria:** Non-RCTs, plant-derived pure compounds (e.g., yohimbine), studies in healthy men or non-human subjects.
- **Quality assessment:** Jadad score (3-item scale for randomization, blinding, dropouts/withdrawals) and 5-point quality scale. Cochrane Collaboration's risk-of-bias tool across 7 domains.
- **Data synthesis:** Meta-analyses performed using RevMan 5.3. Summary effect calculated as standardized mean difference (SMD) with 95% CI. Fixed-effects model used when P > 0.1 and I2 < 50%; random-effects model otherwise.
- **Studies identified:** 2,805 records screened; 1,200 after duplicates removed; 205 abstracts screened; 41 full-text assessed; **24 RCTs included** (12 monopreparations, 7 herbal formulations, 5 monopreparations/formulations with pure compounds). Total participants: n=2,080.
- **Herbs evaluated:** 5 ginseng RCTs (n=399), 3 saffron RCTs (n=397), 2 Tribulus terrestris RCTs (n=202), 1 Pinus pinaster RCT (n=21), 1 Lepidium meyenii RCT (n=50), 7 herbal formulations (n=544), 5 combination products with pure compounds (n=410).

## Detailed Summary

### Ginseng (Panax ginseng)

Five ginseng RCTs from Korea and Brazil were included, covering 399 men (age range 42.8–60.2 years). All were placebo-controlled, with treatment durations of 2–3 months. The meta-analysis of IIEF-5 scores (3 RCTs, n=236) showed a significant positive effect (SMD 0.43; 95% CI 0.15–0.70; P < 0.01; I2 = 0). Subanalysis of the IIEF-15 domains revealed significant improvements in erectile function (SMD 0.34; P = 0.01), sex desire (SMD 0.36; P < 0.01), maintaining erection (SMD 0.37; P < 0.01), and overall satisfaction (SMD 0.35; P = 0.01), but not orgasmic function (SMD 0.26; P = 0.06). Four RCTs assessed testosterone: no significant change was found (SMD 0.26; 95% CI -0.10–0.61; P = 0.15; I2 = 53%).

### Saffron (Crocus sativus)

Three RCTs from Iran (n=80 in placebo-controlled trials, n=317 in the crossover sildenafil comparison) showed divergent findings. The largest trial (Safarinejad et al., crossover, n=317) found saffron ineffective compared to sildenafil — IIEF-EF improved only 1.4 points with saffron vs. 10.0 with sildenafil. Two smaller RCTs by Modabbernia et al. (n=30, antidepressant-induced ED) and Mohammadzadeh-Morghadam et al. (n=50, diabetic men) found significant improvements in IIEF scores at 4–4.5 weeks. Meta-analysis was not possible due to different administration routes (oral vs. topical).

### Tribulus terrestris

Two double-blind, placebo-controlled RCTs (n=202 combined). Kamenov et al. (n=172, 12 weeks) found T. terrestris superior to placebo on IIEF-15 (P < 0.0001) and GEQ. Santos et al. (n=30, 30 days) found no difference from placebo on IIEF-5 or testosterone levels. One trial lacked SDs, precluding meta-analysis.

### Pinus pinaster (Pycnogenol)

A single small double-blind RCT (n=21, 3 months) found Pycnogenol (standardized to contain ≥70% procyanidins, 40 mg tid) significantly improved IIEF-5 (16.8 ± 0.8 vs. 9.0 ± 1.2 for placebo at 3 months; P < 0.01).

### Lepidium meyenii (Maca)

One RCT (n=50, 12 weeks) found a small but statistically significant improvement in IIEF-5 with maca root extract vs. placebo, though testosterone levels were unaffected.

### Herbal Formulations

Seven RCTs evaluated multi-herb formulations including E-MA-H/E-MA-HP, VigRX Plus, Cappra, IDIProst Gold, Leopin Royal (LER), Eurycoma longifolia + Polygonum minus, and a seven-plant Taiwanese formulation. VigRX Plus (containing Panax ginseng root, Serenoa repens berry, Ginkgo biloba leaf, and others) showed significant improvements in IIEF-15, IIEF-EF, and EDITS in a 12-week parallel-group RCT (n=75). Cappra (Cervus Nippon Temminck, Epimedum brevicorium) showed effectiveness in a crossover RCT (n=61) in mild-to-moderate ED. Most formulations had Jadad scores of 1–3, indicating low methodological quality.

### Combination Products with Pure Compounds

Five RCTs evaluated herbal extracts combined with L-arginine, glucosamine oligosaccharide, roburin, citrulline, or vitamin E. The Prelox combination (Pycnogenol + L-arginine ± roburin ± citrulline) was evaluated in 3 RCTs and showed consistent improvements in IIEF-EF, with one 6-month study (n=124) finding continued improvement over time. Tradamix TX1000 (containing Alga Ecklonia bicyclis, Tribulus terrestris, and glucosamine oligosaccharide) improved IIEF-15, IIEF-EF, and testosterone levels vs. placebo (n=177, 3 months).

### Quality and Safety

The median Jadad score across all 24 RCTs was 3.5 out of 5. Only 7 trials had predominantly low risk of bias. Adverse events were generally mild and did not differ significantly between active treatment and placebo in 19 of 24 trials reporting this outcome.

## Relevance to WikiBiome

This systematic review provides a comprehensive evidence base for the role of plant-derived compounds in modulating erectile function. Key connections to WikiBiome's knowledge graph:

- **[[panax-ginseng]]** — The ginsenoside compounds in ginseng may interact with nitric oxide pathways and vascular endothelial function. The lack of testosterone effect suggests the mechanism is not hormonal but likely vascular or neurological, which connects to endothelial function and potentially to [[nitric-oxide]] signaling.
- **[[crocus-sativus]]** — Saffron's crocin and safranal compounds have known antioxidant properties. The positive results in antidepressant-induced ED specifically (SSRI-related) suggest a mechanism distinct from PDE5 inhibition.
- **[[pycnogenol]]** — Pycnogenol's procyanidins enhance endothelial nitric oxide synthase (eNOS) activity and may work synergistically with L-arginine (the substrate for NO synthesis). This connects to the broader concept of [[endothelial-dysfunction]] in ED pathophysiology.
- **[[erectile-dysfunction]]** — ED is increasingly recognized as a sentinel condition for cardiovascular disease, sharing vascular pathology. The herbal interventions reviewed here operate primarily through vascular and antioxidant mechanisms rather than hormonal pathways (no testosterone changes observed with ginseng).

## Contradictions / Tensions

- **Saffron efficacy is contradictory within this review.** The largest and highest-quality trial (Safarinejad et al., n=317, crossover) found saffron ineffective compared to sildenafil, while two smaller trials (n=30 and n=50) found positive effects. Per the Supersession Protocol, the larger trial's finding should take precedence at the population level, but the positive results in specific subpopulations (antidepressant-induced ED, diabetic men) may represent genuine differential efficacy by etiology.
- **Tribulus terrestris results conflict.** Kamenov et al. (n=172) found benefit while Santos et al. (n=30) did not. The larger sample and longer duration (12 weeks vs. 30 days) of the positive study lends it more weight, but the conflicting findings keep confidence at preliminary.
- **No microbiome connection.** This review evaluates herbal supplements purely through clinical outcome and hormonal endpoints. It does not address gut microbiota modulation, which is a gap relative to WikiBiome's core thesis. The absence of microbiome data does not contradict existing content but limits integration into the signature framework.

## Open Questions

1. **Do ginseng ginsenosides modulate the gut microbiome in ways that affect erectile function?** Ginsenosides undergo extensive metabolism by gut bacteria, and emerging evidence suggests gut microbiota composition affects ginsenoside bioavailability. This is a potential bridge to WikiBiome's metallomics framework.
2. **Does Pycnogenol's procyanidin content affect gut microbial communities?** Polyphenols are known to modulate gut microbiota — do procyanidins specifically shift the balance toward beneficial taxa?
3. **What is the role of endothelial microbiome in ED pathophysiology?** Oral and gut microbiota can produce trimethylamine N-oxide (TMAO) which impairs endothelial function — could herbal supplements work partly through TMAO reduction?
4. **Are there metal-dependent mechanisms underlying ED that herbal supplements address?** Zinc deficiency is associated with hypogonadism and ED; do any of these herbs affect zinc or other metal bioavailability?
5. **Why does ginseng improve erectile function without affecting testosterone?** The mechanism remains unclear — is it purely vascular (NO pathway), or does it involve autonomic nervous system modulation, or gut-derived metabolites?
