# Gut Microbiome Versus Thyroid Cancer: Association and Clinical Implications (Review)

## Key Findings

- **TC microbiota signature**: Notable changes in gut microbiota of TC patients include increased [[clostridium]], [[streptococcus-pneumoniae|Streptococcus]], Proteus, and [[lachnospiraceae]], and decreased [[lactobacillus]], [[prevotella]], and [[ruminococcaceae|Ruminococcaceae]] ([[wang-2025-gut-microbiome-thyroid-cancer-review]], expert-opinion).
- **SCFA anti-tumor mechanisms**: [[short-chain-fatty-acids]] derived from fermentation of [[dietary-fiber]] inhibit histone deacetylases (HDACs), promote TC cell cycle arrest at G1 and G2/M phases, and increase expression of p21, p27, and Bax genes. Butyrate decreases c-Myc expression and inhibits miR-92a transcription ([[wang-2025-gut-microbiome-thyroid-cancer-review]], expert-opinion).
- **Estrogen-microbiome-TC axis**: Estrogen metabolism via gut microbiota beta-glucuronidase increases circulating estrogen, increasing TC risk particularly when patient has family history. This estrobolome mechanism connects to the 3:1 female-to-male TC ratio ([[wang-2025-gut-microbiome-thyroid-cancer-review]], expert-opinion).
- **Bidirectional causality**: Multiple Mendelian randomization studies (n up to 6,699 TC vs. 1,620,354 controls) identify Ruminococcaceae UCG004, Christensenellaceae, Methanobrevibacter as risk factors; Betaproteobacteria, Sutterella, Odoribacter as protective ([[wang-2025-gut-microbiome-thyroid-cancer-review]], expert-opinion).
- **Micronutrient-microbiome interactions**: Lactobacillus depletion linked to reduced [[selenium]] bioavailability; iron-poor diet hinders bacterial growth while iron-rich diet reduces diversity; [[zinc]] supplements promote Lactobacillus and Bifidobacterium growth ([[wang-2025-gut-microbiome-thyroid-cancer-review]], expert-opinion).

## Methods

- **Study type**: Comprehensive narrative review (received January 26, 2025; accepted April 14, 2025; 12 pages, 142 references)
- **Data presentation**: Table I summarizes gut microbiota composition in TC across 11 studies (2019-2024) using 16S rRNA sequencing and Mendelian randomization approaches
- **Scope**: Association between GM and TC, intestinal bacteria's roles in TC through metabolism (micronutrients, SCFAs, bile acids, amino acids, TSH, estrogen, IGF-1), role of GM in TC treatment (surgery, probiotics, FMT, RAI therapy, immunotherapy)

## Detailed Summary

**Microbiota composition in TC** (Table I): 11 studies summarized. Cross-sectional and cohort studies show Escherichia-Shigella, Clostridium sensu stricto 1, Klebsiella increased in TC; Bacteroides, Prevotella_9, Roseburia, Megamonas decreased. MR studies (n=6,699 to 1,620,354) consistently identify Ruminococcaceae, Christensenellaceae, Methanobrevibacter as risk factors ([[wang-2025-gut-microbiome-thyroid-cancer-review]], expert-opinion).

**SCFA mechanisms**: Butyrate produced via Lactobacillus glycolysis pathway. Butyrate reduces IL-6, IL-8, modulates endothelial activation. SCFAs promote apoptosis of TC cells and inhibit HDACs. The anti-inflammatory effects of SCFAs are mediated through GPR41/GPR43 receptor signaling ([[wang-2025-gut-microbiome-thyroid-cancer-review]], expert-opinion).

**Bile acid metabolism**: Secondary bile acids like deoxycholic acid (DCA) promote tumorigenesis. Gut microbiota converts primary to secondary bile acids. Bile acid metabolism disruption linked to thyroid hormone homeostasis via enterohepatic recycling ([[wang-2025-gut-microbiome-thyroid-cancer-review]], expert-opinion).

**Estrobolome**: Beta-glucuronidase activity by gut bacteria increases circulating estrogen levels. This mechanism is particularly relevant given TC's 3:1 female predominance and the role of estrogen in TC progression. GM may serve as a biomarker for diagnosis, prognosis, and predicting metastasis ([[wang-2025-gut-microbiome-thyroid-cancer-review]], expert-opinion).

**Drug-microbiota interactions**: Levothyroxine (LT4) absorption lowered by Lactobacillus and Bifidobacterium. Antithyroid drug metabolism influenced by gut microbes. FMT has shown promise in GD mouse models. Controlling gut flora may improve RAI therapy and immunotherapy efficacy ([[wang-2025-gut-microbiome-thyroid-cancer-review]], expert-opinion).

**Obesity and insulin resistance**: Higher BMI associated with increased TC risk (HR=1.18, 95% CI 1.03-1.35 in one meta-analysis). Gut microbiota modulates insulin resistance via SCFA pathways. Lachnospiraceae abundance disturbs Treg/Th17 balance, facilitating immune escape ([[wang-2025-gut-microbiome-thyroid-cancer-review]], expert-opinion).

## Relevance to Cureva

- Beta-glucuronidase inhibition as a potential intervention target for estrogen-dependent TC — connects to existing estrobolome intervention framework
- Dietary fiber increasing SCFA production as a protective intervention for TC — triangle evidence: fiber → SCFA ↑ → TC cell cycle arrest
- FMT for reshaping gut microbiota in thyroid disease treatment — emerging but pre-clinical
- LT4 absorption impairment by probiotics is a potential STOP signal: probiotic supplementation may need timing separation from LT4 dosing

## Contradictions / Tensions

- LT4 absorption impaired by Lactobacillus/Bifidobacterium conflicts with probiotic supplementation recommendations — timing of administration may be critical
- Keto/low-carb diets may reduce protective Prevotellaceae and Ruminococcaceae while being promoted for other health benefits
- The MR studies use different statistical thresholds and populations, making cross-study comparison of risk/protective taxa difficult

## Open Questions

- Whether estrobolome-targeted interventions could reduce TC risk in high-estrogen populations
- Whether timing probiotic supplementation away from LT4 dosing resolves the absorption conflict
- Whether the gut microbiome can serve as a clinically useful biomarker for TC diagnosis or prognosis in practice
- The role of the intratumoral microbiome (distinct from gut) in TC progression
