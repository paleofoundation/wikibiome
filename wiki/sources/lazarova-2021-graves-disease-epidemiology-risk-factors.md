# Graves' disease: Epidemiology, genetic and environmental risk factors and viruses

## Key Findings

- **[[graves-disease]] is the most common cause of hyperthyroidism** in Western countries, with an annual incidence of 20 cases/100,000 persons, a lifetime risk of 3% for women and 0.5% for men, and peak age of onset between 30 and 60 years ([[lazarova-2021-graves-disease-epidemiology-risk-factors]], expert opinion)
- **Genetic predisposition accounts for approximately 79% of GD risk**, with environmental factors explaining ~21%; HLA-DR3 haplotype (DQB1*02, DQA1*0501, DRB1*03) is the strongest genetic association, alongside CTLA-4, PTPN22 R620W, CD40, TSH-R, and thyroglobulin gene polymorphisms ([[lazarova-2021-graves-disease-epidemiology-risk-factors]], expert opinion)
- **Chronic hepatitis C (CHC) is a significant independent risk factor** for autoimmune thyroid disease: a meta-analysis of 12 studies (1,735 HCV-infected vs. 1,868 controls) found pooled OR 2.40 (95% CI 1.85-3.13) for AbTg, OR 1.96 (95% CI 1.19-3.23) for AbTPO, and OR 3.10 (95% CI 2.19-4.40) for hypothyroidism ([[lazarova-2021-graves-disease-epidemiology-risk-factors]], expert opinion)
- **Selenium deficiency increases GD and AITD risk**; selenoproteins (glutathione peroxidase, thioredoxin reductase) protect thyroid from oxidative damage; meta-analyses of 10 RCTs suggest selenium supplementation can help restore biochemical euthyroidism, though evidence for durable clinical outcomes remains limited ([[lazarova-2021-graves-disease-epidemiology-risk-factors]], expert opinion)
- **Epstein-Barr virus (EBV) reactivation** may contribute to TRAb production: cultured PBMCs from 13 GD patients showed TRAb(+)EBV(+) double-positive cells significantly more frequent than in controls (P=0.021), and TRAb were significantly higher in supernatants after EBV reactivation ([[lazarova-2021-graves-disease-epidemiology-risk-factors]], expert opinion)

## Methods

- **Study type**: Comprehensive narrative review integrating epidemiology, genetics, environmental risk factors, and virology literature for Graves' disease
- **Scope**: Covers genetic susceptibility (HLA, CTLA-4, PTPN22, CD40, TSH-R, Tg), endogenous factors (estrogen, X-inactivation, microchimerism), environmental factors (iodine, selenium, vitamin D, smoking, stress, pesticides, Agent Orange), and viral triggers (foamy viruses, parvovirus B19, EBV, HCV, SARS-CoV-2)
- **Population focus**: Primarily Western European populations; notes GD is more frequent in Asian populations and less in Sub-Saharan African populations

## Detailed Summary

**Epidemiology.** GD prevalence is approximately 1-1.5% globally. A French study of 1,572 hyperthyroid patients found 73.3% had GD, of whom 85% were female, with mean age at first onset of 43 years. In Sweden, the risk of GD is 1.7%, about 5-6 times higher in females, with mean diagnosis age of approximately 48 years. GD is more frequent in the Asian population. Twin studies show concordance rates of 0.29-0.36 in monozygotic twins and 0.00-0.04 in dizygotic twins. An increased risk of thyroid cancer, particularly the Tall Cell Variant of papillary thyroid cancer, has been observed in GD patients with thyroid nodules.

**Genetic factors.** About 70% of AITD-associated genes with clear effects are implicated in T-cell function. The DR3 haplotype predisposes to GD, while the DR7 haplotype appears protective. Key susceptibility genes include CTLA-4 (cytotoxic T lymphocyte-associated antigen 4), TSH-R (mutations in the extracellular domain), CD40, PTPN22 (protein tyrosine phosphatase-22), and CD25. X-inactivation asymmetry (inactivation of the same X chromosome in 80% of cells) accounts for the female predominance, confirmed by meta-analysis (OR 2.54; 95% CI 1.58-4.10).

**Environmental factors.** Iodine: The minimum recommended daily dose for thyroid hormone synthesis is 150 micrograms; excess iodine inhibits organification (Wolff-Chaikoff effect) and can trigger iodine-induced hyperthyroidism (Jod-Basedow phenomenon) in previously iodine-deficient areas. Smoking: Increases risk of GD and especially GO through increased reactive oxygen species generation; GO fibroblasts show exaggerated response to cigarette smoke extract with increased oxidative stress and fibrosis-related gene expression. Selenium: The thyroid is one of the tissues with the highest selenoprotein content; selenium supplementation is recommended for mild and short-term orbitopathy. Agent Orange: Vietnam veterans exposed to 2,4-D/2,4,5-T contaminated with TCDD had approximately 3-fold higher prevalence of GD diagnosis vs. controls. Vitamin D: High-dose supplementation did not improve muscle performance or QoL in newly diagnosed GD. Intestinal dysbiosis is also associated with AITD.

**Viral triggers.** Foamy viruses: FV DNA detected by PCR in 19/29 GD patients in one French study, but most subsequent studies found no association. Parvovirus B19: EVB19 can infect and persist in thyrocytes with low-level expression but no direct demonstration of pathogenesis in GD. EBV: TRAb(+)EBV(+) cells in culture were significantly more frequent in GD patients (P=0.021); TRAb-IgM was significantly higher than TRAb-IgG, supporting EBV-related polyclonal B cell activation; estradiol at midluteal levels induced TRAb production while pregnancy levels inhibited it (P=0.028). HCV: Meta-analysis of 12 studies found CHC as independent risk factor for AITD and hypothyroidism but not for GD specifically (OR 1.10, 95% CI 0.71-1.57 for hyperthyroidism). IFN-alpha-treated CHC patients develop GD more frequently. HCV-related mixed cryoglobulinemia also increases GD risk.

- Selenium supplementation evidence (10 RCTs) supports intervention hypothesis for GD, though evidence for durable outcomes is limited; informs triangle validation for selenium interventions
- Smoking cessation as intervention for GO prevention; GO fibroblast sensitivity to smoke-induced oxidative stress is a mechanistic basis
- EBV reactivation as a trigger for TRAb production suggests monitoring EBV status in GD recurrence; potential STOP signal for immunosuppressive therapies that may reactivate EBV
- IFN-alpha therapy in HCV patients as iatrogenic GD trigger constitutes a potential STOP signal

## Contradictions / Tensions

- Selenium supplementation: Meta-analyses of 10 RCTs show restoration of biochemical euthyroidism, but no compelling evidence for routine use in GD hyperthyroidism; selenium is recommended only for mild/short-term orbitopathy — the impact on moderate-to-severe orbitopathy is unknown
- Vitamin D: High-dose supplementation did not improve muscle performance or QoL in newly diagnosed GD, contrasting with the clear epidemiological association between vitamin D deficiency and GD risk
- HCV as GD trigger: The meta-analysis found HCV as risk factor for AITD and hypothyroidism but NOT specifically for hyperthyroidism/GD (OR 1.10, 95% CI 0.71-1.57), creating tension with individual case reports suggesting HCV-GD association
- Foamy viruses: Most studies found no association with GD despite early positive findings

## Open Questions

- What is the precise mechanism by which iodine excess contributes to GD onset vs. other forms of thyrotoxicosis?
- Does EBV reactivation play a causal role in GD relapse, or is it an epiphenomenon of immune dysregulation?
- Can selenium supplementation prevent GD in selenium-deficient populations, or only modify disease course?
- What is the role of gut microbiota dysbiosis as an environmental trigger — is it upstream or downstream of the immune dysregulation in GD?
- Is the post-COVID new-onset GD a transient phenomenon or does SARS-CoV-2 infection create lasting thyroid autoimmunity through molecular mimicry?
