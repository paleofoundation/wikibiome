# Gut Microbiome Remodeling in CKD: Kidney Replacement Therapies and Interventions

## Key Findings

- **Hemodialysis** is associated with Firmicutes/Proteobacteria enrichment and reduced butyrate-producing taxa, reflecting the combined effects of intermittent blood filtration, dietary restrictions, and medication burden (Wang et al. 2025, expert opinion).
- **Peritoneal dialysis** creates a distinct microbiome perturbation driven by glucose absorption from dialysate and **[[iron]]** supplementation, which fosters pathogenic proliferation -- a critical finding linking iron therapy to iatrogenic dysbiosis (Primitive 8).
- **Plant-based diets (PBDs)** reduced inflammatory markers by approximately 24% in peritoneal dialysis patients, providing clinical evidence for dietary modulation in ESRD. <!-- NEEDS VERIFICATION: confirm 24% figure and specific inflammatory markers -->
- **FMT increases SCFA levels and lowers TMAO** in diabetic nephropathy animal models, supporting the therapeutic potential of ecosystem transplantation in CKD.
- The review distinguishes between the microbiome effects of different dialysis modalities, medication classes, and therapeutic interventions.

## Methods

- **Study design**: Narrative review synthesizing evidence on kidney replacement therapy impacts on gut microbiota
- **Scope**: Covers hemodialysis, peritoneal dialysis, renal transplantation, probiotics, FMT, and dietary interventions in CKD
- **Year**: 2025 -- the most recent review in the CKD microbiome collection
- **Limitations**: Narrative review; some findings are from animal models (FMT for TMAO reduction)

## Detailed Summary

Wang et al. provide a comprehensive and current review of how kidney replacement therapies reshape the gut microbiome, and how therapeutic interventions can counteract these effects. The key contribution is the modality-specific analysis: hemodialysis and peritoneal dialysis create different ecological pressures on the gut microbiome, and therefore require different intervention strategies. The iron supplementation finding is particularly relevant to WikiBiome's metallomic framework: routine **[[iron]]** supplementation in CKD patients -- intended to treat renal anemia -- may inadvertently fuel iron-dependent pathogenic bacteria, worsening dysbiosis and potentially accelerating CKD progression through increased uremic toxin production. This is a potential STOP signal for the CKD signature. The FMT evidence (from animal models) suggests that complete ecosystem replacement can restore SCFA production and reduce TMAO more effectively than single-strain probiotics, consistent with the probiotic meta-analysis findings (Liu 2024) showing limited probiotic efficacy for uremic toxin reduction.
