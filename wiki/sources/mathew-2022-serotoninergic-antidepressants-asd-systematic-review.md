# Overview

Systematic review of **18 studies** (from 28,141 screened articles) examining the relationship between maternal antidepressant (AD) use during pregnancy and ASD risk in offspring. Results are **conflicting**: 8 studies found positive association, 2 found no link, and 6 showed reduced association after adjusting for confounders.

# Key Findings

## Serotonin Pathway in ASD

- SSRIs cross the placenta and blood-brain barrier, influencing fetal neurogenesis, migration, and differentiation
- Serotonin levels in blood are **significantly higher** in ASD patients
- Perinatal SSRI exposure reduces plasticity of developing hippocampus, particularly in **male fetuses**
- AD medication during pregnancy has been related to ASD in children via the **developmental hyperserotonemia (DHS) model**

## Conflicting Evidence on AD-ASD Link

- Zhou et al. meta-analysis of 14 studies: pooled adjusted RR for cohort studies was **1.13** (95% CI 0.93-1.39) -- not significant; case-control OR was **1.51** (95% CI 1.15-1.99)
- After adjusting to avoid publication bias, estimates were **non-significant** (RR 0.97, CI 0.79-1.19)
- Rai et al. Swedish cohort (254,610 individuals): adjusted OR **1.45** (CI 1.13-1.85) -- but only for autism without intellectual disability
- Hagberg et al. UK CPRD data (194,494 mother-baby pairs): risk of ASD was **1.72** (CI 1.54-1.93) for treated depression

## Confounding Factors

- Maternal depression itself is a **major confounder** -- untreated depression associated with similar risks
- Paternal AD use, maternal pre-conceptional AD drug use, and maternal depression itself are additional factors
- A "two-hit" model proposed where AD exposure + LGD genetic mutation jointly influence ASD severity
