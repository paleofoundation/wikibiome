# Why This Paper Matters

This is the most direct articulation of how [[mis-metallation]] can be **adaptive** rather than purely damaging. McEwan introduces the concept of "metalloproteome plasticity" -- the idea that bacteria deliberately switch the metal cofactors in their enzymes as an adaptive response to host-imposed metal limitation during infection. This reframes [[mis-metallation]] from a purely pathological event to a survival strategy: under [[oxidative-stress]] or [[nutritional-immunity]], bacteria replace Fe2+ with Mn2+ in mononuclear metalloenzymes, gaining oxidative protection while maintaining catalytic function. This is arguably the most important conceptual paper for the wiki's framework because it shows that the same thermodynamic principles that make mis-metallation toxic in human cells are exploited by pathogens to survive immune attack.

# Key Claims

- **Metalloproteome plasticity** -- under different conditions, the same enzyme can contain different metals at its active site, contributing to dynamic pathogen adaptation during infection
- **Irving-Williams series governs intracellular metal availability** -- cells maintain free Cu+ at ~10-15 M and Mg2+ at ~10-3 M, with other metals between; this inverse relationship prevents mis-metallation under normal conditions
- **Mn2+ and Fe2+ are functionally interchangeable** in many mononuclear metalloenzymes, and this interchangeability is a key adaptive response to [[oxidative-stress]]
- **"Cambialistic" enzymes** (e.g., SodM superoxide dismutases) can function with either Fe or Mn as cofactor -- these are distinct from metal-specific enzymes (SodA for Mn, SodB for Fe)
- **Mn2+ replaces Fe2+ under oxidative stress** because Mn2+ is resistant to Fenton chemistry damage, providing oxidative protection
- **Salmonella Typhimurium** in macrophages increases Mn2+ uptake via MntH and SitABC transporters, simultaneously reducing Fe2+ use -- a deliberate metalloproteome remodeling
- **Mn and Mg are interchangeable** in intermediary carbon metabolism enzymes at the PEP-pyruvate-oxaloacetate (PPO) node -- potentially important for pathogen growth on host-derived substrates like lactate
- **The ribosome** may serve as an intracellular Mn sink, accumulating Mn in a slowly exchangeable form

# Methods & Findings

## Quantitative Metal Physiology

- [[manganese]] is found in ~8% of metalloproteins (MACiE database); [[iron]] in ~25% of metalloenzymes (mostly mononuclear)
- [[magnesium]] in ~40% of metalloenzymes
- Intracellular available Mn2+ and Fe2+ both held at ~10-6 M, though total Mn2+ concentration is higher than Fe2+
- Fe is in high demand for Fe-S clusters and heme cofactors that Mn2+ **cannot** substitute for

## Mn-Fe Interchangeability in Oxidative Stress

- In [[escherichia-coli]], Mn2+ import via MntH is controlled by OxyR -- significant Mn2+ import only occurs under peroxide stress
- In Salmonella, nitrosative stress drives Mn2+ uptake via MntR de-repression and Fur de-repression of sitABC and mntH
- This elevates Mn2+ to exploit its antioxidant properties while reducing the pro-oxidant Fe2+
- Specific enzymes proposed to undergo Fe-to-Mn switching: ribulose-5-phosphate epimerase (Rpe), peptide deformylase, cytosine deaminase, threonine dehydrogenase

## Mn-Mg Interchangeability in Carbon Metabolism

- Mn and Mg are interchangeable in PPO node enzymes (glycolysis/gluconeogenesis and TCA cycle link)
- Mn-containing enzymes may have higher catalytic efficiency than Mg-containing versions
- L-lactate production in M2 macrophages promotes Salmonella intracellular replication -- may relate to Mg/Mn ratio changes
- Slc11a1 (NRAMP1) transporter in macrophages exports Mg from phagosome, limiting this ion for intracellular pathogens

## Ribosome as Manganese Reservoir

- Bacteria with high Mn dependence would need an intracellular Mn sink
- The ribosome is proposed as a site where Mn accumulates in a slowly exchangeable form
- Given the ribosome's central role in growth, Mn sequestration there may link metal status to broader physiological responses
