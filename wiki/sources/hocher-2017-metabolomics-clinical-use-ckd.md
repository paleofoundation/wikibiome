# Metabolomics for Clinical Use and Research in CKD

## Key Findings

- **Metabolomics** is instrumental in identifying new [[chronic-kidney-disease]] biomarkers that outperform traditional markers (creatinine, cystatin C) for disease staging and prognostication.
- **C-mannosyl tryptophan** and **pseudouridine** have been identified as biomarkers that perform better than serum creatinine for CKD stratification and eGFR estimation, particularly in populations where creatinine is unreliable (e.g., patients with low muscle mass, elderly).
- Two key technology platforms — **mass spectrometry** (MS) and **proton nuclear magnetic resonance** (NMR) spectroscopy — enable comprehensive metabolomic profiling, each with distinct strengths (MS for sensitivity and coverage, NMR for reproducibility and quantification).
- Metabolomics can distinguish CKD patients at high risk of progression from those with stable disease, enabling risk-stratified clinical management.
- Many of the identified biomarkers are products of gut microbial metabolism or host-microbial co-metabolism, positioning the gut microbiome as a central contributor to the CKD metabolomic signature.

## Methods

**Narrative review** published in Nature Reviews Nephrology, covering the state of the art in metabolomics applications to CKD. The authors reviewed studies using both targeted and untargeted metabolomic platforms, discussed biomarker discovery pipelines, and assessed the clinical readiness of novel metabolomic biomarkers for CKD. No systematic search protocol was described.

## Detailed Summary

Hocher and Adamski reviewed how metabolomics — the comprehensive study of small molecules in biological samples — has transformed biomarker discovery in CKD. The traditional reliance on serum creatinine for kidney function assessment has significant limitations: creatinine levels are influenced by muscle mass, diet, age, and sex, leading to imprecise eGFR estimates in many clinical populations.

The review highlighted several metabolites identified through discovery metabolomics that provide superior performance. C-mannosyl tryptophan, a modified amino acid, showed strong correlation with measured GFR and was less influenced by non-renal factors than creatinine. Pseudouridine, a modified nucleoside from RNA turnover, similarly outperformed creatinine in validation cohorts.

The authors discussed the broader metabolomic signature of CKD, which includes alterations in amino acid metabolism (particularly tryptophan via the kynurenine and indole pathways), lipid metabolism (acylcarnitines, phospholipids), and nucleotide metabolism. Many of these metabolic alterations have gut microbial origins — for example, indoxyl sulfate and p-cresyl sulfate are derived exclusively from gut bacterial metabolism of tryptophan and tyrosine, respectively.

The review also addressed practical challenges of translating metabolomics into clinical practice, including assay standardization, batch effects, cost, and the need for validation in diverse populations.

## Relevance to WikiBiome

This review underscores that many CKD biomarkers are **microbially derived or microbially modulated**, reinforcing the centrality of the gut-kidney axis in CKD pathobiology. The metabolomic signature of CKD is, in substantial part, a reflection of gut microbial metabolic activity under dysbiotic conditions. This positions microbiome characterization not merely as a research tool but as a potential diagnostic and prognostic approach.

The identification of tryptophan-derived biomarkers connects to WikiBiome's interest in how metal availability (particularly iron, which is required by many indole-producing enzymes) shapes the metabolic output of the gut microbiome.
