# Impact of heavy metals on breast cancer (Review)

## Key Claims

- Environmental heavy metals including [[copper]], [[cadmium]], [[zinc]], [[manganese]], [[nickel]], and [[lead]] are associated with [[breast-cancer]] development and progression
- [[copper]] levels are significantly increased in plasma and serum of breast cancer patients, particularly in metastatic stages, across Africa and Europe
- [[cadmium]] concentrations are considerably elevated in urine and tissue of breast cancer patients; its interaction with [[estrogen receptor alpha]] is a key mechanism
- [[zinc]] and [[manganese]] levels are generally decreased in breast cancer patients compared to controls
- [[nickel]] shows inconsistent results - serum levels higher in some breast cancer populations (China) but no significant differences in plasma/serum or hair across Asian populations overall
- [[lead]] findings are inconsistent across different sample types and regions; hair samples show significantly lower lead in breast cancer patients
- Heavy metals exert carcinogenic effects through [[oxidative-stress|oxidative stress]], [[DNA-damage|DNA damage]], estrogen receptor signaling, and disruption of cellular processes

## Methods/Findings

- **Study type**: Comprehensive narrative review covering Cu, Cd, Zn, Mn, Ni, and Pb
- **Copper**: Higher in plasma/serum of breast cancer patients in Iraq, Taiwan, India, Africa/Europe; associated with lysyl oxidase-like proteins and G protein-coupled estrogen receptor 1 signaling
- **Cadmium**: Elevated in plasma, urine, hair, and tissue of metastatic breast cancer patients; promotes proliferation through ERa interaction, protein kinase activation, and ROS production; half-life of 12-30 years in the body
- **Zinc**: Significantly decreased in plasma (Taiwan, Iraq) and urine (Iraq) of breast cancer patients; essential for immune function and wound healing; deficiency may increase vulnerability to carcinogens
- **Manganese**: Decreased in plasma (Iraq, China, Asia) and hair of breast cancer patients; cofactor for MnSOD antioxidant enzyme; no direct empirical evidence linking Mn directly to cancer
- **Nickel**: Serum significantly increased in China; binds to ERa mimicking estradiol effects; promotes cell proliferation through cyclin D1 overexpression; 4.2 ug Ni/kg/day safe dietary intake (WHO)
- **Lead**: Tissue levels significantly increased in Serbia, Iraq; classified as carcinogen; activates ERa and influences estrogen target gene expression
- **Detection methods**: Atomic absorption spectroscopy, flame atomic absorption, inductively coupled plasma, UV-visible spectrophotometry
