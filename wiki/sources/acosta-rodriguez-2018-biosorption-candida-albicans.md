## Summary

This book chapter studied the resistance and biosorption capacity of a Candida albicans strain isolated from the Bancote River in Mexico. The study characterized the yeast's tolerance to multiple heavy metals and its ability to remove them from solution, with a focus on chromium(VI) biosorption.

## Key Findings

- C. albicans can grow in concentrations of: 2000 ppm zinc, lead, and copper; 2000 ppm Cr(VI); 1500 ppm As(III); 500 ppm silver; 300 ppm cobalt; 200 ppm mercury and cadmium
- Biosorption removal efficiency (modified biomass): Cr(VI) 76%, lead 57%, silver 51%, cadmium 46%, As(III) 40%, cobalt 37%, mercury 36%, copper 31%, zinc 22%, fluoride 10%
- Optimal Cr(VI) removal conditions for living cells: 28 degrees C, pH 7.0, 50 mg/L Cr(VI), 10x10^6 yeast/mL
- At 60 degrees C, 100% Cr(VI) removal was achieved in 20 hours; 76% at 28 degrees C in 24 hours
- Lower initial metal concentrations favor faster removal; at 200 ppm and room temperature, 100% removal in 10 days
- pH 1.0 was optimal for Cr(VI) biosorption by dead biomass; acidic pH favors protonation and anion attraction

## Relevance to Wiki

Demonstrates C. albicans' remarkable tolerance and biosorption capacity for multiple heavy metals, relevant to understanding how this opportunistic pathogen thrives in metal-contaminated environments and how metal-microbe interactions may influence pathogenesis in the human host.
