# Key Claims

- Excessive delivery of FODMAPs (Fermentable Oligo-, Di-, Mono-saccharides And Polyols) to the distal small intestine and colon is proposed as a dietary factor underlying susceptibility to [[crohns-disease]]
- Rapid fermentation of FODMAPs induces bacterial overgrowth, increased intestinal permeability, and conditions predisposing to CD development
- FODMAP intake in Western societies has increased substantially due to rising consumption of fructose, fructans, and sugar alcohols
- Increased intestinal permeability (the only recognized biomarker of CD susceptibility) is found in 10-20% of first-degree relatives
- The association between sugar intake and CD is one of the most consistently reproduced dietary associations

# Methods/Findings

- **Study type**: Hypothesis paper reviewing dietary epidemiology and pathophysiology
- **FODMAP components**: Fructose, lactose, fructans (inulin), galactooligosaccharides, polyols (sorbitol, mannitol), polydextrose
- **Epidemiological evidence**: 11/15 studies showed increased sugar intake in CD; 12/13 showed higher current sugar intake
- **Permeability mechanism**: FODMAPs increase osmotic load, accelerate fermentation, increase luminal distension and gas, leading to epithelial irritation
- **Bacterial overgrowth**: Excessive FODMAP fermentation in distal small intestine promotes bacterial overgrowth, a known CD-associated finding
- **Western lifestyle link**: FODMAP consumption has risen with processed food availability, paralleling CD incidence increases

# Connections

- FODMAP fermentation disrupts the microbiome composition relevant to [[dysbiosis]] in [[crohns-disease]]
- Increased intestinal permeability enables translocation of bacteria including [[escherichia-coli]] across the barrier
- Bacterial overgrowth from excess FODMAPs may alter metal availability in the [[gut-metal-microbiome]]
- [[inflammation]] from barrier disruption connects to [[calprotectin]] elevation as a clinical biomarker
- Fiber type matters: prebiotic fibers (FOS/inulin) at moderate doses may be beneficial, while excessive amounts may be harmful
