# Key Claims

- NSAID-induced small intestinal injury affects 50-70% of chronic NSAID users
- Gram-negative bacterial LPS binding to TLR4 on injured epithelial cells activates NF-kB and NLRP3 inflammasome pathways, driving NSAID enteropathy
- [[probiotics]] may help preserve enteral mucosal functionality in NSAID-treated patients
- Rifaximin prevents diclofenac-induced enteropathy through antibacterial and anti-inflammatory activities in animal models
- Germ-free rats are resistant to NSAID enteropathy but become sensitized when exposed to [[escherichia-coli]]

# Methods/Findings

- **Study type**: Systematic review; PubMed, Scopus, Cochrane databases searched through December 2019
- **Included**: 45 articles/book chapters in qualitative synthesis from 135 screened
- **Pathophysiology**: NSAIDs inhibit COX, impair prostaglandin-dependent mucosal defense; bacterial LPS triggers TLR4-NF-kB-NLRP3 cascade
- **Probiotic evidence**: Rifaximin increases Lactobacilli, reduces IL-1beta, inhibits caspase-1; Lactobacillus and Bifidobacterium strains show protective effects
- **Key finding**: E. coli mono-associated gnotobiotic rats develop ulcers; Bifidobacterium/Lactobacillus-associated rats do not

# Connections

- NSAID enteropathy mechanisms overlap with [[crohns-disease]] pathogenesis through shared TLR4-NF-kB [[inflammation]] pathways
- [[escherichia-coli]] is central to both NSAID enteropathy and CD mucosal damage
- [[probiotics]] targeting Bifidobacterium and Lactobacillus may protect against both conditions
- NLRP3 inflammasome activation connects to [[ferroptosis]] and oxidative stress pathways
- [[hepcidin]] regulation through IL-6/NF-kB connects NSAID-microbiota interactions to [[iron]] homeostasis
