# Key Claims

- **Trace elements in maternal hair affect diversity and composition** of infant gut microbiome at 3, 6, and 12 months
- **Shannon diversity in 3-month infants correlated positively with [[selenium]]** and negatively with copper
- **Bifidobacterium relative abundance increases** under high aluminum and manganese exposure
- **High levels of copper and arsenic** induce enrichment of antibiotic resistance genes (ARGs) in infant gut
- **56 differential metabolites** between first and second visit, 515 between second and third

# Methods & Findings

Longitudinal study of 146 mother-infant pairs:

- Amplicon sequencing (N=353), metagenomics (N=65), metabolomics (N=198)
- Trace elements measured in maternal hair samples (N=119)
- ARG profiles significantly differ between infants and paired mothers

# Relevance to Metallomic Framework

- **[[zinc]]**: Zinc was not specifically highlighted but co-occurs with measured trace elements -- zinc-selenium ratio may be critical for infant microbiome establishment
- **[[iron]]**: Iron is a key trace element shaping infant gut colonization -- not directly measured but implied in metabolomic shifts
- **[[selenium]]**: Direct finding: selenium positively correlated with infant microbial diversity -- selenium-optimized maternal diet could protect infant microbiome
- **[[gut-metal-microbiome]]**: This study provides direct evidence that maternal metal exposure shapes infant [[gut-metal-microbiome]] from birth
- **[[dysbiosis]]**: Toxic metal exposure (copper, arsenic) drives [[dysbiosis]] and antibiotic resistance in infants, potentially increasing T1D risk
- **[[immune-balance]]**: Early-life metal-microbiome interactions establish the immune programming relevant to autoimmune disease risk
