## Key Findings

- **Lower melatonin amplitude and AUC** in PMS/PMDD during the luteal phase, supported by 4 of 8 studies; earlier offset time of melatonin secretion also observed ([[nexha-2024-biological-rhythms-pmdd-systematic-review]], systematic review, 25 studies)
- **Higher nocturnal core body temperature** in PMS/PMDD throughout the menstrual cycle, not limited to the luteal phase — suggests a persistent circadian state alteration rather than a purely luteal-phase phenomenon ([[nexha-2024-biological-rhythms-pmdd-systematic-review]])
- **Sleep architecture disruption**: increased slow-wave sleep (SWS), reduced Stage 1 sleep in the late luteal phase (LLP); higher NREM delta activity; women with severe PMS had significantly poorer subjective sleep quality during LLP ([[nexha-2024-biological-rhythms-pmdd-systematic-review]])
- **Cortisol rhythm**: conflicting results — some studies show delayed acrophase and flattened diurnal slope in PMDD, others show no differences; no consensus ([[nexha-2024-biological-rhythms-pmdd-systematic-review]])
- **Prolactin peak** tends to be higher in PMDD across the menstrual cycle; TSH acrophase and peak time earlier in PMDD ([[nexha-2024-biological-rhythms-pmdd-systematic-review]])
- **Overall biological rhythm disruption** (BRIAN scale, El Dahr 2022, n=104/556): PMDD had greater disruption across all 4 domains — sleep, social rhythms, activity, and eating — than healthy controls ([[nexha-2024-biological-rhythms-pmdd-systematic-review]])
- **Wrist actimetry and objective sleep parameters**: no consistent differences between PMS/PMDD and controls in wrist actimetry measures ([[nexha-2024-biological-rhythms-pmdd-systematic-review]])
- 3 studies found **no differences** in melatonin levels between groups, indicating heterogeneity in the literature ([[nexha-2024-biological-rhythms-pmdd-systematic-review]])

## Methods

PROSPERO-registered systematic review (CRD42020149921). Literature search across PubMed, Embase, Medline, and Web of Science on December 3rd, 2021. 575 articles screened; 25 studies included after exclusion (PRISMA flow: 181 duplicates removed, 341 excluded title/abstract, 32 excluded full-text). Included studies assessed biological rhythm parameters (sleep, melatonin, cortisol, prolactin, TSH, wrist actimetry, core body temperature) in women with PMS/PMDD vs. healthy controls. Studies published 1989–2022; most from USA (n=16) and Canada (n=3). Sample sizes ranged from 12 to 660 (median small). Most studies used DSM-III-R criteria (n=9) or DSM-IV (n=9) for PMDD diagnosis. Quality assessed via Newcastle–Ottawa Quality Assessment Scale (scores 3–7/10, mean 5.84); main limitations were sample representativeness and sample size selection bias.

## Detailed Summary

The review systematically synthesized evidence on circadian and biological rhythm disruptions in PMS/PMDD. Key domains:

**Melatonin (8 studies):** The most consistent finding was reduced melatonin AUC specifically in the late luteal phase (LLP) relative to healthy controls. Parry et al. (1990, 1997a, 2011) documented shorter melatonin secretion duration, earlier offset time, and significantly lower melatonin levels in LLP in PMS/PMDD. Shechter et al. (2012) found lower 6-sulphatoxy-melatonin (main urinary metabolite) throughout the menstrual cycle. Three studies (Parry et al. 2010, Parry et al. 1997c, McIntyre et al. 1990) found no significant differences in plasma melatonin between PMDD and controls.

**Body temperature (4 studies):** Lamarche et al. (2007) and Severino et al. (1991) found consistently higher nocturnal core body temperature in women with PMS/PMDD across the entire menstrual cycle. Parry et al. (1997b) found no differences in core body temperature. Temperature elevation is not confined to the luteal phase, distinguishing it from simple progesterone-mediated effects.

**Sleep — PSG (6 studies):** Five of six PSG studies found at least one sleep alteration. Baker et al. (2007, 2012) documented increased slow-wave sleep (SWS) at both menstrual phases; Parry et al. (1989) found more Stage 2 sleep; women with PMS had longer REM sleep latency; Shechter et al. (2012) found lower Stage 1 sleep and increased slow-wave activity during LLP, and significantly poorer subjective sleep quality in LLP.

**Cortisol (5 studies):** Parry et al. (1994) found cortisol peak significantly delayed in PMDD LLP. Bedding et al. (2019) found a delayed awakening response peak and flattened cortisol slope in PMDD regardless of menstrual cycle phase. Segeblad et al. (2013) and Izakova et al. (2021) found no differences in diurnal cortisol between PMS and controls. Miura and Honma (2019) found severity of daytime sleepiness inversely correlated with premenstrual symptom severity.

**Overall rhythm disruption (El Dahr et al. 2022, two-study cross-sectional, n=104+PMDD/556+HC):** Women with PMDD had greater overall biological rhythm disruption across all four BRIAN domains (sleep, social, activity, eating) compared to healthy controls. This is the only study using a multidomain biological rhythm assessment tool.

## Relevance to WikiBiome

This systematic review establishes that PMS/PMDD involves widespread biological rhythm disruption — not confined to the luteal phase — with melatonin reduction and elevated core body temperature as the most replicated findings. This informs the PMDD entity and signature pages. The gut microbiome is subject to circadian entrainment; disrupted melatonin signaling may alter gut epithelial permeability and microbiota composition, providing a mechanistic bridge from circadian disruption to dysbiosis. Relevant to concept pages on [[circadian-rhythms-gut-microbiome]] and [[pmdd]].

## Relevance to Cureva

For Cureva practitioners: melatonin supplementation and sleep hygiene interventions targeting the LLP are supported by this SR as potential modulators of the PMDD biological rhythm pattern. The body temperature elevation throughout the cycle (not just LLP) suggests a systemic circadian reset may be needed, not a phase-specific intervention. Cortisol rhythm normalization is a candidate target but evidence is weaker and conflicting.

## Contradictions / Tensions

- 3 of 8 melatonin studies found no group differences — heterogeneity may relate to diagnostic criteria (DSM-III-R vs. DSM-IV vs. DSM-5) and menstrual phase timing precision
- Most studies used small samples (n=5–23 PMDD), limiting generalizability
- Wrist actimetry consistently shows no differences despite subjective sleep complaints — possible poor sensitivity of actimetry in this population

## Open Questions

- Does melatonin reduction in PMDD drive dysbiosis, or are both downstream of a shared circadian clock gene disruption?
- Is elevated body temperature in PMDD related to alterations in heat-shock proteins or inflammatory cytokines?
- No study measured gut microbiome composition alongside melatonin levels — the gut-circadian bridge is inferred, not demonstrated
- Most studies are US/Canadian; circadian rhythm disruption in PMDD may interact with light exposure latitude differences
