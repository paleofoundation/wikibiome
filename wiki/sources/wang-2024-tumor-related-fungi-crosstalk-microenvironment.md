# Tumor-Related Fungi and Crosstalk with Gut Fungi in the Tumor Microenvironment

## Key Findings

- **Cancer type-specific intratumoral fungal profiles** have been identified across multiple tumor types, suggesting that the mycobiome is a consistent component of the tumor microenvironment rather than a contaminant ([[wang-2024-tumor-related-fungi-crosstalk-microenvironment]], expert-opinion).
- **Fungi influence tumorigenesis through four main mechanisms**: host immune modulation (suppressing antitumor immunity), bioactive metabolite production, cross-kingdom microbiome interactions, and epigenetic modification of host cells.
- **Gut-tumor mycobiome crosstalk** enables fungal translocation from the gut to distant tumor sites, establishing that the gut mycobiome can seed intratumoral fungal communities.
- **[[candida-albicans]]** is the most frequently identified intratumoral fungus, with its morphogenetic switching (yeast-to-hyphal transition) and biofilm formation facilitating tumor colonization and immune evasion.
- **Fungal biofilm formation** within tumors creates microniches that protect both fungi and co-resident bacteria from immune clearance, representing a form of interkingdom functional shielding.

## Methods

- **Study type**: Comprehensive narrative review
- **Scope**: Synthesizes findings from pan-cancer mycobiome studies, intratumoral fungal profiling, mechanistic investigations, and gut-tumor translocation research
- **Coverage**: Includes evidence from colorectal, pancreatic, breast, ovarian, and other cancer types
- **Limitations**: As a review, evidence quality varies across the synthesized studies; intratumoral mycobiome research is still an emerging field with methodological challenges (contamination control, low biomass sequencing)

## Detailed Summary

Wang et al. provide a comprehensive overview of the emerging field of intratumoral mycology. The review synthesizes evidence that fungi are a consistent and functionally important component of the tumor microenvironment across cancer types. The pan-cancer mycobiome studies (particularly the landmark Narunsky-Haziza 2022 analysis) demonstrated cancer type-specific fungal signatures, moving the field beyond contamination concerns.

The mechanistic framework presented covers four pathways by which fungi promote tumorigenesis. First, fungal cell wall components (beta-glucans, mannans) interact with pattern recognition receptors (Dectin-1, TLR2/4) on immune cells, often promoting immunosuppressive phenotypes including M2 macrophage polarization and regulatory T cell expansion. Second, fungal metabolites including candidalysin (from C. albicans) and aflatoxins (from Aspergillus) directly damage host cells or promote proliferation. Third, fungi engage in cross-kingdom interactions with bacteria — forming mixed-species biofilms that create immune-privileged niches within tumors. Fourth, fungal products can induce epigenetic modifications (DNA methylation, histone modification) in host cells.

The gut-tumor translocation pathway is particularly relevant: fungi from the gut can migrate to tumor sites through disrupted intestinal barriers, establishing intratumoral communities. This pathway connects gut [[dysbiosis]] to distant tumor biology and suggests that gut mycobiome modulation could influence intratumoral fungal colonization.
