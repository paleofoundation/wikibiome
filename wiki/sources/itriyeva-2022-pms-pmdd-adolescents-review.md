## Key Findings

- **Pathophysiology: hormonal sensitivity, not excess**: Women with PMS/PMDD show increased sensitivity to the normal cycling of estrogen and progesterone; GABA-A receptor subunit changes reduce allopregnanolone (ALLO) sensitivity → paradoxical anxiogenic response to progesterone metabolite ([[itriyeva-2022-pms-pmdd-adolescents-review]], review)
- **Serotonin–estrogen link**: decreased estrogen in late luteal phase → reduced tryptophan hydroxylase activity → reduced serotonin synthesis; explains why SSRIs are effective and why symptom timing mirrors estrogen withdrawal ([[itriyeva-2022-pms-pmdd-adolescents-review]])
- **GABA disruption**: GABA levels lower in PMDD compared to healthy controls who show GABA increase in luteal phase; cytokine-mediated GABA-A receptor subunit changes reduce inhibitory neurotransmitter availability ([[itriyeva-2022-pms-pmdd-adolescents-review]])
- **SSRIs: intermittent or continuous**: fluoxetine, escitalopram, citalopram, sertraline, clomipramine — all effective; intermittent (luteal phase only) reduces cost and side effects; serotonin mechanism distinct from antidepressant effect (faster onset in PMDD) ([[itriyeva-2022-pms-pmdd-adolescents-review]])
- **Combined OCP (drospirenone)**: FDA-approved OCP containing drospirenone 3mg + EE 20mcg for PMDD; shorter hormone-free interval (4 days) more effective than standard 7-day interval; mechanism via suppression of ovulation → elimination of hormonal fluctuation ([[itriyeva-2022-pms-pmdd-adolescents-review]])
- **Calcium 1200 mg/day**: strongest evidence of non-pharmacological supplement for PMS; prospective study demonstrating reduced symptom severity; mechanism linked to NMDA modulation and muscle function ([[itriyeva-2022-pms-pmdd-adolescents-review]])
- **Spirinolactone**: effective for physical/fluid symptoms of PMS; aldosterone antagonist with anti-androgenic properties ([[itriyeva-2022-pms-pmdd-adolescents-review]])

## Methods

Narrative review focused on adolescent presentations of PMS/PMDD. Covers epidemiology, pathophysiology, diagnosis criteria (ISPMD classification, DSM-5), pharmacological treatment (SSRIs, OCPs, GnRH agonists, progesterone, drospirenone), symptomatic treatment (NSAIDs, spirinolactone), and non-pharmacological approaches (diet, CBT, supplements, acupuncture). Published May 2022, Current Problems in Pediatric and Adolescent Health Care.

## Detailed Summary

**Epidemiology in adolescents:**
80% of menstruating women experience some premenstrual symptoms; 20-40% experience PMS; 2.5-5% have PMDD-level functional impairment; 1.2-6.4% meet strict PMDD criteria. Adolescent prevalence is comparable to adult prevalence. Physiological premenstrual symptoms (80-95%) should be distinguished from pathological PMS/PMDD requiring treatment.

**ISPMD classification:**
International Society for Premenstrual Disorders (ISPMD) defines two categories: core PMDs (PMS, PMDD) and variant PMDs (PMDD with additional criteria such as premenstrual exacerbation of other conditions). Core PMS requires affective or somatic symptoms impairing function, occurring in the luteal phase and resolving after menstruation, prospectively documented across at least two cycles. PMDD additionally requires ≥5 of 11 DSM-5 symptoms with at least one mood symptom.

**Pathophysiology summary:**
The primary theory centers on sensitivity to the hormonal cycle rather than absolute hormonal levels. Evidence: (1) GnRH agonist suppression eliminates symptoms; (2) readdition of estrogen/progesterone reproduces symptoms in PMDD women but not controls; (3) SSRIs are effective at lower doses and faster onset than for depression — serotonin dysregulation is not generalized but cycle-sensitive. The HPO axis drives estrogen → progesterone → ALLO cascade. ALLO normally binds GABA-A receptors and produces anxiolytic effects. In PMDD, altered GABA-A receptor subunit expression (possibly cytokine-mediated) converts this anxiolytic signal to insufficient or paradoxically anxiogenic — the "withdrawal" effect.

**Pharmacological treatment hierarchy:**
1. **SSRIs** — first line; intermittent (luteal days 14-28) effective; avoids continuous use
2. **Combined OCP with drospirenone** — FDA-approved; especially useful in adolescents wanting contraception + PMDD control
3. **GnRH agonists** — suppress ovulation completely; effective but side effects (osteoporosis, vasomotor) limit use; add-back estrogen + progestin if >6 months
4. **Progesterone/progestin** — suppresses ovulation; data mixed
5. **Calcium + vitamin D** — calcium 1200 mg/day has prospective evidence; vitamin D supplementation being studied

**Non-pharmacological approaches:**
- Carbohydrate-rich diet during luteal phase → increases tryptophan uptake → raises brain serotonin → may reduce cravings and mood symptoms
- Exercise → endorphin release, anti-inflammatory effects
- CBT → Cochrane review: CBT reduces PMS symptom frequency and improves quality of life; effective for psychological symptoms
- St. John's Wort, ginkgo biloba, saffron: varying evidence; smaller studies; not standard of care
- Acupuncture: some improvement reported; evidence quality limited
- Vitamin B6 (pyridoxine): modest evidence; 9 RCTs, no clinically significant difference vs. placebo per systematic review

## Relevance to Cureva

- **Treatment framework:** SSRIs (intermittent preferred), drospirenone OCP, calcium 1200 mg/day — all evidence-graded
- **Adolescent-specific guidance:** PMDD diagnosis requires 2-cycle prospective documentation; treatment sequencing follows adult guidelines but with preference for non-pharmacological first in adolescents with mild-moderate symptoms
- **Calcium as first-line supplement**: strongest evidence among non-pharmacological approaches; relevant to nutritional immunity (NMDA modulation)
- **STOP consideration**: routine progesterone supplementation NOT recommended — data mixed and may worsen PMS in some patients

## Contradictions / Tensions

- Review includes adult and adolescent literature without always distinguishing — adolescent-specific evidence is thin
- Herbal supplements (St. John's Wort, ginkgo) have study limitations (small samples, variable quality) that preclude firm recommendations
- Vitamin B6 effect: individual older studies positive, systematic review finds no significant difference — reflects publication bias in older literature
