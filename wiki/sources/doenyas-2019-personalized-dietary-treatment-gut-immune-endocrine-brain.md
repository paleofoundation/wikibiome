## Key Findings

- **Non-personalized dietary approaches** such as gluten-free/casein-free (GFCF) diets, ketogenic diets, and generic probiotic supplementation produce inconsistent results across ASD populations because they fail to account for individual variation in immune reactivity ([[doenyas-2019-personalized-dietary-treatment-gut-immune-endocrine-brain|this study]], expert-opinion).
- **Not all ASD children show immune reactivity** to dietary proteins in wheat and milk — the heterogeneity in response explains why some children improve on elimination diets while others do not.
- **Dietary aquaporins** (water channel proteins present in many plant foods) that share structural homology with human aquaporins may elicit cross-reactive antibody responses in genetically susceptible individuals, contributing to neuroinflammation.
- **A three-step personalized treatment plan** is proposed: (1) identify individual dietary immune triggers through antibody testing, (2) eliminate only the foods that elicit immune reactivity in that specific individual, (3) monitor neuroautoimmune markers to assess treatment response.
- **The gut-immune-endocrine-brain axis** framework extends beyond the simpler gut-brain axis by incorporating immune and endocrine signaling as mediators between gut perturbation and neurological outcomes.

## Methods

- **Study type**: Narrative review and hypothesis paper synthesizing evidence from immunology, gastroenterology, endocrinology, and neuroscience to propose a novel personalized dietary framework.
- **Evidence base**: Draws on clinical studies of GFCF diets in ASD, food antibody profiling research, aquaporin immunology, and neuroautoimmune literature.
- **Framework**: Systems biology approach connecting four domains — gut, immune, endocrine, and brain — into an integrated axis.

## Detailed Summary

Doenyas argues that the inconsistent results of dietary interventions in ASD stem from treating the condition as immunologically homogeneous. The GFCF diet, for example, assumes all autistic children are reactive to gluten and casein, but antibody profiling studies show that only a subset exhibit IgG or IgA reactivity to these proteins. Similarly, the ketogenic diet may benefit some children through anti-inflammatory or metabolic mechanisms but is not universally effective.

The aquaporin hypothesis is the paper's most novel contribution. Aquaporins are ubiquitous water channel proteins found in both plants and animals. The author highlights that certain plant aquaporins (found in foods like tomatoes, spinach, and soy) share enough structural similarity with human aquaporins in the brain, gut, and kidneys to potentially trigger molecular mimicry-based autoimmune responses. In a genetically susceptible individual (particularly those with certain HLA haplotypes), dietary exposure to plant aquaporins could generate antibodies that cross-react with the individual's own aquaporins, contributing to blood-brain barrier disruption, gut barrier compromise, and neuroinflammation.

The proposed three-step protocol emphasizes that dietary treatment should be personalized: test first, then eliminate only what is specifically problematic for that individual, then monitor. This contrasts with blanket elimination approaches.

## Open Questions

- Whether aquaporin cross-reactivity can be confirmed in larger clinical studies with ASD populations.
- How gut microbiome composition interacts with dietary aquaporin exposure — do certain bacterial communities modulate the immune response to plant aquaporins?
- Whether the proposed three-step protocol has been empirically validated in any clinical setting since publication.
- How metal exposures might influence gut barrier permeability and thus alter the immune response to dietary proteins in ASD.
