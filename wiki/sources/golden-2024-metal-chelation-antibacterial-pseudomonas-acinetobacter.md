## Key Findings

- **Disrupting metal homeostasis disarms virulence** without directly killing bacteria — a fundamentally different mechanism from conventional antibiotics that reduces resistance selection pressure ([[golden-2024-metal-chelation-antibacterial-pseudomonas-acinetobacter]], expert-opinion).
- **[[pseudomonas-aeruginosa]] and [[acinetobacter-baumannii]]** rely on species-specific siderophore uptake systems exploitable for Trojan horse antibiotic delivery — each pathogen has distinct metal acquisition machinery that can be targeted selectively.
- **Competitive [[iron]] deprivation** — chelators that outcompete bacterial siderophores — offers an alternative to bactericidal strategies, extending to [[zinc]], [[manganese]], and [[copper]] dependencies.
- **Multi-metal strategy**: Simultaneous chelation of multiple metals (Fe + Zn, Fe + Mn) may be more effective than single-metal approaches, as pathogens can compensate for one metal restriction by upregulating alternative pathways.
- **Reduced resistance evolution**: Because metal acquisition is a fundamental metabolic requirement (not a single targetable enzyme), chelation-based strategies face lower resistance evolution pressure than conventional antibiotics.

## Detailed Summary

This review systematically examines how disrupting metal homeostasis in *[[pseudomonas-aeruginosa]]* and *[[acinetobacter-baumannii]]* can serve as an antibacterial strategy distinct from conventional antibiotics. The central argument is that these pathogens depend on specific metals ([[iron]], [[zinc]], [[manganese]], [[copper]]) for virulence factor expression, and that chelating these metals disarms pathogenicity without necessarily killing the organism — thereby reducing the selective pressure that drives antimicrobial resistance. For *P. aeruginosa*, the review details pyoverdine and pyochelin siderophore systems, zinc-dependent metalloproteases (elastase, alkaline protease), and iron-dependent biofilm formation. For *A. baumannii*, it covers acinetobactin-mediated iron uptake, zinc-dependent OXA-type beta-lactamases, and manganese-dependent superoxide dismutase. The Trojan horse strategy — conjugating antibiotics to siderophore structures so that pathogens actively import their own destruction — is discussed with specific examples including cefiderocol (an FDA-approved siderophore cephalosporin). The review also addresses the concept of competitive iron deprivation: using chelators with higher affinity than bacterial siderophores to starve pathogens of iron. This extends beyond iron to zinc (calprotectin mimetics), manganese (as demonstrated in pneumococcal studies), and copper (relevant to intramacrophage killing).
