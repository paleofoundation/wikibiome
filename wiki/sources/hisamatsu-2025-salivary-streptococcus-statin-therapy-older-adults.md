# Alteration of Salivary Streptococcus is Associated with Statin Therapy in Older Adults: A Cohort Study

## Summary

This cohort study of 82 Japanese older adults (age >= 70) examines the relationship between prescription drug use and salivary microbiome composition using 16S rRNA gene amplicon sequencing. It finds that [[pharmacomicrobiomics|statins]], proton pump inhibitors, and transporter/symporter inhibitors had the most prominent effects on salivary microbiome diversity.

## Key Findings

- Statin use (38% of cohort) was strongly correlated with a decrease in *Streptococcus* abundance
- Statin intensity and obesity may be associated with altering the salivary microbiome
- Functional predictions for statin users showed altered vitamin biosynthesis and purine nucleotide degradation pathways
- Drug effects on salivary microbiome diversity were more prominent than host variables (age, sex, BMI)
- Among 26 drug categories analyzed, statins had the strongest association at the genus level

## Relevance to Gut-Metal-Microbiome Axis

Statins have been investigated as potential [[drug-repurposing]] candidates for [[multiple-sclerosis]] due to their anti-inflammatory and neuroprotective properties. This study demonstrates that statins significantly alter the oral microbiome, particularly *Streptococcus* -- a genus that is increased in MS patients. If statins reduce pathogenic *Streptococcus* populations, this microbiome-modulating effect could contribute to their potential therapeutic benefit in MS through the [[gut-brain-axis]], independent of their cholesterol-lowering action.

## Limitations

- Small cohort (n=82), Japanese population only
- Salivary rather than gut microbiome assessed
- Cross-sectional design cannot establish causality
- Polypharmacy confounders difficult to fully control
