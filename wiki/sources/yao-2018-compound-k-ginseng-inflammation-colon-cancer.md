# Effects of Compound K, an Enteric Microbiome Metabolite of Ginseng, in the Treatment of Inflammation Associated Colon Cancer

## Key Claims

- Compound K, a major intestinal microbiome metabolite of ginsenoside Rb1 from American ginseng, showed significantly stronger anti-proliferative effects than its parent compound Rb1 in both HCT-116 and HT-29 human CRC cell lines (P < 0.01)
- Compound K significantly arrested HCT-116 and HT-29 cells in the G1 phase of the cell cycle (P < 0.01) and induced cell apoptosis, while Rb1 did not markedly influence cell cycle at comparable concentrations
- At the molecular level, compound K upregulated p21 (CDKN1A) and downregulated CDK6, cyclin D1, and cyclin E (G1 phase arrest regulators), while also upregulating p53 and Bax (pro-apoptotic) and downregulating Bcl2 (anti-apoptotic)
- Compound K significantly reduced IL-8 production in LPS-stimulated HT-29 cells (from 180 to 70 pg/ml at 20 uM, P < 0.01), demonstrating anti-inflammatory effects even at low concentrations
- LC-Q-TOF-MS confirmed that ginsenoside Rb1 is biotransformed by human enteric microflora to compound K through stepwise deglycosylation, identifying the gut microbiome as essential for generating this bioactive metabolite

## Methods/Findings

- **Design**: In vitro comparison of ginsenoside Rb1 and compound K effects on CRC cell lines
- **Cell lines**: HCT-116 (inflammation-linked) and HT-29 (chemo-resistant) human colorectal cancer cells
- **Methods**: MTS proliferation assay; flow cytometry for cell cycle and apoptosis; RT-qPCR for gene expression; ELISA for IL-8; LC-Q-TOF-MS for metabolite identification
- **Key concentrations**: Compound K effective at 40-80 uM; anti-inflammatory effects at concentrations as low as 20 uM
