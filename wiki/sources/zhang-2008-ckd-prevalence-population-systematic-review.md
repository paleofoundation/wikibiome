# CKD Prevalence in Population-Based Studies

## Key Findings

- The **median prevalence** of [[chronic-kidney-disease]] (stages 3-5) was **7.2%** in persons aged 30 years and older across the included population-based studies.
- In the **elderly population** (aged 64+ years), CKD prevalence ranged from **23.4% to 35.8%**, highlighting the dramatic age-dependence of the condition.
- CKD prevalence estimates **strongly depend** on which eGFR equation is used (Cockcroft-Gault, MDRD, CKD-EPI) — different equations applied to the same population can yield prevalence estimates differing by several-fold.
- The review documented substantial geographic variation in CKD prevalence, with higher rates in populations with greater burdens of diabetes, hypertension, and cardiovascular disease.
- Many studies used single serum creatinine measurements without confirmation, potentially overestimating true CKD prevalence (as KDOQI requires persistent GFR reduction for at least 3 months for diagnosis).

## Methods

**Systematic review** of 26 population-based studies that assessed CKD prevalence using the KDOQI (Kidney Disease Outcomes Quality Initiative) definition. Studies were included if they measured kidney function in a general population sample (not clinical or referred populations) and reported prevalence of CKD stages 3-5. The authors examined prevalence by age group, eGFR equation used, and geographic region. No meta-analytic pooling was performed due to substantial methodological heterogeneity across studies.

## Detailed Summary

Zhang and Rothenbacher conducted one of the early systematic reviews cataloging CKD prevalence across population-based studies worldwide. The review documented that CKD is a highly prevalent condition — affecting approximately 1 in 14 adults over 30 — but that precise prevalence estimates are highly sensitive to measurement methodology.

The age-dependence of CKD prevalence is striking: while approximately 7% of middle-aged adults meet criteria for CKD stages 3-5, this rises to 24-36% in the elderly. This age gradient raises the question of whether CKD in the elderly represents true pathological kidney disease or physiological age-related decline in GFR — a debate that has implications for clinical management and resource allocation.

The methodological finding regarding eGFR equations is important for interpreting the CKD literature: the Cockcroft-Gault, MDRD, and CKD-EPI equations differ in their accuracy across age groups, body compositions, and ethnic populations. Studies using the older Cockcroft-Gault equation tend to overestimate CKD prevalence in the elderly (because it is more affected by low muscle mass), while the MDRD equation may underestimate prevalence in younger, healthier populations.

The geographic variation documented in the review — higher prevalence in regions with more diabetes, hypertension, and cardiovascular disease — is consistent with the understanding of CKD as a condition driven by metabolic and vascular risk factors. However, the review did not examine environmental risk factors (heavy metals, agricultural chemicals) that may contribute to geographic variation, particularly in the form of CKDu.

## Relevance to WikiBiome

This systematic review provides the **epidemiological baseline** for understanding CKD as a global health problem. The high prevalence — particularly in elderly populations — establishes the potential impact of gut-kidney axis interventions: even modest reductions in CKD progression through microbiome-targeted therapies could affect millions of patients worldwide.

The age-dependence of CKD also connects to the immunosenescence-microbiome axis: age-related gut microbiome changes (declining diversity, loss of Bifidobacterium, expansion of Proteobacteria) parallel age-related kidney function decline, suggesting that the two processes may be mechanistically linked.

The geographic variation in CKD prevalence — partially unexplained by traditional risk factors — leaves room for environmental factors including heavy metal exposure and waterborne toxins, which vary dramatically by region and are central to WikiBiome's metallomics thesis.
