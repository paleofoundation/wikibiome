# Key Claims

- [[metabolomics]] is a state-of-the-art analytical tool for identifying perturbations in metabolic pathways caused by [[heavy metals]] exposure, with critical roles in [[oxidative-stress|oxidative stress]], lipid metabolism, and amino acid metabolism
- Arsenic exposure disrupts amino acid metabolism and TCA cycle, with urinary biomarker metabolites strongly associated with endocrine disturbances and oxidative stress
- Lead exposure alters four lipid metabolites and six amino acids, providing evidence of impairment in specific metabolic pathways through induction of oxidative stress
- Cadmium induces mitochondrial electron transport chain dysfunction, causing oxidative stress via ROS generation and disruption of cellular redox homeostasis
- Mercury (MeHg) exposure is linked to hepatotoxicity through altered bile acid metabolism and cholesterol pathways
- Heavy metals collectively impair carbohydrate, lipid, and amino acid metabolism, with ROS as the central mediator of toxicity mechanisms

# Methods/Findings

- **Study type**: Review of metabolomics applications in heavy metal toxicology
- **Metabolomics workflow**: Biological sample collection, homogenization, analytical platforms (GC-MS, LC-MS, NMR, CE-MS, IMS-MS), data analysis, compound identification (Figure 1)
- **Table 1**: Permissible limits, sources of exposure, and health hazards for Pb (0.1 mg/L), As (0.02), Cd (0.06), Hg (0.01), Cu (0.1)
- **Arsenic metabolomics**: Martin et al. found metabolite alterations in diabetic and non-diabetic individuals related to amino acid and TCA cycle; Zhang and Shen identified top 5 urinary biomarker metabolites
- **Lead metabolomics**: Kelly et al. found Pb signature metabolites in toenails and blood; quercetin showed ameliorative effects on lead-induced oxidative stress
- **Cadmium metabolomics**: Hepatotoxicity via altered bile acid metabolism; breast cancer patients showed increased Cd and altered lipid/amino acid profiles
- **Mercury metabolomics**: High-fat diet (HFD) induced increased Cd/Hg bioaccumulation; altered cholesterol pathways and atherosclerosis risk
- **Toxicity mechanisms (Figure 2)**: Genotoxicity, nephrotoxicity, hepatotoxicity, neurotoxicity, cardiovascular toxicity, skin toxicity, reproductive toxicity -- all mediated through oxidative stress
