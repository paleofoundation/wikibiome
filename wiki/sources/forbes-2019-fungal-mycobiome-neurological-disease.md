# A Fungal World: Could the Gut Mycobiome Be Involved in Neurological Disease?

## Summary

This review discusses the emerging role of the gut mycobiome (fungal microbiome) in neurological diseases including [[multiple-sclerosis]], ALS, and Alzheimer's disease. It provides an overview of methods for studying the mycobiome and summarizes evidence linking fungi to neuroinflammatory and neurodegenerative conditions.

## Key Findings

### The Gastrointestinal Mycobiome
- The Ascomycota phylum (including *Candida*, *Cladosporium*, *Saccharomyces cerevisiae*) and Basidiomycota dominate most human anatomic sites
- Less than 0.4% of microbiome literature addresses fungal communities
- Competitive associations exist between bacterial and fungal microorganisms in the gut
- Prolonged antibiotic use promotes fungal overgrowth, particularly *Candida*
- Commensal bacteria *Bacteroides thetaiotaomicron* and *Blautia producta* can induce secretion of antifungal peptides via colonic epithelial cells

### Fungi in Neurological Disease
- Bacterial (*Mycobacterium tuberculosis*, *Neisseria meningitidis*), viral (herpes simplex virus-1), fungal (*Cryptococcus*, *Aspergillus*), and parasitic (*Trypanosoma cruzi*, *Toxoplasma gondii*) infections can affect the CNS
- Either directly or indirectly, the gut microbiome may have a role in the pathogenesis of neurological diseases

### Mycobiome in MS
- Gut microbiota [[dysbiosis]] (specifically bacteria and archaea) has been reported in MS
- Very few studies have examined the mycobiome in MS specifically
- A consensus healthy mycobiome has yet to be established, making comparisons difficult
- *Candida* spp. and *Saccharomyces* spp. are the most commonly detected gut fungi
- A review of 36 studies (1917-2018) using culture-dependent or -independent approaches found that combined, 267 distinct fungal species had been reported from the human gut

### Methodological Challenges
- ITS1 and ITS2 regions are used for fungal identification (analogous to 16S for bacteria)
- Fungal genome databases are less comprehensive than bacterial databases
- Primer selection biases may affect detected fungal species
- Shotgun metagenomics can sequence across kingdoms but fungal reads are in low abundance

## Relevance to Gut-Metal-Microbiome Axis

This review is relevant to the [[gut-metal-microbiome]] model because:

1. **Fungi have well-documented metal-binding capacity** - *Candida*, *Saccharomyces*, and *Aspergillus* species possess metallothioneins and metal biosorption mechanisms that allow them to thrive in metal-rich environments
2. **Metal exposure disrupts bacterial-fungal balance** - by depleting metal-sensitive bacteria, [[heavy-metals]] may create niches for metal-tolerant fungi, explaining the increased fungal diversity seen in MS
3. **Fungal [[dysbiosis]]** may compound bacterial dysbiosis in driving [[neuroinflammation]] through the [[gut-brain-axis]]
4. **Antifungal peptide production** depends on healthy bacterial communities (*Bacteroides*, *Blautia*) that are depleted by metals

## Limitations

- Review with limited MS-specific mycobiome data available at time of publication
- Most mycobiome studies are cross-sectional
- Consensus healthy mycobiome not yet established
- Fungal taxonomic databases remain incomplete
