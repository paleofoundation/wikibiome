# Why This Paper Matters

This pilot study is the first to examine the impact of fecal microbiota transplantation (FMT) on gut fungal and archaeal species in a neurodevelopmental rat model of schizophrenia (MAM-E17). While most microbiome studies in schizophrenia focus exclusively on bacteria, this work addresses the understudied mycobiome and archaeome. The finding that FMT had **limited impact on gut fungal populations** -- with C. tropicalis colonization increasing to nearly 100% across ALL groups regardless of treatment source -- suggests that microbiome maturation rather than transplant specificity drives fungal colonization. This has important implications for FMT-based therapeutic approaches to schizophrenia.

# Key Claims

- **88 Sprague-Dawley rats**: 48 MAM-exposed (prenatal methylazoxymethanol acetate, neurodevelopmental SCZ model) and 39 healthy controls, subdivided into 4 groups (MAM+placebo, MAM+FMT, HC+placebo, HC+FMT)
- FMT was performed three times at 7-day intervals (days 42, 49, 56 of life) via oral gavage; fecal samples collected on day 86
- **Candida tropicalis**: slightly higher prevalence in MAM-exposed rats (19% vs 10% in controls) pre-FMT; post-FMT, colonization increased to **nearly 100% across ALL groups** regardless of transplant source
- **Malassezia spp.**: commonly present before treatment in all groups; abundance significantly **declined** after both FMT and placebo administration, suggesting procedural stress effects
- **Cryptococcus neoformans**: absent in all samples (both pre- and post-FMT)
- **Methanogenic archaea** (Methanobrevibacter stadtmanae and Methanosphaera smithii): completely **absent** in all rat fecal samples, suggesting these organisms may not colonize this rat model
- The results suggest FMT has **limited impact on fungal populations** in the developmental stage examined, possibly due to natural microbiome maturation dominating over transplant effects
- The kynurenine pathway connecting gut microbiome to brain function is highlighted as a key mechanistic pathway in SCZ pathophysiology

# Methods & Findings

## MAM-E17 Model
- Pregnant dams injected with 22 mg/kg MAM on gestational day 17
- This produces offspring with histological, neurophysiological, and behavioral abnormalities similar to schizophrenia
- MAM rats show disrupted prepulse inhibition, social interaction deficits, and cognitive impairment

## FMT Protocol
- Fresh fecal suspensions prepared without freezing (to preserve viability)
- Homogenized through 70 um nylon strainer
- Administered via oral gavage at 500 uL per dose
- Three doses at weekly intervals

## qPCR Analysis
- TaqMan probe-based qPCR targeting specific fungal species (C. tropicalis, Malassezia spp., C. neoformans)
- Archaeal targets: M. stadtmanae and M. smithii
- Pre- and post-FMT samples compared within and between groups

## Key Limitations
- Small sample sizes per group (19-24 rats)
- Only select fungal and archaeal species examined (not comprehensive mycobiome profiling)
- Single time point post-FMT assessment
- Absence of archaea may be model-specific rather than generalizable
