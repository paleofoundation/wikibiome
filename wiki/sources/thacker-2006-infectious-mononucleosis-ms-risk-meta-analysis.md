# Infectious Mononucleosis and Risk for Multiple Sclerosis: A Meta-analysis

## Summary

This meta-analysis of 14 studies characterizes the association between infectious mononucleosis (IM), a frequent clinical manifestation of primary Epstein-Barr virus (EBV) infection after childhood, and the risk for [[multiple-sclerosis]].

## Key Findings

- Combined relative risk of MS after IM: **2.3 (95% CI 1.7-3.0; p < 10^-6)**
- No significant heterogeneity among studies (Q = 16.074; p = 0.245)
- Adjusted RRs (accounting for confounders) yielded a slightly higher estimate of 2.5 (95% CI 2.0-3.2)
- Results robust across study designs: case-control (RR 2.1) and cohort (RR 2.7) studies
- Incident MS cases showed higher risk (RR 3.0) compared to prevalent cases (RR 1.9)

## EBV-MS Model

The authors propose a model relating EBV infection timing to MS risk:
- Risk is close to zero in individuals never infected with EBV
- Intermediate risk in those infected with EBV in early childhood
- Highest risk in those first infected in adolescence or later (manifesting as IM)
- MS incidence peaks around age 25-30, then declines to near zero by age 60
- Many cases of MS could theoretically be prevented by vaccination against EBV or early childhood exposure

## Relevance to Gut-Metal-Microbiome Axis

EBV infection profoundly impacts the immune system and may interact with the [[gut-microbiome]] through multiple pathways. EBV establishes latent infection in B cells, which are key regulators of gut-immune homeostasis. The timing-dependent risk (adolescent vs. childhood infection) parallels the critical developmental windows during which the [[gut-microbiome]] is established. EBV-driven immune dysregulation could synergize with [[dysbiosis]]-induced immune imbalance, and both may be exacerbated by [[heavy-metals]] exposure that simultaneously disrupts microbial communities and impairs antiviral immunity.

## Limitations

- Observational data cannot prove causation
- Recall bias possible in case-control studies relying on self-reported IM history
- Does not address microbiome interactions with EBV
