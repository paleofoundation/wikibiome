# Key Claims

- Cigarette smoking (current and former) is significantly associated with increased risk of [[dysmenorrhea]]: pooled OR = 1.45 (95% CI: 1.30-1.61) across 24 studies with 27,091 participants
- Current smoking: OR = 1.50 (95% CI: 1.33-1.70); former smoking: OR = 1.31 (95% CI: 1.18-1.46)
- Primary dysmenorrhea risk increased by 56% among smokers: OR = 1.56 (95% CI: 1.31-1.86)
- Results were consistent across geographic regions: Europe OR = 1.45, Asia OR = 1.47, North America OR = 1.34, Oceania OR = 1.64
- Nicotine causes vasoconstriction reducing endometrial blood flow, and smoking increases [[inflammation|prostaglandin]] production, both contributing to menstrual pain

# Methods/Findings

- **Design**: Systematic review and meta-analysis following MOOSE guidelines
- **Search**: PubMed, Medline, Web of Science, EMBASE through November 2019
- **Included studies**: 24 observational studies (18 cross-sectional, 4 case-control, 2 cohort); sample sizes 251-9,067; ages 11-59
- **Prevalence of dysmenorrhea**: Ranged from 20.1% to 91.8% across studies
- **Statistical methods**: Mantel-Haenszel procedure for ORs; random-effects model; I2 statistic for heterogeneity; Begg's and Egger's tests for publication bias
- **Heterogeneity**: Medium (I2 = 41.0%, p = 0.02) for overall analysis
- **Subgroup analyses**: Consistent across study designs, locations, dysmenorrhea types, and methodological quality levels
- **Sensitivity analysis**: Exclusion of any single study did not materially alter the overall combined effect
- **Publication bias**: No significant bias (Begg's z = 1.66, p = 0.097; Egger's t = 1.97, p = 0.061)
- **Mechanisms**: Nicotine induces vasoconstriction decreasing endometrial blood flow; smoking increases prostaglandin production and inflammation; may have antiestrogenic extraovarian effects
