# Fecal Microbiota Transplantation in Systemic Sclerosis: A Double-Blind, Placebo-Controlled Randomized Pilot Trial

## Summary

This first-in-man RCT evaluates [[fecal-microbiota-transplant]] using commercially available anaerobic cultivated human intestinal microbiota (ACHIM) in systemic sclerosis (SSc) patients with GI involvement (n=10, 5 FMT, 5 placebo) over 16 weeks.

## Key Findings

- FMT via gastroduodenoscopy was performed at weeks 0 and 2
- ACHIM side effects were mild and transient
- Decreased bloating, diarrhea, and/or fecal incontinence observed in 4/5 FMT patients (also 2/4 placebo)
- Relative abundance, richness, and diversity of IgA- and IgM-coated fecal bacteria fluctuated more after FMT than after placebo
- Primary endpoint: safety and clinical efficacy assessed via UCLA GIT score
- Secondary endpoints included microbiome composition, SCFA concentrations, fecal calprotectin

## Study Design Strengths

- Double-blind, placebo-controlled
- Standardized single-donor ACHIM product (avoiding donor variability)
- Multiple biological endpoints including immune markers
- Registered on clinicaltrials.gov (NCT03444220)

## Relevance to Gut-Metal-Microbiome Axis

While focused on SSc rather than [[multiple-sclerosis]], this pilot trial provides a methodological template for FMT RCTs in autoimmune diseases. The use of standardized ACHIM overcomes a major challenge in FMT research (donor variability). The findings that FMT alters Ig-coated bacteria (reflecting host-microbiome immune interactions) and potentially improves GI symptoms suggest that similar approaches could benefit MS patients with GI [[comorbidities]]. The [[short-chain-fatty-acids]] analysis connects FMT effects to metabolites known to modulate [[neuroinflammation]] through the [[gut-brain-axis]].

## Limitations

- Very small sample size (n=10)
- SSc, not MS
- Short follow-up (16 weeks)
- Descriptive statistics only due to small sample
