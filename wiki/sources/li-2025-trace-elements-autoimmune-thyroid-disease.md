# Recent advances of trace elements in autoimmune thyroid disease

## Key Findings

- **Selenium levels are significantly lower in both HT and GD** compared to controls: HT 104.36 ug/l, GD 97.68 ug/l, vs. controls 122.63 ug/l (P<0.001); GD patients with ophthalmopathy have even lower Se (96.82 +/- 30.3 ug/dL); Se supplementation (combined myo-inositol + Se) reduced TSH, TPOAb, and TgAb after 6 months ([[li-2025-trace-elements-autoimmune-thyroid-disease]], expert opinion)
- **Zinc deficiency is highly prevalent in hypothyroid patients** at 49.1% (OR 5.926, 95% CI 3.756-9.351); in hyperthyroid patients at 37.5% (OR 3.683, 95% CI 1.628-8.33); Zn mediates T lymphocyte differentiation, IL-1, IL-6, TNF-alpha synthesis, and thyroid hormone receptor activity ([[li-2025-trace-elements-autoimmune-thyroid-disease]], expert opinion)
- **Iron deficiency is associated with impaired thyroid function**: meta-analysis of 47 studies (53,152 pregnant women) found higher TSH (2.31 vs. 1.75 mIU/L) and lower free T4 (10.7 vs. 13.3 pmol/L) in iron-deficient women; dual-fortified salt (iodine + iron) improved both iron and iodine status without inhibiting thyroid function (RCT, n=377 schoolchildren, 9 months) ([[li-2025-trace-elements-autoimmune-thyroid-disease]], expert opinion citing systematic-review-meta-analysis)
- **Vitamin D deficiency prevalence is significantly higher in AITD** (72%) vs. healthy controls (30.6%) (P<0.001); 1,25-(OH)2D3 blocks NF-kappaB activation, downregulates IL-8 and IL-12, and reduces T cell numbers in thyroid tissue; AITD patients have lower 25(OH)D levels (SMD: -0.99, 95% CI -1.31 to -0.66) and higher risk of deficiency (OR 2.99, 95% CI 1.88-4.74) ([[li-2025-trace-elements-autoimmune-thyroid-disease]], expert opinion)
- **Iodine has a U-shaped dose-response relationship with AITD**: excess iodine directly inhibits TPO activity and NIS expression, induces ROS in thyroid cells, and promotes Th17 cell proliferation; iodine deficiency also increases risk through impaired TH synthesis ([[li-2025-trace-elements-autoimmune-thyroid-disease]], expert opinion)

## Detailed Summary

**Selenium and AITD.** Selenium serves three critical functions: (1) antioxidant defense through GPx removing H2O2 from thyroid cells, (2) immunomodulation by supporting regulatory T cells and suppressing Th1/Th17 responses, (3) hormone metabolism via deiodinases (DIO1/DIO2). Se deficiency lowers deiodinase activity, impairing T4-to-T3 conversion. Clinical data (Table 1): Se significantly lower in GD with ophthalmopathy (96.82 ug/dL) vs. without (94.53 ug/dL) vs. controls (102.55 ug/dL); serum Se negatively correlated with FT3, FT4, and TSH in GD patients without GO. Combined MI-Se supplementation (68 HT patients, 6 months) reduced TSH, TPOAb, and TgAb. Se monotherapy (83 ug/day selenomethionine, 196 patients, 4 months) normalized TSH in 17.2% of subjects. A meta-analysis confirmed Se supplementation reduced FT3 (MD=-0.40, 95% CI -0.70 to -0.10, P=0.009), FT4 (MD=-0.76, 95% CI -1.58 to -0.07, P=0.07), and TPOAb (MD=-150.25, 95% CI -04.06 to -96.43, P<0.00001). Se modulates Th1/Th2 balance, increases Tregs, and reduces Foxp3 deficiency. Mice on high-Se diet: increased T cell receptor signaling in CD4+ T cells, shifted balance toward Th1 phenotype.

**Iodine and AITD.** U-shaped dose-response: excess iodine inhibits TPO activity and NIS expression, induces ROS, promotes Th17 proliferation; activates NF-kappaB/NLRP3 inflammasome in Hashimoto's. At cellular level, high iodine causes oxidative damage → suppression of protective autophagy → cellular apoptosis → neo-antigen exposure → immune response initiation. Animal models: 8 weeks of high-iodine diet induced experimental autoimmune thyroiditis (EAT) with lymphocyte infiltration, reduced thyroid function, reduced TH and autoantibody levels. In mice, extremely high iodine doses enhanced Th1 polarization and prevented Treg production.

**Vitamin D and AITD.** VDR is expressed on immune cells (DCs, monocytes, lymphocytes). 1,25-(OH)2D3 blocks NF-kappaB activation → downregulates IL-8, IL-12 → reduces T cell numbers in thyroid. Cross-sectional studies consistently show lower 25(OH)D in AITD (AITD patients had lower levels: SMD -0.99, 95% CI -1.31 to -0.66; OR of deficiency 2.99, 95% CI 1.88-4.74). However, the causal direction remains unclear.

**Zinc and AITD.** Zn serves as cofactor for >300 metalloenzymes, modulates thyroid hormone receptor activity, and contributes to HPT axis function. Thyroid autoantibodies and zinc were significantly positively correlated in the AITD group. Prevalence of low zinc: 49.1% in hypothyroid (OR 5.926), 37.5% in hyperthyroid (OR 3.683) vs. healthy individuals.

**Iron and AITD.** Fe is essential for TPO (cofactor for heme-dependent enzyme). Meta-analysis of 47 studies with 53,152 pregnant women: Fe deficiency → higher TSH (2.31 vs. 1.75 mIU/L), lower FT4 (10.7 vs. 13.3 pmol/L). RCT in Moroccan schoolchildren (n=377, 9 months): dual-fortified salt (iodine + iron) improved both Fe and I status without inhibiting thyroid function. Fe serves as epigenetic regulatory factor — its deficiency may lead to genomic-level alterations triggering AITD.

**Copper and AITD.** Cu is important for SOD (antioxidant defense) and interacts with thyroid hormone receptor beta. Evidence is inconsistent: some studies report positive correlation between serum Cu and thyroid autoantibodies, others find no significant association. Cu/Se ratio may be more clinically relevant than absolute Cu concentration.

**Magnesium and AITD.** Mg modulates Se bioavailability and tissue distribution; indirectly supports T4-to-T3 conversion as cofactor for deiodinases. Serum Mg<0.55 mmol/L associated with higher TgAb positivity and increased risk of overt hypothyroidism. Mechanistic insights are limited.

## Relevance to Cureva

- Se supplementation evidence (meta-analysis: TPOAb reduction MD=-150.25, P<0.00001) provides strong triangle evidence for Se intervention in AITD
- Combined MI-Se (myo-inositol + selenium) reduced TSH, TPOAb, and TgAb — specific intervention formulation
- Dual-fortified salt (I + Fe) RCT provides evidence for safe co-supplementation
- Zn supplementation in hypothyroid patients with 49.1% deficiency prevalence — screening and supplementation intervention
- STOP signal: excessive iodine supplementation in AITD patients could worsen autoimmunity via NLRP3 inflammasome activation
- Vitamin D supplementation (target 25(OH)D >30 ng/mL) as adjunctive AITD intervention, though causal evidence is incomplete

## Contradictions / Tensions

- Se supplementation: meta-analysis shows TPOAb reduction, but most clinical trials are limited by small sample sizes, short follow-up, and inconsistent selenium forms and dosages
- Vitamin D: consistent epidemiological association with AITD, but causal direction is unclear — is deficiency a cause or consequence?
- Cu evidence is inconsistent: one study shows positive correlation with thyroid autoantibodies, another shows no significant association; Cu may be an acute-phase reactant confounding results
- Iron: the association between Fe deficiency and AITD is confounded by comorbid autoimmune gastritis and celiac disease (causing malabsorption)
- The review notes this is primarily a narrative review based on observational studies with limitations including heterogeneous populations and insufficient exploration of trace element interactions

## Open Questions

- What are the optimal timing and dosage for selenium supplementation in AITD — before or after diagnosis?
- Do trace element interactions (Se-I, Zn-Cu, Mg-Se) create synergistic or antagonistic effects on thyroid autoimmunity?
- Can multi-element panel testing predict AITD onset in genetically susceptible individuals?
- Does correcting Zn deficiency in hypothyroid patients improve thyroid function independently of levothyroxine?
- What is the molecular mechanism by which Mg modulates Se bioavailability in thyroid tissue?
- Would personalized nutritional strategies based on trace element status and gut microbiota composition improve AITD outcomes?
