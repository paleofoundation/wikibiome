## Key Findings

- **Significant inverse dose-response association** between dietary fiber intake and ovarian cancer risk — highest vs. lowest fiber intake category yielded a pooled relative risk of 0.78 (95% CI: 0.70-0.88) with no significant heterogeneity (I-squared approximately 0%) ([[zheng-2018-dietary-fiber-ovarian-cancer-risk-meta-analysis|this study]], systematic-review-meta-analysis, n=142,189).
- **Each 10 g/day increase in dietary fiber** was associated with a 12% reduction in OC risk (RR: 0.88, 95% CI: 0.83-0.94), demonstrating a clear dose-response gradient.
- **Consistency across studies**: The absence of heterogeneity across 13 studies strengthens the reliability of the inverse association, as does the dose-response pattern.
- **Subgroup analyses** showed the protective effect was consistent across study designs (case-control and cohort studies) and geographic regions.

## Methods

- **Study type**: Systematic review and meta-analysis with dose-response analysis.
- **Included studies**: 13 studies (specific mix of case-control and cohort studies) comprising 5,777 OC cases among 142,189 total participants.
- **Exposure**: Dietary fiber intake assessed by food frequency questionnaires or dietary records.
- **Outcome**: Incident ovarian cancer.
- **Analysis**: Random-effects meta-analysis for pooled RR; dose-response analysis using restricted cubic splines; heterogeneity assessment (I-squared, Q statistic); publication bias evaluation (funnel plots, Egger's test).
- **Databases**: PubMed, Embase, and Web of Science searched for eligible studies.

## Detailed Summary

This meta-analysis provides high-quality epidemiological evidence that dietary fiber intake is protective against ovarian cancer in a dose-dependent manner. The strength of this evidence lies in the consistency across multiple studies, the absence of heterogeneity, and the clear dose-response relationship — all features that support a causal interpretation under Hill's criteria.

The biological mechanisms connecting fiber to OC protection are multifaceted and directly relevant to WikiBiome's microbiome-centric framework:

1. **Microbiome modulation**: Dietary fiber is the primary substrate for gut microbial fermentation, producing short-chain fatty acids (SCFAs) including butyrate, propionate, and acetate. Higher fiber intake selectively enriches SCFA-producing bacteria (Roseburia, Faecalibacterium, Eubacterium), creating a microbial ecology associated with anti-inflammatory and anti-proliferative signaling.

2. **SCFA anti-cancer effects**: Butyrate is a histone deacetylase (HDAC) inhibitor with documented anti-proliferative, pro-apoptotic, and anti-inflammatory effects on cancer cells. This connects directly to the MR findings from [[guo-2025-causal-gut-microbiota-metabolites-ovarian-cancer-mr|Guo 2025]], which identified butyric acid metabolites as protective against OC.

3. **Estrogen modulation**: Fiber intake influences the estrobolome by altering the gut microbial community that metabolizes estrogens. Higher fiber intake reduces enterohepatic estrogen recirculation by binding bile acids and estrogens in the intestinal lumen, potentially reducing estrogen-driven ovarian stimulation.

4. **Metal binding**: Dietary fiber can bind metals (including cadmium, lead, and iron) in the intestinal lumen, reducing their absorption and potentially lowering systemic metal burden — connecting to the metallomics framework.

## Open Questions

- Whether specific fiber types (soluble vs. insoluble, fermentable vs. non-fermentable) differ in their OC-protective effects.
- Whether the protective effect is mediated primarily through microbiome/SCFA pathways, estrogen modulation, or metal binding.
- How fiber intake interacts with the specific gut microbiome taxa identified as OC risk and protective factors in MR studies.
- Whether fiber supplementation in established OC patients provides therapeutic benefit as an adjunct to standard treatment.
