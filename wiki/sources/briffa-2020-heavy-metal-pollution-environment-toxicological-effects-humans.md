# Heavy Metal Pollution in the Environment and Their Toxicological Effects on Humans

## Why This Paper Matters

This is one of the most comprehensive single-source reviews of heavy metal environmental pollution and human health effects available in the literature. It covers **18 individual metals** -- aluminum, vanadium, chromium, manganese, cobalt, nickel, copper, zinc, molybdenum, arsenic, selenium, silver, cadmium, tin, platinum, gold, mercury, and lead -- providing for each metal a structured profile of physical/chemical properties, industrial uses, effects on humans, food sources, regulatory thresholds, pharmacokinetic processes, and toxicological mechanisms. The paper serves as a master reference that links environmental contamination pathways (soil, water, air, food chain) to specific organ system damage in humans, making it indispensable for understanding the full landscape of [[heavy-metals]] toxicity. Its IARC carcinogenicity classification table (Table 1) and its detailed regulatory threshold tables for each metal across WHO, OSHA, EPA, NIOSH, and FDA provide an authoritative regulatory reference framework.

## Key Claims

- Environmental pollution by [[heavy-metals]] is increasingly a global concern due to rapid industrialization, improper waste disposal, fertilizers, and pesticides
- Heavy metals enter humans through four routes: ingestion of contaminated food, inhalation from atmosphere, drinking contaminated water, and skin contact from agriculture/pharmaceutical/industrial/residential areas
- Metals are non-biodegradable and bioaccumulate -- organisms detoxify by hiding active elements within proteins or depositing them in intracellular granules in insoluble form
- Essential elements follow a dose-response curve: deficiency causes death at low concentrations, optimal performance at moderate levels, toxicity and death at high concentrations (Figure 2)
- Heavy metal toxicity causes damage at multiple molecular targets: DNA (damage and repair inhibition), lipids (cell membrane damage), proteins (misfolding, aggregation, conformational change, enzyme activation), and the nervous system (neurotransmitter inhibition, neuron damage) -- leading to [[carcinogenesis]], cell damage, and cellular function loss (Figure 3)
- Metal-induced [[oxidative-stress]] operates through Fenton reactions generating hydroxyl radicals, superoxide, and hydrogen peroxide -- iron, copper, vanadium, chromium, and cobalt follow this pathway (Figures 4-7)
- Cadmium, [[nickel]], and [[arsenic]] are specifically noted to inhibit DNA repair mechanisms
- Protection against metal-induced free radical damage is provided by enzymatic and non-enzymatic antioxidants including glutathione (thiol compounds) and vitamin E

## Metal-by-Metal Summary of Key Toxicological Findings

### Aluminium
- No known biological function; competes with cations especially magnesium; binds citrate and transferrin in blood
- Gut bioavailability increased by citrate; enhanced by parathyroid hormone and vitamin D
- Target organs: lungs, central nervous system, bone
- Causes osteomalacia (seen in antacid users and dialysis patients), neurotoxicity via neurofilamentous aggregates
- Linked to [[Alzheimers Disease]] and ALS-PDC (Amyotrophic Lateral Sclerosis-Parkinsonism Dementia Complex)
- Affects blood-brain barrier permeability, cholinergic activity, lipid peroxidation, glutamate nitric oxide-cyclic GMP pathway

### Nickel
- Absorbed as lipophilic compound with low molecular weight through GI tract; gut ligands affect absorption
- Accumulates in lungs, thyroid, adrenal glands, brain, kidneys, heart, liver, spleen, pancreas
- NF-kB activation mediates inflammatory process alongside apoptosis; ATF-1 (ATF/CREB family) promotes angiogenesis via TSP1 diminution
- Carcinogenic mechanisms include regulation of long non-coding RNAs, miRNAs, methylation of MEG3 promoter, HIF-1alpha upregulation
- [[Contact Dermatitis]] is the most common health effect; also lung fibrosis, lung and nasal cancer

### Cadmium
- 64th most abundant metal; found with zinc; uses include batteries, fertilizers, pigments, plastics
- Kidneys primarily affected causing nephrotoxicity; also reproductive system failure, calcium metabolism alterations, bone fracture, immune deficiencies, cancer
- DNA damage is genotoxic and ecotoxic in animals
- Food sources: shellfish, mussels, dried seaweed, mushrooms

### Arsenic
- Exists in three allotropic forms; 55th most abundant; used in wood preservation, glass, insecticides, pyrotechnics
- Inorganic arsenic causes gastro-intestinal irritation, lung irritation, skin changes, decreased blood cell production
- Organic arsenic may cause stomach upsets and nerve damage but does not affect DNA
- Absorbed through ingestion primarily; inhalation and dermal routes less significant

### Chromium
- Cr(VI) is more toxic than Cr(III) due to higher redox potential and ability to enter cells as tetrahedral chromate anion
- Oral Cr(VI) causes gastrointestinal ulceration, nausea, vomiting, fever, diarrhea, vertigo, toxic nephritis, liver damage, death at 1-3g
- Carcinogenic only following inhalation, not through ingestion or skin contact
- Causes allergic contact dermatitis, gingivitis, bronchitis, lung cancer, chrome holes in skin

### Lead
- Causes hypertension, miscarriages, premature births, stillbirths, renal impairment, abdominal pain, brain damage
- In children: reduced intelligence, decline in educational achievement, reduced attention span, increased anti-social behavior
- More acidic soil increases leaching; lead-contaminated gasoline historically significant source

### Mercury
- 66th most abundant; only liquid metal at room temperature
- Causes Minamata disease (neurological disorder from methylmercury); learning disabilities, speech defects, memory loss, tremors, paralysis, coma, death
- Genotoxic and teratogenic

## Environmental Transport and Fate

- **Soil pollution**: Wastewater irrigation, pesticides, animal manures, mine waste, sewage sludge, coal combustion residues
- **Water pollution**: Industrial effluents, atmospheric deposition, acid mine drainage; metal solubility depends on pH
- **Air pollution**: Combustion, industrial processes, volcanic activity; particulate matter can travel long distances as gaseous state
- **Food chain bioaccumulation**: Heavy metals increase in toxicity as they move through trophic levels; sewage sludge on farmland introduces arsenic, copper, lead, cadmium, chromium into soil and crops

## Regulatory Thresholds

The paper provides detailed regulatory threshold tables for each metal across multiple agencies (WHO, OSHA, NIOSH, EPA, FDA) for air, workplace air, drinking water, and bottled water -- a critical reference for exposure assessment. Notable thresholds for [[nickel]]:
- WHO Air Quality Guidelines: 3.8 x 10^-4 ug/m3
- OSHA workplace: 1 mg/m3 for 8-hour workday
- WHO drinking water: 0.02 mg/L
- EPA drinking water: 0.1 mg/L
