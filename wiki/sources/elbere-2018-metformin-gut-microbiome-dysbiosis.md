# Metformin and Gut Microbiome Dysbiosis in Healthy Volunteers

## Key Findings

- **Significant reduction in inner diversity** (alpha diversity) of gut microbiota was observed as early as 24 hours after the first metformin dose in healthy individuals, demonstrating the drug's rapid impact on the gut ecosystem.
- **Escherichia-Shigella** abundance increased after metformin treatment, indicating that the drug selectively promotes gram-negative, potentially pro-inflammatory taxa.
- Metformin has an **immediate and sustained effect** on the healthy human gut microbiome, with changes persisting throughout the 7-day treatment period.
- The findings suggest that some of metformin's therapeutic effects (and its gastrointestinal side effects) may be mediated through the gut microbiome rather than through direct pharmacological action alone.
- Individual variation in microbiome response to metformin was substantial, suggesting that baseline microbial composition may predict drug tolerance.

## Methods

**Exploratory longitudinal study** of 18 healthy individuals (no diabetes, no prior metformin use) treated with metformin (850 mg twice daily) for 7 days. Fecal samples were collected at baseline (day 0), day 1, day 4, and day 7. Gut microbiota composition was assessed by **16S rRNA gene sequencing** (V3-V4 region). Alpha diversity (Shannon index, observed OTUs) and beta diversity (weighted and unweighted UniFrac) were calculated. Differential abundance analysis was performed at genus level.

## Detailed Summary

Elbere et al. conducted one of the few studies examining metformin's effects on the gut microbiome of healthy individuals, removing the confounding effects of diabetes, hyperglycemia, and other medications. The key insight was that metformin rapidly and substantially alters gut microbial composition even in the absence of metabolic disease.

The reduction in alpha diversity was evident within 24 hours — an unusually fast perturbation timeline for a pharmaceutical agent. By day 7, the microbiome composition had shifted significantly from baseline, with the most notable change being the expansion of Escherichia-Shigella. This genus includes species capable of producing uremic toxins (indole, p-cresol precursors) and lipopolysaccharide (LPS), raising questions about whether metformin-induced dysbiosis could contribute to gut barrier dysfunction in susceptible individuals.

The study is relevant to CKD because metformin is commonly prescribed to type 2 diabetes patients, many of whom have or will develop CKD. The metformin-induced expansion of Escherichia-Shigella — a genus already enriched in CKD patients — could theoretically compound the existing dysbiosis in patients with both conditions. Additionally, metformin is typically contraindicated in advanced CKD due to lactic acidosis risk, but it remains widely used in early-stage CKD with diabetes.

## Relevance to WikiBiome

This study contributes to WikiBiome's understanding of **pharmacomicrobiomics** — how commonly prescribed drugs reshape the gut ecosystem. The finding that metformin selects for Escherichia-Shigella connects to the metals-as-selective-pressures framework: Escherichia species are iron-dependent organisms with robust siderophore systems, and their enrichment in both metformin-treated and CKD patient guts may reflect convergent selection pressures favoring iron-pirating taxa.

The rapid (24-hour) onset of dysbiosis also illustrates how pharmaceutical interventions can act as acute ecological perturbations, analogous to metal exposure events that rapidly reshape microbial community structure.

## Open Questions

- Does metformin-induced enrichment of Escherichia-Shigella increase uremic toxin production in CKD patients with residual renal function?
- Do CKD patients on metformin show accelerated gut dysbiosis compared to CKD patients not on metformin?
- Could co-administration of specific probiotics mitigate metformin-induced dysbiosis while preserving its glucose-lowering effects?
