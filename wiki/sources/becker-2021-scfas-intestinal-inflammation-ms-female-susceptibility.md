# Short-Chain Fatty Acids and Intestinal Inflammation in Multiple Sclerosis: Modulation of Female Susceptibility by Microbial Products?

## Summary

This case-control study investigated fecal [[short-chain-fatty-acids]] and fecal calprotectin (intestinal inflammation marker) in 41 RRMS patients and 35 age-matched controls from Germany.

## Key Findings

### Fecal Calprotectin
- No significant difference in fecal calprotectin between RRMS patients (median: 19 microg/g) and controls (median: 19 microg/g)
- Both groups within normal range, suggesting no active intestinal [[inflammation]] in this MS cohort
- Most patients were on immunotherapy, which may have affected results

### Fecal SCFA Concentrations
- SCFA concentrations were descriptively lower in RRMS patients vs controls (not statistically significant overall)
- Median fecal butyrate reduced by 77% in RRMS patients
- Median fecal acetate reduced by 72% in active/highly active RRMS
- All fecal SCFA concentrations showed significant negative correlation with age in controls

### Sex-Associated Differences
- **Female subjects showed significantly lower SCFA concentrations compared to males** across all SCFAs except isobutyrate and isovalerate
- In the control group: acetate (p=0.005), propionate (p=0.004), and butyrate (p=0.016) were all significantly lower in women vs. men
- This sex difference was also observed descriptively in RRMS patients
- RRMS patients did not show the normal age-dependent decline in SCFAs seen in controls

### Clinical Correlations
- Branched SCFAs (isobutyrate and isovalerate) were nominally inversely correlated with EDSS disability
- No significant correlation between EDSS and other fecal SCFA concentrations

## Relevance to Gut-Metal-Microbiome Axis

This study contributes to the [[gut-metal-microbiome]] model by:

1. **Sex-specific SCFA differences** may partially explain female predominance in MS (3:1 ratio); if women have baseline lower SCFAs, they may be more vulnerable to the additional SCFA depletion caused by metal-induced [[dysbiosis]]
2. **Butyrate reduction** (77% lower) in MS aligns with depletion of metal-sensitive Clostridia
3. **Immunotherapy confounding** - the lack of calprotectin elevation may reflect treatment-mediated suppression of intestinal inflammation, while the underlying [[dysbiosis]] (potentially metal-driven) persists
4. **Age-SCFA correlation loss** in MS patients suggests disease-driven disruption of normal microbiome aging patterns

## Limitations

- Small sample size
- Most patients on immunotherapy (potential confounding)
- Diet not assessed
- Fecal SCFAs may not fully reflect colonic production
- Non-significant primary outcome
