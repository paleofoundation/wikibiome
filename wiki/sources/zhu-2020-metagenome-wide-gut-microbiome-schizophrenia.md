# Metagenome-wide association of gut microbiome features for schizophrenia

## Summary

This landmark study explored the fecal microbiome of 90 medication-free schizophrenia patients and 81 controls using shotgun metagenomic sequencing. A microbial species classifier distinguished patients from controls with AUC = 0.896, replicated in a validation cohort (AUC = 0.765). Functional pathways associated with schizophrenia include differences in [[short-chain-fatty-acids]] synthesis, [[tryptophan-metabolism]], and neurotransmitter synthesis/degradation.

## Key Findings

- 26 gut mOTUs from random forest classifier distinguished SZ from controls (AUC = 0.896)
- Classifier replicated in independent cohort: AUC = 0.765
- Higher abundance of tryptophan metabolism modules in SZ gut microbiota
- Lower serum tryptophan and higher kynurenic acid (KYNA) levels in SZ patients
- Serum tryptophan negatively correlated with 38 SZ-enriched bacterial species
- Serum KYNA positively correlated with 10 SZ-enriched and negatively with 3 control-enriched species
- Functional potentials included altered [[short-chain-fatty-acids]] synthesis and neurotransmitter pathways
- *Streptococcus vestibularis* transplantation to mice induced social behavior deficits and altered neurotransmitter levels
- 11 bacterial species significantly enriched in SZ were associated with symptom severity, cognitive performance, and diagnosis

## Relevance to Gut-Metal-Microbiome Axis

This is one of the most comprehensive metagenomic studies of the schizophrenia gut microbiome. The altered [[tryptophan-metabolism]] -- increased microbial tryptophan catabolism leading to elevated KYNA and depleted serotonin -- directly links to cognitive deficits via NMDA receptor antagonism. The depletion of [[short-chain-fatty-acids]] biosynthesis pathways aligns with reduced butyrate-producing genera. Heavy metals like [[lead]] and [[arsenic]] could exacerbate these metabolic shifts by selectively inhibiting metal-dependent enzymes in tryptophan and SCFA pathways. The causal evidence from FMT and *S. vestibularis* transplantation strengthens the argument that [[dysbiosis]] drives schizophrenia symptoms through the [[gut-brain-axis]].

## Limitations

- Chinese population may limit generalizability
- Cross-sectional design
- Medication-free status may represent a specific subpopulation
- Follow-up cohort was smaller with lower AUC
