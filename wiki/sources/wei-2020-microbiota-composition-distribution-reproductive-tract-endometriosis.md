# Key Claims

- A continuous change in microbiota distribution was identified along the reproductive tract in [[endometriosis]], with diversity increasing from lower to upper tract
- *Lactobacillus* was reduced in the cervical mucus of endometriosis patients, with signature OTUs gradually increasing from lower to upper reproductive tract
- *Sphingobium* sp. (29) and *Pseudomonas viridiflava* (327) were significantly enriched in endometrium (ET) and peritoneal fluid (PF) of endometriosis patients
- Seven microbiota community types were identified across five reproductive tract sites
- The proportion of non-*Lactobacillus* dominated microbiota significantly increased in cervical samples of endometriosis patients

# Methods/Findings

- **Study design**: Case-control study, Peking University Shenzhen Hospital
- **Participants**: 36 endometriosis patients and 14 controls undergoing laparoscopic surgery
- **Sample sites**: Lower vagina (CL), posterior vaginal fornix (CU), cervical mucus (CV), endometrium (ET), peritoneal fluid (PF)
- **Methods**: 16S rRNA V4-V5 Ion Torrent PGM sequencing
- **Key results**:
  - 237 samples classified into 7 community types
  - Type II (*Lactobacillus* dominant) reduced in CV of EMS patients
  - Type IV and V (non-*Lactobacillus*) increased in upper reproductive tract
  - Signature OTUs identified at each site with p<0.05 by Wilcoxon rank sum
  - *Comamonas* (type VII) appeared only in PF samples

# Relevance to Wiki

This is the first study to comprehensively map microbiota composition across all five sites of the female reproductive tract in [[endometriosis]] patients. The gradient of increasing diversity and decreasing *Lactobacillus* from lower to upper tract suggests ascending microbial colonization, directly supporting the [[bacterial-contamination-hypothesis]]. The identification of *Sphingobium* and *Pseudomonas viridiflava* as endometriosis-specific species in the upper reproductive tract points to specific bacterial candidates that may drive peritoneal inflammation.
