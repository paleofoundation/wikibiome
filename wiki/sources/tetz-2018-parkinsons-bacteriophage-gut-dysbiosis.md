# Bacteriophage and Gut Dysbiosis in Parkinson's Disease

## Summary

This seminal study demonstrates that lytic [[bacteriophages]] targeting lactic acid bacteria are significantly enriched in the gut of [[parkinsons-disease]] patients. It proposes a phage-driven model of gut [[dysbiosis]] wherein bacteriophage predation selectively depletes beneficial bacterial populations, contributing to the microbial imbalance characteristic of PD.

## Key Findings

- Lytic phages targeting *[[lactococcus]]* were >10-fold enriched in PD gut microbiomes
- *Lactococcus* bacterial abundance was correspondingly decreased in PD patients
- Phage enrichment correlated with disease severity markers
- The phage-bacterial predator-prey dynamic offers a mechanistic explanation for selective bacterial depletion in PD
- Phage-mediated dysbiosis preceded measurable bacterial community shifts in some cases
- The gut phageome may represent a novel biomarker and therapeutic target in PD

## Relevance to Gut-Metal-Microbiome Axis

This is a foundational paper for understanding how [[bacteriophages]] drive bacterial [[dysbiosis]] in [[parkinsons-disease]]. *Lactococcus* and other lactic acid bacteria are key producers of [[short-chain-fatty-acids]] and contribute to metal homeostasis by binding dietary metals and maintaining gut barrier integrity. Their phage-mediated depletion removes this metal-buffering capacity, leading to increased free [[iron]], [[zinc]], and [[manganese]] availability for metal-tolerant pathogens. The phage-dysbiosis-metal axis represents a critical but underexplored pathway in PD pathogenesis.

## Limitations

- Relatively small sample size
- Cross-sectional design
- Phage taxonomy and characterization were limited by available databases
- Medication confounders (particularly levodopa)
- Replication in larger independent cohorts needed
