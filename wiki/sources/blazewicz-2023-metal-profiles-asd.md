# Key Claims

- A primary pathology of ASD may be a **trace metal imbalance** characterized by either the presence of toxic metals and/or the overload or lack of essential metals, particularly a **lack of zinc during brain development (pregnancy)**
- Rather than individual metals, a unique **metal profile (metallome)** is linked to ASD behavioral traits -- changes in one metal trigger parallel changes in others
- **Toxic metals (Pb, Hg, Cd)** and essential metal deficiency (especially Zn) may be **two sides of the same coin**: toxic metals compete with zinc for binding sites, effectively creating functional zinc deficiency
- **Pathomechanisms of toxic metal overload in ASD**:
  1. **Oxidative stress and mitochondrial dysfunction**: Hg inhibits GSH, increases ROS; Cd disrupts thiol groups; Pb affects ALA dehydrase
  2. **Neuroinflammation**: Pb and Hg trigger glial reactivity, increase TNF, IL-1, IL-6; autoantibodies against neuronal proteins
  3. **Axonal demyelination**: Pb taken up by oligodendrocytes reduces CNPase activity; Cd damages oligodendrocyte progenitors; MeHg downregulates MBP
  4. **Competition with essential metals (Zn, Ca)**: ~10% of human genome encodes zinc-binding proteins; Pb and Hg compete for same binding sites
- **Zinc deficiency** during pregnancy causes ASD-like behavior in mice; prenatal zinc therapy prevents VPA-induced ASD-like behaviors
- Zinc regulates key **synaptic ASD-associated pathways**: NLGN-NRXN-SHANK and mTOR/PI3K, both modified by zinc and calcium and affected by toxic metals
- Multiple ASD candidate genes encode proteins involved in metal transport: COMMD1 (copper), MTF1 (metal regulatory transcription), SLC30A5 (zinc transporter)
- The **exposome concept** -- totality of environmental exposures from conception onward -- provides the appropriate framework for studying metals in ASD
- **Chemical speciation** of metals (oxidation state, molecular form) is critically understudied in ASD and may determine bioavailability and toxicity

# Methods/Findings

- **Design**: Comprehensive narrative review
- **Epidemiological evidence table**: 25+ studies across hair, blood, urine, teeth samples consistently showing:
  - Pb elevated in ASD (hair, blood, teeth, nails)
  - Hg elevated in ASD (blood, urine, hair, teeth)
  - Cd elevated in ASD (hair, urine)
  - Zn decreased in ASD (hair, most consistent finding)
  - Cu variable (some elevated, some decreased in ASD)
- **Key pathomechanisms reviewed in depth**:
  - BBB permeability to toxic metals (developing BBB is particularly vulnerable)
  - Oxidative stress: increased ROS, depleted GSH, mitochondrial damage
  - Neuroinflammation: microglial activation, proinflammatory cytokines, autoantibodies
  - Demyelination: oligodendrocyte damage by Pb, Cd, MeHg
  - Zinc competition: toxic metals displace Zn from metalloenzymes, transcription factors, synaptic proteins
- **Model proposed**: Metal profiles (not isolated metals) should be studied; presence of heavy metals + zinc deficiency during pregnancy = consolidated non-genetic ASD risk factor
- **Research gaps identified**: Limited metal speciation studies in ASD; need for metallomics/ionomics approaches; limited PET imaging of metals in ASD (vs Alzheimer's)
