# Effects of Dietary Fat on Gut Microbiome and Ovarian Cancer in Mouse Model

## Key Findings

- **Ketogenic and high-fat diets accelerated epithelial ovarian cancer (EOC) tumor growth** compared to a low-fat/high-carbohydrate (LF/HC) diet in a KPCA mouse model ([[alhilli-2025-dietary-fat-gut-microbiome-ovarian-cancer-mouse]], animal-model).
- **Gut microbiome diversity was markedly reduced** in KD and HF/LC-fed mice, with diversity loss correlating with tumor growth rate.
- **LF/HC-fed mice maintained higher microbial diversity** and harbored distinct protective taxa.
- **Polyamine biosynthesis and fatty acid oxidation pathways were enriched** in KD mice, suggesting these metabolic shifts may support tumor progression.
- **[[akkermansia-muciniphila]] and [[bacteroides-vulgatus]]** showed differential abundance across diet groups, linking specific taxa to diet-cancer interactions.

## Methods

Animal model study using KPCA (K-ras/Pten/Apc-driven) ovarian cancer mice assigned to three dietary groups: ketogenic diet (KD), high-fat/low-carbohydrate (HF/LC), and low-fat/high-carbohydrate (LF/HC). Gut microbiome composition assessed by shotgun metagenomics. Functional pathway analysis performed on metagenomic data. Tumor growth monitored over the study period. ## Detailed Summary

This study investigates how dietary macronutrient composition reshapes the gut microbiome and, in turn, influences ovarian cancer progression. The authors used a genetically defined mouse model (KPCA) that recapitulates key features of human epithelial ovarian cancer.

**Diet-driven tumor acceleration.** Both high-fat dietary patterns (KD and HF/LC) significantly accelerated tumor growth compared to the LF/HC control diet. This challenges the popular narrative around ketogenic diets as anti-cancer interventions and suggests that in the context of ovarian cancer, high dietary fat — regardless of carbohydrate content — promotes tumor progression.

**Microbiome collapse.** Shotgun metagenomic sequencing revealed that KD and HF/LC diets caused substantial reduction in microbial diversity. The diversity loss was not merely coincidental — it correlated quantitatively with tumor growth, suggesting a mechanistic link between diet-induced [[dysbiosis]] and cancer progression. The LF/HC group maintained a more diverse and functionally distinct microbiome.

**Functional pathway shifts.** In KD-fed mice, metagenomic analysis revealed enrichment of polyamine biosynthesis pathways and fatty acid oxidation capacity. Polyamines (putrescine, spermidine, spermine) are known to promote cell proliferation and have been implicated in tumor growth across multiple cancer types. The enrichment of these pathways in the dysbiotic KD microbiome suggests that microbial metabolic output directly supports the tumor.

**Iron metabolism connections.** The study touches on [[iron]]-related metabolic pathways altered by dietary fat, connecting to the broader metallomics framework. Fatty acid oxidation pathway enrichment is also relevant to [[ferroptosis]] — iron-dependent lipid peroxidation cell death — though the study does not explore this connection directly.

## Open Questions

- Do these findings translate to human ovarian cancer patients, or are they specific to the KPCA mouse model?
- Which specific taxa are responsible for the polyamine biosynthesis enrichment in KD-fed mice?
- Does the microbiome mediate the tumor-promoting effect of high-fat diets, or is it a parallel consequence of the same dietary exposure?
- How do iron-related pathway changes interact with the ferroptosis sensitivity of ovarian cancer cells?
