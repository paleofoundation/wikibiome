# Key Claims

- Prenatal lead (Pb) exposure during pregnancy is negatively associated with the gut microbiome composition in children at age 9-11, with consistent effects across both second and third trimester exposures
- The weighted quantile sum (WQS) regression showed negative overall mixture effects (beta=-0.17 for both trimesters), with Ruminococcus gnavus, Bifidobacterium longum, Alistipes indistinctus, Bacteroides caccae, and Bifidobacterium bifidum having the highest importance weights
- Bacteroides caccae, a common fiber degrader, showed decreased abundance with Pb exposure across multiple studies from different populations, representing the most reproducible finding
- Prenatal Pb may alter the trajectory of immune system development and gut microbial composition through the life course, potentially via maternal gut microbiome transfer at birth

# Methods/Findings

- **Study type**: Prospective birth cohort (PROGRESS study, Mexico City); 123 children aged 9-11 years
- **Exposure**: Maternal whole blood Pb by ICP-MS at 2nd trimester (mean 33.6 ug/L) and 3rd trimester (mean 34.9 ug/L)
- **Microbiome**: Shotgun metagenomic sequencing of stool samples
- **Alpha/beta diversity**: Slight negative associations (not statistically significant); 3rd trimester Pb associated with beta diversity R^2=1.1% (p=0.066)
- **WQS results**: Both trimesters showed negative associations; >80% of repeated holdout estimates below zero
- **Individual taxa** (TWAS): 2nd trimester -- Alistipes putredinis, Ruminococcus gnavus, Bacteroides caccae negatively associated; B. coprocola positively associated. 3rd trimester -- Bifidobacterium bifidum, B. longum, A. indistinctus negatively; B. coprocola, Eubacterium eligens, B. finegoldii positively
- **Gene function pathways**: 2nd trimester associated with nucleic acid biosynthesis; 3rd trimester with amino acid biosynthesis; shared pathways included adenosine/guanosine ribonucleotide biosynthesis
