# Gut Mycobiome Dysbiosis and Hypertension

## Key Findings

- **Pre-hypertensive and hypertensive groups showed distinct gut mycobiome profiles** compared to normotensive controls, with significant differences in fungal community composition.
- **[[malassezia]] relative abundance was increased** in the hypertension group, suggesting this typically skin-associated fungal genus may play a role in systemic immune activation relevant to blood pressure regulation.
- **Malassezia abundance positively correlated with serum immunoglobulin light chain concentrations**, linking fungal dysbiosis to B-cell hyperactivation and immune complex formation — mechanisms relevant to both hypertension and [[chronic-kidney-disease]] progression.
- **Mortierella** was among the differentially abundant fungi, though its functional significance in hypertension pathogenesis is not well established.
- The findings suggest the **gut mycobiome may serve as a predictive biomarker** for hypertension development.

## Methods

- **Design**: Cross-sectional study with three comparison groups (normotension, pre-hypertension, hypertension).
- **Mycobiome profiling**: ITS (Internal Transcribed Spacer) sequencing of fecal samples for fungal community characterization.
- **Immunological assessment**: Immunoturbidimetric assay for serum immunoglobulin light chain (kappa and lambda) quantification.
- **Statistical analysis**: Differential abundance testing across blood pressure categories, correlation analysis between fungal taxa and immunological markers.

## Detailed Summary

This study investigated the gut mycobiome as a potential contributor to hypertension pathogenesis. Hypertension is both a cause and consequence of [[chronic-kidney-disease]], creating a bidirectional relationship that gut microbial ecology may modulate. By profiling fungal communities via ITS sequencing across three blood pressure categories, the authors identified significant mycobiome dysbiosis in hypertensive individuals. The enrichment of [[malassezia]] — a lipophilic fungus typically associated with skin conditions (dandruff, seborrheic dermatitis) — in the gut of hypertensive patients is a novel finding. Malassezia species are known to produce lipase and phospholipase enzymes that can trigger inflammatory cascades, and their positive correlation with immunoglobulin light chains suggests a mechanism by which fungal antigens drive systemic immune activation. The immunoglobulin light chain connection is particularly relevant to CKD, where free light chain clearance is impaired and their accumulation may contribute to both cardiovascular and renal damage.

## Relevance to WikiBiome

Extends the mycobiome-disease axis to hypertension, a major CKD comorbidity and risk factor. The [[malassezia]] finding adds an unexpected player to the gut mycobiome in cardiovascular-renal disease. The immunoglobulin light chain correlation provides a mechanistic bridge between fungal dysbiosis and immune-mediated organ damage, relevant to the interkingdom relationships concept.

## Open Questions

- Whether gut Malassezia colonization is a cause or consequence of hypertension.
- How gut Malassezia relates to skin Malassezia — whether gut colonization represents translocation from skin or an independent ecological niche.
- Whether antifungal therapy targeting gut Malassezia could lower blood pressure or slow CKD progression in hypertensive patients.
