# Role of Food and Nutrition in Pathogenesis and Prevention of Hashimoto's Thyroiditis

## Key Claims

- The diet of patients with [[hashimotos-thyroiditis]] should be based on increased content of complete protein, polyunsaturated [[short-chain-fatty-acids|fatty acids]], and complex carbohydrates with a low glycaemic index
- [[dietary-fiber]] intake of approximately 30 g per day in women with Hashimoto's thyroiditis increases the rate of weight loss and improves the efficacy of nutritional therapy
- Essential nutrients for thyroid function include [[iodine]], [[iron]], [[selenium]], [[zinc]], and vitamins A, D, E, B2, B3, and B12, and their supply is important due to their impact on secretory activity of the thyroid
- Excess [[iodine]] in patients with Hashimoto's can lead to breakdown in tolerance mechanisms and exacerbation of inflammatory response against thyroid antigens
- There is a relationship between [[vitamin-d-supplementation]] deficiency and the risk of development of [[thyroid-autoimmunity|autoimmune thyroiditis]], with significantly lower 25(OH)D3 concentrations in HT patients compared to controls
- Foods to limit include dairy (contains casein with antigenic properties and gluten), goitrogen-rich Brassicaceae vegetables (contain glucosinolates blocking iodine uptake), and heavily processed products

## Methods/Findings

- **Study type**: Narrative review
- **Scope**: Examination of food ingredients that support thyroid gland activity and those that should be limited in Hashimoto's thyroiditis
- **Iron**: Ingredient of [[thyroid-autoimmunity|thyroid peroxidase]] (TPO); deficiency reduces synthesis of thyroid hormones through reduced T4 to T3 conversion and contributes to increased TSH secretion
- **Selenium**: Acts as antioxidant via selenoenzymes; proper dosing can reduce anti-TPO concentration and reduce frequency of chronic thyroiditis and risk of permanent hypothyroidism
- **Zinc**: Lower blood zinc concentration correlates with higher anti-thyroid antibodies; component of receptor proteins T3; deficiency impairs hormone binding and reduces T3 and T4 concentrations
- **Vitamin D**: Studies confirm significantly lower 25(OH)D3 in HT patients; involved in calcium-phosphate metabolism regulation and immunomodulatory functions
- **Dietary recommendations**: Holistic approach combining hormone supplementation with dietary modifications and supplementation of necessary mineral nutrients and vitamins
