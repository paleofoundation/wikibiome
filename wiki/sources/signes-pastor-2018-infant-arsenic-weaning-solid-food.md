## Key Findings

- **Weaning to solids significantly increases infant arsenic exposure**: paired pre-post weaning urine samples (15 infants) showed statistically significant increases in urinary i-As (p=0.04), MMA (p=0.002), DMA (p=0.01), and the sum of arsenic species (p=0.01) from 4 to 6 months. 1.5-, 5.8-, 2.4-, and 3.8-fold increases respectively. AsB did not differ (remains from fish/marine sources via breastmilk).
- **Rice cereal is the dominant contributor** to infant arsenic burden — consumption correlated with i-As (ρ=0.90, p=0.03), sum species (ρ=0.90), MMA (ρ=0.86, p=0.01), and DMA (ρ=0.86, p=0.01).
- **Fruits and vegetables also contribute** (ρ=0.70, 0.80 respectively for sum species).
- **Breastfeeding is protective**: inversely associated with urinary As species at 6 months (ρ=-0.71, p=0.003) — prior New Hampshire cohort found 0.31 µg/L in breastmilk vs 20-fold higher in reconstituted formula.
- Formula-fed infants had higher urinary i-As at 4 months than exclusively breastfed (1.6, 1.4, 4.2, 4.9-fold higher median for i-As, MMA, DMA, AsB).

## Methods

15 infants (8 female, 7 male) from the New Hampshire Birth Cohort Study. Paired urine samples at 4 months (pre-weaning) and 6 months (during weaning). 3-day food diary documenting breast milk, formula, rice cereal, other cereals, fruits, vegetables, yogurt, meats. Urinary arsenic species measured by HPLC-ICP-MS with NIST 1640a water reference. Paired Wilcoxon signed-rank tests; Spearman correlation with dietary intake.

## Relevance to Cureva

- Early-life arsenic exposure programs long-term disease risk (DNA methylation studies).
- Potential STOP consideration for infant rice cereal as first complementary food.
- Dietary intervention: delaying rice cereal introduction or preferring non-rice grains.

## Contradictions / Tensions

- Small sample (n=15) with wide variability; findings need replication in larger cohorts.
- Paddy-managed soils cause DMA and MMA to be enriched in certain rice varieties; organic form concentrations not fully characterized.

## Open Questions

- Long-term consequences of elevated infant urinary As on microbiome development and later disease.
- Whether brown rice cereal (with higher As) vs white rice cereal differs in bioavailability for infants.
