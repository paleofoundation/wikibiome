# Bacteriome, Mycobiome and Metabolome Alterations in PCOS

## Key Findings

- **Metabolite-derived diagnostic models outperform microbe-based models** for discriminating PCOS from healthy controls, suggesting that functional metabolic output is more consistently altered than taxonomic composition ([[yin-2022-bacteriome-mycobiome-metabolome-pcos]], cross-sectional).
- **Hyperandrogenemia plays a central role** in driving dysbiosis of the intestinal microecology in PCOS, establishing a hormone-microbiome feedback loop.
- **Fungal genus [[mortierella]] is uniquely enriched** in PCOS patients — a novel interkingdom finding not previously reported in PCOS microbiome studies.
- **Multi-kingdom dysbiosis**: PCOS involves simultaneous alterations in bacterial (bacteriome), fungal (mycobiome), and metabolic profiles, demonstrating that single-kingdom analysis is insufficient.
- **[[prevotella]] abundance was altered** in PCOS patients, consistent with its role in hormonal and metabolic dysregulation.

## Methods

- **Study design**: Cross-sectional multi-omics study
- **Population**: PCOS patients with normal weight and overweight individuals, compared to healthy controls
- **Key measurements**: 16S rRNA sequencing (bacteriome); ITS2 sequencing (mycobiome); untargeted metabolomics; machine learning classifiers (random forest) for diagnostic discrimination
- **Distinguishing feature**: Simultaneous characterization of bacterial, fungal, and metabolomic profiles in the same cohort — rare in PCOS research

## Detailed Summary

This study took a multi-omics approach to characterizing the intestinal microecology of PCOS, simultaneously profiling bacteria (16S rRNA), fungi (ITS2 sequencing), and metabolites in patients with and without obesity. The most striking finding was the superiority of metabolite-based classifiers over taxonomy-based classifiers for PCOS diagnosis, suggesting that while individual taxa may vary between patients, the functional metabolic output of the dysbiotic community is more consistent. The enrichment of the fungal genus **[[mortierella]]** in PCOS patients is a notable interkingdom discovery. Mortierella species are known producers of arachidonic acid and other polyunsaturated fatty acids, which could influence inflammatory and hormonal signaling. This fungal enrichment, combined with bacterial shifts including **[[prevotella]]** abundance changes, demonstrates that PCOS-associated dysbiosis is a multi-kingdom phenomenon involving bacterial-fungal interactions. The study also linked hyperandrogenemia to the observed microbial changes, proposing that elevated androgens reshape the gut environment to favor certain microbial communities, which in turn may amplify hormonal imbalance through metabolic feedback.
