## Key Findings

- **27 virus species** have been detected in human semen in studies published over the last 5 years, with HIV being the most frequently studied, followed by Zika virus, Ebola virus, HPV, and HCMV ([[de-albuquerque-2022-human-seminal-virome-panel]], cross-sectional literature review, n=175 articles).
- Of these 27 viruses, **13 were associated with abnormalities in seminal parameters** including decreased motility, concentration, morphology, DNA fragmentation, and increased reactive oxygen species (ROS).
- The authors propose a **diagnostic viral panel** for infertile men composed of both DNA viruses (HPV, HBV, HSV-1/2, HCMV, HHV-6, VZV) and RNA viruses (HIV, ZIKV, SARS-CoV-2, HCV, HEV, EBOV).
- **Ebola virus disease (EVD) survivors** commonly report sexual health complaints including **erectile dysfunction and decreased libido**, with viral RNA persisting in semen for up to 470 days post-illness.
- **SARS-CoV-2** was absent from testes in 90% of cases, but all patients exhibited significant seminiferous tubular injury, reduced Leydig cells, and mild lymphocytic inflammation.

## Methods

- **Study design:** Narrative literature review of the human seminal virome.
- **Search strategy:** PubMed database searched using virology/virus AND semen/sperm/seminal terms. All studies published up to May 8, 2021, initially identified without language restriction.
- **Screening:** 4,239 records identified; filtered to 1,030 published in the last 5 years (2016 onward); 257 full-text articles assessed for eligibility; 175 articles included after excluding 75 unrelated and 7 unavailable.
- **Inclusion criteria:** Articles discussing the presence of viruses in semen by isolation, amplification, or detection of nucleic acids or specific antigens. Original clinical data only — reviews, meta-analyses, and publications lacking original data were excluded. *In vitro* and animal-model-only studies excluded.
- **Analysis:** 27 virus species cataloged with taxonomy, genome type, clinical presentation, sexual transmission status, and effects on male reproductive health.

## Detailed Summary

### Viruses with Established Impact on Seminal Parameters

**HIV:** The most studied virus in semen. HIV viral load in seminal fluid is generally lower than in blood, but shedding persists even during antiretroviral therapy. Semen alterations include decreased motility, sperm concentration, total sperm count, and ejaculate volume. Abnormal morphology and high percentage of sperm with DNA damage are reported. Decreased motility may relate to mitochondrial toxicity from nucleoside reverse transcriptase inhibitors. HIV prevalence is significantly higher in infertile men than in the general population.

**HPV:** Found throughout the male genital system — external genitalia, urethra, prostate, epididymis, vas deferens, testes, and semen. HPV interferes with sperm count, vitality, motility, morphology, pH, semen viscosity, and increases leukocyte-related DNA fragmentation and anti-sperm antibodies. HPV16 is the most frequent type (5.9% infertile vs. 4.7% general population). HPV-infected sperm serve as vectors for HPV transfer; penetration of HPV-infected sperm into oocytes delivers viral DNA intracellularly. ESHRE guidelines confirm negative impact on assisted reproduction outcomes — lower fertilization rates, increased abortions, and reduced clinical pregnancy rates (14.2% HPV-positive vs. 38.4% HPV-negative via ICSI).

**ZIKV:** Enters the testicular microenvironment, disrupts cellular metabolism, alters testicular physiology, and activates intense immune response causing severe testicular damage and infertility. Detected in semen up to 6 months post-infection. ZIKV preferentially infects spermatogonia, primary spermatocytes, and Sertoli cells, resulting in cell death and destruction of seminiferous tubules. Associated with oligospermia, hematospermia, painful ejaculation, and erectile dysfunction. Sperm head defects observed. Sexual transmission is possible and relevant to microcephaly prevention during pregnancy.

**EBOV:** The testis is an anatomical reservoir for EBOV persistence. Viral RNA detected in semen up to 470 days post-illness. EVD survivors commonly report **erectile dysfunction and decreased libido**. Sexual transmission from male survivors to female partners has been documented, prompting WHO to update guidelines for safe sex for 12 months post-symptom onset. The WHO recommends investigation of EBOV in semen as a preventive measure.

**SARS-CoV-2:** Studies show conflicting results. Some found SARS-CoV-2 RNA in semen of hospitalized patients, while others did not detect it. The virus was absent from testes in 90% of cases, but seminiferous tubular injury, reduced Leydig cells, swelling, vacuolization, tryptase-positive mast cells, and mild lymphocytic inflammation were observed. Male patients with COVID-19 showed reduced testosterone levels, suggesting hypogonadism. SARS-CoV-2 can activate cellular oxidative stress leading to sperm DNA fragmentation.

**HBV:** Reduces sperm concentration, motility, morphology, and viability. HBV integrates DNA into male germ cells, raising safety concerns for vertical transmission. HBV induces oxidative stress and reduces antioxidant capacity of sperm cells, leading to ROS generation, membrane integrity loss, mitochondrial damage, genome damage, and apoptosis. HBV infection associated with lower success rates in assisted reproduction and higher risk of low fertilization rate after IVF.

**HCV:** Negative impact on seminal quality — reduced total sperm count, motility, and morphology. Higher frequency of disomy for chromosomes 18, X, and Y. Reduced LH, FSH, and testosterone levels. Chronic hepatitis C patients with secondary infertility showed impaired progressive motility and sperm morphology. HCV can be transmitted during IVF; sequential semen preparation recommended.

**HSV-1/2:** 2–50% of infertile men positive for HSV-1/2. HSV-2 can internalize into healthy, motile sperm and cause direct damage to male germ cells. Associated with abnormal sperm parameters including hematospermia, oligospermia, and increased apoptotic cells. HSV-2 infection reduces seminal volume, abnormal viscosity, and indicates prostate dysfunction.

**HCMV:** Most frequently detected pathogen in semen of infertile patients in some studies (18.6% prevalence). Attaches to sperm surface and infects immature germ cells, producing direct gametotoxic effects. Contradictory findings — some studies show no association with sperm parameters, while others demonstrate significant reductions in sperm morphology and count.

**HHV-6:** Frequently found in semen, but effect on male fertility unclear. Notable because HHV-6 is the only human herpes virus that integrates into the germline — found on chromosomes close to telomeric ends, transmitted vertically from parents to children.

### Proposed Diagnostic Panel

The review proposes a panel of 13 viruses of medical importance for infertile men, divided into:

- **DNA viruses:** HPV, HBV, HSV-1/2, HCMV, HHV-6, VZV
- **RNA viruses:** HIV, ZIKV, SARS-CoV-2, HCV, HEV, EBOV

This panel is intended as a starting point for standardizing viral identification kits in semen quality assessment and fertility diagnostics.

### Additional Viruses Detected in Semen (Limited Evidence)

VZV, HHV-7, HHV-8, Lassa virus, Nipah virus, West Nile virus, AAV, Andes virus, Dengue virus, Yellow Fever virus, JC polyomavirus, Chikungunya virus, Rift Valley fever virus — detected in semen but with unknown or limited evidence of impact on reproductive parameters.

## Contradictions / Tensions

- **HCMV findings are contradictory:** Some studies (Bezold et al.) found no significant association between HCMV DNA and sperm parameters, while others (Jahromi et al.) observed significant reductions in sperm morphology and count. The review notes this tension without resolution.
- **SARS-CoV-2 in semen is contested:** Li et al. detected SARS-CoV-2 in semen of COVID-19 patients, while multiple other studies found no viral RNA in semen. The testicular damage observed without viral presence in testes suggests an indirect immune-mediated mechanism rather than direct viral infection.
- **HEV genotype discrepancy:** All HEV isolates in one Chinese study belonged to genotype 4, while another study detected HEV RNA in urine but not serum, and a third found no association between HEV-3 and male infertility — suggesting genotype-specific effects.

## Open Questions

1. **What is the interaction between seminal virome composition and gut/genital microbiome dysbiosis?** The review focuses exclusively on viruses without addressing bacterial or fungal co-infections that may modulate viral persistence or pathogenicity.
2. **Do metal exposures (e.g., cadmium, lead) synergize with viral-induced oxidative stress to worsen sperm damage?** Both pathways converge on ROS generation, mitochondrial dysfunction, and DNA fragmentation — but no studies have examined the combined effect.
3. **What explains the persistence of EBOV in the male genital tract for >1 year?** The "immunological privilege" of the testes is proposed but not mechanistically validated — is metal sequestration or nutritional immunity involved in testicular immune privilege?
4. **Could the proposed 13-virus panel be integrated with metallomic and taxonomic profiling for a multi-domain fertility assessment?**
5. **Why do some men clear seminal viruses rapidly while others show prolonged shedding?** Individual variation in immune response, microbiome composition, and metal status may all contribute but remain unexplored.
