# Key Claims

- Environmental [[tin]] exposure in the U.S. population is nearly ubiquitous, with detection frequencies of 87.05% in adults and 91.29% in children
- Age is the most important factor associated with urinary tin levels, with levels in the >=60 year age group almost 2-fold higher than the 20-39 group
- Urinary tin levels in children (median 0.60 ug/L) are approximately 2-fold higher than in adolescents (0.43 ug/L)
- Race/ethnicity and household income are significantly associated with tin levels in both adults and children
- The study does not distinguish between organic and inorganic forms of tin

# Methods/Findings

- **Study type**: Cross-sectional analysis of NHANES 2011-2014 data
- **Population**: 3,522 adults and 1,641 children from nationally representative U.S. survey
- **Measurement**: Total urinary tin by ICP-MS at CDC (LLOD = 0.090 ug/L)

 - Median urinary tin: 0.42 ug/L; geometric mean: 0.49 ug/L
 - Creatinine-adjusted geometric mean: 0.56 ug/g creatinine
 - Age: >=60 group levels 2-fold higher than 20-39 group (beta=0.19 and 0.77, p<0.0001)
 - Sex: higher creatinine-adjusted levels in females (beta=0.31, p<0.0001)
 - Race: Non-Hispanic Blacks highest at 0.74 ug/L (p=0.03)
 - Income: inverse association; low-income (PIR<1.30) significantly higher than high-income (PIR>3.50; beta=-0.18, p=0.02)
 - Physical activity: inverse association; >1200 MET-min/week lower than <600 group (beta=-0.17, p=0.01)
 - Alcohol: associated with tin adjusted for volume (p=0.02) and creatinine (p=0.001)

 - Median urinary tin: 0.60 ug/L; geometric mean: 0.66 ug/L
 - Significantly higher in children age 6-11 vs 12-19 (beta=1.08, p<0.0001)
 - Non-Hispanic Black children 2-fold higher median than non-Hispanic White (1.16 vs 0.48 ug/L; beta=0.30, p<0.01)
 - Income: inverse relationship similar to adults
- **Canned food consumption**: Not consistently associated with tin levels despite being assumed major dietary source
