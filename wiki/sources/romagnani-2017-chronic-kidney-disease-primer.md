# Chronic Kidney Disease: Disease Primer

## Key Findings

- **CKD affects approximately 10% of the global population** and is a leading cause of cardiovascular morbidity and mortality worldwide ([[romagnani-2017-chronic-kidney-disease-primer]], expert-opinion)
- **Pathophysiology involves a final common pathway** regardless of initial cause: glomerular hyperfiltration, tubular injury, interstitial fibrosis, and progressive nephron loss
- **Major etiologies** include diabetes mellitus, hypertension, glomerulonephritis, and polycystic kidney disease
- **Staging is based on GFR and albuminuria** using the KDIGO classification system (G1-G5, A1-A3)
- **Uremic toxin accumulation** is a hallmark of advanced CKD, affecting virtually every organ system
- **Mineral and bone disorder** (CKD-MBD) involving calcium, phosphorus, vitamin D, and parathyroid hormone dysregulation is a major complication

## Detailed Summary

This Disease Primer provides an authoritative overview of chronic kidney disease from the perspective of leading experts in nephrology. CKD is defined as abnormalities in kidney structure or function present for more than 3 months, with implications for health. The primer emphasizes that CKD is not merely a kidney disease but a systemic condition affecting cardiovascular health, bone metabolism, hematological function, and neurological status.

The pathophysiology section describes how diverse initial insults (diabetic nephropathy, immune-mediated injury, toxic exposure) converge on a common pathway of nephron loss. As nephrons are lost, remaining nephrons undergo compensatory hyperfiltration, which paradoxically accelerates further damage. This is accompanied by tubulointerstitial fibrosis, the strongest histological predictor of progression to end-stage renal disease (ESRD).

The review covers current management strategies including RAAS inhibition, blood pressure control, glycemic management, and renal replacement therapy (dialysis, transplantation). Importantly, it notes the growing recognition that gut-derived uremic toxins (indoxyl sulfate, p-cresyl sulfate) contribute to CKD progression and cardiovascular complications.
