## Key Claims

- Nickel compounds are classified as human carcinogens (IARC Group 1 for all nickel compounds except metallic nickel) based on increased incidence of lung and nasal cancer in exposed workers
- Nickel has not been recognized as an essential element in humans, though animal studies suggest it may have a physiological function; nickel deficiency in rats causes reduced growth, altered iron metabolism, and anemia
- The estimated body burden of a healthy adult is about 7.3 ug Ni/kg body weight; nickel intake occurs via inhalation, ingestion, and dermal absorption; only 1-2% of ingested nickel is normally absorbed
- The molecular basis of nickel carcinogenesis involves epigenetic changes (DNA methylation, histone modification), activation of hypoxic signaling pathways (HIF-1), oxidative stress, and inhibition of DNA repair rather than direct mutagenesis

## Methods/Findings

- **Design**: Comprehensive review of nickel biology, toxicity, and carcinogenesis mechanisms
- **Nickel chemistry**: Metallic element, atomic mass 58.69; most common oxidation state +2; five natural isotopes; forms various coordination complexes
- **Environmental exposure**: Atmospheric nickel 1-3 ng/m3 rural to 120-170 ng/m3 industrialized; water 0.1-0.5 ppb; dietary intake estimated 150-900 ug/day depending on diet
- **Essentiality evidence**: Nickel-deprived animals show reduced growth, altered reproduction, impaired iron metabolism, decreased hematocrit; nickel may be involved in vitamin B12 metabolism and lipid metabolism
- **Nickel homeostasis**: Average daily intake ~200 ug; bioavailability from food estimated 1-10%; serum nickel 0.2-2.0 ug/L; urinary excretion half-life 20-60 hours; sweat can contain up to 20x plasma levels

 - Epigenetic: DNA hypermethylation, histone deacetylation, chromatin condensation
 - HIF-1 activation: Nickel mimics hypoxia by replacing iron in oxygen sensors
 - NF-kB activation: Pro-inflammatory and anti-apoptotic signaling
 - p53 modulation: Both activation and inactivation reported
 - Oxidative stress: Depletes glutathione, generates ROS (though weakly mutagenic)
 - Calcium homeostasis disruption via Ca2+ channel interference
- **Nickel-binding proteins**: Nickeloplasmin, albumin, histidine-rich glycoprotein; nickel transported into cells via Ca2+ channels and phagocytosis
- **Cancer association**: Primarily respiratory (lung, nasal) in occupational exposure; no tumors from oral/drinking water exposure in animal studies
