# Interventional Influence of the Intestinal Microbiome Through Dietary Intervention and Bowel Cleansing Might Improve Motor Symptoms in Parkinson's Disease

## Summary

This interventional study tests whether a high-fiber dietary intervention, combined with bowel cleansing, can modify the gut [[gut-microbiome|microbiome]] and improve motor symptoms in [[parkinsons-disease]]. It represents one of the first dietary intervention trials specifically targeting the PD microbiome.

## Key Findings

- High-fiber dietary intervention increased gut microbial diversity in PD patients
- SCFA-producing bacteria increased following the dietary intervention
- Fecal [[butyrate]] and propionate levels rose after the fiber-rich diet
- Motor symptoms (UPDRS scores) showed improvement in some participants
- Constipation severity decreased with increased fiber intake
- Bowel cleansing followed by fiber reintroduction produced more marked microbiome shifts
- The intervention was safe and feasible in PD patients

## Relevance to Gut-Metal-Microbiome Axis

Dietary fiber is the primary substrate for [[butyrate]] production, which regulates [[iron]] absorption through the HIF-2alpha pathway. This trial demonstrates that fiber intervention can restore [[short-chain-fatty-acids]] production in PD patients, potentially re-engaging the [[iron]] regulatory mechanism disrupted by [[dysbiosis]]. The improvement in constipation reduces colonic transit time, directly decreasing the contact time for absorption of dietary metals ([[iron]], [[nickel]], [[lead]], [[manganese]]). The bowel cleansing component is intriguing from a metal perspective, as it would also flush accumulated metals from the colon. This study supports dietary fiber as a low-risk, accessible intervention for correcting the gut-metal-microbiome axis in [[parkinsons-disease]].

## Limitations

- Small sample size without placebo control
- Open-label design
- Short intervention period
- Dietary adherence challenging for PD patients with motor impairments
- Motor symptom improvements may reflect placebo or general health effects
