# Cardio-Metabolic Effects of Nickel: A Narrative Review

## Key Claims

- **Nickel exposure is potentially associated with cardiovascular disease (CVD) and metabolic syndrome (MetS)**, primarily indicated by urinary nickel (UNi) levels, but findings are **inconclusive at low doses**.
- Studies using the **same NHANES 2017-2018 database** reach different conclusions depending on inclusion criteria, outcome measures, statistical methods, and covariates used (see Table 2 in paper).
- Experimental (animal) research shows **significant toxic effects across multiple organs**, especially heart and liver, associated with [[oxidative-stress]].
- The review calls for more research on **low-level environmental nickel exposure** in humans.

## Nickel and CVDs

- Early evidence from Narang et al.: elevated serum nickel at 12, 24, 36, 48h post-myocardial infarction.
- Szabo et al.: increased serum nickel after severe burns or cerebral injury — suggesting **endogenous nickel release** contributes to post-traumatic myocardial damage.
- **NHANES 2017-2000 cross-sectional analysis** (n=2702): elevated UNi in CVD patients. Prevalence of CVDs was 3.57, 3.61, and 2.40 fold for 2nd, 3rd, and 4th UNi quartiles vs. lowest. Cubic spline regression showed a **non-monotonic, inverse U-shaped** association.
- A 2024 comprehensive review of 38 studies (2012-2022) on nickel in ambient PM2.5/PM10: statistically associated with worsening cardiovascular morbidity, but **methodological limitations** persist (ecological fallacy, short-term designs, confounding).

## Nickel and MetS

- MetS defined by ≥3 of: high BP, impaired fasting glucose, insulin resistance, abdominal obesity (BMI ≥25), high triglycerides, low HDL-C.
- **Occupational exposure**: Study of 35,104 Chinese nickel-exposed workers in Jinchuan showed age-adjusted MetS prevalence of 13.9% (BMI ≥25), 29.5% (hypertension), 27.5% (abnormal lipid), 47.1%.
- **Diabetes risk**: nickel-exposed workers had 12.8% overall diabetes prevalence (vs. national average 11.6%).
- **Non-occupational**: 2015 study of 2115 adults (Beijing/Shanghai) found link between environmental nickel and T2DM with an **initial sharp, positive association** that **plateaued at ~0.8 μg/L** UNi — L-shaped curve.
- Zhang et al.: plasma nickel was **lower** in T2DM patients vs. controls, raising questions about nickel's essentiality in humans.

## NHANES 2017-2018 Studies (Table 2)

Eight studies used this same database with different designs and reached varying conclusions:

| Study | Outcome | Key Finding |
|-------|---------|-------------|
| Dong & Yang 2023 | BMI, waist, obesity | Negative association with BMI/waist |
| Wang GX et al. 2023 | BMI, waist, obesity | Positive correlation with BMI/WC in males, negative in females |
| Chen Z et al. 2022 | 4 lipids | Not associated with triglycerides; linearly correlated with serum cholesterol |
| Shen S et al. 2023 | Fasting glucose, diabetes | Positively associated with fasting glucose and diabetes |
| Liu Y et al. 2022 | Blood pressure | Negatively correlated with diastolic BP (without hypertension) |
| Su X et al. 2023 | Body composition | No clear dose-response |
| Luo XM et al. 2024 | MetS composite | Inverted L-shaped dose-response; inflection at 0.2141 μg/L |

**This inconsistency is a central finding of the review.**

## Experimental Evidence

- **Dietary nickel in Swiss mice** (1,100 and 1,600 ppm for 4 weeks): reduced cytochrome c oxidase in liver, reduced isocitrate/malate dehydrogenase in cardiac and renal cells. Female body weights suppressed at both doses.
- **Isolated rat hearts**: dose-dependent inhibition of cardiac contractility at nickel concentrations of 0.1 nM to 1.0 mM (2.8-28 mg/L).
- **Cardiac damage** reported due to endogenous nickel release in animals and humans with acute injuries.
- Cardiac toxicity **mitigated by antioxidants** (melatonin, ascorbic acid); **exacerbated in mice deficient in endogenous antioxidants**.
- Nickel exposure raises triglycerides, total cholesterol, LDL while depleting HDL in rats.
- Japanese quails exposed to 2.0 mg/L dietary nickel for 30 days: increased serum glucose, cholesterol, triglyceride levels.

## Mechanisms

- **[[oxidative-stress]]** is the primary proposed mechanism: increased SOD activity in liver, reduced catalase in heart/kidneys, elevated MDA levels.
- In humans: UNi shows **inverse U-shaped relationship with SOD** and **positive linear correlation with MDA**.
- **Metal interactions**: plasma nickel positively correlated with zinc, vanadium, chromium; negatively with copper.

## Limitations Noted

- Most human studies are cross-sectional (cannot establish causation).
- No animal studies at very low nickel doses (<6.1 μg/L UNi equivalent).
- Nickel may be essential at low levels — complicating dose-response interpretation.
