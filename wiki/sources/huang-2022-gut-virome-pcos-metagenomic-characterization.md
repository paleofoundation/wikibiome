# Gut Virome in PCOS Patients

## Key Findings

- **PCOS patients showed significantly decreased within-sample (alpha) viral diversity** compared to healthy controls ([[huang-2022-gut-virome-pcos-metagenomic-characterization]], cross-sectional)
- **Siphoviridae (tailed bacteriophages) were depleted** while **Quimbyviridae were enriched** in PCOS, indicating disease-specific viral compositional shifts
- **Gut viral signatures achieved an AUC of 0.938** for discriminating PCOS from healthy controls, demonstrating strong diagnostic potential
- **Virome alterations correlated with bacterial microbiome changes**, suggesting coordinated phage-bacterium dysbiosis
- **Bacteroidaceae-targeting phages** showed notable shifts, potentially reflecting changes in the Bacteroides populations they infect <!-- NEEDS VERIFICATION: exact correlation statistics -->

## Methods

Cross-sectional metagenomic study using whole-genome shotgun sequencing of fecal samples from PCOS patients and healthy controls. Viral reads were extracted and classified using viral reference databases. Alpha and beta diversity analyses, differential abundance testing, and machine learning classification were performed. <!-- NEEDS VERIFICATION: exact sample sizes per group -->

## Detailed Summary

This study represents one of the first characterizations of the gut virome in polycystic ovary syndrome. While numerous studies have documented gut bacterial dysbiosis in PCOS, the viral component of the gut microbiome (virome) has been largely overlooked. The virome, dominated by bacteriophages, plays a critical role in shaping bacterial community composition through predation, horizontal gene transfer, and metabolic complementation.

The decreased viral diversity in PCOS mirrors bacterial diversity loss, suggesting a coordinated ecological disruption. The depletion of Siphoviridae (temperate tailed phages) and enrichment of Quimbyviridae indicates a shift in phage-host dynamics. Because bacteriophages are obligate parasites of specific bacterial hosts, virome changes directly reflect and influence bacteriome composition. The remarkably high AUC (0.938) for PCOS discrimination suggests that viral signatures may capture aspects of dysbiosis not evident from bacterial profiling alone.

The shifts in Bacteroidaceae-targeting phages are particularly relevant, as Bacteroides species play central roles in bile acid metabolism, short-chain fatty acid production, and immune modulation — all pathways implicated in PCOS pathophysiology.
