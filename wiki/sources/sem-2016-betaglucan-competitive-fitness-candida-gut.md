# β-Glucan Exposure Tightly Correlates with Competitive Fitness of Candida in the Mouse GI Tract

## Key Findings

- **β-glucan exposure = fitness determinant**: Using isogenic *C. albicans* cell wall mutants in quantitative GI tract competition assays, the strongest predictor of competitive fitness was the **amount of exposed β-1,3-glucan on the cell surface** — not total cell wall composition, not in vitro virulence, not growth rate [[sem-2016-betaglucan-competitive-fitness-candida-gut]], animal-model.

- **Masking is everything**: Total levels of individual cell wall components (mannan, glucan, chitin) did NOT correlate with fitness. Only **surface-accessible** β-glucan — the fraction visible to Dectin-1 — determined colonization success. Strains that mask glucan well colonize; strains that expose it are eliminated by the immune system [[sem-2016-betaglucan-competitive-fitness-candida-gut]].

- **Dectin-1 as the gatekeeper**: Recognition of exposed β-glucan by Dectin-1 receptor on host immune cells severely limits Candida GI colonization. This represents a "promising target to severely limit Candida GI fitness and hence represents a promising target to reduce fungal colonization in patients at risk of systemic candidiasis" [[sem-2016-betaglucan-competitive-fitness-candida-gut]].

- **Cross-species**: Similar results obtained with distantly-related *Candida* species (C. parapsilosis, C. krusei), suggesting β-glucan exposure is a universal determinant of fungal GI fitness, not species-specific.

## Relevance to WikiBiome

- **[[functional-shielding]]** — provides the mechanistic basis: masking β-glucan is HOW Candida persists in the gut. Any intervention that unmasks glucan (caspofungin, baicalin) should reduce Candida colonization and collapse the shielding of bacterial partners.
- **[[candida-albicans]]** — β-glucan masking as the core survival strategy in the GI tract.
- **[[innate-immunity]]** — Dectin-1 as the critical pattern recognition receptor for fungal surveillance.
