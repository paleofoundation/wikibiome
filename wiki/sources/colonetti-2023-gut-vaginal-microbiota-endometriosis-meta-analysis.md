# Key Claims

- Meta-analysis of 16 studies found no significant difference in intestinal alpha diversity (Shannon index) between endometriosis and controls (SMD=-0.28; 95% CI -0.70 to 0.14; P=0.19)
- No significant difference in vaginal alpha diversity by Shannon index (SMD=-0.68; 95% CI -1.72 to 0.35; P=0.19)
- No significant difference in intestinal or peritoneal fluid alpha diversity by Simpson index
- However, individual studies did show significant differences, suggesting the relationship is complex
- High heterogeneity (I2=52-66%) across studies limits definitive conclusions

# Methods/Findings

- **Study design**: Systematic review and meta-analysis (PRISMA, registered CRD42021260972)
- **Databases**: Medline, Embase, Web of Science, Cochrane Library, Google Scholar (to April 2022)
- **Included studies**: 16 observational studies (1 cohort, 1 cross-sectional, 14 case-control)
- **Total participants**: 1,151 women (556 endometriosis, 595 controls)
- **Key results**:
  - Intestinal Shannon: 4 studies, 244 women, SMD=-0.28, P=0.19, I2=52%
  - Vaginal Shannon: 2 studies, 25 women, SMD=-0.68, P=0.19, I2=66%
  - Intestinal Simpson: 2 studies, 33 women, SMD=-0.14, P=0.56, I2=0%
  - Peritoneal fluid Simpson: 2 studies, 131 women, SMD=0.34, P=0.17, I2=42%
  - Considerable heterogeneity attributed to diet, medication use, sequencing methods

# Relevance to Wiki

This meta-analysis provides a sobering counterpoint to individual studies showing [[dysbiosis]] in [[endometriosis]], finding no statistically significant pooled differences in alpha diversity. The high heterogeneity underscores that microbiome-endometriosis associations may depend heavily on body site sampled, disease stage, methodology, and confounders like diet and medications. This highlights the need for standardized protocols and larger cohorts in [[endometriosis]] microbiome research, and suggests that specific taxa rather than overall diversity may be more informative [[biomarkers]].
