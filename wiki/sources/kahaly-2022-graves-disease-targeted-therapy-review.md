# Graves' Disease: Is It Time for Targeted Therapy? A Narrative Review

## Key Findings

- **Current ATDs achieve only approximately 50% permanent remission** after 12-18 months of treatment; RAI and thyroidectomy manage symptoms but do not address the underlying autoimmune pathology ([[kahaly-2022-graves-disease-targeted-therapy-review]], expert-opinion, narrative review)
- **Rituximab (anti-CD20) shows promise but inconsistent results**: the RIGD multicenter phase 2 trial (n=27 young GD patients, 12-20 years old) treated with single 500mg RTX + ATDs for 12 months showed 48% hyperthyroidism remission after 2 years; another study showed 40% remission after 24 months; most effective in patients with TRAb <5 IU/L at presentation ([[kahaly-2022-graves-disease-targeted-therapy-review]], expert-opinion, citing multiple case series)
- **Iscalimab (anti-CD40 mAb) achieved euthyroidism in 47% (7/15) of untreated GD patients** in an open-label phase I study over 12 weeks; all responders had TRAb <20 IU/L; CD40 gene variants predict response ([[kahaly-2022-graves-disease-targeted-therapy-review]], expert-opinion, citing phase I trial)
- **Batoclimab (anti-FcRn mAb) showed TRAb and total IgG reduction** in a proof-of-concept RCT for Graves' orbitopathy (680mg x2 weeks then 340mg x4 weeks), though improvements in proptosis and Clinical Activity Scores did not reach statistical significance at 12 weeks ([[kahaly-2022-graves-disease-targeted-therapy-review]], expert-opinion, citing RCT)
- **K1-70 (TSHR-blocking antibody) showed clinically significant improvement in both GD and GO symptoms** in a Phase I trial; well tolerated at 25mg IM, 50mg or 150mg IV doses; shifted thyroid hormones and TSH toward the hypothyroid range ([[kahaly-2022-graves-disease-targeted-therapy-review]], expert-opinion, citing phase I trial)

## Detailed Summary

### Pathogenesis Overview

GD arises from TSH-R autoantibodies (TRAbs) that stimulate thyroid hormone production via the phospholipase-C and cyclic AMP pathways. Key susceptibility genes include CTLA-4 (reduced inhibitory function), PTPN22 (increased T-cell activation), FCRL3 (enhanced B-cell activation and TRAb production), CD40 (overexpression contributing to excessive immune responses), and FOXP3 (reduced Treg activity). The gut microbiota plays an important role: GD patients have reduced diversity, increased Bacteroidetes and Proteobacteria, and decreased Firmicutes. Epigenetic factors include miRNAs (miR-181d, miR-16, miR-22, miR-375, miR-451 upregulated; miR-346, miR-23a-3p downregulated).

### Conventional Treatment Limitations

ATDs (methimazole, carbimazole, PTU) achieve only ~50% permanent remission after 12-18 months. TRAb levels may not permanently decrease for 5-6 years of ATD treatment. Adverse effects: rash (3-6%), pruritus (2-3%), urticaria (1-2%), dyspepsia (3-4%), nausea (2.4%), arthralgia (1.6%), agranulocytosis (major), hepatotoxicity, vasculitis. RAI causes permanent hypothyroidism requiring lifelong T4 replacement. Thyroidectomy risks include laryngeal edema, recurrent laryngeal nerve injury, hypocalcemia, bleeding, and hypoparathyroidism.

### Novel Therapeutic Approaches

**Anti-CD20 (Rituximab)**: Depletes B cells via complement-mediated cytotoxicity and direct apoptosis. RIGD study: 13/27 (48%) achieved remission. Most effective early in disease course with TRAb<5 IU/L. Side effects: infusion reactions, hypogammaglobulinemia. Not consistently effective for established GD.

**Anti-CD40 (Iscalimab)**: Blocks CD40-CD40L co-stimulatory pathway. Open-label phase I (n=15): 7/15 (47%) achieved euthyroidism at 12 weeks; 4/7 relapsed. All responders had TRAb<20 IU/L. CD40 gene expression levels predict response. Side effects mild (fatigue, headache, nausea).

**Anti-BAFF (Belimumab)**: Blocks B-cell activating factor. Rationale from SLE success. Elevated BAFF levels in GD patients correlate with TRAb. Mouse model studies show blocking BAFF-specific receptor-Fc mitigates hyperthyroidism. No GD clinical trial data yet.

**Anti-FcRn (Batoclimab)**: Promotes IgG degradation including TRAb by blocking FcRn-IgG recycling. Proof-of-concept RCT in GO showed TRAb and IgG reduction but proptosis/CAS improvements did not reach significance at 12 weeks. Multicenter trials underway.

**TSHR-Blocking Antibodies (K1-70)**: Monoclonal TSHR-blocking antibody. Phase I clinical trial showed safety at all doses (25mg IM, 50mg IV, 150mg IV) with clinically significant improvement in GD and GO symptoms. Shifted hormones toward hypothyroid range without significant immunogenic events.

**Small Molecule TSHR Antagonists (ANTAG-3, VA-K-14, S37)**: Function as inverse agonists of TSHR. ANTAG-3 reduced T4 by 44% in TRH-treated mice. VA-K-14 inhibited TSHR stimulation by sera from GD patients. S37 suppressed thyrotropin-induced cAMP accumulation. Concern about structural homology with FSH and LH receptors (potential reproductive off-target effects). None tested in humans.

**DRbeta1-Arg74 Blocker (Cepharanthine)**: Plant alkaloid from Stephania cepharantha Hayata. Blocks HLA-DRbeta1-Arg74 pocket, reducing TSHR peptide presentation. Personalized approach for HLA-DRbeta1-Arg74 risk variant carriers.

**Immune Tolerance (ATX-GD-59)**: Peptide-based immunotherapy. Open-label phase I (n=10): 5/10 (50%) achieved FT3 within reference range after 10 intradermal doses over 18 weeks. TRAb concentrations decreased. Mild side effects (swelling, pain at injection site). Apitope technology: binds HLA-DR on immature DCs, reducing antigen presentation.

**CAR-T Cell Therapy**: TSHR-CAR-T cells engineered to recognize and eliminate TRAb-producing B cells. Novel TSHR-CAR-T with extracellular domain of TSH receptor fused to CD8 transmembrane and intracellular signal domain. Demonstrated ability to eliminate TRAb-producing B lymphocytes in vitro and in vivo. Challenges: off-target effects, durability, cytokine release syndrome, cost.
