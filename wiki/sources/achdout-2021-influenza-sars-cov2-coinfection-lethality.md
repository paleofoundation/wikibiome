# Increased Lethality in Influenza and SARS-CoV-2 Coinfection

Achdout et al. (2021). *Nature Communications* 12:5819.

## Overview

This study examines the disease outcome of influenza A virus (IAV) and SARS-CoV-2 coinfection in K18-hACE2 mice. The key finding is that IAV infection enhances susceptibility to severe SARS-CoV-2 disease upon coinfection 2 days later, resulting in nearly complete mortality -- far exceeding either infection alone. Critically, prior immunity to influenza (but not to SARS-CoV-2) prevents severe disease and mortality, with protection being antibody-dependent.

## Key Findings

### Coinfection Severity
- IAV followed by SARS-CoV-2 coinfection (2 days apart) resulted in severe morbidity and nearly complete mortality
- IAV-infected mice started losing weight at 4 dpi and exhibited maximal mortality at 9-10 dpi (75% of initial weight)
- In contrast, nonfatal influenza alone and lower mortality from SARS-CoV-2 alone were observed separately
- Coinfection was lethal regardless of timing (2, 5, or 8 days between infections)

### Viral Load and Tissue Distribution
- SARS-CoV-2 viral load was reduced in coinfected mice compared to single SARS-CoV-2 infection in lungs
- However, IAV viral load was increased in nasal turbinates of coinfected mice
- Remaining SARS-CoV-2 levels, though reduced, were sufficient to trigger lethal outcome
- The induction of innate immune response by IAV may inhibit SARS-CoV-2 replication while exacerbating overall pathology

### Immune Response
- Coinfected mice showed massive upregulation of inflammation-related genes:
  - Complement system: C1ra, C1rb, C3, C1s1
  - MHC: H2-eb1, H2-K1
  - Cytokines/chemokines: IL-1a, IL6, Ccl5, Ccl8, Ccl12, Cxcl10
  - Interferons: Ifit1, Ifi3, Il27l2a, Irf7
  - Matrix metalloproteinases: Mmp3, Mmp8, Mmp9
  - Apoptosis: Fas, Cd2, Gzma, Prf1
- Histopathology: Necrosis of bronchiolar epithelium, massive peribronchiolar infiltration of neutrophils and lymphocytes, small hemorrhages, pleural thickening

### Immunity Protection
- **Influenza immunity protected**: Prior IAV immunization (intramuscular) prevented severe disease and mortality in coinfected mice
- **SARS-CoV-2 immunity did NOT protect**: Prior SARS-CoV-2 immunization had no effect on coinfection outcome
- Protection from influenza vaccination was **antibody-dependent**: passive transfer of anti-IAV sera rescued coinfected mice
- T cell depletion (CD4 or CD8) did not affect survival of vaccinated coinfected mice

### Mechanism
- IAV infection likely damages respiratory epithelium and creates an inflammatory environment that amplifies SARS-CoV-2 pathology
- The elevated IAV viral load in coinfected animals suggests SARS-CoV-2 may interfere with anti-IAV immune responses
- Immune-mediated tissue destruction (immunopathology) rather than direct viral damage appears to drive lethality

## Methods

- **Animal model**: K18-hACE2 mice (expressing human ACE2)
- **Viruses**: IAV (A/Puerto Rico/8/1934 H1N1, 80 PFU intranasal); SARS-CoV-2 (BavPat1/2020, 10 PFU intranasal)
- **Coinfection protocol**: IAV day 0, SARS-CoV-2 at day 2, 5, or 8
- **Readouts**: Weight loss, survival, viral titers (plaque assay), histopathology, qRT-PCR (inflammation genes), flow cytometry, ELISA, ELISPOT
- **Immunization**: IAV (intramuscular, inactivated); SARS-CoV-2 (intranasal, Ad5-hACE2 followed by SARS-CoV-2)
