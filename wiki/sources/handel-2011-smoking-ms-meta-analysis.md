# Smoking and Multiple Sclerosis: An Updated Meta-Analysis

## Summary

This updated meta-analysis of 14 studies (3,052 MS cases and 457,619 controls) confirms that cigarette smoking is significantly associated with [[multiple-sclerosis]] susceptibility and examines the relationship between smoking and secondary progression.

## Key Findings

- **MS susceptibility (conservative model)**: RR 1.48 (95% CI 1.35-1.63, p < 10^-15), no significant heterogeneity
- **MS susceptibility (non-conservative model)**: RR 1.52 (95% CI 1.39-1.66, p < 10^-19)
- **Secondary progression**: RR 1.88 (95% CI 0.98-3.61, p = 0.06) -- just short of significance with high heterogeneity
- No significant interaction with latitude or sex ratio
- Dose-response relationship: increasing susceptibility with increasing numbers of cigarettes smoked
- Evidence suggests smoking at an early age increases the probability of progressive and severe MS

## Mechanistic Discussion

- Smoking effect is not mediated solely by nicotine (smokeless tobacco does not increase risk)
- Cigarette smoke components are more likely responsible
- Nitric oxide is one candidate: has putative roles in demyelination and axonal loss
- Interaction between HLA alleles and smoking may be important in susceptibility
- Possible involvement of post-translational modifications of autoantigens

## Relevance to Gut-Metal-Microbiome Axis

Smoking is a well-established disruptor of the [[gut-microbiome]], altering microbial composition and increasing intestinal permeability. Cigarette smoke contains [[heavy-metals]] including cadmium, lead, and nickel, which accumulate in tissues and further perturb [[gut-microbiome]] ecology. The confirmed association between smoking and MS risk could be partially mediated through smoking-induced [[dysbiosis]] and metal accumulation, which together impair [[short-chain-fatty-acids]] production and promote [[neuroinflammation]] through the [[gut-brain-axis]]. The dose-response relationship parallels known dose-dependent effects of metals on microbial communities.

## Limitations

- Limited data on smoking and secondary progression (only 4 studies)
- Cannot establish causation from observational data
- Does not address biological mechanisms in detail
