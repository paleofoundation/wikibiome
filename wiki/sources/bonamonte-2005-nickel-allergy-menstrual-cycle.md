## Key Claims

- The ovulatory phase of the menstrual cycle has a significant inhibitory effect on delayed hypersensitivity type reactions to nickel, with patch tests showing significantly lower responses during ovulation than during the progestinic phase (p<0.0001)
- Negative patch test results during the ovulatory phase could be false-negatives; it may be advisable to repeat tests during the progestinic phase
- Oestradiol has been demonstrated in vitro to inhibit cell-mediated hypersensitivity by acting on cells regulating type IV immunity
- The findings have practical clinical implications for the timing of diagnostic patch testing in fertile women

## Methods/Findings

- **Design**: Prospective study at University of Bari, Italy
- **Population**: 30 selected fertile women (age 25-32 years) allergic to nickel sulfate with regular menstrual cycles; subdivided into 2 groups of 15
- **Protocol**: Patch tests performed with 10 serial aqueous dilutions of nickel sulfate (5% to 0.0013%) at 2 different times: ovulatory phase and progestinic phase; minimum 5-week interval between tests; ovulation confirmed by transvaginal ultrasound
- **Measurements**: Minimal eliciting concentration (MEC) - lowest concentration causing at least a + reaction; summarized test score (STS) - sum of all positive reaction scores

 - MEC was lower (more sensitive) in progestinic phase in 23/30 subjects, same in 4, increased in 3 (signed rank test = -142, p<0.0001)
 - STS values significantly higher during progestinic phase than ovulatory phase (p<0.0001)
 - Variations not affected by test order, atopy, preventive measures, or premenstrual exacerbation history
- **Proposed mechanism**: Oestrogen receptors on peripheral OKT8+ (cytotoxic/suppressor) lymphocytes suggest hormonal modulation of immune response; oestradiol reduces cortical thymus cells; peak oestrogen levels during ovulation may suppress type IV reactions
