# Metabolomic Profiling of Parkinson's Disease and Disease Severity

## Summary

This metabolomics study characterizes the serum/plasma metabolomic signature of [[parkinsons-disease]] and its association with disease severity. Using untargeted metabolomics, it identifies metabolic pathways dysregulated in PD and correlating with motor and non-motor symptom burden.

## Key Findings

- Multiple metabolic pathways are significantly altered in PD versus healthy controls
- [[tryptophan-metabolism]] is shifted toward the kynurenine pathway, producing neurotoxic quinolinic acid
- Purine metabolism alterations indicate mitochondrial dysfunction and oxidative stress
- Lipid metabolism perturbations, including altered ceramide and sphingolipid profiles
- Branched-chain amino acid metabolism is disrupted in PD
- Several metabolite alterations correlate with disease severity (Hoehn-Yahr stage)
- A multi-metabolite panel distinguishes early from advanced PD

## Relevance to Gut-Metal-Microbiome Axis

The metabolomic alterations in PD reflect both gut microbiome changes and metal-driven metabolic disruption. The kynurenine pathway shift in [[tryptophan-metabolism]] is particularly relevant: [[iron]] is required by both tryptophan hydroxylase (serotonin pathway) and indoleamine 2,3-dioxygenase (kynurenine pathway), and [[iron]] dysregulation in PD may favor kynurenine production. The resulting quinolinic acid is itself an [[iron]] chelator that generates hydroxyl radicals via Fenton chemistry, creating a feed-forward loop of [[iron]]-mediated neurotoxicity. Purine metabolism changes reflect mitochondrial dysfunction driven by [[iron]] and [[manganese]] accumulation. Altered lipid profiles connect to [[ferroptosis]], as lipid peroxidation is the terminal executor of iron-dependent cell death. These metabolomic signatures may serve as systemic biomarkers for the gut-metal-microbiome axis disruption that begins in the gut and manifests in the brain.

## Limitations

- Cross-sectional design
- Serum metabolites reflect whole-body metabolism, not specific to gut or brain
- Medication effects on metabolome not fully controlled
- Relatively small cohort size
