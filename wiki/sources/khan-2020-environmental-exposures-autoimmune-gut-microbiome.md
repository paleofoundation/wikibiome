# Environmental Exposures and Autoimmune Diseases: Contribution of Gut Microbiome

## Key Claims

- Environmental factors account for up to 70% of autoimmune disease (AD) risk; the gut microbiome is a critical mediator between environmental exposures and autoimmunity
- Oxidative stress (OS) is a common mechanism shared by multiple environmental toxicants (mercury, silica, TCE, pesticides, pristane, smoking) that contributes to AD pathogenesis through NF-kB activation, NLRP3 inflammasome, and neoantigen formation
- Mercury exposure induces lupus-like syndrome in autoimmune-prone mice; Hg alters B cell receptor signaling via SYK phosphorylation, reduces Bank1 expression, and increases NF-kB and TLR-9
- Silica exposure linked to SLE, RA, and systemic sclerosis; induces autoantibodies, circulating immune complex, C3 deposits via NLRP3 inflammasome and STING activation
- TCE (trichloroethene) exposure causes gut microbiome alterations with increased *Bifidobacterium* and *Enterobacteriaceae* and decreased *Bacteroides* and *Lactobacillus*; also induces OS-mediated autoimmunity via Nrf2 and NF-kB pathways
- Smoking decreases Actinobacteria and Firmicutes; increases Proteobacteria and Bacteroidetes phyla in gut
- SLE patients show lower Firmicutes/Bacteroidetes ratio, reduced *Lactobacillaceae*, increased *Lachnospiraceae*, and elevated LPS from leaky gut
- SCFAs (acetate, propionate, butyrate) exhibit immunoregulatory functions: inhibit NF-kB, promote Tregs, provide protection against type 1 diabetes in NOD model, and ameliorate EAE
- Fecal microbiota from SLE mice can induce autoimmune responses in germ-free mice (anti-dsDNA antibodies, inflammatory responses)

## Methods/Findings

- **Study type**: Review (2020) integrating environmental toxicology, autoimmunity, and gut microbiome literature
- **Key environmental agents covered**: Mercury, pesticides (chlorpyrifos, chlordecone), pristane, silica, TCE, smoking
- **Microbiome mechanisms discussed**: Probiotics (Lactobacillus in MRL/lpr mice reduces inflammation, restores barrier function, improves Treg/Th17 ratio); antibiotics (modify gut flora, reduce inflammatory cytokines); FMT (transfers autoimmune phenotype); oxidative stress (microbial ROS production)
- **Figure 1**: Schematic of OS as common mediator -- environmental agents generate ROS, lipid peroxidation, protein modification, leading to DC activation, Th1/Th17 promotion, autoantibody production
- **Figure 2**: Chemical-induced dysbiosis alters intestinal barrier, promotes bacterial/LPS translocation, drives systemic autoimmunity
- **Limitations**: Review focused primarily on SLE; limited discussion of MS-specific mechanisms; causality between chemical-induced dysbiosis and autoimmunity not fully established
