# Synbiotics Reduce Chemotherapy-Induced Side Effects in Breast Cancer

## Key Findings

- **Synbiotics significantly reduced abnormal defecation severity** (P=0.005) and **fatigue** (P<0.001) after 8 weeks of supplementation alongside chemotherapy ([[khazaei-2023-synbiotics-chemotherapy-breast-cancer-rct]], randomized-controlled-trial, n=60)
- **First clinical trial** specifically evaluating synbiotic supplementation for reducing chemotherapy-induced gastrointestinal and systemic side effects in breast cancer patients
- **No significant differences** were observed for nausea/vomiting or appetite loss between synbiotic and placebo groups
- **Synbiotic formulation** contained probiotic bacteria (likely [[lactobacillus]] and [[bifidobacterium]] strains) combined with prebiotic fiber <!-- NEEDS VERIFICATION: exact probiotic strains and doses -->
- **Quality of life improvements** were reported alongside the gastrointestinal symptom reduction

## Methods

Randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial. 60 breast cancer patients undergoing chemotherapy were randomized to synbiotic or placebo groups. Supplementation lasted 8 weeks. Outcomes included severity of gastrointestinal side effects (abnormal defecation, nausea/vomiting, appetite loss) and fatigue, assessed using validated questionnaires. <!-- NEEDS VERIFICATION: specific questionnaire instruments used -->

## Detailed Summary

Chemotherapy-induced gastrointestinal toxicity is a major cause of treatment discontinuation and reduced quality of life in breast cancer patients. Chemotherapeutic agents disrupt the gut epithelial barrier and alter the intestinal microbiome, leading to dysbiosis characterized by depletion of beneficial bacteria and overgrowth of potentially pathogenic organisms. This study tested whether synbiotic supplementation (probiotics + prebiotics) could mitigate these effects.

The trial demonstrated significant benefits for bowel function normalization and fatigue reduction, both of which are closely linked to gut microbiome integrity. The mechanism likely involves restoration of short-chain fatty acid production by beneficial bacteria, maintenance of gut barrier function, and modulation of systemic inflammation. The lack of significant effect on nausea/vomiting suggests these symptoms may be driven more by direct chemotoxicity on the chemoreceptor trigger zone than by gut microbial disruption.
