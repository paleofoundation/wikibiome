## Key Findings

- **Roadside open-air market leaves** had the highest contamination across all 10 elements tested (Al, As, Cd, Co, Cr, Cu, Hg, Ni, Pb, Zn). Mean ranking in OMUW: Al > Zn > Ni > Cr > Cd > Cu > Co > As > Hg > Pb.
- **Washing reduced non-carcinogenic risk by 84% and cancer risk by 74-87%** — but did not eliminate risk; washed OM leaves still had elevated Cd, Hg, Ni, Pb suggesting bioaccumulation, not just surface deposition.
- **Cancer risk for Cd and As exceeded 1×10^-6** (the USEPA safe threshold) in ALL samples from all markets and washing conditions.
- Johannesburg contamination sources: gold mines in Witwatersrand Basin, road traffic, industrial activities. Leafy vegetables act as sentinel accumulators of air deposition and soil uptake.

## Methods

5 leafy vegetables (coriander, lettuce, mint, spring onion, swiss chard) from 10 shops each in three market categories (OM roadside, SM supermarket, VM vegetable market). Washed and unwashed pairs analyzed. HNO3/HCl microwave digestion (Milestone Ethos UP Maxi 44) at 200°C. Agilent ICP-MS quantification. EDI, HQ, HI, and lifetime cancer risk calculated per USEPA guidelines.

## Contradictions / Tensions

- Public-health messaging to eat more leafy greens (WHO 400-500 g/day fruits and vegetables) needs regional contextualization where urban air deposition is high.
