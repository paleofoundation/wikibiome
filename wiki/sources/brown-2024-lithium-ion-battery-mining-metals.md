## Key Findings

- **Cobalt mining hazards**: respiratory disease dominant — upper/lower respiratory infections, lung cancer, hard metal lung disease, pneumoconiosis. Cobalt levels in blood/urine/hair up to 40x reference values. Cross-placental transfer documented. Male sexual dysfunction, decreased testosterone in exposed populations.
- **Manganese mining** (73 papers, most of any metal): neurologic toxicity dominant — muscle weakness, gait instability, tremors, parkinsonism, memory impairment, psychiatric symptoms (instability, mania, hallucinations). Increased iron deficiency in Mn miners. Children near mines had decreased IQ and skeletal deformities.
- **Nickel mining** (56 papers): respiratory toxicity (lung cancer, chronic bronchitis, infections), plus increased mortality from cardiovascular disease and cancer. Nickel miners showed elevated diabetes rates vs office workers. Elevated nickel in blood, urine, hair, breast milk.
- **Lithium mining**: **no identified studies on toxicological effects** despite exponential demand growth and environmental impact — a critical evidence gap.
- **Psychiatric/mental health**: chronic occupational stress, mood instability, psychosis; 80% of burnout in 1000+ Chinese nickel miners.

## Methods

PRISMA-compliant narrative review. PubMed searched Jan 2024 for ((cobalt OR lithium OR manganese OR nickel) AND (health)) OR (mining). 651 unique articles reduced to 183 after screening. Two independent reviewers; discrepancies resolved by a third. Study designs included case reports, case series, cross-sectional, cohort, environmental sampling.

## Contradictions / Tensions

- Heterogeneity across study designs precludes quantitative meta-analysis.
- Lithium mining evidence absent — could reflect true low toxicity or study absence.

## Open Questions

- Lithium mining occupational health outcomes.
- Nickel miner diabetes mechanism (separate from cobalt-respiratory or manganese-neurologic).
- Whether battery recycling workers face similar or different risk profiles.
