# The Inflamed Brain in Schizophrenia: The Convergence of Genetic and Environmental Risk Factors That Lead to Uncontrolled Neuroinflammation

## Key Claims

- [[Schizophrenia]] is driven by genetics (risk increases from 1% in general population to 50% in monozygotic twins) and environmental risk factors that converge on immune/inflammatory pathways
- The major histocompatibility complex (MHC) locus on chromosome 6 has the highest association with schizophrenia in GWAS
- Complement component 4 (C4A) is highly associated with SCZ-specific structural variants; increased C4A expression increases risk
- Multiple immune receptor genes (IL-1beta, IL-6, IL-10, TNF-alpha, TGF-beta) are altered at mRNA level in SCZ patients
- Exposure to pollution (NO2, PM, benzene, CO) increases risk of developing SCZ and induces [[neuroinflammation]]; diesel exhaust particles cause 70% decrease in hippocampal neurogenesis with 35% increase in Iba1 (microglial activation marker)
- Gut [[dysbiosis]] influences schizophrenia pathogenesis through the [[gut-brain-axis]]; maternal immune activation models show that gut microbiome alterations persist into adulthood
- Morphological and signaling deficits of the blood-brain barrier in some SCZ individuals act as gateway between peripheral and central nervous system inflammation
- Microglia are central sentinels (CNS phagocytes) that detect and respond to inflammatory signals; their abnormal activation contributes to synaptic pruning deficits

## Methods/Findings

- **Study type**: Review article (Frontiers in Cellular Neuroscience)
- **Table 1**: Comprehensive overview of pollutant effects on neuroinflammation across species (human, mouse, rat) including NO2, PM, DEP, nanoscale PM
- **Key framework**: Genetic risk (MHC, complement, cytokine genes) + environmental risk (pollution, infection, stress, gut dysbiosis) converge on microglial dysfunction
