# Intestinal Flora Changes in ESRD and CKD Patients

## Key Findings

- **Microbial diversity was decreased in peritoneal dialysis (PD) patients** compared to healthy controls and CKD non-dialysis patients, with altered community composition reflecting the uremic intestinal environment.
- **Microbial diversity positively correlated with serum albumin levels**, linking gut ecology to nutritional status — a key prognostic indicator in CKD.
- **Butyrate-producing [[faecalibacterium-prausnitzii]] and Prevotellaceae** were dominant in healthy controls and CKD non-dialysis groups but depleted in dialysis patients, consistent with loss of SCFA-producing commensals as CKD progresses.
- **Urease-containing and p-cresol-forming taxa** were enriched in the PD group, indicating a shift toward proteolytic fermentation that generates uremic toxin precursors.
- The study identified distinct enterotype-like clustering patterns across CKD stages, suggesting stage-specific microbial signatures.

## Methods

- **Design**: Cross-sectional comparative study across four groups.
- **Subjects**: 166 participants — 17 healthy controls, 47 CKD non-dialysis, 49 hemodialysis (HD), and 53 peritoneal dialysis (PD) patients.
- **Sequencing**: 16S rDNA gene sequencing of fecal samples.
- **Analysis**: Alpha and beta diversity, taxonomic composition, correlation analysis with clinical parameters (albumin, creatinine, BUN).

## Detailed Summary

This study provides a comprehensive comparison of gut microbiota across the full spectrum of [[chronic-kidney-disease]], from non-dialysis CKD through two dialysis modalities. The key finding is a progressive loss of saccharolytic, SCFA-producing organisms (particularly [[faecalibacterium-prausnitzii]]) with advancement through CKD stages, replaced by proteolytic taxa that generate uremic toxin precursors. The positive correlation between microbial diversity and serum albumin connects gut ecology to the malnutrition-inflammation-atherosclerosis (MIA) syndrome that drives morbidity in advanced CKD. The comparison between HD and PD modalities is clinically informative: PD patients showed distinct microbial profiles from HD patients, suggesting that dialysis modality independently influences gut ecology. The enrichment of urease-producing organisms in PD is particularly relevant, as urease activity generates ammonia that raises intestinal pH and further disrupts the ecological balance favoring proteolytic over saccharolytic fermentation.

## Open Questions

- Whether restoring Faecalibacterium through targeted probiotics or FMT could improve albumin levels and nutritional status in dialysis patients.
- How peritoneal dialysis fluid composition (glucose-based vs. icodextrin) influences gut microbial ecology.
- Whether the enterotype-like patterns identified could guide personalized microbiome interventions by CKD stage.
