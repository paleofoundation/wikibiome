# Overview

Summary of a CDC and Autism Speaks co-sponsored workshop examining changes in ASD prevalence, identification factors, and risk factors. ASD estimated to occur in about **one percent of US children**, with prevalence increasing significantly over time.

# Key Findings

## Prevalence Trends

- From 1940s-1980s: autism estimated at 1 in 2,000 (0.05%) children
- Current estimates: **approximately 1 in 88 children** (1.14%) as of 2008 ADDM data
- Overall 78% increase from 2002 to 2008 across ADDM sites
- Male-to-female ratio approximately **4-5:1**

## Factors Influencing Prevalence

- **Intrinsic identification**: better analytic tools, improved screening methods
- **Extrinsic identification**: changes in diagnostic criteria, increased awareness, access to services
- **Risk factors**: genetic variations, environmental exposures, gene-environment interactions
- About 50% of CA DDS increase could be explained by several identification factors combined

## Data Sources

- Service-based data (education, developmental services): underestimate true prevalence
- Population-based studies (epidemiologic screening): yield higher estimates
- CDC ADDM Network: record review across multiple sites; most recent estimates show 1 in 88

## Key Uncertainties

- Cannot fully disentangle true prevalence increase from improved identification
- Multiple factors are not independent of each other
- Need for more complex methods to evaluate overlapping factors
