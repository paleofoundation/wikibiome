## Key Findings

- **VPA reduced mast cell degranulation and cytokine release** induced by *Listeria monocytogenes*, demonstrating that this anticonvulsant drug suppresses innate immune responses to intracellular pathogens ([[soria-castro-2022-valproic-acid-mast-cells-listeria]], in-vitro).
- **Mast cell activation during L. monocytogenes infection relies on TLR2 engagement**; VPA did not affect TLR2 cell surface expression but suppressed downstream signaling events.
- **VPA decreased critical signaling events** in mast cells including intracellular Ca2+ increase, phosphorylation of p38, ERK1/2, and the p65 subunit of NF-kB — key nodes in the innate immune signaling cascade.
- **VPA also reduced cytokine production** in response to Listeriolysin O (LLO), which activates mast cells by a TLR2-independent mechanism — indicating that VPA's immunosuppressive effect operates through multiple signaling pathways.
- **VPA can broadly modulate** the functional activity of immune cells that participate in L. monocytogenes infection control, with implications for infection susceptibility in VPA-treated patients.

## Methods

**In-vitro study** using bone marrow-derived mast cells (BMMCs) from mice. Mast cells were pre-treated with valproic acid (VPA) and then challenged with either live *L. monocytogenes* bacteria or purified Listeriolysin O (LLO, the primary virulence factor). Degranulation was measured by beta-hexosaminidase release. Cytokine production (TNF-alpha, IL-6, IL-13) was quantified by ELISA. Signaling pathway activation was assessed by Western blot for phosphorylated p38, ERK1/2, and NF-kB p65. Intracellular calcium flux was measured by fluorometric assay. TLR2 surface expression was assessed by flow cytometry. Multiple VPA concentrations were tested to establish dose-response relationships.

## Detailed Summary

Soria-Castro et al. investigated how valproic acid (VPA) — a widely used anticonvulsant and mood stabilizer — affects mast cell responses to *Listeria monocytogenes*, an intracellular pathogen. This study is relevant to ASD for two interconnected reasons: (1) prenatal VPA exposure is an established risk factor for ASD (the VPA animal model is a standard preclinical model of ASD), and (2) immune dysregulation, including mast cell abnormalities, has been documented in ASD.

Mast cells are tissue-resident immune cells positioned at host-environment interfaces (gut, skin, respiratory tract) where they serve as sentinels for pathogen detection. Upon activation, they degranulate (releasing histamine, proteases, and pre-formed cytokines) and synthesize inflammatory mediators (TNF-alpha, IL-6). In the context of *Listeria* infection, mast cells play a protective role by recruiting neutrophils, activating macrophages, and amplifying the inflammatory response needed to clear this intracellular pathogen.

VPA suppressed mast cell activation at multiple levels: reduced degranulation, reduced cytokine production, and suppressed the intracellular signaling cascades (Ca2+ flux, MAPK phosphorylation, NF-kB activation) that drive these effector functions. Importantly, VPA did not reduce TLR2 surface expression, indicating that the suppressive effect operates downstream of receptor engagement — at the level of signal transduction rather than pathogen recognition.

The fact that VPA also suppressed mast cell responses to purified LLO (which activates mast cells independently of TLR2, through cholesterol-dependent membrane pore formation) demonstrates that VPA's immunosuppressive effect is not limited to a single receptor pathway but reflects broad modulation of mast cell activation machinery, likely through its activity as a histone deacetylase (HDAC) inhibitor.

The implications for ASD pathogenesis are multifaceted: (1) prenatal VPA exposure may impair fetal/neonatal immune responses to gut-colonizing bacteria, potentially shaping the trajectory of microbiome establishment; (2) VPA-induced mast cell suppression in the gut could alter gut barrier function and immune surveillance of the microbiome; (3) the NF-kB suppression pathway links VPA's effects to broader neuroinflammatory mechanisms implicated in ASD.

## Relevance to WikiBiome

This study connects VPA (a known ASD risk factor in prenatal exposure) to immune modulation of responses against gut-relevant pathogens. **[[listeria]]** monocytogenes is a foodborne pathogen that traverses the gut barrier and crosses the blood-brain barrier and placental barrier — making mast cell surveillance of Listeria in the gut particularly relevant to both maternal infection risk during pregnancy and fetal brain development.

The mast cell-microbiome interaction is relevant to WikiBiome's framework: mast cells in the gut mucosa are key regulators of the host-microbiome interface, and their suppression by VPA could alter the ecological balance between host immune surveillance and microbial colonization. This connects to the broader question of how prenatal drug exposures shape the offspring microbiome.

## Open Questions

- Does prenatal VPA exposure impair neonatal mast cell function in the gut, contributing to altered microbiome establishment and ASD-associated dysbiosis?
- Are VPA-treated epilepsy patients at increased risk for *Listeria* and other intracellular infections due to mast cell suppression?
- Do the immunosuppressive effects of VPA on mast cells extend to other gut-associated immune cells (dendritic cells, ILCs, macrophages) that regulate microbiome composition?
- Whether the HDAC inhibitor activity of VPA is the mechanism for mast cell suppression, and if so, whether other HDAC inhibitors produce similar immune effects.
