# Metallomics in Deep Time and the Influence of Ocean Chemistry on the Metabolic Landscapes of Earth's Earliest Ecosystems

Hickman-Lewis et al. (2020). *Scientific Reports* 10:4965.

## Overview

This study establishes a framework for estimating the "palaeo-metallome" -- the metallic complement of ancient biological systems preserved in the fossil record. Using microbeam particle-induced X-ray emission (uPIXE), the authors spatially quantify transition metals and metalloids within carbonaceous material from 3.33 billion-year-old cherts of the Barberton greenstone belt (South Africa). The study extends the metallome record by over 3 billion years and demonstrates that elements key to anaerobic prokaryotic metabolism (Fe, V, Ni, As, Co) are enriched in biogenic carbonaceous material, while Mo and Zn -- incorporated into enzymes only after the Great Oxygenation Event -- are absent or below detection limits.

## Key Findings

### The Palaeo-Metallome Concept
- The metallome is the inorganic complement to the proteome and genome -- the totality of metals and metalloids in a biological system
- Oldest metallomes date from 131-120 Ma (feathers) and 50 Ma (leaves)
- This study extends the record to 3.33 Ga (billion years ago)
- The term "palaeo-metallome" introduced for the trace element signature preserved in ancient fossil biomass

### Trace Element Enrichments in Biogenic Material
- Nine bio-functional elements enriched in carbonaceous material (CM) relative to matrix: P, S, Ti, V, Fe, Co, Ni, As
- Furthermore, Mn, Cu, Zr, and Mo appear modestly enriched (100-150% relative to matrix)
- Zn and Cr occur at concentrations below detection limits
- Average elemental concentrations in irregular clots follow the trend: K > Ni > Cu > Fe > V > S > As > P > Ti > Co > Zr > Ca > Zn > Mo > Mn

### Pre- vs Post-Great Oxygenation Event
- Mo and Zn, likely incorporated into enzymes only after the Great Oxygenation Event (GOE), are either absent or below detection
- This suggests minor biological utilization of Mo and Zn in the pre-GOE anoxic environment
- Consistent with the hypothesis that modern biological dependency on trace metals is a consequence of the environments in which early life evolved

### Comparison with Modern Prokaryotic Metallomes
- The palaeo-metallome of Josefsdal Chert CM shows strong resemblance to modern anaerobic, methanogenic, or diazotrophic thermophiles
- Closest match: *M. jannaschii* -- a metallomic contribution of V, Co, Ni, Cu, and As (with bio-essential Fe)
- Mo absence/depletion consistent with anoxic Archaean ocean conditions where Mo was not bioavailable
- Elements reflect a ferro-sulphidic environment requiring Co/Ni and lacking Zn

### Biosignature Implications
- The diffuse, spatially-correlated trace element enrichments represent biologically accumulated metals, not mineral phases
- Metal:metal ratios (V/Fe, Co/Fe, Ni/Fe, Cu/Fe) distinguish biogenic microstructures (clots, coatings) from generic CM (flakes) and matrix
- Higher V/Fe, Co/Fe, Ni/Fe ratios in clots and coatings suggest active bio-accumulation vs passive accumulation in flakes
- Carbon isotope compositions (delta-13C between -41.3 and +0.03 permil) consistent with biological fractionation

### Astrobiology Applications
- Palaeo-metallomic signatures could be used to evaluate preserved organic materials on Mars
- The PIXL instrument on Mars 2020 rover has the capability to measure trace element co-occurrences in possible CM
- Elemental signatures associated with putative biomass represent highly strategic biosignature targets

## Methods

- **Samples**: 3.33 Ga Josefsdal Chert, Barberton greenstone belt (South Africa/Eswatini)
- **uPIXE**: 3 MeV proton microbeam, 1 um spatial resolution, AIFIRA facility
- **SIMS**: Carbon isotope measurements (delta-13C) at high mass resolution
- **ICP-MS**: Bulk rock analysis for comparison
- **SEM-EDS**: Micromineral identification

## Relevance to Wiki

While this paper focuses on deep-time geobiology rather than human health, it provides fundamental context for the wiki's metallomics framework. It demonstrates that metal-biology co-evolution is >3.3 billion years old, and that the specific metals organisms depend on reflect the geochemical environment of their evolutionary origin. The absence of Zn and Mo in pre-GOE organisms, their later incorporation after atmospheric oxygenation, and the ancient dependence on Fe, V, Ni, and Co illustrate why these metals remain central to biology today. This evolutionary perspective illuminates why metal dyshomeostasis is so pathologically consequential.
