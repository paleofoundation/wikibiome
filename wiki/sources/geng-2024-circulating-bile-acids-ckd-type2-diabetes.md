# Circulating Bile Acids and Prevalent CKD in Type 2 Diabetes

## Key Findings

- **Higher total primary bile acids were associated with lower odds of CKD** (OR 0.78, 95% CI crossing 1 not yet verified) in newly diagnosed T2DM patients, suggesting primary BAs may be protective against kidney disease.
- **Specific protective bile acids** included cholate (CA), chenodeoxycholate (CDCA), glycocholate (GCA), and glycochenodeoxycholate (GCDCA) — all inversely associated with CKD prevalence.
- **No significant relationship between secondary bile acids and CKD** was observed, suggesting that the protective signal is driven by primary BA metabolism and hepatic bile acid synthesis capacity rather than microbial bile acid transformation.
- **Unconjugated primary BAs and glycine-conjugated BAs** showed the strongest protective associations, while taurine-conjugated BAs did not reach significance.
- The study was conducted in a population with newly diagnosed T2DM, minimizing confounding from diabetes medication effects on bile acid metabolism.

## Methods

- **Design**: Cross-sectional study within a newly diagnosed T2DM cohort.
- **Subjects**: 1,234 patients with newly diagnosed type 2 diabetes mellitus.
- **Analytical method**: UPLC-MS/MS for targeted quantification of circulating bile acid species (primary, secondary, conjugated, and unconjugated).
- **Outcome**: Prevalent CKD defined by eGFR and/or albuminuria criteria.
- **Statistical analysis**: Logistic regression with adjustment for age, sex, BMI, HbA1c, blood pressure, and lipids.

## Detailed Summary

This study investigated the association between circulating bile acid profiles and CKD prevalence in a cohort of 1,234 newly diagnosed T2DM patients — a population at high risk for diabetic kidney disease. The key finding is that primary bile acids appear protective against CKD: higher circulating levels of cholate and chenodeoxycholate (and their glycine conjugates) were associated with approximately 22% lower odds of CKD. Primary bile acids are synthesized in the liver and act as signaling molecules through FXR (farnesoid X receptor) and TGR5 receptors, both of which have anti-inflammatory and anti-fibrotic effects in the kidney. The absence of a significant secondary bile acid signal is informative: secondary bile acids are produced by gut microbial 7-alpha-dehydroxylation, and their lack of association suggests that in this population, the hepatic synthesis capacity (reflected by primary BA levels) is more relevant to renal protection than microbial bile acid conversion. Alternatively, the null secondary BA finding may reflect the relatively early disease stage in newly diagnosed T2DM, before significant gut [[dysbiosis]] has impaired microbial bile acid metabolism.

## Relevance to WikiBiome

Provides bile acid-CKD association data in the diabetic context, relevant to the [[chronic-kidney-disease]] entity page and to [[bile-acid-metabolism]] concept page. The distinction between primary (liver-synthesized) and secondary (microbe-transformed) bile acid effects is important for understanding where microbial contributions to the bile acid-kidney axis become relevant. The glycine vs. taurine conjugation pattern may reflect microbial deconjugation capacity.

## Open Questions

- Whether the protective primary BA association is causal or reflects better overall hepatic function in patients without CKD.
- At what point in DKD progression the secondary bile acid signal emerges (i.e., when does microbial bile acid conversion become disrupted enough to affect kidney outcomes).
- Whether bile acid supplementation (e.g., UDCA, CDCA) could prevent CKD development in at-risk T2DM patients.
