## Key Findings

- **Brown rice contains ~80% more inorganic arsenic than white rice** because inorganic arsenic concentrates in the bran and germ layers that are removed during refining. FDA averages: white rice 92 ppb, brown rice 154 ppb, infant white-rice cereal 104 ppb, infant brown-rice cereal 119 ppb.
- **Rice consumption rivals drinking water as a source of inorganic arsenic exposure** in U.S. populations; rice grown in Southern U.S. states (>47% of U.S. market) has the highest arsenic content compared to California or Asian imports.
- **Chronic arsenic exposure** is linked to cancers (prostate, lung, bladder, pancreatic, skin, digestive tract, liver, kidney, lymphatic/hematopoietic), cardiovascular disease, type-2 diabetes, hypertension, metabolic syndrome, and obesity. IARC classifies inorganic arsenic compounds as Group 1 carcinogens.
- **Mechanisms**: AsIII (trivalent arsenic) is more toxic than AsV (pentavalent arsenate) because of greater chemical reactivity and easier cellular entry. Arsenic disrupts DNA mismatch repair, induces epigenetic dysregulation (DNA methylation), upregulates IL-6, and suppresses GLUT4 expression, linking exposure to insulin resistance and obesity.

## Relevance to Cureva

- Dietary STOP consideration: "switch to brown rice" advice may be counterproductive in populations with high rice intake and existing metal burden.
- Supports cross-condition pattern: arsenic as a shared dietary exposure across diabetes, CVD, and several cancers.

## Contradictions / Tensions

- Notes that brown rice has been advocated as healthier (Sun et al. 2010 — 16% reduced diabetes risk vs white rice) but counters that the arsenic burden may offset this benefit; risk-benefit assessment is an open research frontier.

## Open Questions

- Long-term low-dose dietary arsenic exposure effects remain unclear.
- No human risk-benefit assessment specifically comparing brown vs white rice consumption.
- Mechanism linking arsenic to obesity via GLUT4 silencing needs further validation.
