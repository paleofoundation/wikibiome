## Key Findings

- **Vaginal [[lactoferrin]] concentration is positively associated with systemic [[iron]] biomarkers** — serum [[hepcidin]] (P = 0.047), serum ferritin (P = 0.018), and total body iron stores (P = 0.042), demonstrating that mucosal innate immune iron-sequestration capacity is linked to host iron status ([[roberts-2019-mucosal-lactoferrin-genital-infections-iron]], cross-sectional, n=1015).
- **Genital tract infections dramatically increase vaginal Lf**: [[bacterial-vaginosis]] raised Lf 6.6-fold (P < 0.001) and [[trichomonas-vaginalis]] raised it 11.5-fold (P < 0.001), consistent with neutrophil-mediated iron withholding at infection sites ([[roberts-2019-mucosal-lactoferrin-genital-infections-iron]]).
- **Vaginal microbiota community state types (CSTs) III and IV** — dominated by [[lactobacillus-iners]] and mixed anaerobes respectively — showed significantly higher Lf compared to CST I ([[lactobacillus-crispatus]]-dominated), with log10 differences of 0.59 and 0.58 (P < 0.001) ([[roberts-2019-mucosal-lactoferrin-genital-infections-iron]]).
- **Nutritional status influences mucosal Lf response**: BMI (P = 0.018) and MUAC (P = 0.047) were positively associated with vaginal Lf, suggesting undernourished women may have impaired mucosal iron-sequestration defenses ([[roberts-2019-mucosal-lactoferrin-genital-infections-iron]]).
- **Within-individual Lf levels persist over time** (ICC = 0.57), indicating stable hyper- and hypo-responsive cohorts that maintain their Lf profile from before through pregnancy ([[roberts-2019-mucosal-lactoferrin-genital-infections-iron]]).

## Methods

- **Design**: Cross-sectional sub-study nested within a randomized, controlled, double-blind periconceptional iron supplementation trial. Trial arms were pooled because weekly iron supplementation did not significantly improve iron status (likely due to chronic malaria-related malabsorption).
- **Population**: Young nulliparous and primigravid women aged 15–24 years from 30 rural villages in Burkina Faso. 93% were adolescents (<20 years). HIV prevalence <2%.
- **Sample sizes**: 712 non-pregnant women at end assessment (18 months post-randomization); 303 pregnant women at ANC1 or ANC2 (271 at ANC1, 241 at ANC2); 1224 total Lf samples. Longitudinal subset of 51 women assessed at all three time points.
- **Lf measurement**: Competitive double-antibody sandwich ELISA (Kamiya Biomedical Co, KT-489) on vaginal eluates from self-taken swabs. Intra-assay CV = 9.7%. Values normalized by mean weight per visit type.
- **Iron biomarkers**: Plasma ferritin and sTfR by ELISA (RAMCO); CRP by ELISA (IBL International); hepcidin by competitive ELISA; body iron stores calculated via Cook equation with inflammation-adjusted ferritin (BRINDA method).
- **Microbiota**: 16S rRNA gene V4 region sequencing (MiSeq, NU-OMICS). Three CSTs identified: I (L. crispatus, 40.4%), III (L. iners, 25.8%), IV (mixed/reduced lactobacilli, 33.8%).
- **BV**: Nugent scoring on Gram stain (7–10 = BV). T. vaginalis by PCR.
- **Statistics**: Linear mixed models with log10(Lf) as outcome, random effects for participant, adjusted for visit and MUAC. R v3.3.1.

## Detailed Summary

This study measured vaginal lactoferrin concentrations in a large cohort of young Burkinabe women to characterize the factors influencing this mucosal innate immune biomarker. The study leveraged samples from a periconceptional iron supplementation RCT, but because weekly iron supplementation failed to improve iron status (attributed to chronic malaria impairing absorption), both trial arms were pooled.

### Lactoferrin and infection

Infection was the dominant driver of vaginal Lf. BV (Nugent 7–10) was associated with a 6.6-fold increase in Lf (log10 difference = 0.82, P < 0.001). *T. vaginalis* infection produced an even larger 11.5-fold elevation (log10 difference = 1.06, P < 0.001). Intermediate Nugent scores (4–6) also showed elevated Lf (log10 difference = 0.41, P < 0.001). Prior antifungal prescriptions were associated with higher Lf (P < 0.001), likely reflecting preceding fungal infection that triggered the Lf response. These findings are consistent with neutrophil degranulation releasing Lf from secondary granules to sequester iron away from pathogens at the infection site.

### Microbiota community state types

CST I (L. crispatus-dominated) had the lowest Lf — the "healthy" baseline. CSTs III (L. iners-dominated) and IV (mixed anaerobic community including [[prevotella]], [[dialister]], [[gardnerella-vaginalis]], [[atopobium]], [[sneathia]], [[actinomyces]], [[parvimonas]], [[peptoniphilus]], [[gemella]], [[anaerococcus]], [[finegoldia]], [[veillonella]]) both showed significantly elevated Lf. The authors note that *L. iners*, unlike other vaginal lactobacilli, requires iron for growth and probably utilizes iron released from erythrocyte destruction by *[[gardnerella-vaginalis]]* during menses.

### Iron biomarker associations

After adjustment for visit and MUAC, vaginal Lf was positively associated with:
- Adjusted serum ferritin: effect = 0.07 per log unit (P = 0.018)
- Serum hepcidin: effect = 0.04 per log unit (P = 0.047)
- Total body iron stores: effect = 0.014 per mg/kg (P = 0.042)
- Serum CRP: borderline association (P = 0.048)

sTfR, ZPP, hemoglobin, and the sTfR/ferritin ratio were NOT significantly associated with Lf. This pattern suggests that women with higher iron stores mount stronger mucosal lactoferrin responses. The hepcidin association is particularly notable: the authors speculate that hepcidin may be locally synthesized by myeloid cells migrating to infection sites, creating a local iron-restricted environment, in addition to its systemic role via hepatocyte production.

### Nutritional status

37% of non-pregnant women had BMI < 18.5 (underweight). Both BMI (P = 0.018) and MUAC (P = 0.047) were positively associated with Lf, suggesting that malnourished women may have compromised mucosal iron-sequestration capacity. The authors link this to leptin's role in mucosal immunity — leptin, produced by adipose tissue, may mediate constitutive Lf production via IL-23.

### Longitudinal persistence

The within-patient ICC of 0.57 indicates that more than half the total variation in Lf is attributable to stable between-individual differences. The longitudinal subset (n=51) showed women maintaining their Lf quartile ranking from non-pregnant status through pregnancy, even as BV and T. vaginalis infections occurred in both high and low Lf groups. This suggests individual variation in iron-based innate immune capacity that persists regardless of reproductive state.

### Pregnancy

Pregnant women had higher Lf than non-pregnant women overall (log10 difference = 0.45, P < 0.0001), but Lf in pregnancy was comparable to that of sexually active non-pregnant women and was independent of gestational age (slope P = 0.14). The pregnancy effect likely reflects greater exposure to sexually transmitted infections and higher estrogen-driven constitutive Lf production.

## Relevance to Cureva

- **Primitive 2 (Nutritional Immunity as Interpretive Constraint)**: The positive association between body iron stores and vaginal Lf suggests that iron-replete women mount stronger mucosal defenses. This complicates the interpretation of iron supplementation in women with genital infections — supplementation might improve Lf capacity but simultaneously feed iron-dependent pathogens.
- **Primitive 4 (Microbial Metal Dependencies as Achilles' Heels)**: *G. vaginalis* has a specific lactoferrin-binding protein, making it capable of overcoming the host's primary iron-sequestration defense. This identifies lactoferrin-piracy as an Achilles' heel to target.
- **Primitive 8 (Siderophore Competition and Iron Ecology)**: The entire Lf-infection-iron axis described here is a mucosal instance of iron ecology competition. Pathogens with iron-acquisition mechanisms (lactoferrin-binding proteins, siderophores) succeed in the iron-restricted environment.
- **Signature relevance**: The vaginal microbiota CST data (CST III/IV associations with elevated Lf, the role of iron-dependent L. iners) are relevant to the [[endometriosis-signature]] — endometriosis involves altered vaginal and peritoneal microbiota with enrichment of iron-dependent pathobionts in an iron-rich environment (menstrual reflux). The finding that iron-deficient women were paradoxically more likely to have normal vaginal flora (from the parent trial) directly supports the nutritional immunity framework.
- **STOP signal**: The parent trial finding that weekly iron supplementation did not improve iron status due to malaria-related malabsorption, combined with this sub-study's evidence that iron availability feeds genital pathogens, suggests caution with iron supplementation protocols in populations with high genital infection burden.

## Contradictions / Tensions

- **Iron supplementation paradox**: The parent trial found no iron status improvement from supplementation, yet this sub-study shows higher iron stores correlate with better mucosal Lf defenses. This creates a tension: should iron supplementation be pursued to boost Lf-mediated innate immunity, or avoided to starve iron-dependent pathogens? The answer likely depends on infection context.
- **L. iners ambiguity**: The paper notes L. iners' unique iron requirement among vaginal lactobacilli. L. iners is variously described as commensal, transitional, or potentially pathogenic. Its dependence on iron released by *G. vaginalis*-mediated erythrocyte destruction places it in an ecologically ambiguous position — it may be a passive beneficiary of dysbiosis rather than a driver, or it may facilitate the transition from healthy to dysbiotic states by exploiting the iron-rich environment.
- **Hepcidin at mucosal sites**: The authors speculate about local hepcidin synthesis by migrating myeloid cells, but this has not been directly demonstrated in the genital tract. The observed hepcidin-Lf association could reflect systemic inflammation driving both markers independently rather than a direct mucosal hepcidin mechanism.

## Open Questions

1. **Does genital tract infection induce local hepcidin synthesis?** The authors propose this pathway but have not directly measured mucosal hepcidin. If confirmed, it would establish a local iron-restriction circuit at mucosal sites independent of hepatic hepcidin.
2. **What determines hyper- vs. hypo-responsive Lf phenotypes?** The persistent individual variation (ICC = 0.57) is unexplained — is it genetic (Lf gene promoter polymorphisms, estrogen receptor variants), epigenetic, or driven by early-life iron/nutritional programming?
3. **Does lactoferrin supplementation (oral or vaginal) enhance mucosal defenses against genital infections?** Given that Lf is both bacteriostatic and a target for pathogen iron-piracy (*G. vaginalis* lactoferrin-binding protein), the net effect of exogenous Lf is unclear.
4. **How does menstrual iron availability interact with the Lf response?** The study excluded menstrual samples. Given that heme iron is freely available during menses, the Lf iron-withholding defense may be overwhelmed cyclically, creating a recurring window of pathogen vulnerability.
5. **Does the Lf-iron-hepcidin axis operate similarly in the peritoneal cavity?** Relevant to endometriosis, where peritoneal iron overload from menstrual reflux creates an environment analogous to the iron-rich dysbiotic vaginal milieu described here.
