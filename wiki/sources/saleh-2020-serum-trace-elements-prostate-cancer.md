# Key Claims

- Serum trace element disturbances are associated with prostate cancer (PCa), with decreased [[selenium]], [[zinc]], and [[manganese]] and increased [[copper]] and [[iron]] in PCa patients compared to controls
- Low Se levels correlate with carcinogenesis risk and may play significant roles in PCa initiation through impaired antioxidant defense
- Elevated Cu and Fe may increase [[oxidative-stress]] and contribute to PCa development through free radical generation
- Decreased Zn in PCa reflects disrupted Zn transporter function and metalloenzyme impairment, potentially enabling malignant transformation

# Methods/Findings

- **Study design**: Case-control study in Saudi Arabia; 40 newly diagnosed PCa patients, 22 BPH patients, and 30 healthy males
- **Analytical method**: ICP-MS for Se, Zn, Cu, Mn, Fe; ELISA for PSA
- **Key findings** (mean +/- SD):
 - **Se**: PCa 0.07 ug/ml vs healthy 0.13 ug/ml (p < 0.005) -- significantly decreased
 - **Zn**: PCa 0.51 ug/ml vs healthy 0.82 ug/ml (p < 0.005) -- significantly decreased
 - **Cu**: PCa 1.69 ug/ml vs healthy 1.02 ug/ml (p < 0.005) -- significantly increased
 - **Mn**: PCa 0.001 ug/ml vs healthy 0.0024 ug/ml (p < 0.005) -- significantly decreased
 - **Fe**: PCa 1.96 ug/ml vs healthy 1.24 ug/ml (p < 0.05) -- significantly increased
 - **PSA**: PCa 18.3 ng/ml vs healthy 2.8 ng/ml (p > 0.05 for age comparisons)
- **BPH vs healthy**: No significant differences in trace element levels, suggesting trace element disturbances are specific to malignancy rather than benign prostatic conditions
- **Cu-Zn antagonism**: Elevated Cu accompanied by decreased Zn may be explained by Cu replacing Zn binding to metallothionein due to higher Cu affinity, disrupting Zn homeostasis

# Connections

- [[selenium]] decrease in PCa connects to impaired selenoprotein function (glutathione reductases, antioxidant defense), consistent with Se decreases across multiple cancer types
- [[copper]] elevation mirrors the nearly universal Cu increase in cancer biofluids documented in comprehensive [[metallomics]] reviews, supporting Cu as a general [[metal-carcinogenesis]] marker
- [[zinc]] depletion relates to Zn's critical roles in >300 metalloenzymes, DNA stabilization, immune function, and cellular signaling pathways -- Zn deficiency in prostate may enable malignant transformation
- [[iron]] elevation and associated [[oxidative-stress]] via Fenton reaction parallels Fe dysregulation seen in cardiovascular disease and other cancers
- [[manganese]] decrease may impair Mn-SOD (SOD2) antioxidant activity, reducing protection against mitochondrial superoxide
- The Cu/Zn ratio imbalance connects to similar findings in colorectal, breast, and other cancers, reinforcing its potential as a pan-cancer biomarker
- Results support the broader paradigm that essential metal dyshomeostasis in cancer involves simultaneous Cu accumulation and Zn/Se depletion
