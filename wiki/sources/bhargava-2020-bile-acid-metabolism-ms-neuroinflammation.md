# Bile Acid Metabolism Is Altered in Multiple Sclerosis and Supplementation Ameliorates Neuroinflammation

## Summary

This landmark study from Johns Hopkins identified altered [[bile-acid-metabolism]] metabolism in both adult and pediatric MS patients using global and targeted metabolomic profiling, and demonstrated that TUDCA supplementation ameliorates [[neuroinflammation]] in EAE through GPBAR1 receptor signaling.

## Key Findings

### Bile Acid Alterations in MS
- Lower levels of multiple primary bile acid metabolites in PMS group vs controls (discovery cohort: n=56 RRMS, n=51 PMS, n=52 HC)
- Lower levels of multiple secondary bile acid metabolites in both RRMS and PMS groups
- Significantly higher pathway deregulation scores for both primary and secondary bile acid metabolism in PMS
- Validated in larger cohort (n=175 MS, n=75 controls): confirmed bile acid alterations
- **Pediatric-onset MS** (n=31) also showed lower taurocholate, glycochenodeoxycholate, and glycohyocholate, confirming bile acid disruption exists from disease onset

### Bile Acid Receptors in MS Brain
- FXR and GPBAR1 expressed in white matter lesions from PMS brain tissue
- GPBAR1+ cells detected in areas of demyelination
- GPBAR1 staining in CD68+ macrophages and GFAP+ astrocytes in MS lesions
- Decreased PLP (myelin marker) with increased GPBAR1 expression in MS white matter lesions

### TUDCA Effects In Vitro
- TUDCA prevented neurotoxic (A1) polarization of astrocytes
- TUDCA prevented proinflammatory polarization of microglia in a dose-dependent manner
- Effects mediated through GPBAR1 receptor

### TUDCA Ameliorates EAE
- TUDCA supplementation reduced EAE severity in WT mice
- No therapeutic effect in GPBAR1-KO mice, confirming GPBAR1 dependence
- TUDCA reduced innate immune cell infiltration and astrocytosis in spinal cord

## Relevance to Gut-Metal-Microbiome Axis

This study is crucial for the [[gut-metal-microbiome]] framework:

1. **Secondary bile acids are produced by gut bacteria** - the deconjugation and dehydroxylation steps require bacterial enzymes (primarily from Clostridia and *Bacteroides*), which are precisely the taxa depleted by [[heavy-metals]]
2. **Bile acid depletion in pediatric MS** demonstrates that this metabolic disruption exists from disease onset, consistent with environmental (metal) triggers rather than disease consequence
3. **GPBAR1** blockade of NLRP3 inflammasome connects to metal-activated inflammatory pathways and [[nf-kappa-b]] signaling
4. **Astrocyte and microglial polarization** by bile acids parallels the neuroprotective effects of [[indoles]] and [[short-chain-fatty-acids]], all gut-derived metabolites disrupted by metal-induced [[dysbiosis]]

## Limitations

- Cross-sectional metabolomics (cannot establish causality)
- TUDCA administered pharmacologically, not via gut bacteria
- EAE model limitations
- No direct measurement of gut microbiome composition
