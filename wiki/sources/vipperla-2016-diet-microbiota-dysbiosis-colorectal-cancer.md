# Vipperla 2016 -- Diet, Microbiota, and Dysbiosis: A Recipe for Colorectal Cancer

## Key Claims

- The gut microbiota produces a wide variety of bioactive metabolites that are not only active at the mucosal surface but also absorbed and circulated throughout the body
- [[dysbiosis]] is now recognized as a root cause for a growing list of diseases including [[colorectal-cancer]]; diet selects for microbiota composition and many health effects of diet are mediated by the microbiota
- Dietary fiber boosts colonic microbial synthesis of anti-proliferative metabolites (particularly butyrate) and counters carcinogenic metabolites, underscoring dietary modification as a key measure to curb CRC
- Over 90% of CRC cases are sporadic with complex interplay between genetic and environmental factors; over 90% of gastrointestinal cancers can be attributed to dietary habits

## Methods/Findings

- **Type**: Review

 - Three human fecal enterotypes based on Bacteroides (1), Prevotella (2), and Ruminococcus (3) abundance
 - CRC rates: common in African Americans (65:100,000) vs rare in rural Africans (<5:100,000); Prevotella predominant in native Africans, Bacteroides in African Americans
 - Bacteria consistently augmented in CRC: Fusobacteria, Alistipes, Porphyromonadaceae, Coriobacteridae, Staphylococcaceae, Akkermansia, Methanobacteriales
 - Bacteria underrepresented in CRC: Bifidobacterium, Lactobacillus, Ruminococcus, Faecalibacterium, Roseburia, Treponema
 - Reduced butyrate and elevated amino acid metabolites identified throughout colonic carcinogenesis
 - High red meat/high fat diet increases secondary bile acids and hydrogen sulfide; dietary fiber increases butyrate production

- [[dysbiosis]] -- dysbiosis is the root cause linking diet to CRC; enrichment of pathobionts and decrease in symbiotic species
