# Gargiulo Isacco 2024 -- SSRIs and Peri/Post-Menopausal Cervical Cancer Risk

## Key Claims

- Long-term SSRI use may contribute to cervical cancer onset through dysbiosis-immunity-carcinogenesis axis: SSRIs promote antibiotic resistance in bacteria and disrupt estrogen-serotonin-prolactin cross-talk
- SSRIs at clinically relevant concentrations promote antibiotic resistance in commensal bacteria including *E. coli*, *P. aeruginosa*, *Staph. aureus*, and *Streptococcus agalactiae* even at very low doses, via increased bacterial oxidative stress (ROS) and SOS response
- The postmenopausal period shows higher proliferation of harmful microorganisms including HPV, *C. trachomatis*, *E. coli*; the higher the dysbiosis, the higher the presence of harmful microorganisms and chances of developing malignancies
- Three common SSRIs (sertraline, duloxetine, fluoxetine) confirmed profound influence on bacterial resistance to antibiotics; SOS response DNA repair mechanisms (lexA, recA) activated, contributing to DNA damage and mutations
- Antidepressants excreted through GI tract and kidneys interact with local commensal bacteria, potentially contributing to plasmid exchange and evolution of multi-antibiotic resistant superbugs

## Methods/Findings

- **Type**: Review with logistic growth model analysis
- **Key data**: HPV prevalence 22.6% among all women (11.5% among postmenopausal); presence of invasive tumors 4x higher in perimenopausal and 2x higher in postmenopausal groups relative to baseline
- **Mechanisms**: (i) dysbiosis-immunity-carcinogenesis axis, (ii) SSRIs promoting antibiotic resistance in oncogenic pathogens, (iii) estradiol-serotonin-prolactin axis disruption
