# B-Cell-Depletion Reverses Dysbiosis of the Microbiome in Multiple Sclerosis Patients

## Summary

This longitudinal observational study followed 36 MS patients (22 initiating ocrelizumab B-cell depletion therapy, 14 untreated) over 12 months at four timepoints, analyzing both stool and oral swab microbiota via 16S rDNA sequencing. It is one of the first studies to examine the longitudinal effect of B-cell depletion on both oral and gut microbiome composition.

## Key Findings

### Baseline Dysbiosis
- MS patients had significantly higher Bacteroidetes and lower Firmicutes in stool compared to healthy controls
- Higher abundance of several Bacteroidetes members (*Prevotella*, *Butyricimonas*, *Coprobacter*, *Parabacteroides*, *Alistipes*)
- Firmicutes members less abundant, particularly Clostridiales (*Romboutsia* and *Turicibacter*)
- Oral microbiota showed increased Proteobacteria and decreased Actinobacteria in MS
- Higher abundance of *Campylobacter*, *Haemophilus*, and *Neisseria* in oral cavity of MS patients
- Oral [[dysbiosis]] more pronounced as a "disease trait" than gut dysbiosis

### Effects of B-Cell Depletion (Ocrelizumab)
- Increased alpha diversity in both gut and oral cavity following treatment
- Long-term sustained reduction of pro-inflammatory Gram-negative bacteria (e.g., *Escherichia/Shigella*) in oral cavity
- Decrease of *Parabacteroides*, *Bacteroides*, and *Prevotellaceae* in stool
- Increase of *Ruminococcus* and *Lachnospiraceae* in stool (beneficial SCFA producers)
- *Streptococcus* and *Actinomyces* consistently increased in oral cavity post-treatment
- Changes persisted over the 6-12 month observation period

### Disease Severity Correlation
- Stool microbial changes associated with disease severity at baseline were most pronounced in clinically more severe MS patients (MS-O group)
- Oral changes in disease activity did not correlate clearly with severity

## Relevance to Gut-Metal-Microbiome Axis

This study provides important evidence that [[dysbiosis]] in MS is partially reversible through immune-modulating therapy. For the [[gut-metal-microbiome]] model, key insights include:

1. The restoration of Firmicutes (especially *Lachnospiraceae* and *Ruminococcus*) after B-cell depletion suggests that immune-driven [[inflammation]] perpetuates the loss of metal-sensitive SCFA producers
2. Oral microbiota changes may be a more sensitive disease marker than gut changes, relevant to oral metal exposure
3. The reduction of Gram-negative [[inflammation]]-promoting bacteria post-treatment suggests a feedback loop between immune activation and [[dysbiosis]] that could be initially triggered by [[heavy-metals]]

## Limitations

- Small cohort (n=36 MS patients)
- Most ocrelizumab patients had prior disease-modifying therapy exposure
- No direct measurement of environmental exposures
- 12-month follow-up may be insufficient for full microbiome stabilization
