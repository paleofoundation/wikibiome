## Key Findings

- **Multiple viruses impair fertility through diverse mechanisms.** HIV, HPV, HBV, HCV, CMV, HTLV, Zika, mumps, and SARS-CoV-2 each affect reproductive organs via distinct pathways including direct gonadal infection, hormonal disruption, sperm DNA fragmentation, and immune-mediated damage ([[laudanski-2025-viral-infection-fertility-art-review]], expert-opinion, narrative review).
- **HTLV-1 is associated with erectile dysfunction in up to 55.2% of infected men** based on existing evidence, attributed to HTLV-1-related myelopathy and autonomic neuropathy ([[laudanski-2025-viral-infection-fertility-art-review]], expert-opinion, citing refs 36-39).
- **The human virome is a component of the reproductive microbiome.** The quantity of viruses in a single human body is roughly ten times greater than total bacteria (~380 billion particles), and viromes at various levels of the reproductive system are interdependent with bacterial composition ([[laudanski-2025-viral-infection-fertility-art-review]], expert-opinion).
- **HPV infection in men decreases sperm quality** through interference with mitochondrial activity, increased oxidative stress, and induction of apoptosis. HPV DNA has been detected in semen and can attach to sperm cell heads via L1 capsid protein and syndecan-1, affecting motility ([[laudanski-2025-viral-infection-fertility-art-review]], expert-opinion, citing refs 41-48).
- **SARS-CoV-2 may affect male reproduction** through ACE-2 receptor expression in testicular tissue. ACE-2 and TMPRSS2 are expressed in spermatogonia, Leydig cells, and Sertoli cells, providing viral entry points. Conflicting data exist on clinical significance ([[laudanski-2025-viral-infection-fertility-art-review]], expert-opinion, citing refs 75-87).

## Methods

This is a **narrative review** summarizing current evidence on viral pathogens and their effects on fertility, assisted reproductive technologies (ART), and early pregnancy. The authors searched existing literature across multiple viral infections (HIV, HPV, HBV, HCV, CMV, HSV, HTLV, Zika, mumps, rubella, SARS-CoV-2, and others) and synthesized findings on mechanisms of reproductive impairment, ART outcomes, and pregnancy complications. No systematic search protocol, inclusion/exclusion criteria, or meta-analytic methods are reported. The review cites 126 references.

## Detailed Summary

### HIV and Fertility

HIV infection affects fertility through multiple routes. In men, HIV alters semen parameters including reduced sperm motility and morphology, though data are conflicting on whether sperm concentration is consistently reduced. The virus can penetrate the oocyte along with the sperm cell, potentially inhibiting embryo development. ART outcomes in HIV-positive couples show conflicting results: some studies report lower pregnancy and live birth rates, while others find no significant differences or even better outcomes when the man is HIV-positive. Recent research suggests poorer outcomes particularly among women and couples where both partners are HIV-positive.

### HPV and Male Fertility

HPV infection in men is particularly relevant to the WikiBiome framework. The virus interferes with sperm cell mitochondria (affecting motility), increases oxidative stress leading to apoptosis, and can attach to sperm heads via L1 capsid protein and syndecan-1. HPV DNA has been detected in semen, and viral DNA can integrate into the host genome. Studies on HPV vaccination in males suggest potential improvements in pregnancy rates and reduced miscarriage risk, though evidence remains preliminary.

### Hepatitis B and C

HBV may be associated with longer delay in conception and ovulation disorders. HBV surface protein interferes with sperm cell mitochondrial activity. HCV infection often causes elevated estrogen levels and impaired progesterone metabolism through cirrhosis, potentially leading to diminished ovulation stimulation response. HCV may also decrease ovarian reserve as measured by anti-Mullerian hormone (AMH). Despite these effects, IVF treatment in women with chronic liver disease is generally well tolerated.

### HTLV and Erectile Dysfunction

HTLV-1 has been associated with **erectile dysfunction in up to 55.2% of men** in existing studies. The mechanism is linked to HTLV-1-related myelopathy and autonomic neuropathy. HTLV-2 is associated with neurological diseases in tropical countries. There is limited data on HTLV-1's direct effect on fertility parameters, and no differences in HTLV-1 infection prevalence were noted based on fertility status in women.

### CMV and Reproduction

CMV is the most frequently identified virus at the maternal-fetal interface, with an incidence of 0.5-1.0% of all live births in developed countries. CMV interacts with heparan sulfate on cell surfaces. Congenital CMV infection is a primary cause of sensorineural hearing loss and neurodevelopmental delays. In the context of sperm donation, preference is given to seronegative donors in both IgM and IgG antibody classes.

### SARS-CoV-2 and Male Reproduction

ACE-2 and TMPRSS2 receptors are expressed in testicular tissue including spermatogonia, Leydig cells, and Sertoli cells. SARS-CoV-2 may enter these cells, potentially affecting spermatogenesis, testosterone production, and sperm quality. However, studies show conflicting data: some report significant effects on sperm parameters and testosterone, while others find no lasting impact. The expression of ACE-2 in the male reproductive system is generally low compared to respiratory tissue.

### Zika Virus

Zika virus infection causes reduced sperm concentration and abnormal morphology. The virus has been detected in various components of semen and sperm cells. Zika can replicate in placental cells and poses significant risk during pregnancy, with fetal microcephaly being the most recognized complication.

### Virome as Reproductive Microbiome Component

The review emphasizes that the virome is a component of the reproductive system's microbiome. The quantity of viruses in a single human body (~380 billion) is roughly ten times the total bacteria. Viromes and bacterial composition within various levels of the reproductive system are interdependent and change along with the development of pregnancy.

### ART in Virally Infected Individuals

The review details virological screening recommendations (HIV, HBV, HCV, rubella) prior to ART and outlines specific protocols for managing reproduction in discordant couples, including sperm washing techniques, IVF-ICSI, and timing of antiretroviral therapy. Table 1 summarizes virological tests for donors of reproductive cells.

## Contradictions / Tensions

- **HIV and ART outcomes.** The review itself acknowledges conflicting data: some studies show lower pregnancy rates in HIV-positive couples, while others show no differences or better outcomes. This is noted as an unresolved tension in the field, not a contradiction with existing vault content.
- **SARS-CoV-2 and male fertility.** Data are conflicting on whether SARS-CoV-2 has lasting effects on sperm parameters and testosterone. Some studies report significant declines; others find no durable impact. The review does not resolve this tension.
- **HPV and ART outcomes.** The review notes that HPV infection does not affect final IVF procedure results (fertilization, implantation, pregnancy rates) despite affecting sperm quality parameters, creating a disconnect between mechanistic evidence and clinical outcomes.
- **Limited metallomics content.** This review does not discuss metals, metal-dependent enzymes, or nutritional immunity. Its relevance to the vault's core thesis (metallomics-microbiome nexus) is therefore peripheral. The connection to existing vault content is primarily through shared disease entities ([[erectile-dysfunction]]) and ecological concepts (virome-microbiome interactions) rather than through metallomic mechanisms.

## Open Questions

1. **Does the reproductive virome modulate metal availability in the reproductive tract?** Viral infections alter immune activation and inflammatory metal sequestration (e.g., hepcidin upregulation during chronic viral infection). The intersection of virome composition and metallomic signatures in reproductive disease is unexplored.
2. **Does HTLV-1-associated ED involve gut-microbiome alterations?** The neurological mechanism (myelopathy) is distinct from the gut-penis axis described in [[gut-penis-axis]], but chronic HTLV-1 infection may also alter gut permeability and microbiome composition, potentially compounding neurological with metabolic pathways.
3. **How does HPV-induced oxidative stress in sperm interact with metal-dependent antioxidant defenses?** HPV triggers ROS production, and metal cofactors (zinc, selenium, manganese) are critical for antioxidant enzymes (SOD, GPx) in seminal fluid. This intersection is not explored in the review.
4. **What is the role of CMV in modulating the endometrial microbiome?** CMV interacts with heparan sulfate and integrin subunits on cell surfaces — molecules also involved in bacterial adhesion. Whether CMV infection alters the endometrial bacterial niche is unknown.
5. **Do viral infections in the reproductive tract alter biofilm dynamics?** The review does not address biofilm formation, but viral-bacterial interactions in biofilms are documented in other body sites (oral, respiratory). Whether reproductive tract viruses promote or disrupt bacterial biofilms relevant to fertility is an open question.
