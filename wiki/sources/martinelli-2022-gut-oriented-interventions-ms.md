# Gut-Oriented Interventions in Patients with Multiple Sclerosis: Fact or Fiction?

## Summary

This narrative review from the MS COMPLETE Project -- a multidisciplinary group of 16 Italian neurologists -- examines evidence for gut-directed interventions in [[multiple-sclerosis]], including diet, supplementation, [[FMT]], and microbiome-targeted therapies.

## Key Findings

### Targeting the Gut Microbiome
- Experimental MS models show deficits in Treg function related to aberrant gut microbiota composition
- Th17 cells triggering [[neuroinflammation]] originate in the intestine
- MS patients have subtle but distinct differences in microbial community profiles vs. controls
- Lower species richness in active RRMS
- DMT treatment normalizes some microbiota changes

### Fecal Microbiota Transplantation
- Mouse studies: FMT from MS-twin-derived microbiota transplanted into transgenic mice increased autoimmune incidence
- *[[sutterella|Sutterella]]* was reduced in mice colonized with MS-twin microbiota
- FMT from healthy donors to MS model mice showed marked reduction in *Sutterella* and protective immunoregulatory activity
- Preliminary human FMT studies in MS showed benefit but data remain very limited

### Antibiotic Therapy
- Early childhood antibiotic exposure may result in lower gut microbial diversity
- Reports about associations between antibiotic use and MS risk are conflicting
- Tetracycline-derived antibiotics (minocycline) have anti-apoptotic, anti-inflammatory, and antioxidant properties with neuroprotective effects in experimental disease models

## Relevance to Gut-Metal-Microbiome Axis

This expert consensus review validates that gut-directed interventions are a legitimate therapeutic avenue for [[multiple-sclerosis]]. The identification of specific taxa (*Sutterella*, *Prevotella*, *Streptococcus*) linking gut dysbiosis to MS autoimmunity provides targets that are known to be sensitive to [[heavy-metals]] exposure. The bile acid supplementation approach is particularly relevant as [[gut-microbiome]]-mediated bile acid metabolism is disrupted by both [[dysbiosis]] and metal exposure.

## Limitations

- Narrative review without systematic methodology
- Limited clinical trial evidence for most interventions
- Expert opinion may carry biases
