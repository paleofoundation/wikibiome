# Body Mass Index and Gastroesophageal Reflux Disease: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis

## Key Claims

- Increasing BMI is positively associated with [[gerd]] in US studies: overweight OR=1.57 (95% CI 1.36-1.80), obese OR=2.15 (P=0.10), demonstrating a dose-response relationship
- The BMI-GERD association became apparent only after stratification by country and level of BMI, with US studies showing the strongest association
- European studies provided heterogeneous results despite stratification, with individual studies showing both positive associations and no association
- Twenty studies including 18,346 GERD patients were included in the primary analyses across multiple countries and study designs
- The relationship supports weight reduction as a potential therapy for GERD
- Heterogeneity was significant across studies, likely due to differences in study design, exposure/outcome definitions, and population characteristics

## Methods/Findings

- **Study type**: Systematic review and meta-analysis
- **Search**: MEDLINE (1966-June 2005), Web of Science, manual review, expert bibliographies
- **Inclusion**: Studies evaluating obesity/BMI with reflux symptoms, esophagitis, or GERD-related hospitalization, reporting ORs with CIs
- **BMI categories**: Normal (<25), overweight (25-28/30), obese (>=28/30)
- **Studies included**: 20 studies (cross-sectional, case-control, cohort) from China, Spain, USA, Japan, Italy, Sweden, UK, Norway, Australia
- **Stratification**: By BMI level, gender, study design, population source, country, confounder adjustment
- **Publication bias**: Assessed by correlation coefficient (P^2<=0.1) and funnel plot analysis
