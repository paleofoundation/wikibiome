# Diagnosis and Treatment of Multiple Sclerosis: A Review

## Summary

This JAMA clinical review summarizes current evidence on the diagnosis and treatment of [[multiple-sclerosis]] (MS). It covers epidemiology, pathophysiology, diagnostic criteria (2017 McDonald Criteria), disease-modifying therapies (DMTs), and clinical management. The review is a comprehensive clinical reference for MS management.

## Epidemiology

- MS affects ~900,000 people in the US
- Mean age of onset: 20-30 years
- Prevalence: 5-300 per 100,000 (higher at higher latitudes)
- Female:male ratio ~3:1
- Life expectancy is less than general population (75.9 vs 83.4 years)
- HLA-DRB1*15:01 is the most frequent genetic risk factor (OR 2.92)
- Childhood obesity, cigarette smoking, and EBV infection are associated risk factors

## Diagnosis

- Based on 2017 McDonald Criteria: demonstration of dissemination of MS disease characteristics in space and time
- Sensitivity of 68% and specificity of 61% for a first classic clinical attack
- Alternative diagnoses must be excluded: other CNS inflammatory conditions, sarcoidosis, hereditary disorders, infections, neoplastic diseases, vascular diseases

## Disease-Modifying Therapies

Comprehensive table of DMTs with relapse rate reductions:
- **Interferons**: 34-37% reduction
- **Glatiramer acetate**: 29% reduction
- **S1P receptor modulators** (fingolimod, siponimod, ozanimod): 31-55% reduction
- **Fumarates** (dimethyl, diroximel): 44-53% reduction
- **Monoclonal antibodies** (natalizumab, ocrelizumab, alemtuzumab, cladribine): various mechanisms targeting B cells, T cells, or lymphocyte trafficking
- Ocrelizumab is the only DMT approved for PPMS

## Treatment Strategy

- Escalation approach: start with least potent medications, escalate if disease activity increases
- Alternative: initiate higher-efficacy medication at diagnosis
- Initial higher-efficacy treatment may be associated with lower rate of conversion to SPMS

## Relevance to Gut-Metal-Microbiome Axis

This review provides essential clinical context for understanding how DMTs affect the immune system, which in turn shapes the [[gut-microbiome]]. Many DMTs alter lymphocyte populations and cytokine profiles that are also influenced by [[gut-microbiome]] composition and [[microbial-metabolites]]. The environmental risk factors noted (EBV, smoking, vitamin D, obesity) overlap with factors known to influence [[dysbiosis]], suggesting that the [[gut-brain-axis]] may mediate some of these associations.

## Limitations

- Does not address microbiome-based biomarkers or therapies
- Limited discussion of environmental exposures beyond established risk factors
