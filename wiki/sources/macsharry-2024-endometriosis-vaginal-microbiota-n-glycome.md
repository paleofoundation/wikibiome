## Key Findings

- **Endometriosis patients harbour a distinct vaginal microbiota** characterised by loss of dominant *Lactobacillus* species (especially *L. crispatus*), increased bacterial diversity, and enrichment of species such as *Anaerococcus senegalensis*, *Prevotella jejuni*, *Porphyromonas bennonis*, and *Anaerococcus octavius* ([[macsharry-2024-endometriosis-vaginal-microbiota-n-glycome]], cross-sectional, n=40).
- **Vaginal microbiota clustered into four distinct groups** based on dominant species: Group 1 (*L. crispatus*-dominant), Group 2 (*L. iners*-dominant), Group 3 (*L. gasseri*-dominant), and Group 4 (*Gardnerella vaginalis*-dominant). Endometriosis patients were enriched in Groups 3 and 4, which displayed the highest alpha diversity.
- **N-glycome profiles correlate with endometriosis-specific microbiota**: core fucosylated mono-antennary glycans (GP2) from IgG and trigalactosylated/triantennary serum glycans correlated with vaginal presence of *A. senegalensis* (rho = -0.6957, p = 0.0417 for IgG GP2; rho = 0.5018, p = 0.0226 for serum G3; rho = 0.4991, p = 0.0241 for serum A3).
- **Four previously unrevealed modified N-glycans** were identified on urinary IgG: A1GalNAc1[SO4^-2]1, FA1GalNAc[SO4^-2]1, A2G1GalNAc1[SO4^-2]1S1, and FA2G1GalNAc1[SO4^-2]1S1.
- **Urine IgG glycosylation differs significantly from serum IgG** in both controls and endometriosis patients, with increases in sialylation, galactosylation, oligomannosylation, and bisecting glycans, and decreases in branching and core fucosylation in urine IgG.

## Methods

- **Study design**: Cross-sectional observational study. Patients underwent laparoscopy for investigation of infertility, pelvic pain, or pelvic pathology suspected on ultrasound scan at the National Maternity Hospital, Dublin, Ireland.
- **Participants**: 40 women — 19 controls, 11 mild/minimal endometriosis (rASRM stage I-II), 10 moderate/severe endometriosis (rASRM stage III-IV). None had used hormonal medications or anti-inflammatory therapy for at least three months prior to surgery.
- **Sample types**: High vaginal swabs, peripheral blood serum, and fasting urine samples.
- **Microbiota analysis**: Shotgun metagenomic sequencing (WGS) of vaginal swabs using Illumina NovaSeq platform (paired-end 150 bp). Bioinformatic analysis via CosmosID-HUB Microbiome Platform. Average 63.29 M reads per sample.
- **Glycan analysis**: HILIC-UPLC for N-glycan profiling of whole urine glycoproteins and IgG from urine and serum. LC-MS for structural confirmation. 181 N-glycans identified from whole urine glycoproteins; 55 distinct structures on urinary IgG.
- **Statistics**: Non-parametric tests (Mann-Whitney, Kruskal-Wallis), Spearman correlations, Bonferroni and Holm-Sidak corrections for multiple testing. Significance at p <= 0.05.
- **Ethics**: Research Ethics Committee of the National Maternity Hospital, Dublin (Ref. No.: EC19.2018).

## Detailed Summary

### Vaginal Microbiota Composition

Shotgun metagenomic sequencing revealed four distinct vaginal microbiota groups based on dominant species. The *Gardnerella vaginalis*-dominant group (Group 4) displayed the highest alpha diversity (Simpson and Shannon indices). Endometriosis patients were enriched in microbiota groups 3 (*L. gasseri*-dominant) and 4 (*G. vaginalis*-dominant), which showed the highest diversity. All microbiota groups were significantly separate from each other (p = 0.001, PCOA Bray-Curtis based on bacteria).

Endometriosis was associated with a decrease in *Lactobacillus iners* compared to controls. Three bacterial species were found to be specific for endometriosis (occurring in more than 3 patients): *Anaerococcus senegalensis*, *Prevotella jejuni*, and *Anaerococcus octavius*. Additional endometriosis-enriched species included *Porphyromonas bennonis*, *Alloscardovia omnicolens*, *Corynebacterium* species, and *Candida albicans*.

*A. octavius* and *A. senegalensis* were both more specific for mild/minimal stages of endometriosis rather than moderate/severe disease. Alpha diversity based on GO (Gene Ontology) in combined kingdom was significantly increased in later stages of endometriosis.

Novel bacteria found in endometriosis patients included *Parvimonas*, *Facklamia*, and a *Streptococcus* phage phiARI0746. The presence of *Gardnerella vaginalis* + *Prevotella* species group in the endometrium was associated with a low abundance of *Lactobacillus* in infertile patients including individuals suffering from endometriosis.

### Urine N-Glycome

181 N-glycans from all glycoproteins were characterised from human urine samples. The main group of glycans from both control and endometriosis pooled samples were complex glycans, highly branched, over two-thirds galactosylated, and more than half sialylated. About half were also fucosylated.

The endometriosis-associated pool encompassed more complex glycans with a decrease in oligomannose and hybrid glycans. Significant changes included increased branching, polylactosamine extensions, galactosylation and sialylation including presence of glycolylneuraminic acid. Fucosylation was altered — core fucosylation increased and outer arm fucosylation decreased. Sulfation was increased and acetylation decreased. After correction for multiple testing, there was no significant change in the overall identified urine glycome in endometriosis.

### Serum and Urine IgG N-Glycome

Urinary IgG glycosylation has not previously been described in the literature. The study identified 55 distinct structures on urinary IgG, including four previously unrevealed modified N-glycans containing sulfate groups. Urinary vs. serum IgG N-glycans showed significantly different glycosylation features: increased sialylation, galactosylation, oligomannosylation, and bisecting glycans in urine IgG, with decreased branching and core fucosylation. No significant differences were found in urinary IgG glycans between endometriosis and control groups.

### Microbiota-Glycome Correlations

N-glycome correlated with the endometriosis-specific microbe *A. senegalensis*: core fucosylated mono-antennary glycans (GP2) from IgG and trigalactosylated and triantennary glycans from serum showed significant correlations. Certain glycans also associated with other microbes not associated with endometriosis, and with clinical factors including endometrioma, peritoneal endometriosis, and age. Urine glycans associated with diabetic markers such as fasting glucose and HbA1c.

### Clinical Implications

The study supports the [[bacterial-contamination-hypothesis]] of endometriosis — that dysbiotic vaginal microbiota contribute to pathogenesis. Disruption of the dominant *Lactobacillus* population results in increased diversity with known pathogens. Dysbiotic microbiota may affect oestradiol metabolism by increasing circulating oestrogen in the body, resulting in a hyper-oestrogenic state known to contribute to endometriosis development. Host glycosylation of proteins interacting with pathogenic microbes may play a role in the aetiology.

## Contradictions / Tensions

- **Perrotta et al. (2020)** using 16S rRNA gene-based amplicon sequencing did not find changes in vaginal microbiome in endometriosis — this study using shotgun metagenomics (a more sensitive approach) found significant species-level differences. The methodological difference (16S vs WGS) likely explains the discrepancy rather than a true contradiction.
- **Fusobacterium**: Muraoka et al. found significantly more *Fusobacterium* (particularly *F. nucleatum*) in endometrial tissues and vaginal swabs of endometriosis patients. This study found *Fusobacteria* in only two laparoscopy-confirmed controls and one advanced endometriosis patient, suggesting *Fusobacterium* presence is not specific for endometriosis. The tissue (endometrial vs vaginal) and detection method differences may explain this.
- **Urinary IgG glycans**: No significant differences in urinary IgG glycans between endometriosis and controls, despite significant differences in whole urine glycoproteins and serum glycans — possibly due to low sample size or because urine IgG may not be impacted by endometriosis.

## Open Questions

1. **Do the endometriosis-specific vaginal microbiota changes cause or result from the disease?** Longitudinal studies with serial sampling at different timepoints are needed to establish temporal relationships.
2. **Can serum N-glycan profiles serve as non-invasive biomarkers for endometriosis diagnosis?** The correlations between specific glycans and endometriosis-specific microbiota are promising but need validation in larger, diverse cohorts.
3. **What is the mechanistic link between vaginal dysbiosis and altered glycosylation?** Do the pathogenic bacteria directly modify host glycosylation, or is it an indirect effect via inflammatory pathways?
4. **How do menstrual cycle phase and hormonal changes affect both the microbiota and glycome simultaneously?** Samples were collected at different cycle phases, and this was not consistently controlled.
5. **Do the four novel sulfated N-glycans on urinary IgG have functional significance in endometriosis immunology?** Their role in immune function and potential as biomarkers is unexplored.
6. **What metal dependencies do the endometriosis-enriched anaerobic species (*A. senegalensis*, *P. bennonis*, *A. octavius*) have?** This would connect to the metallomic layer of the endometriosis signature and identify potential Achilles' heels (Karen's Brain Primitive 4).
