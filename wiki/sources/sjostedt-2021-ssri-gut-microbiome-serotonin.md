# Key Claims

- **Majority of serotonin** in the human body is produced within the gut, and SSRIs affect enteric neurons
- **SSRIs exhibit antimicrobial properties** that can disrupt microbial hemostasis
- **The role of the gut-brain axis** and gut microbiome in psychopharmacology should be more highlighted
- **SSRI side effects and tachyphylaxis** may be partially explained by microbiome interactions
- Proposes a **paradigm shift** in psychopharmacology to include microbiome considerations

# Methods & Findings

**Hypothesis and theory article** proposing a framework for understanding SSRI-microbiome interactions.

- Reviewed evidence for SSRIs affecting gut microbiome composition
- Highlighted antimicrobial properties of SSRIs
- Discussed implications for treatment response variability
- Proposed microbiome as mediator of SSRI side effects and loss of efficacy (tachyphylaxis)
- Called for more personalized and tailored treatment approaches

# Relevance to ASD Framework

- **SSRIs commonly prescribed in ASD** for comorbid anxiety and depression
- **Serotonin dysregulation** is one of the most consistent neurochemical findings in ASD
- **SSRI-microbiome interaction** may affect gut-brain axis already disrupted in ASD
