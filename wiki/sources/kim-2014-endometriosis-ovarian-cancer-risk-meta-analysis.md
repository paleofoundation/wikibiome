# Risk and Prognosis of Ovarian Cancer in Women with Endometriosis

## Key Findings

- **Endometriosis significantly increased ovarian cancer risk** (RR 1.265, 95% CI 1.214-1.318) in this meta-analysis of multiple epidemiological studies ([[kim-2014-endometriosis-ovarian-cancer-risk-meta-analysis]], systematic-review-meta-analysis)
- **Endometriosis-associated ovarian cancer (EAOC)** showed favorable prognostic characteristics including earlier stage at diagnosis
- **Clear cell carcinoma and endometrioid carcinoma** were the histological subtypes most strongly associated with endometriosis, consistent with the "endometriosis to cancer" transformation hypothesis
- **EAOC patients had better overall survival** compared to non-EAOC patients, likely attributable to earlier stage at detection and favorable histology <!-- NEEDS VERIFICATION: exact survival statistics -->
- **The risk elevation, while statistically significant, is modest** (approximately 27% increased relative risk), suggesting that endometriosis is one of multiple contributing factors

## Methods

Systematic review and meta-analysis of studies examining the association between endometriosis and ovarian cancer risk and prognosis. Included both case-control and cohort studies. Risk ratios calculated using random-effects meta-analysis models. Subgroup analyses performed by histological subtype and disease stage. <!-- NEEDS VERIFICATION: exact number of studies included and total participant count -->

## Detailed Summary

This meta-analysis provides robust quantitative evidence for the long-debated association between endometriosis and ovarian cancer. Endometriosis, characterized by ectopic endometrial tissue growth, affects approximately 10% of reproductive-age women and has been hypothesized to be a precursor lesion for certain OC subtypes.

The finding that the association is strongest for clear cell and endometrioid subtypes supports a biological continuum model: endometriotic lesions undergo progressive genetic alterations (ARID1A mutations, PI3K pathway activation) that can lead to malignant transformation. The iron-rich microenvironment of endometriotic cysts ("chocolate cysts") is a plausible driver of this transformation, as chronic iron exposure generates reactive oxygen species that cause oxidative DNA damage and genomic instability.

The favorable prognosis of EAOC may reflect the fact that these cancers arise in a context of clinical surveillance (women with endometriosis are monitored more closely) and that the endometrioid and clear cell subtypes, while aggressive locally, tend to present at earlier stages and may respond differently to treatment than high-grade serous carcinoma.
