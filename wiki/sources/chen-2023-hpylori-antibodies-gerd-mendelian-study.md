# Causal relationship between Helicobacter pylori antibodies and gastroesophageal reflux disease (GERD): A mendelian study

## Key Claims

- Genetically predicted serum levels of [[Helicobacter pylori]] IgG antibodies were positively associated with an increased risk of [[GERD]] (OR=1.001, 95% CI 1.000-1.003, P=0.043), supporting a causal link
- No causal relationship was found between other specific H. pylori antibodies (CagA, VacA, UreA, hydrogen peroxide enzyme, OMP, GroEL) and GERD individually
- The overall IgG antibody finding suggests that H. pylori infection (as indicated by IgG seropositivity) may slightly increase GERD risk, contrary to some observational studies suggesting a protective effect
- The MR approach eliminates confounding from observational studies that have shown conflicting results regarding H. pylori-GERD relationships
- H. pylori prevalence surpasses 60.3% globally, exceeding 80% in some economically disadvantaged regions

## Methods/Findings

- **Study type**: Two-sample univariable Mendelian randomization
- **Data sources**: IEU GWAS database; H. pylori antibody data from 16,404 individuals; GERD candidate genes from 4,360 cases and 458,650 controls (European ancestry)
- **H. pylori antibodies tested**: Seven specific protein antibodies (IgG, CagA, VacA, UreA, hydrogen peroxide enzyme, OMP, GroEL)
- **IV selection**: p-value threshold of 5E-06; LD clumping R^2<0.001 within 10,000 kb; F-statistic >10
- **Methods**: IVW, MR-Egger, weighted median, weighted mode, simple mode; Cochran's Q for heterogeneity
- **Key limitation**: Effect size for overall IgG is very small (OR barely above 1.0), suggesting clinical significance may be limited
