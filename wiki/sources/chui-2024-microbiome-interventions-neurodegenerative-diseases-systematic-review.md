# Effects of Microbiome-Based Interventions on Neurodegenerative Diseases: A Systematic Review and Meta-analysis

## Summary

This systematic review (PROSPERO: CRD42023457490) and meta-analysis evaluates the efficacy of microbiome-modulating interventions (diet, biotics, [[FMT]], medications) on neurodegenerative diseases (NDDs) including [[multiple-sclerosis]], Parkinson's disease, Alzheimer's disease, and ALS. It included 15 clinical trials from 4,051 screened articles.

## Key Findings

- Most microbiome-modulating interventions changed gut microbiome composition
- Despite inconsistent changes in composition, meta-analysis showed interventions improved disease burden: **SMD = -0.57 (95% CI -0.93 to -0.21; I-squared = 42%; P = 0.002)** with qualitative trend of improvement in constipation
- Overall risk of bias was moderate in most studies

### MS-Specific Findings (5 studies)
- Three RCTs and two single-arm studies specifically on MS
- Sample sizes ranged from 9 to 50 (small)
- Study durations varied from 1 week to 1 year
- Interventions: probiotic supplementation, dietary intervention (intermittent fasting), FMT, and multidimensional programs
- **FMT in MS (Al et al.)**: Pilot RCT; no significant change in alpha/beta diversity; donor-specific alterations; normalized intestinal permeability post-FMT
- **Mediterranean diet + rehabilitation (Barone et al.)**: Partial recovery of dysbiosis; improved MFIS-5, 6MWT; decreased proinflammatory CD4+/IFN-gamma+, Th1 cells
- Three of four MS studies reported reduced inflammatory response or improved autoimmune response

## Relevance to Gut-Metal-Microbiome Axis

This meta-analysis provides the first quantitative evidence across NDDs that microbiome-modulating interventions improve disease burden (SMD = -0.57). For [[multiple-sclerosis]], the specific MS studies reviewed show that FMT normalizes intestinal permeability and dietary interventions can partially reverse [[dysbiosis]] with concomitant immune benefits. These findings support the therapeutic potential of targeting the [[gut-brain-axis]] in MS, and the moderate effect size suggests room for optimization -- potentially by addressing environmental drivers of [[dysbiosis]] such as [[heavy-metals]] exposure alongside microbiome interventions.

## Limitations

- Small sample sizes across all NDD studies
- High methodological heterogeneity
- Only 5 MS studies included, with inconsistent intervention types
- Short follow-up periods in most studies
