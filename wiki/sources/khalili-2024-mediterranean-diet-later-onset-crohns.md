# Key Claims

- Greater adherence to a Mediterranean diet is associated with a significantly lower risk of later-onset Crohn's disease (HR=0.42, 95% CI 0.22-0.80 for highest vs lowest mMED score) but not ulcerative colitis
- Poor adherence to Mediterranean diet (mMED score 0-2) confers a population attributable risk of 12% for later-onset CD, meaning 12% of later-onset CD cases could theoretically be prevented
- The association was not modified by age, sex, education level, BMI, or smoking (all P_interaction >0.30)
- The protective effect is specific to CD and not UC, consistent with the greater environmental contribution to later-onset IBD

# Methods/Findings

- **Study type**: Prospective cohort study; 83,147 participants aged 45-79 years from Swedish Mammography Cohort (SMC) and Cohort of Swedish Men (CoSM)
- **Dietary assessment**: Validated 96-item semi-quantitative food frequency questionnaire (SFFQ); modified Mediterranean diet score (mMED, 0-8 points)
- **Follow-up**: Average 17 years through December 2017; 164 incident CD and 395 incident UC cases
- **CD results**: P_trend = 0.03 across mMED categories; HR for highest category = 0.42 (95% CI 0.22-0.80)
- **UC results**: P_trend = 0.61; HR = 1.08 (95% CI 0.74-1.58) -- no significant association
- **mMED components**: Fruits/vegetables, legumes/nuts, whole grains, fermented dairy, fish, olive oil use, moderate alcohol; low red/processed meat
