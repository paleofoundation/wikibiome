# Sex, Gut Microbiome, and Cardiovascular Disease Risk

## Summary

Review examining key sex differences in gut microbiome interactions with four primary determinants of [[cardiovascular-disease]]: blood pressure, lipid metabolism, glucose metabolism, and body weight. Proposes that gut microbiota-dependent metabolites may be key mediators of sexual dimorphism in CVD onset and progression.

## Key Findings

### Sexual Dimorphism in CVD Risk Factors
- CVD mortality rates approximately 32% in men and 35% in women
- Age-adjusted CVD mortality higher in men compared to premenopausal women
- Nearly 50% of women in Western countries will die from coronary heart disease or stroke
- Key risk factor differences tabulated across glucose regulation, dyslipidemia, hypertension, and obesity (Table 1)

### Sexual Dimorphism in Microbiome-Related Metabolites
- **BCAAs**: Men have increased serum BCAAs and decreased branched-chain 2-oxoacid dehydrogenase; women show increased branched-chain 2-oxo acid and dehydrogenase activity
- **SCFAs**: Men show decreased SCFAs with decreased dietary fiber intake and decreased PPAR-gamma; women show increased susceptibility to dyslipidemia via 17-beta-estradiol-mediated PPAR-gamma increase
- **[[tmao]]**: Women have greater thrombotic risk; increased TLR and TMA activation of platelets; accelerated TMA production compared to men; sex hormones regulate hepatic FMO3 expression and secondary bile acid FXR activation
- **LPS**: Sex differences in TLR4/TLR2 signaling; estrogens, progesterone, and testosterone regulate LPS-mediated signaling through TLR4

### Key Mechanistic Model (Figure 1)
- **Dyslipidemia**: Men show decreased PPAR-gamma activation and increased total bile acid pool with increased FXR activation; women show increased PPAR-gamma activation and increased secondary bile acids with increased FXR activation
- **Hypertension**: Men show Lactobacilli-mediated inhibition of ACE1 with increased Th17/Treg ratio and decreased SCFAs; women show increased Lactobacilli-mediated ACE1 inhibition with decreased Th17/Treg ratio and increased SCFAs
- **Obesity**: Men show decreased F/B ratio in setting of high BMI with decreased LPS and decreased SREBP1 expression; women show increased F/B ratio with increased LPS
- **TMAO and General CVD Risk**: Men show decreased platelet TLR mRNA transcripts and decreased FMO3 expression; women show increased platelet TLR mRNA transcripts and increased FMO3 expression

### Gut Microbiome and Disease Markers
- TMAO associated with increased CVD risk and events in humans and animals
- Heart failure patients have increased TMAO levels compared to age- and sex-matched controls
- Elevated TMAO associated with shorter survival in heart failure
- Firmicutes/Bacteroidetes ratio perturbations may provide insight into gut health
- Few studies have specifically explored gut microbiome sex differences between men and women
