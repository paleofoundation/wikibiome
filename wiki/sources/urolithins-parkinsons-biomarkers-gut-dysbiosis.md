# Urolithins: Potential Biomarkers of Gut Dysbiosis and Disease Stage in Parkinson's Patients

## Summary

This study investigates urolithins -- microbial metabolites of dietary ellagic acid (from pomegranates, berries, nuts) -- as potential biomarkers of gut [[dysbiosis]] and disease stage in [[parkinsons-disease]]. Urolithin production depends on specific gut bacteria, making urolithin levels a functional readout of microbiome capacity.

## Key Findings

- Urolithin profiles differ between PD patients and healthy controls
- Urolithin A (the most common and beneficial metabolite) production is reduced in PD
- Urolithin-producing bacteria (*Gordonibacter*, *Ellagibacter*) are altered in PD gut microbiomes
- Urolithin levels correlate with disease stage (Hoehn-Yahr) in PD
- Urolithins have mitophagy-promoting effects relevant to PD neurodegeneration
- Urolithin A enhances mitochondrial function and reduces neuroinflammation in models
- Urolithin profiling offers a non-invasive functional biomarker of PD-associated dysbiosis

## Relevance to Gut-Metal-Microbiome Axis

Urolithins connect polyphenol metabolism to the metal axis in [[parkinsons-disease]]. Ellagic acid and its urolithin metabolites are potent [[iron]] chelators that bind Fe2+ and Fe3+ in the gut lumen, reducing dietary [[iron]] absorption. Urolithin A specifically promotes mitophagy -- the selective degradation of damaged mitochondria that accumulate [[iron]] and generate ROS in PD neurons. Reduced urolithin production in PD means less gut-level [[iron]] chelation and less mitophagy of iron-laden mitochondria, compounding the [[ferroptosis]] risk. The urolithin-producing bacteria (*Gordonibacter*) are members of the Eggerthellaceae family, which are sensitive to [[nickel]] and [[lead]] exposure. Environmental metal exposure could selectively suppress these beneficial organisms, reducing both polyphenol bioactivation and metal chelation capacity. Urolithin supplementation or restoration of urolithin-producing bacteria could address the metal axis.

## Limitations

- Cross-sectional study
- Urolithin production depends on dietary ellagic acid intake (not controlled)
- Only a subset of individuals harbor urolithin-producing bacteria ("metabotype" variation)
- Small study cohort
