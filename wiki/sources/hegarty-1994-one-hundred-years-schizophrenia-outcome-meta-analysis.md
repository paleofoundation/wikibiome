# One Hundred Years of Schizophrenia: A Meta-Analysis of the Outcome Literature

## Key Claims

- Meta-analysis of international outcome literature from 1895 to 1992 identified 821 studies; 320 (51,800 subjects in 368 cohorts) met inclusion criteria
- Only 40.2% of patients were considered improved after follow-ups averaging 5.6 years (range 1-40)
- Outcome was significantly better when diagnosed using broad criteria (46.5% improved) or undefined criteria (41.0%) rather than narrow criteria (27.3%)
- Proportion of improved patients increased significantly after mid-century (1956-1985: 48.5% vs 1895-1955: 35.4%), reflecting improved treatment and broadened diagnostic concepts
- In the last decade studied, the average rate of favorable outcome declined to 36.4%, perhaps reflecting re-emergence of narrow diagnostic concepts
- Less than half of patients diagnosed with [[schizophrenia]] showed substantial clinical improvement after ~6 years of follow-up
- Treatment predominantly involved neuroleptics (47.0%), followed by convulsive methods (27.4%) and lobotomy (3.5%)

## Methods/Findings

- **Study type**: Meta-analysis of outcome studies spanning 100 years
- **Cohorts analyzed**: 368 cohorts from 320 studies
- **Outcome measure**: Proportion of patients showing favorable outcome (broadly or narrowly defined)
- **Key moderator**: Diagnostic system used (Kraepelinian vs broader systems)
