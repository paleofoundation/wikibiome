## Summary

This study is the first to attempt to explain the mechanism of iron acquisition by Streptococcus pneumoniae. The authors demonstrate that S. pneumoniae requires iron for viability and identify two membrane proteins (22 and 37 kDa) that bind both hemoglobin and haem, potentially serving as iron acquisition receptors.

## Key Findings

- S. pneumoniae can support growth using hemoglobin (Hb) and haem as sole iron sources, but not holo-transferrin or holo-lactoferrin
- Iron starvation arrested cellular growth but cells remained viable; supplementation with Hb restored growth and viability within 5 hours
- Under iron starvation, only ~50% of cells remained viable at 5 hours; Hb supplementation recovered viability to ~90%
- Seven membrane proteins were purified by haem-affinity chromatography (22, 37, 43, 45, 50, 67, and 71 kDa)
- The 22 kDa protein was identified as a stress general protein; 37 kDa as a lipoprotein; 43 kDa as elongation factor Tu; 45 kDa as maltose binding protein
- Only the 22 and 37 kDa proteins bind both Hb and haem and compete for the tetrapyrrole ring (Vit B12)
- Amino acid alignment of the 22 and 37 kDa proteins revealed the KVAFDH motif essential for haem binding, conserved with S. pyogenes Shr protein
- These are the first findings attempting to explain how S. pneumoniae acquires iron from Hb or haem in the host

## Relevance to Wiki

Reveals that S. pneumoniae depends on hemoglobin and haem (not transferrin) as iron sources, relevant to understanding how iron status and metal availability in the host affects susceptibility to pneumococcal infection and how bacterial metal acquisition intersects with host nutritional immunity.
