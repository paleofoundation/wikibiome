# Key Findings

- **Paradoxical relationship**: in the general population, increased heavy metal levels were associated with **decreased eGFR** (kidney decline), but in environmentally vulnerable areas, eGFR **increased** with higher metal levels ([[rho-2025-heavy-metals-kidney-function-paradoxical]], cross-sectional).
- In the **general population**, a rise in urinary cadmium increased the risk of eGFR decline by **19.9%**.
- In **vulnerable areas**, higher urinary cadmium **reduced** eGFR decline risk by **23.3%** — possibly due to long-term exposure leading to reduced renal excretion (reverse causation).
- Integrated heavy metal concentration (Pb + Hg + Cd combined) was higher in vulnerable areas than the general population.

# Methods

- Comparative analysis using KoNEHS (4th cycle, general population) and FROM study (vulnerable areas, n=1,157).
- eGFR classified as normal, mildly reduced, or impaired.
- Blood lead, mercury measured; urinary cadmium measured.
- Correlation and logistic regression analyses.

# Contradictions / Tensions

- Directly contradicts the simple model of "more metal = worse kidney function" — the paradoxical finding in vulnerable areas likely reflects survivor bias or reduced excretion capacity.
