# Key Findings

- **Trichotomy framework**: The paper proposes three non-mutually-exclusive roles for the gut microbiome in psychiatric illness: (1) **causal driver** — microbiome shifts actively modulate neurobiological circuits governing mood and cognition; (2) **pathophysiological consequence** — microbiota alterations arise from psychiatric symptoms, diet, medication, or stress; (3) **bidirectional interplay** — gut microbial changes and psychiatric pathology influence each other in a feedback loop. Most existing literature conflates these, making therapeutic translation difficult.
- **SCFA mechanistic evidence**: Butyrate, propionate, and acetate modulate neuroinflammation (NLRP3 inflammasome inhibition), fortify gut and blood-brain barriers (JAM-A/ZO-1 stabilisation), shape synaptic plasticity (LTP via cAMP/PKA-dependent CREB phosphorylation), and epigenetically regulate BDNF via HDAC inhibition. Butyrate signals through GPCRs FFAR2 and FFAR3 modulating neuroplasticity and immune tone. These are among the most mechanistically well-supported pathways for gut-brain causation [[kamath-2025-gut-microbiome-mental-health-causation-correlation-review]].
- **Tryptophan pathway**: Microbial states divert tryptophan from serotonin (5-HT) synthesis toward the kynurenine pathway via IDO1 activation. In the brain, astrocytes produce neuroprotective kynurenic acid while microglia produce neurotoxic quinolinic acid — creating a neuroinflammatory balance disrupted in schizophrenia and depression. Over 90% of serotonin originates in intestinal enterochromaffin cells; germ-free mice exhibit markedly reduced 5-HT with depressive phenotypes reversed by microbial colonisation.
- **FMT evidence**: Systematic review of 28 studies (11 preclinical, 9 preclinical with human donors, 8 clinical trials): consistent short-term behavioural/neurobiological changes across study types. Clinical trials showed transient HAM-D and HAM-A improvements (p<0.05) lasting 3-6 months before returning to baseline. Depressed-donor FMT into antibiotic-depleted rats induced depression-like behaviours and increased inflammation — strongest causal evidence in the review.
- **Methodological critique**: Most human evidence is cross-sectional; psychotropic medications significantly alter gut microbiota, confounding interpretation. Germ-free mouse models exhibit neurodevelopmental endophenotypes relevant to mental health even without microbiota, limiting translational validity. Longitudinal, mechanistically driven approaches and standardised dietary protocols required.

# Methods

Narrative review. Authors at 8 institutions spanning Australia, USA, UK, Ireland, Canada. IUPHAR-themed review. Covers mechanistic pathways (SCFAs, tryptophan, vagal circuits, immune modulation), FMT evidence, probiotic evidence, and methodological frameworks for causal inference in microbiome-mental health research.

# Relevance to Cureva

- **Framework for intervention confidence grading**: The trichotomy model directly maps onto triangle evidence requirements — causal driver evidence (preclinical FMT) supports I→f edges; bidirectional evidence supports f→D edges; clinical RCTs needed for I→D.
- **SCFA restoration strategies**: Butyrate/propionate supplementation as Cureva intervention hypothesis with mechanistic grounding in HDAC inhibition and BBB stabilisation.
- **FMT**: Emerging Cureva intervention candidate for schizophrenia and depression; evidence grade currently quasi-experimental/case-series; durable only if combined with dietary synchronisation.

# Contradictions / Tensions

- Clinical FMT effects dissipate within 3-6 months — challenges the assumption that microbiome restoration alone sustains psychiatric benefit. Suggests dietary co-intervention required.
- Germ-free mouse data (used as strongest causation evidence) may not translate to clinical populations due to abnormal neurodevelopment in the GF state.

# Open Questions

- Can microbiome signatures stratify psychiatric patients before symptom onset (biomarker use case)?
- What combination of FMT + prebiotic + dietary synchronisation achieves durable microbiome restructuring in schizophrenia?
- Do SCFA-producing taxa depletion patterns in schizophrenia share mechanistic pathways with those in depression — suggesting a shared intervention target?
