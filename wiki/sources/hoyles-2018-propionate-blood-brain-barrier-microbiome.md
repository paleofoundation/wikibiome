# Key Claims

- **Propionate** produced by colonic bacteria from dietary substrates interacts with the blood-brain barrier (BBB) via FFAR3 receptor
- **Physiologically relevant propionate concentrations** (1 microM) protect BBB properties in human brain endothelium in vitro
- **Propionate inhibited** pathways associated with non-specific microbial infections via a CD14-dependent mechanism
- **Suppressed LRP-1 expression** and protected the BBB from oxidative stress via NRF2 (NFE2L2) signaling
- Gut-derived microbial metabolites represent a **fourth facet of the gut-brain axis** beyond autonomic, immune, and neuroendocrine pathways

# Methods & Findings

**In vitro study** using human brain endothelial cells to investigate propionate-BBB interactions.

- Demonstrated expression of propionate receptor FFAR3 on human brain endothelium
- Propionate at physiological concentration (1 microM) enhanced BBB integrity
- CD14-dependent inhibition of pathways associated with microbial infections
- NRF2-mediated protection from oxidative stress
- Results suggest SCFAs directly modulate BBB permeability and immune function

# Relevance to ASD Framework

- **Propionic acid** is elevated in some ASD children and has been used to model ASD in rodents; this paper shows it can also be protective at physiological concentrations, suggesting a dose-dependent or context-dependent role
- **BBB integrity** is relevant to ASD pathogenesis as disrupted BBB may allow neurotoxic metabolites or immune factors to access the brain
- **NRF2 pathway** links to oxidative stress mechanisms implicated in ASD
