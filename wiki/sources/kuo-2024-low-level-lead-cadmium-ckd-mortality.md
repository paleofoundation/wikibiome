# Key Claims

- Elevated blood [[lead]] (>=1.5 ug/dL) independently associated with increased CKD risk (OR 1.41, 95% CI 1.15-1.74)
- Elevated blood [[cadmium]] (>=0.4 ug/L) independently associated with increased CKD risk (OR 1.23, 95% CI 1.03-1.46)
- Combined elevated lead and cadmium (Group 4) showed highest CKD risk (OR 1.65, 95% CI 1.27-2.14)
- Elevated cadmium associated with 42% increased mortality risk in CKD population (HR 1.42, 95% CI 1.07-1.88)
- Elevated cadmium associated with 40% increased mortality in non-CKD population (HR 1.40, 95% CI 1.24-1.58)
- Lead alone was NOT significantly associated with mortality in either group
- Synergistic interaction between lead and cadmium on mortality risk (HR 1.32, 95% CI 1.13-1.54, p for interaction < 0.01)

# Methods/Findings

- **Data**: NHANES 2003-2012, n = 24,810 participants; CKD defined as eGFR < 60 mL/min/1.73m2
- **CKD prevalence**: 3.9% (1,309 participants)
- **Follow-up**: Median 141 months (linked to National Death Index through Dec 31, 2019); 2,255 deaths
- **Exposure cutoffs**: Pb >= 1.5 ug/dL (39% had elevated); Cd >= 0.4 ug/L (40.3% had elevated)
- Mean Pb in CKD: 2.14 vs 1.58 ug/dL in non-CKD (p < 0.001)
- Mean Cd in CKD: 0.60 vs 0.53 ug/L in non-CKD (p < 0.01)
- Sensitivity analysis: both metals associated with albuminuria in those with preserved eGFR
- Socioeconomic disparities: elevated metals more common in lower education, lower income, smokers
