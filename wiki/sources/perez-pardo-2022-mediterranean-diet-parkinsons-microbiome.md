# Mediterranean Diet Adherence in People with Parkinson's Disease Reduces Constipation Symptoms and Changes Fecal Microbiome

## Summary

This study examines the association between Mediterranean diet adherence and gastrointestinal outcomes, microbiome composition, and [[short-chain-fatty-acids]] levels in [[parkinsons-disease]] patients. It provides evidence that dietary patterns rich in fiber, polyphenols, and healthy fats can modulate the PD gut microbiome.

## Key Findings

- Higher Mediterranean diet adherence scores correlate with reduced constipation severity in PD
- Mediterranean diet adherence is associated with increased abundance of SCFA-producing bacteria
- Fecal [[butyrate]] levels are higher in PD patients with greater Mediterranean diet adherence
- The Mediterranean diet promotes microbial diversity in PD gut microbiomes
- Polyphenol-rich components of the diet have prebiotic effects on beneficial bacteria
- Olive oil, nuts, and fish intake correlate with specific beneficial microbial shifts
- The Mediterranean diet may partially counteract PD-associated [[dysbiosis]]

## Relevance to Gut-Metal-Microbiome Axis

The Mediterranean diet addresses the gut-metal-microbiome axis through multiple mechanisms. Its high fiber content promotes [[butyrate]] production, restoring [[iron]] regulation via HIF-2alpha. Polyphenols (from olive oil, red wine, berries) are potent metal chelators that bind [[iron]], [[nickel]], and [[lead]] in the gut lumen, reducing their bioavailability. Omega-3 fatty acids from fish and nuts have anti-inflammatory effects that may reduce [[hepcidin]]-driven [[iron]] trapping. The diet is naturally low in processed foods that are significant sources of [[nickel]] contamination. The improvement in constipation directly reduces metal absorption by decreasing colonic transit time. This study supports the Mediterranean diet as a comprehensive dietary strategy for the gut-metal-microbiome axis in [[parkinsons-disease]].

## Limitations

- Observational/cross-sectional design; cannot prove causation
- Self-reported dietary data subject to recall bias
- Mediterranean diet adherence may be a marker of overall health-conscious behavior
- PD motor symptoms may impair ability to prepare Mediterranean-style meals
