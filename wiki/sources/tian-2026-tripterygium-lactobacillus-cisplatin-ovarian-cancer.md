## Key Findings

- **Tripterygium glycosides (TG) significantly enhanced cisplatin sensitivity** in epithelial ovarian cancer (EOC), either alone or synergistically with fecal microbiota transplant (FMT) from healthy donors ([[tian-2026-tripterygium-lactobacillus-cisplatin-ovarian-cancer]], animal-model).
- **TG inhibited the Keap1-Nrf2-GPX4 axis**, inducing tumor [[ferroptosis]] — iron-dependent regulated cell death — as the primary mechanism of chemosensitization.
- **Gut microbiota mediated TG's chemosensitizing effects**: antibiotic-depleted mice showed significantly reduced benefit from TG, demonstrating that an intact gut microbiome is required for TG efficacy.
- **Lactobacillus paracasei co-administration with TG produced synergistic effects**, identifying a specific probiotic organism that potentiates the TG-cisplatin combination.
- The findings establish a **gut microbiome-ferroptosis axis** in ovarian cancer chemosensitivity: the microbiome modulates the tumor's antioxidant defense (Nrf2-GPX4) and thereby its susceptibility to iron-dependent cell death.

## Methods

**In-vivo mouse models** of epithelial ovarian cancer with multiple treatment groups: cisplatin alone, TG alone, TG + cisplatin, TG + cisplatin + FMT (from healthy controls), TG + cisplatin + L. paracasei, and antibiotic-depleted mice + TG + cisplatin. FMT was used to test whether a healthy microbiome enhances treatment efficacy. Antibiotic depletion was used to test whether the microbiome is required for TG's effects. **Mechanistic analyses** included Western blotting for Keap1, Nrf2, and GPX4 protein levels; lipid peroxidation assays (ferroptosis markers); and tumor [[iron]] content measurement. Tumor volume, weight, and survival were the primary efficacy endpoints.

## Detailed Summary

Tian et al. investigated the intersection of traditional Chinese medicine (Tripterygium glycosides), probiotics (Lactobacillus paracasei), and conventional chemotherapy (cisplatin) in ovarian cancer, revealing a novel gut microbiome-ferroptosis axis that modulates chemotherapy sensitivity.

**Tripterygium glycosides (TG)** are bioactive compounds from *Tripterygium wilfordii* (thunder god vine), traditionally used in Chinese medicine for autoimmune conditions. TG has documented immunosuppressive and anti-inflammatory properties, and emerging evidence suggests anticancer activity through multiple pathways.

The **Keap1-Nrf2-GPX4 pathway** is a central antioxidant defense system: under normal conditions, Keap1 sequesters Nrf2 in the cytoplasm for proteasomal degradation; under oxidative stress, Nrf2 is released, translocates to the nucleus, and activates transcription of antioxidant genes including GPX4 (glutathione peroxidase 4). GPX4 is the only enzyme that can reduce lipid hydroperoxides within membranes, making it the critical gatekeeper against [[ferroptosis]]. Cancer cells often upregulate this pathway to resist ferroptosis — making Nrf2-GPX4 inhibition a promising chemosensitization strategy.

TG's inhibition of this axis allows accumulation of lipid peroxides in tumor cells, triggering ferroptosis. This is synergistic with cisplatin because cisplatin itself generates reactive oxygen species and depletes glutathione — when GPX4 is simultaneously inhibited by TG, the combined oxidative insult overwhelms cancer cell defenses.

The **microbiome dependency** of TG's effects is the most novel aspect: antibiotic depletion of the gut microbiota abolished TG's chemosensitizing benefit, indicating that gut bacteria are required — either for TG metabolism/activation, for systemic immune priming, or for modulation of tumor-level ferroptosis sensitivity through microbial metabolites. The specific identification of **[[lactobacillus]]** paracasei as a potentiating organism provides a defined probiotic intervention: L. paracasei may enhance TG efficacy through metabolite production (SCFAs, lactic acid), immune modulation (Treg/Th17 balance), or direct effects on systemic iron metabolism.

The [[iron]] connection is central: ferroptosis is iron-dependent cell death, and the gut microbiome profoundly influences systemic iron homeostasis through siderophore production, iron absorption regulation, and hepcidin signaling. A healthy microbiome (particularly Lactobacillus species) may promote the iron availability at the tumor level needed for ferroptosis while maintaining host iron homeostasis.

## Open Questions

- What specific metabolites does L. paracasei produce that potentiate TG's chemosensitizing effects — SCFAs, lactic acid, or specific secondary metabolites?
- Does the gut microbiome modulate tumor iron content directly, and is this the mechanism by which antibiotic depletion reduces ferroptosis induction?
- Can the TG + L. paracasei + cisplatin combination be translated to human clinical trials for platinum-resistant ovarian cancer?
- Whether other probiotic species (Bifidobacterium, Faecalibacterium) produce similar chemosensitizing effects through the ferroptosis pathway.
