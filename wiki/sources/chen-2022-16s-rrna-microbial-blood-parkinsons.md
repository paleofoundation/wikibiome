# Detection of Microbial 16S rRNA Gene in the Blood of Patients with Parkinson's Disease

## Summary

This study detects bacterial 16S rRNA gene sequences in the blood of [[parkinsons-disease]] patients, providing direct evidence that bacterial components (and potentially viable bacteria) translocate from the gut to the systemic circulation in PD.

## Key Findings

- Bacterial 16S rRNA gene was detected at higher levels in the blood of PD patients compared to controls
- The blood microbiome composition differed between PD and controls
- Gut-associated bacterial signatures were identified in the blood, supporting gut-to-blood translocation
- Blood bacterial load correlated with markers of gut permeability
- Pro-inflammatory bacterial taxa were enriched in PD blood samples
- Blood microbiome signatures correlated with disease severity

## Relevance to Gut-Metal-Microbiome Axis

Detection of bacterial DNA in PD blood provides direct evidence for the gut-to-systemic translocation that is central to the metallomic-microbiome framework. Translocated bacterial components (LPS, peptidoglycan) activate systemic immune responses that drive [[hepcidin]] expression and [[iron]] redistribution. Viable bacteria in the bloodstream carry their metal-acquisition systems with them, potentially disrupting [[iron]] homeostasis in tissues including the brain. The correlation between blood bacterial load and gut permeability confirms the gut barrier as the gateway for this systemic metal-immune disruption.

## Limitations

- Blood microbiome studies are technically challenging with high contamination risk
- Low biomass samples require rigorous controls
- 16S rRNA detection does not confirm bacterial viability
- Cross-sectional design
- Potential reagent contamination in low-biomass studies
