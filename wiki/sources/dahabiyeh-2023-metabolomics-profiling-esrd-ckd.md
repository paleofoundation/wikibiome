# Metabolomics Profiling Distinguishes ESRD from CKD

## Key Findings

- A total of **193 metabolites** were significantly altered in ESRD patients compared to non-dialysis CKD patients, indicating that the transition to ESRD involves broad metabolic reprogramming beyond simple uremic toxin accumulation.
- **Three kynurenine pathway derivatives** were markedly upregulated in ESRD, suggesting that tryptophan metabolism shifts toward the kynurenine pathway as kidney function declines — a pathway associated with inflammation, immune dysregulation, and neurotoxicity.
- **Taurine** levels were significantly altered, pointing to disruption of sulfur amino acid metabolism.
- **Branched-chain amino acids (BCAAs)** — leucine, isoleucine, valine — showed significant dysregulation, reflecting the catabolic state and altered protein metabolism in advanced CKD.
- The authors proposed that inhibiting the kynurenine pathway could potentially delay CKD-to-ESRD progression, though this remains a hypothesis.

## Methods

**Cross-sectional metabolomics study** using chemical isotope labeling liquid chromatography-mass spectrometry (CIL-LC-MS) on serum samples from 32 patients (13 ESRD on hemodialysis, 19 non-dialysis CKD). The CIL-LC-MS approach enabled detection and quantification of amine- and phenol-containing metabolites. Univariate and multivariate statistical analyses (PCA, PLS-DA) were applied to identify differentially abundant metabolites. Pathway enrichment analysis was performed to contextualize findings.

## Detailed Summary

Dahabiyeh et al. applied a targeted metabolomics approach to distinguish the metabolic profiles of ESRD patients from those with earlier-stage CKD. The study identified 193 metabolites that were significantly different between the two groups, far exceeding the handful of traditional uremic toxin markers used in clinical practice.

Among the most notable findings was the upregulation of kynurenine pathway metabolites in ESRD. Tryptophan is metabolized through two major routes — the serotonin pathway and the kynurenine pathway. In CKD, the balance shifts toward kynurenine production, driven by inflammation-induced activation of indoleamine 2,3-dioxygenase (IDO). Kynurenine and its downstream metabolites (3-hydroxykynurenine, quinolinic acid) are neurotoxic, immunomodulatory, and pro-oxidant — connecting metabolically to both the cognitive impairment and the cardiovascular complications seen in advanced CKD.

Taurine dysregulation was another key finding. Taurine is a conditionally essential amino acid with antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, and osmoregulatory functions. Its disruption in ESRD reflects both impaired renal handling and altered gut microbial metabolism of sulfur-containing amino acids.

BCAA alterations were consistent with the protein-energy wasting syndrome characteristic of advanced CKD, where muscle catabolism is accelerated and anabolic pathways are impaired.

## Relevance to WikiBiome

This study directly informs WikiBiome's understanding of the **metabolomic layer** of CKD. The kynurenine pathway upregulation is particularly significant because tryptophan metabolism is heavily influenced by the gut microbiome — indole-producing bacteria shunt tryptophan away from serotonin synthesis and toward indolic compounds, while inflammation-driven IDO activation shunts the remaining tryptophan toward kynurenine. Both pathways converge on the gut-kidney axis.

The identification of 193 differentially abundant metabolites provides a rich dataset for understanding how CKD reshapes the metabolic landscape — many of these metabolites are likely of microbial origin or are influenced by microbial co-metabolism.
