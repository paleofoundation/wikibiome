## Key Findings

- **Inflammation centrality**: Inflammatory reaction is well-established in general depression and is increasingly implicated as a core mechanism in postpartum depression (systematic review level).
- **Cytokine elevation**: Multiple pro-inflammatory cytokines (TNF-α, IL-1β, IL-6, IL-8) elevated in PPD, driving neuroinflammatory cascade (systematic review level).
- **Mechanistic pathways**: Inflammation impairs monoamine neurotransmission, HPA axis function, and neuroplasticity, creating mood dysregulation (systematic review level).

## Methods

Systematic review of inflammatory pathophysiological mechanisms in postpartum depression, examining cytokine dysregulation, inflammatory signaling pathways, and downstream effects on brain function and mood regulation.

## Detailed Summary

The review establishes inflammation as a core pathophysiological mechanism in PPD:

- **Peripheral inflammation**: Elevated circulating cytokines (TNF-α, IL-1β, IL-6, IL-8, IL-10) in postpartum depression
- **Central neuroinflammation**: Cytokine-mediated blood-brain barrier disruption, microglial activation, and astrocyte-mediated neuroinflammation
- **Serotonin dysregulation**: Inflammatory cytokines inhibit tryptophan metabolism via IDO pathway, reducing serotonin precursor availability
- **HPA axis**: Pro-inflammatory cytokines enhance glucocorticoid insensitivity and impair cortisol negative feedback
- **Neuroplasticity impairment**: TNF-α and IL-1β reduce brain-derived neurotrophic factor (BDNF), impairing hippocampal neurogenesis
- **Monoamine system**: Inflammation reduces dopamine and norepinephrine synthesis and turnover

The review highlights that understanding inflammatory mechanisms in PPD is crucial for developing both anti-inflammatory pharmacological interventions and microbiota-based approaches targeting dysbiotic-immune-brain interactions.
