# Key Findings

- **Cadmium exposure significantly perturbed gut microbiota composition** in wild hamsters, particularly enhancing potentially pathogenic bacteria such as **Prevotella and Treponema** ([[tao-2024-cadmium-gut-microbiota-hamsters]], animal model).
- **Organ damage**: Cd exposure caused liver, spleen, and kidney damage, with increased intestinal permeability and inflammation.
- **Metabolic function changes**: pathways related to disease and environmental information processing were notably enhanced, possibly indicating **stronger innate defense mechanisms** against external injuries.
- Cd exposure did **not significantly impact the overall structure** of the gut microbiota but shifted specific pathogenic taxa.
- The study used wild-caught hamsters from Cd-contaminated areas in Shanxi Province (heavy industrial region), providing ecological relevance.

# Methods

- Wild-caught male *Cricetulus longicaudatus* from Wutai, Lishi, Yuci, Xi, and Zuoquan counties, Shanxi Province, China.
- Cd exposure at 6, 12, 24, and 48 mg/L in drinking water for 35 consecutive days.
- Histopathological analysis, 16S rDNA sequencing, and metagenome sequencing of gut microbiota.
- HE staining for tissue damage assessment.

# Contradictions / Tensions

- Wild hamster model — species-specific microbiome may not translate directly to humans.
- Cd doses (6–48 mg/L) in drinking water may exceed typical human dietary exposure.

# Open Questions

- Do Cd-selected Prevotella and Treponema strains carry virulence factors relevant to human disease?
- Is the enhanced innate defense pathway response protective or maladaptive in the long term?
- Can Cd-induced dysbiosis be reversed by removing Cd exposure, or is it persistent?
