# The Effects of Gut Dysbiosis via Bacteriophages and Its Role in Parkinson's Disease

## Summary

This review synthesizes evidence for bacteriophage-driven gut [[dysbiosis]] as a pathogenic mechanism in [[parkinsons-disease]]. It elaborates on the Tetz group's hypothesis that lytic phages selectively depleting beneficial gut bacteria initiate a cascade of events leading to PD.

## Key Findings

- Bacteriophages are the dominant regulators of bacterial population dynamics in the gut
- Lytic phages targeting lactic acid bacteria (Lactococcus, Lactobacillus) are enriched in PD
- Phage-driven depletion of [[short-chain-fatty-acids]] (SCFA)-producing bacteria reduces gut barrier integrity
- Loss of bacterial-derived neuroprotective metabolites (butyrate, propionate) increases vulnerability to neurodegeneration
- Prophage induction under stress conditions may contribute to bacterial lysis events
- The phage-bacteria-brain axis represents a distinct pathway within the [[gut-brain-axis]]

## Relevance to Gut-Metal-Microbiome Axis

Phage-driven loss of SCFA-producing bacteria directly connects to metal [[dyshomeostasis]]. Butyrate and propionate maintain gut epithelial integrity and regulate [[iron]] absorption through modulation of [[HIF-2alpha]] and [[ferroportin]] expression. When phages eliminate these SCFA producers, [[iron]] absorption becomes dysregulated, contributing to the systemic and brain iron accumulation seen in [[parkinsons-disease]]. Additionally, SCFA depletion impairs [[zinc]]-dependent tight junction protein expression, increasing gut permeability to both metals and microbial products.

## Limitations

- Review article drawing on limited primary studies
- Phage-PD connection is still based primarily on correlational data
- The Tetz group's findings require independent replication
- Phage taxonomy and functional characterization are incomplete
