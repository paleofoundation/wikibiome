## Key Findings

- **Childhood asthma significantly increases PMD risk in adulthood**: aRR 1.20 (95% CI 1.07–1.34, p=0.001) for premenstrual disorders; symptom severity score β=0.13 (95% CI 0.08–0.19) ([[yang-2023-childhood-asthma-allergy-premenstrual-disorders]], prospective cohort, n=6,524)
- **Food allergy carries the strongest atopic association**: aRR 1.28 (95% CI 1.06–1.54) for PMD cases; egg and milk allergy specifically notable ([[yang-2023-childhood-asthma-allergy-premenstrual-disorders]])
- **All allergies**: aRR 1.11 (95% CI 1.00–1.24) for PMDs; β=0.09 for symptoms — weaker than asthma or food allergy ([[yang-2023-childhood-asthma-allergy-premenstrual-disorders]])
- **Probable PMDD > PMS**: associations stronger for the more severe PMDD end of the spectrum; PMDD cases showed higher aRR for asthma than PMS cases ([[yang-2023-childhood-asthma-allergy-premenstrual-disorders]])
- **Later-onset asthma > early-onset**: asthma present in adulthood aRR 1.34 vs. preschool-onset asthma only; preschool asthma did not independently predict PMDs after adjustment ([[yang-2023-childhood-asthma-allergy-premenstrual-disorders]])
- **Anxiety partially mediates** the asthma–PMD relationship; depression does not independently moderate the association; BMI does not explain the effect ([[yang-2023-childhood-asthma-allergy-premenstrual-disorders]])
- **19.3% of women** in this prospective cohort met criteria for PMDs at mean age 25.7 years; base rate consistent with existing prevalence estimates ([[yang-2023-childhood-asthma-allergy-premenstrual-disorders]])

## Methods

Prospective cohort. Growing Up Today Study (GUTS) — US cohort of children of Nurses' Health Study II participants. Enrolled 1996. Assessed 2013 for PMDs (n=6,524 female participants, mean age 25.7 years). Self- and mother-reported childhood asthma and allergy diagnoses assessed prospectively before age 18. PMDs evaluated using validated tools in 2013 (Daily Record of Severity of Problems, PRISM calendar). Asthma diagnosis: confirmed if self-reported at follow-up; allergy categories: allergic rhinitis, eczema, food allergy, hay fever. Log-binomial and linear regression models with three model stages (adjusting for BMI, parity, contraceptives, smoking, alcohol, physical activity). Cohort sub-analyzed by age at asthma onset (preschool 0–5, teen 6–12, young adult 12–17). Two cohort subgroups: GUTS I (1996) and GUTS II (2004).

## Detailed Summary

**Asthma–PMD relationship:**
Across multivariable models, a robust association was found between childhood asthma and PMD risk in adulthood (aRR 1.20, CI 1.07–1.34). This finding was independent of major confounders including maternal education, birth year, cohort membership, marital status, use of multivitamins, physical activity, smoking, alcohol, and family history of asthma/allergy. When further adjusting for BMI, parity, and contraceptive use, the aRR (1.20, CI 1.07–1.34) and β (0.13, CI 0.08–0.19) remained minimally changed — indicating a genuine relationship.

**Food allergy:**
Food allergy showed a statistically significant association with PMDs (aRR 1.28, CI 1.06–1.54), not explained by eczema (NS after full adjustment) or hay fever (NS). The food allergy–PMD connection was particularly notable for egg and milk allergy and was stronger for probable PMDD than for PMS. The authors propose that dietary antigen exposure and associated immune activation may share pathways with luteal-phase inflammatory signaling.

**Age-at-onset effect:**
Asthma with onset in teen (6–12) and young adult (12–17) years showed stronger associations with PMDs than asthma with preschool onset (0–5 years). Asthma persisting into adulthood was most strongly associated (aRR 1.34 vs. 1.23 for asthma at childhood only). This dose-duration pattern suggests that chronic systemic inflammatory burden — rather than atopic predisposition alone — drives the PMD risk.

**Mechanistic hypothesis:**
The authors propose shared inflammatory pathways: menstruation involves a local inflammatory cascade (prostaglandins, cytokines) in the endometrium; asthma and atopy are characterized by systemic allergic inflammation. Both involve inflammatory markers (IL-2, TNF-alpha) also implicated in PMDD. Additionally, serotonin levels are lower in asthmatic women; the ALLO-GABA pathway may be dysregulated by chronic cortisol/stress response from persistent asthma; and repeated allergic immune activation in early development may prime the immune system toward abnormal luteal-phase inflammatory responses in adulthood.

**PMD prevalence in cohort:**
19.3% met PMD criteria at mean age 25.7 years — consistent with published prevalence estimates of 20-30% for PMS and 3-8% for PMDD. Participants with PMDs were more likely to be overweight/obese, have anxiety/depression comorbidity, and have no current hormonal contraceptive use.

- **Clinical profiling:** Women with histories of childhood asthma, food allergy, or current atopy have significantly elevated PMDD risk — a useful clinical risk stratification factor
- **STOP consideration:** Inflammatory burden from poorly managed atopy may worsen PMDD; anti-inflammatory strategies may serve dual purposes

## Contradictions / Tensions

- The study relies on self- and mother-reported diagnoses of childhood asthma/allergy — potential recall bias
- Unable to distinguish PMDD from PMS in most analyses (combined PMD outcome); PMDD-specific estimates are post-hoc subanalyses
- No gut microbiome measurements taken; the food allergy → PMD pathway is speculative at the mechanistic level
- Cohort is predominantly White US women — limited generalizability to other ethnicities

## Open Questions

- Does atopy history correlate with the specific gut microbiome pattern (Bacteroidetes depletion, Escherichia enrichment) seen in PMDD?
- Is the food allergy–PMD association mediated by altered gut barrier function (leaky gut) driving systemic inflammation?
- Can anti-inflammatory interventions targeting atopy improve PMDD outcomes in asthmatic women?
- Does the timing of first asthma/allergy onset predict the PMDD severity subtype?
