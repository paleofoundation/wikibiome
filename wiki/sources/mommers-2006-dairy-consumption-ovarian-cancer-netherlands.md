## Key Findings

- **No association between consumption of milk, yoghurt, cheese, or fermented dairy products and ovarian cancer risk** in this large prospective cohort of Dutch postmenopausal women
- **Lactose intake was not associated with serous ovarian cancer risk**, contradicting the lactose-galactose hypothesis that proposed galactose as gonadotoxic
- **Dairy fat intake showed no association** with ovarian cancer risk across intake quartiles
- **The null finding is robust** due to the large cohort size (62,573 women), long follow-up (11.3 years), 252 incident OC cases, and prospective design that minimizes recall bias
- **Subgroup analyses** by histological type, dairy product type, and nutrient component consistently showed no significant associations

## Methods

- **Study design**: Prospective cohort study (Netherlands Cohort Study on Diet and Cancer, NLCS)
- **Population**: 62,573 postmenopausal women aged 55-69 at baseline (1986)
- **Follow-up**: 11.3 years of cancer incidence follow-up through record linkage
- **Exposure assessment**: Self-administered food frequency questionnaire at baseline covering detailed dairy product consumption
- **Outcomes**: Incident ovarian cancer cases (252 total), analyzed overall and by histological subtype (serous, mucinous, endometrioid)
- **Statistical analysis**: Cox proportional hazards models with adjustment for potential confounders (age, parity, oral contraceptive use, etc.)
- **Case-cohort design**: Subcohort of ~2,589 women for efficient analysis

## Detailed Summary

This prospective cohort study from the Netherlands Cohort Study on Diet and Cancer evaluates whether dairy product consumption is associated with ovarian cancer risk. The study was motivated by the "galactose hypothesis," which proposed that galactose (a component of lactose) is directly toxic to ovarian germ cells, and that populations with high dairy consumption should therefore have elevated ovarian cancer rates.

The NLCS is one of the largest prospective dietary studies in Europe, and this analysis benefits from its well-characterized cohort, validated food frequency questionnaire, and long follow-up period. The null finding across all dairy product types, fat components, and lactose intake provides strong evidence against a dairy-ovarian cancer association in this population.

The analysis examined multiple dairy product categories individually: total milk, buttermilk, yoghurt, cheese (Dutch, other), butter, and total dairy. Nutrient-level analyses examined lactose, calcium, total dairy fat, and saturated fat from dairy. None showed significant associations with ovarian cancer risk in multivariable-adjusted models.

Subgroup analyses by histological type (serous, mucinous, endometrioid/clear cell) also showed no associations, arguing against subtype-specific effects of dairy components. This is important because different ovarian cancer subtypes have distinct etiologies, and a subtype-specific effect might have been masked in overall analyses.

The study's limitations include the single baseline dietary assessment (which may not capture changes over the follow-up period), the predominantly white Dutch population (limiting generalizability), and the relatively modest number of ovarian cancer cases despite the large cohort.

From a microbiome perspective, the null finding is relevant because fermented dairy products (yoghurt, cheese) contain live bacterial cultures and could theoretically influence cancer risk through microbiome modulation. The absence of any dairy-OC association suggests that the microbiome-modulating properties of fermented dairy do not measurably affect ovarian cancer risk in this population and dietary context.

## Open Questions

- Would studies in populations with different dairy consumption patterns or genetic backgrounds (e.g., lactose persistence vs. non-persistence) yield different results?
- Does the specific microbial content of fermented dairy (which varies by product and region) matter for cancer-related outcomes?
- Could dairy-microbiome interactions affect ovarian cancer risk through pathways not captured by this epidemiological design?
- Do metal contaminants in dairy (cadmium from animal feed, lead from processing) have separate effects on ovarian cancer risk independent of dairy nutrients?
