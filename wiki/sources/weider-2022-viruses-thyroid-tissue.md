# High Prevalence of Common Human Viruses in Thyroid Tissue

## Key Findings

- **75% of thyroid tissue samples (40/53) carried at least one infectious virus**; 23 samples (43%) had a single virus, 17 samples (32%) were coinfected with two or more virus types, and only 13 samples (25%) were virus-free ([[weider-2022-viruses-thyroid-tissue]], cross-sectional, n=53)
- **Enterovirus was the most prevalent virus in thyroid tissue**: PCR detected it in 19/53 (36%), immunofluorescence (IF) in 25/53 (47%), with combined detection in 27/53 (51%); IF was more sensitive than PCR for enterovirus ([[weider-2022-viruses-thyroid-tissue]], cross-sectional, n=53)
- **HHV-6 was detected in 16/53 (30%) samples** with PCR and IF always concordant; parvovirus B19 in 12/53 (23%); EBV in 3/53 (6%); CMV in 1/53 (2%); HCV in 0/53; all five gastroenteric virus groups (adenovirus, astrovirus, norovirus, rotavirus, sapovirus) were negative ([[weider-2022-viruses-thyroid-tissue]], cross-sectional, n=53)
- **Virus distribution was NOT statistically different between AITD cases and controls**: enterovirus-positive samples were slightly higher in AITD (albeit not significantly: 67% HT, 50% GD, 63% controls); HHV-6 and PV-B19 also showed no significant group differences ([[weider-2022-viruses-thyroid-tissue]], cross-sectional, n=53)
- **This is the first study exploring multiple viral agents in thyroid tissue** using a combined approach of virus enrichment in five cell lines followed by molecular detection (PCR) and protein visualization (immunofluorescence) ([[weider-2022-viruses-thyroid-tissue]], cross-sectional, n=53)

## Methods

- **Study type**: Cross-sectional tissue analysis study
- **Population**: 53 thyroid tissue samples from Oslo University Hospital: 15 HT (core needle biopsy), 20 GD (core needle biopsy or surgical samples), 18 controls (neck surgery for non-thyroid reasons, e.g., thyroid tumors or parathyroid adenomas; controls with positive anti-thyroid antibodies excluded)
- **Patient characteristics**: GD: mean age 47.5, disease duration 12.0 months (median), TSH <0.03, FT4 27.6, TRAb 218.0; HT: mean age 45.0, TPOAb 927.0; Controls: mean age 53.2
- **Cell culture enrichment**: Five cell lines (AV3, RD, Ht-29, VC3, HEK293) selected for susceptibility to enteroviruses and HHV-6. Thyroid homogenates incubated with cell line mix, 2-3 serial passages, then extracted for RNA/DNA (PCR) and fixed for immunofluorescence (IF).
- **PCR detection**: Enterovirus (5 primer pairs targeting 5'UTR), HHV-6 (pan-HHV-6 primers targeting CEP2/TRM3), plus qPCR via WGA for CMV, EBV, PV-B19, HCV, adenoviruses F40-F41, astrovirus species 1-8, norovirus GI and GII, rotaviruses, sapoviruses
- **Immunofluorescence**: Anti-EV mAbs (9D5, 6-E9/2, 5-D8/1 for VP1; 3D-02 and 3D-05 for 3D RNA polymerase); anti-HHV-6 mAb for 140kDa capsid polypeptide
- **Blood donor controls**: 19 healthy blood donors from Varese, Italy, tested for viral presence in peripheral blood leukocytes
- **Statistics**: Student's T-test, Mann-Whitney U, Pearson's chi-square; significance at p<0.05

## Detailed Summary

### Enterovirus Detection

PCR detected enteroviruses in 19/53 (36%) thyroid specimens. IF was more sensitive, with 25/53 (47%) showing positive VP1 capsid protein staining with granular cytoplasmic fluorescence. Combined PCR+IF concordance was 17 samples. Overall enterovirus positivity: 27/53 (51%). Enterovirus sequences matched coxsackievirus A6, enterovirus A71, echovirus E19, echovirus E20, rhinovirus C strains (5'UTR region analysis). Enteroviruses were more frequent in AITD vs controls (albeit not significantly): 67% in HT, 50% in GD, 63% in controls.

### HHV-6 Detection

16/53 (30%) samples were HHV-6 positive, with PCR and IF always concordant. The 140kDa capsid protein showed perinuclear cytoplasmic fluorescence. The sensitive PCR method targeting CEP2-TRM3 could not distinguish HHV-6A from HHV-6B. Detection rates: 35% in GD, 27% in HT, 28% in controls — not statistically different.

### Parvovirus B19

12/53 (23%) samples were PV-B19 PCR positive. A single GD case carried rotavirus close to the detection limit. None of the five gastroenteric virus groups (adenovirus, astrovirus, norovirus, rotavirus, sapovirus) were detected otherwise. EBV in 3 (6%), CMV in 1 (2%), HCV in 0.

### Coinfection Patterns

The most common coinfections were enterovirus + HHV-6, and enterovirus + PV-B19. A single virus was found in 23/53 (43%), while 17/53 (32%) harbored two or more different agents. Viral load was consistently low but variable; inter-sample differences were insignificant and could not be related to clinical status.

### Blood Donor Comparison

Of 19 healthy blood donors, viruses were rarely detected in peripheral blood leukocytes: single separated cases of EV, HHV-6, and EBV in three donors, confirming that viral persistence is tissue-specific rather than systemic.

## Relevance to WikiBiome

- Foundational for understanding the thyroid virome and viral trigger hypothesis for [[hashimotos-thyroiditis]] and [[graves-disease]]
- Enterovirus as the most common thyroid virus (51%) informs viral entity pages and [[viral-microbiota]] concept
- HHV-6 prevalence (30%) connects to emerging literature on herpesvirus reactivation in autoimmune disease
- The lack of AITD-specific viral enrichment challenges simple causal models and supports a "bystander infection + genetic predisposition" framework
- Methodology (cell culture enrichment + molecular + IF) represents gold standard for thyroid tissue virome characterization

## Relevance to Cureva

- **Clinical interpretation**: The high prevalence of viruses in both AITD and control thyroid tissue means viral detection alone cannot serve as a diagnostic marker
- **Mechanism consideration**: Viral infection may be a necessary but not sufficient trigger for AITD — genetic predisposition and additional factors (metals, microbiome) determine whether infection leads to autoimmunity
- The multi-virus co-infection finding (32% of samples) suggests that viral burden, not single virus identity, may be relevant
- Connects to [[parrish-2025-blood-dna-virome-autoimmune-diseases]] on virome complexity in autoimmune disease

## Contradictions / Tensions

- The lack of significant difference between AITD and controls contradicts simpler viral causation models for thyroid autoimmunity
- IF was substantially more sensitive than PCR for enterovirus (47% vs 36%), raising questions about detection sensitivity in earlier PCR-only studies
- The control group included patients undergoing neck surgery (thyroid tumors, parathyroid adenomas), which may not represent truly healthy thyroid tissue
- Small sample sizes per group (15 HT, 20 GD, 18 controls) limit power to detect moderate group differences

## Open Questions

- Does viral load (rather than mere presence) differ between AITD and controls? More quantitative methods needed
- Do specific enterovirus serotypes correlate with AITD risk? Serotype resolution was limited by 5'UTR sequencing
- Is viral infection of the thyroid a cause, cofactor, or bystander in AITD? Longitudinal studies needed
- How does the thyroid virome interact with the gut microbiome in AITD pathogenesis?
- Could antiviral therapy targeting persistent thyroid enterovirus or HHV-6 improve AITD outcomes?
- Do metal exposures (selenium deficiency, mercury) alter viral susceptibility of thyroid tissue?
