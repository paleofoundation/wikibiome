# Overview

Combined *in vivo* rat study and clinical study exploring the relationship between **indomethacin (NSAID)-induced intestinal dysbiosis**, autoimmune hepatitis (AIH), and ASD. Demonstrates that NSAID-induced dysbiosis and AIH are robust triggers for ASD, with identified biomarkers holding promise for early diagnosis.

# Key Findings

## Animal Study

- Indomethacin (3 mg/kg/day for 4 weeks) administered to juvenile and adult Wistar rats
- Induced intestinal dysbiosis confirmed by microbiological analysis showing **positive bacterial translocation in blood cultures**
- Disturbed intestinal permeability: widened intestinal tight junctions, decreased mucosal thickness, inflammatory cell infiltrates, and collagen deposition
- Activated **NLRP3 inflammasome pathway**: elevated TLR4, IL18, caspase-1, and JAK1 in liver tissue
- Altered liver function: elevated **ASMA and ANA** (autoimmune hepatitis markers) in serum

## Clinical Study

- 100 male children with ASD (ages 4-6) without AIH history
- 60 children with ASD who also had **AIH and history of NSAID intake** during early life
- Children exposed to NSAIDs + dysbiosis + AIH exhibited elevated serum levels of: NLRP3, IL8, liver enzymes, ASMA, ANA, JAK1, and IL6
- **Correlation analysis**: positive relationship between measured parameters and **ASD severity**
- Identified markers as potential indicators for early diagnosis and prognosis of ASD

## Mechanistic Pathway

- NSAIDs --> gut dysbiosis --> bacterial translocation --> intestinal permeability disruption
- --> immune activation in liver tissue --> AIH development
- --> systemic inflammation (NLRP3/IL18/caspase-1) --> BBB disruption --> neuroinflammation --> ASD
