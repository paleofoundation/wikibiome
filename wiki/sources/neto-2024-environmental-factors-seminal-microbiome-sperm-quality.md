## Key Findings

- **The male reproductive tract harbors a diverse microbiome** — all NGS-based studies identified bacteria in all semen samples, with dominant phyla being Proteobacteria, Bacteroidetes, Firmicutes, and Actinobacteria, and dominant genera including **Lactobacillus**, **Pseudomonas**, **Gardnerella**, **Prevotella**, **Streptococcus**, and **Staphylococcus** ([[neto-2024-environmental-factors-seminal-microbiome-sperm-quality]], cross-sectional review, n=9,310).
- **Bacteriospermia is associated with impaired semen parameters** — multiple studies report reduced sperm concentration, motility, and morphology in men with bacteriospermia, though culture-based studies often fail to establish statistically significant associations ([[neto-2024-environmental-factors-seminal-microbiome-sperm-quality]]).
- ***Prevotella* enrichment is a recurring marker of poor semen quality** — significantly enriched in men with abnormal semen analysis parameters across multiple independent cohorts, while *Lactobacillus* predominance is associated with normal morphology ([[neto-2024-environmental-factors-seminal-microbiome-sperm-quality]]).
- **Sexually active couples share 56% of predominant genera** between seminal and vaginal microbiomes, and 61% of couples with positive cultures shared at least one genital pathogen ([[neto-2024-environmental-factors-seminal-microbiome-sperm-quality]]).
- **High-fat diet alters gut microbiota composition and reduces sperm quality** — HFD decreased Bacteroidetes and Verrucomicrobia while increasing Firmicutes and Proteobacteria, with downstream effects on sperm concentration, motility, and spermatocyte/round spermatid numbers ([[neto-2024-environmental-factors-seminal-microbiome-sperm-quality]]).

## Methods

**Study design:** Comprehensive narrative review of all studies on the male reproductive tract microbiome in humans from 1980 to 2023.

**Methods assessed:** Culture-based (16 studies), NGS-based (16 studies), combination methods (5 studies). Sample types included semen and testicular tissue.

**Quality assessment:** Study Quality Assessment Tool for Before-After (Pre-Post) Studies with no Control Group, developed by the National Heart, Lung, and Blood Institute. Studies categorized as high, medium, or low quality.

**Inclusion:** 37 studies comprising 9,310 participants total. Notably, none of the included studies assessed fungi or viruses.

## Detailed Summary

### Seminal Microbiome of Healthy Men

Studies examining the testicular microbiome revealed the testicular compartment is not sterile. The dominant phyla identified across healthy men include **Proteobacteria**, **Bacteroidetes**, **Firmicutes**, and **Actinobacteria**. At the genus level, **[[lactobacillus]]**, **[[pseudomonas]]**, **[[gardnerella]]**, **[[prevotella]]**, **[[streptococcus]]**, and **[[staphylococcus]]** were most frequently reported.

Notable findings in healthy men:
- *Lactobacillus*-enriched group predominated in men with normal semen analysis and normal morphology
- *Streptococcus*-enriched group predominated in men with leukocytospermia
- Three broad microbiota profiles identified: *Prevotella*-dominant, *Lactobacillus*-dominant, and polymicrobial
- Substantial variability in species composition among individuals, with no single dominant species

### Semen Quality Abnormalities

**Oligozoospermia** (low sperm concentration): Associated with enrichment of *[[prevotella]]*, *[[escherichia-coli]]*, *Lactobacillus*, *Shuttleworthia*, and *[[serratia]]* in the study by Okwelogu et al. An inverse association between *Prevotella* abundance and sperm concentration was corroborated by Lundy et al.

**Asthenozoospermia** (reduced motility): Men showed increased abundance of *Sneathia*, *Ralstonia*, *[[ureaplasma]]*, *[[bacteroides]]*, and *Chryseobacterium*. *Lactobacillus* was suggested as a potential bacterial biomarker (ROC AUC 0.841). *Parabacteroides*, *Lachnospira*, and *Phascolartobacterium* were more abundant in men with oligoasthenozoospermia.

**Oligoasthenoteratozoospermia (OAT)**: Associated with *Cyanobacteria* and *[[fusobacterium]]*, increased prevalence of *[[pseudomonas]]*, *[[klebsiella]]*, *[[aerococcus]]*, *Actinobaculum*, and *Neisseria*, and decreased *Lactobacillus* and *Propionibacterium*.

**Azoospermia**: *Lactobacillus*, *[[enterococcus]]*, *[[corynebacterium]]*, *[[veillonella]]*, and *[[gardnerella]]* were the most abundant genera in azoospermic men.

**Leukocytospermia**: *Streptococcus*-enriched bacterial community predominated; increased prevalence of *Bacteroidetes* associated with leukocytospermia. The mean proportion of Proteobacteria was higher in leukocytospermic men.

### Oxidative Stress and DNA Fragmentation

Bacteriospermia was associated with reduced sperm concentration and motility. A large study of nearly 5,000 infertile men identified a negative association between culture-positive semen and sperm DNA integrity. However, culture-based methods found no differences in sperm DNA fragmentation between men with or without bacteriospermia.

### Inflammatory Markers

*Staphylococcus* isolated from semen of healthy men degraded IL-10 and IL-17 more intensely than from infertile men. *Enterococcus* from infertile men reduced IL-1 levels, and *Corynebacterium* reduced TNF-alpha levels to a greater extent than isolates from healthy subjects. Culture-based studies did not establish an association between semen bacteria and seminal inflammatory markers.

### Diet (Gut Microbiome Connection)

High-fat diet (HFD) altered gut microbiota composition with downstream effects on spermatogenesis:
- Decreased *Bacteroidetes* and *Verrucomicrobia*
- Increased *Firmicutes* and *Proteobacteria*
- Gut microbiota in the HFD group showed increased *Bacteroides*, *Prevotella*, *Rikenella*, and *Lactobacillus*
- Strong negative correlation between sperm motility and combined abundance of *Bacteroides* and *Prevotella*
- *Prevotella copri* specifically implicated in spermatogenic defects

### Sexual Transmission

Partnered sexual activity influences seminal microbiome composition. Couples shared 56% of predominant genera. Men who had never had sexual intercourse exhibited lower total seminal bacterial concentration and diversity. Men who have sex with men showed overlap between semen and vaginal bacteria of their partners. HPV infection was associated with reduced semen quality.

### Impact on ART Outcomes

- Semen samples from couples with clinical pregnancy after ICSI exhibited increased *Lactobacillus jensenii* and *Faecalibacterium*, with decreased *Proteobacteria*, *Prevotella*, and *Bacteroidetes*
- *Alphaproteobacteria*, *Gammaproteobacteria*, and *Corynebacterium* sp. in raw semen were associated with reduced embryo quality
- *Enterobacteriaceae* group associated with improved embryo quality
- Bacteria in IVF culture media did not seem to impact pregnancy rates

## Relevance to Cureva

- **Signature development**: The review maps which taxa are enriched vs. depleted in various semen abnormalities — this feeds the taxonomic layer of a male infertility / erectile dysfunction signature. Key pattern: *Prevotella* enrichment + *Lactobacillus* depletion = impaired semen quality.
- **Intervention hypotheses**: The HFD-to-gut-dysbiosis-to-spermatogenesis pathway suggests dietary interventions targeting gut *Prevotella* and *Bacteroides* abundance could improve semen parameters. *Lactobacillus*-based probiotics may have a role given the protective association.

## Contradictions / Tensions

- **Bacteriospermia and fertility outcomes are inconsistent**: Multiple culture-based studies found no significant association between bacteriospermia and semen parameters, while NGS-based studies consistently found associations. This methodological divide is a key unresolved tension — culture-based approaches miss the majority of seminal bacteria.
- **IVF culture media bacteria**: Despite clear associations between seminal microbiome composition and semen quality, bacteria in IVF culture media did not seem to impact pregnancy rates, suggesting the effect may be upstream of fertilization itself.
- **Inflammatory markers**: *Staphylococcus* and *Enterococcus* from healthy vs. infertile men showed opposite cytokine modulation patterns, yet culture-based studies failed to link seminal bacteria to inflammatory markers overall. Strain-level variation may explain this discrepancy.
- **Contamination concerns**: The review acknowledges that 50-70% of detected bacterial reads from testicular samples may represent contamination, casting doubt on the testicular microbiome concept specifically (Molina et al., 2021).

## Open Questions

1. **What is the functional role of the seminal microbiome?** Most studies are descriptive (who is there) rather than functional (what are they doing). The review explicitly calls for in vitro and ex vivo systems to study host-microbiome interactions in the reproductive tract.
2. **Does the gut-reproductive axis operate through metals?** The HFD-dysbiosis-spermatogenesis pathway was documented without metal measurements. Given that HFD alters metal absorption and that Proteobacteria enrichment in the gut implies siderophore competition, the metallomic layer is entirely unexplored.
3. **Are there strain-level differences that explain the conflicting bacteriospermia findings?** The same genus (e.g., *Staphylococcus*, *Lactobacillus*) shows protective and pathogenic associations depending on the study. Strain-level resolution is needed.
4. **What role do fungi and viruses play?** None of the 37 included studies assessed fungi or viruses in the seminal microbiome. This is a major gap given known fungal-bacterial interactions in other body sites.
5. **Can seminal microbiome profiling predict IVF outcomes?** The association between *Lactobacillus jensenii* / *Faecalibacterium* and clinical pregnancy after ICSI suggests biomarker potential, but no prospective validation exists.
