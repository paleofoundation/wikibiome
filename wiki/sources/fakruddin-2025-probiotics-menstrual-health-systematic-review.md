## Key Findings

- **L. casei for PMS**: randomized controlled evidence (Pérez et al. 2015, n=100 women with PMS, 3 months) showing significant reduction in self-rated irritability, bloating, and mood symptoms; probiotic supplementation reduced inflammatory response and decreased cytokine activity ([[fakruddin-2025-probiotics-menstrual-health-systematic-review]], systematic review, 15 studies)
- **Vaginal microbiome restoration**: L. rhamnosus GR-1 + L. fermentum RC-14 (Reid et al. 2003 RCT, n=64 healthy women) significantly altered vaginal flora and reduced bacteriuria; L. crispatus (Petrova et al. 2015) identified as Lactobacillus strains as biomarkers of vaginal health ([[fakruddin-2025-probiotics-menstrual-health-systematic-review]])
- **Dysmenorrhea**: multiple studies indicate probiotics reduce inflammatory markers (prostaglandins) associated with uterine contractions; women with dysmenorrhea who supplemented described less intense cramps and reduced reliance on OTC analgesics during menstruation ([[fakruddin-2025-probiotics-menstrual-health-systematic-review]])
- **PCOS**: L. rhamnosus GR-1 supplementation → improved insulin sensitivity, lowered androgen levels, and promoted menstrual regularity — addressing both metabolic and hormonal components via gut-microbiome axis ([[fakruddin-2025-probiotics-menstrual-health-systematic-review]])
- **Estrobolome modulation**: Lactobacillus strains regulate estrogen metabolism via the estrogen–gut-microbiome axis (Baker et al. 2017); microbiota-mediated estrogen recirculation influences systemic hormone levels and menstrual cycle regularity ([[fakruddin-2025-probiotics-menstrual-health-systematic-review]])
- **Bacterial vaginosis (BV)**: L. crispatus, L. rhamnosus, L. reuteri reduce vaginal pH and BV recurrence; oral probiotic supplementation modulates vaginal environment via gut-vagina immune axis ([[fakruddin-2025-probiotics-menstrual-health-systematic-review]])

## Methods

Systematic review. PubMed, Scopus, Web of Science, and Google Scholar searched in English. Keywords: "probiotics AND menstrual health," "probiotics AND dysmenorrhea," "probiotics AND PMS," "probiotics AND PCOS." Inclusion criteria: (1) investigated role of probiotics in menstrual/reproductive disorders; (2) included human participants; (3) provided mechanistic data on microbiome, hormonal regulation, or inflammatory markers. Excluded: studies without empirical evidence or published outside English. 15 studies included after full-text review from 416 initial records. Thematic synthesis: (1) probiotics and infection/BV control; (2) probiotics and menstrual pain disorders; (3) probiotics and metabolic hormonal conditions (PCOS). No meta-analysis due to heterogeneity in designs.

## Detailed Summary

**Mechanism overview:**
Probiotics address menstrual disorders through three interconnected mechanisms: (1) modulation of gut-vaginal microbiota balance via competitive exclusion and immune regulation; (2) systemic hormonal modulation through the estrogen–gut-microbiome (estrobolome) axis; (3) reduction of inflammatory cytokines that drive menstrual pain, PMDD mood symptoms, and PCOS metabolic dysfunction. This multi-pathway capacity makes probiotics particularly suitable for the overlapping pathophysiologies of menstrual disorders.

**PMS/PMDD-specific evidence:**
Pérez et al. (2015): L. casei supplementation for 3 months produced a statistically significant reduction in self-rated PMS irritability, bloating, and mood symptoms compared to baseline. Miller et al. (2016) review confirmed that multi-strain probiotics reduce PMS-related irritability and depression symptoms, likely via serotonin modulation through the gut-brain axis. Probiotics may also function as a supportive adjunct for mood-related symptoms of PMS, influencing the gut-brain axis so that probiotics may alleviate mood-related symptoms such as irritability and depression.

**PCOS evidence:**
L. rhamnosus GR-1 → restored vaginal flora, lowered vaginal pH, inhibited bacteriuria (Reid et al. 2003). Foster et al. (2016): gut dysbiosis exacerbates PCOS symptoms through disrupting metabolic pathways; probiotics counter this by improving insulin sensitivity and lowering androgen levels. Probiotic supplementation in PCOS patients was associated with improved menstrual regularity and hormone profile normalization.

**Dysmenorrhea evidence:**
Probiotics associated with reduction in inflammatory cytokines (prostaglandins and COX-2 pathway mediators); women receiving probiotics described reduced reliance on OTC analgesics (NSAIDs) during menstruation. Mechanism: reduction of inflammation-mediated uterine contractions.

**Estrobolome mechanism:**
The estrogen-gut microbiome axis (Baker et al. 2017; Al-Nakkash & Herbst-Kralovetz 2017): microbial activity in the gut regulates estrogen metabolism, influencing systemic hormone levels and menstrual cycle regularity. Lactobacillus species support estrogen balance by maintaining the correct bacterial community structure in the gut; dysbiosis → altered estrogen recirculation → dysregulated hormone levels → menstrual disorder susceptibility.

## Contradictions / Tensions

- Small sample sizes in included studies (n=64–100 in RCTs)
- High variability in probiotic strains, dosages, and administration routes across studies — limits standardized recommendations
- Mechanistic studies (in vitro, animal) included alongside RCTs; evidence hierarchy mixed within thematic groups
- No included study specifically assessed PMDD (DSM-5 diagnosis) vs. general PMS — extrapolation to PMDD is inferential

## Open Questions

- What is the optimal Lactobacillus strain combination for PMDD mood symptoms specifically?
- Does probiotic cycling (synchronized to the menstrual cycle, higher dose in luteal phase) improve efficacy over continuous dosing?
- Is the estrobolome mechanism measurable as a biomarker to guide probiotic selection in individual PMDD patients?
