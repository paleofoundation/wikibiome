# Association of Intestinal Dysbiosis with Susceptibility to Multiple Sclerosis: Evidence from Different Population Studies (Review)

## Summary

This comprehensive review examined 77 studies (60 reviews, 4 case-controls, 4 systematic reviews, 10 in vitro, 13 animal, 3 case reports, 1 meta-analysis) on the relationship between intestinal [[dysbiosis]] and [[multiple-sclerosis]] susceptibility. It covers evidence from multiple populations and synthesizes the mechanistic pathways linking gut microbiota to MS pathogenesis.

## Key Findings

### Gut Microbiota and MS Pathogenesis
- [[dysbiosis]] affects intestinal permeability, allowing chronic low-grade bacterial translocation that overstimulates immune cells in the CNS
- Toxins, inflammatory molecules, and [[oxidative-stress]] molecules can pass freely into the CNS when the blood-brain barrier is compromised
- Commensal bacteria such as *Lactobacillus* and *Bacteroides fragilis* produce neurotransmitters (histamine, dopamine, norepinephrine, acetylcholine, serotonin, GABA) important for neurological regulation

### Immune Mechanisms
- Autoreactive CD4+ T cells are activated at the intestinal mucosal surface by dysbiotic microbiota
- *Clostridium* XIVa and IV clusters and *Bacteroides fragilis* induce Foxp3+ regulatory T cells (Tregs) that suppress [[inflammation]]
- Direct binding of *Streptococcus* sp. and *Lactobacillus* ligands to C-type lectin-positive macrophages increases IL-10 expression
- *Bifidobacterium* promotes IL-10/IL-10R alpha circuit in intestinal Treg cells
- GM-derived neurotransmitters have direct CNS activity via the [[gut-brain-axis]]

### Pro-inflammatory Dysbiosis in MS
- Dysbiosis-elevated pro-inflammatory cytokines activate indoleamine 2,3-dioxygenase and tryptophan 2,3-dioxygenase, diverting tryptophan from serotonin to kynurenine pathway (relevant to [[indoles]])
- Approximately 90% of serotonin is synthesized by gut enterochromaffin cells and is mediated by the microbiome
- CNS-directed suppressor mechanisms are disrupted: self-reactive B cells and T cells become activated and cross the BBB

### Therapeutic Approaches
- [[probiotics]] show promise: *Lactobacillus fermentum* reduces AFBI levels in liver and kidneys, modulates immune response
- *Lactobacillus* supplementation decreased IL-6 and increased anti-inflammatory cytokines in MS patients
- *Bifidobacterium infantis* and *Lactobacillus casei* showed significant inflammatory pathway modulation

## Relevance to Gut-Metal-Microbiome Axis

This review reinforces the [[gut-metal-microbiome]] framework by documenting how [[dysbiosis]] enables CNS [[inflammation]] in MS through multiple converging pathways. The protective taxa depleted in MS (*Clostridium* clusters XIVa/IV, *Bacteroides fragilis*, *Prevotella*) are precisely those most vulnerable to [[heavy-metals]] toxicity. The review's emphasis on environmental factors triggering dysbiosis aligns with the hypothesis that chronic [[lead]], [[cadmium]], and [[nickel]] exposure initiates and maintains the dysbiotic state driving MS [[neuroinflammation]] via the [[gut-brain-axis]].

## Limitations

- Narrative review; no meta-analytic quantification
- Heterogeneous study designs and populations
- Limited discussion of specific environmental toxicant-microbiome interactions
