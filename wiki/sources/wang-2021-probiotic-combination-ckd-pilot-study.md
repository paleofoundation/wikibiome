# Probiotic Combination in CKD: Mouse Model and Clinical Pilot

## Key Findings

- A specific **probiotic combination** (Lactobacillus acidophilus, Bifidobacterium longum, and Bifidobacterium bifidum) attenuated **renal fibrosis** and improved renal function in an adenine-induced CKD mouse model.
- In the clinical pilot, the rate of **eGFR decline was significantly slower** in the probiotic group compared to the control group over a 6-month intervention period, suggesting that probiotics may slow CKD progression.
- **Serum inflammatory markers** — TNF-alpha, IL-6, IL-18, and endotoxin (LPS) — were significantly decreased after probiotic administration in the clinical cohort, indicating reduction in both systemic inflammation and gut-derived endotoxemia.
- The probiotic combination showed effects at both the mechanistic level (fibrosis reduction in mice) and the clinical level (eGFR preservation and inflammatory marker reduction in humans), providing translational evidence across species.
- The reduction in endotoxin levels suggests that probiotics improved **intestinal barrier integrity**, reducing bacterial translocation — a key pathogenic mechanism in CKD.

## Methods

**Two-component study**: (1) **Animal model** — adenine-induced CKD in mice, with probiotic treatment group and controls. Renal histology, fibrosis markers, and renal function were assessed. (2) **Clinical pilot study** — 38 patients with stage 3-5 CKD received a 6-month probiotic intervention (Lactobacillus acidophilus, Bifidobacterium longum, Bifidobacterium bifidum). eGFR, serum creatinine, inflammatory cytokines (TNF-alpha, IL-6, IL-18), and serum endotoxin were measured at baseline and after intervention. The clinical component was not randomized or blinded.

## Detailed Summary

Wang et al. investigated a specific three-strain probiotic combination in both a CKD animal model and a human clinical pilot study, providing complementary mechanistic and clinical data.

In the mouse model, adenine-induced CKD produced progressive renal fibrosis, elevated BUN and creatinine, and histological kidney damage. Probiotic treatment attenuated these changes, with treated mice showing less tubulointerstitial fibrosis, lower expression of fibrotic markers (TGF-beta, alpha-SMA), and improved renal function parameters. The mechanism likely involves reduction of gut-derived inflammatory stimuli — by strengthening the intestinal barrier and reducing bacterial translocation, probiotics decrease the delivery of LPS, uremic toxin precursors, and other pro-inflammatory molecules to the kidney.

In the clinical pilot, 38 CKD patients (stages 3-5) received the same probiotic combination for 6 months. The key clinical outcome — rate of eGFR decline — was significantly slower in the probiotic group. This is a clinically meaningful endpoint because even modest slowing of eGFR decline can delay the need for dialysis by months to years. The reduction in inflammatory markers (TNF-alpha, IL-6, IL-18) and endotoxin provides mechanistic support for the clinical benefit.

The study's limitations include the small sample size, single-arm design (historical or concurrent controls, not randomized), lack of blinding, and absence of detailed microbiome analysis to confirm whether probiotics actually altered gut microbial composition. The specific strains chosen — Lactobacillus acidophilus, Bifidobacterium longum, and B. bifidum — are among the most commonly studied probiotic organisms, with established safety profiles but variable efficacy across different clinical contexts.

## Relevance to WikiBiome

This study provides preliminary but promising evidence for **microbiome-targeted intervention** in CKD, demonstrating effects at both the mechanistic (fibrosis reduction, barrier restoration) and clinical (eGFR preservation) levels. The reduction in endotoxin is particularly relevant — endotoxemia is a direct consequence of gut barrier failure, which in CKD is driven by uremia, dysbiosis, and loss of SCFA-producing commensals.

The use of Bifidobacterium and Lactobacillus species connects to WikiBiome's framework of microbial metal dependencies: these genera have relatively low iron requirements and do not express siderophores, making them ecologically disadvantaged in iron-replete or iron-supplemented environments. Understanding why these probiotics work (or fail) in CKD may require considering the metal environment of the CKD gut.

## Open Questions

- Would a randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial confirm the eGFR-preserving effects seen in this pilot?
- Does iron supplementation (standard in many CKD patients) undermine probiotic efficacy by favoring iron-pirating pathogens over iron-modest probiotics?
- Can the probiotic effects be enhanced by co-administration of prebiotic fiber to provide substrate for the introduced organisms?
