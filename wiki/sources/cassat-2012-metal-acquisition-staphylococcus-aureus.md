## Summary

A comprehensive review of metal ion acquisition systems in Staphylococcus aureus and how the pathogen overcomes host nutritional immunity (the process by which vertebrate hosts sequester transition metals to limit bacterial growth). Covers iron, manganese, zinc, and copper acquisition and their roles in staphylococcal virulence.

## Key Findings

- Iron acquisition: S. aureus produces two siderophores (staphyloferrin A and B) and uses the Isd heme acquisition system; heme is the preferred iron source during infection
- Siderophore-mediated iron acquisition is essential to virulence; inactivation of siderophore production results in reduced colony recovery from infected organs
- Host iron sequestration involves transferrin, lactoferrin, hemopexin, and haptoglobin; S. aureus hemolysins lyse red blood cells to release hemoglobin
- Manganese acquisition: via MntABC and MntH transporters; manganese is critical for superoxide dismutase (SodA/SodM) activity and oxidative stress defense
- Calprotectin (released by neutrophils) binds manganese and zinc to limit staphylococcal survival in abscesses
- Zinc limitation: calprotectin-mediated zinc sequestration impairs zinc-dependent bacterial processes; disruption of zinc acquisition increases susceptibility to oxidative killing
- Copper: S. aureus encounters copper in phagolysosomes; copper resistance mechanisms and copper export are important for intracellular survival
- Nutritional immunity extends beyond iron to manganese, zinc, and copper sequestration

## Relevance to Wiki

Establishes how S. aureus has evolved sophisticated metal acquisition systems to overcome host nutritional immunity, directly relevant to understanding how dietary metal status (iron, zinc, manganese) affects susceptibility to staphylococcal infections and how metal dyshomeostasis may promote pathogen virulence.
