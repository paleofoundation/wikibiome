## Key Findings

- **[[copper]] chelators reshape the immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment**, converting it from immune-evasive to immune-permissive — a metal-based approach to enhancing immunotherapy ([[chen-2026-metalloimmunology-tumor-microenvironment]], expert-opinion).
- **Metal homeostasis modulation enhances checkpoint blockade and CAR-T efficacy** — [[iron]], [[zinc]], and [[manganese]] alongside copper are all modulatable targets.
- **Cuproptosis** — copper-dependent cell death via lipoylated protein aggregation — identified as an emerging therapeutic mechanism distinct from apoptosis and ferroptosis.
- **Metal-dependent immune cell polarization**: Zinc deficiency impairs T cell function, iron overload promotes M2 macrophage polarization, manganese activates the cGAS-STING pathway.

## Methods

Comprehensive review and synthesis of recent literature on metal homeostasis in the tumor microenvironment (TME), covering studies on copper, iron, zinc, and manganese in tumor biology and immune cell function. Integrates findings from in-vitro cell culture, animal tumor models, and early clinical data on metal-modulating agents in combination with immunotherapy. Reviews the molecular mechanisms of cuproptosis (copper-dependent cell death) and ferroptosis (iron-dependent lipid peroxidation).

## Detailed Summary

Chen et al. introduce the field of "metalloimmunology" — the intersection of metal biology and tumor immunology — arguing that metals are critical but overlooked regulators of anti-tumor immune responses.

**Metal dysregulation in the TME.** Tumors actively remodel their metal microenvironment. Copper accumulates in many solid tumors, where it promotes angiogenesis (via copper-dependent LOX, VEGF signaling) and suppresses anti-tumor immunity. Iron is both hoarded by tumor cells (for proliferation) and used to polarize tumor-associated macrophages toward the immunosuppressive M2 phenotype. Zinc depletion in the TME impairs T cell receptor signaling and cytotoxic function. Manganese is depleted, reducing cGAS-STING innate immune sensing of tumor DNA.

**Copper and cuproptosis.** The review highlights cuproptosis as a newly characterized cell death mechanism (distinct from apoptosis, necroptosis, and ferroptosis). Excess intracellular copper binds lipoylated proteins in the mitochondrial TCA cycle (particularly DLAT — dihydrolipoamide S-acetyltransferase), causing protein aggregation and proteotoxic stress. Copper chelators (e.g., tetrathiomolybdate) can reduce TME copper levels, simultaneously inhibiting angiogenesis and releasing anti-tumor immune responses. Conversely, copper ionophores (e.g., elesclomol) can induce cuproptosis selectively in tumor cells that have higher copper uptake.

**Iron and ferroptosis.** Iron-dependent lipid peroxidation (ferroptosis) is an established tumor-suppressive mechanism. The TME's iron economy determines whether ferroptosis is active: tumor cells with high ferritin and low labile iron resist ferroptosis, while iron-loading interventions can sensitize resistant tumors.

**Zinc and immune function.** Zinc is essential for T cell maturation, TCR signaling, and cytotoxic granule release. Zinc depletion in the TME — driven by tumor cell zinc uptake and altered zinc transporter expression — functionally impairs infiltrating T cells, contributing to immune evasion. Zinc supplementation in animal models has restored T cell function within tumors.

**Manganese and innate sensing.** Mn2+ is a natural activator of the cGAS-STING pathway, which senses cytosolic DNA (including tumor-derived DNA) and triggers type I interferon responses. Manganese supplementation enhances STING-mediated anti-tumor immunity, with early clinical data suggesting benefit as an immunotherapy adjuvant.
