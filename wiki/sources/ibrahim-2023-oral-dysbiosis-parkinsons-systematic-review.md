# Association Between Oral Dysbiosis and Parkinson's Disease: A Systematic Review

## Summary

This systematic review examines the evidence linking oral [[dysbiosis]] to [[parkinsons-disease]]. It synthesizes studies comparing the oral microbiome composition of PD patients versus healthy controls, assessing whether oral microbial alterations may serve as early biomarkers or contribute to PD pathogenesis.

## Key Findings

- PD patients show distinct oral microbiome profiles compared to healthy controls
- Periodontal pathogens (*Porphyromonas gingivalis*, *Treponema denticola*) are enriched in PD
- *Streptococcus* species are overrepresented in PD saliva
- *Prevotella* is altered in PD oral samples, mirroring gut-level changes
- Periodontitis prevalence is higher in PD patients than age-matched controls
- Oral dysbiosis may precede gut dysbiosis in the oral-gut-brain axis model
- Oral bacteria can translocate to the gut and contribute to gut microbiome alterations
- Inflammatory mediators from oral infections may contribute to systemic and neuroinflammation

## Relevance to Gut-Metal-Microbiome Axis

Oral [[dysbiosis]] in PD has important implications for metal exposure. The oral cavity is the first site of contact for dietary metals ([[nickel]], [[lead]], [[manganese]], [[iron]]), and oral microbiome composition influences metal speciation and bioavailability. *Streptococcus mutans*, enriched in PD oral microbiomes, is uniquely [[manganese]]-dependent, using Mn instead of Fe for metalloenzymes -- potentially creating a local Mn sink that alters metal distribution. Periodontal pathogens like *P. gingivalis* produce gingipains that degrade host metal-binding proteins, releasing free metals. The oral-to-gut translocation of these organisms could seed gut [[dysbiosis]] and propagate metal dysregulation along the entire GI tract, connecting to the broader [[gut-brain-axis]] pathology in PD.

## Limitations

- Limited number of studies available for systematic review
- Heterogeneous methodologies across included studies
- Oral hygiene practices and dental status not consistently controlled
- Causality between oral dysbiosis and PD not established
