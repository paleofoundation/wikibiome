# Why This Paper Matters

This comprehensive review synthesizes the evidence linking infections, immune system dysfunction, and inflammation to schizophrenia risk and pathophysiology. It covers the full spectrum from prenatal maternal infections (which increase SCZ risk by up to 39%) to specific pathogen associations (Toxoplasma gondii, herpesviruses, Chlamydophila) to the emerging SARS-CoV-2 connection. The review establishes that immune abnormalities are not merely correlates but likely play a causal role in a subset of schizophrenia cases, connecting the infective hypothesis with the immuno-inflammatory dysregulation hypothesis into a unified framework.

# Key Claims

- **Maternal infections** during pregnancy increase the risk of SCZ in offspring: the largest Danish study (3722 SCZ patients) found maternal infections increased risk by **39%**, decreasing to 23% after adjusting for parental mental illness
- **Toxoplasma gondii**: levels of T. gondii antibodies are higher in SCZ patients; T. gondii may contribute to SCZ by elevating kynurenic acid (NMDA receptor antagonist) in the CNS
- **Herpes simplex virus** has been linked to SCZ; patients with SCZ exhibited higher serum CMV antibody titers, particularly among those newly diagnosed
- **Chlamydophila**: population-based studies reported higher rates of Chlamydophila infections in SCZ, correlated with genetic markers related to the immune system
- **SARS-CoV-2**: the COVID-19 pandemic raised questions about virus-SCZ connections; SARS-CoV-2 may affect the brain through direct infection and inflammation; some cases of psychosis following COVID-19 have been reported
- **HLA genes** on chromosome 6 link both SCZ and autoimmune diseases; parental autoimmune diseases (type 1 diabetes, Sjogren's syndrome, psoriasis, dermatomyositis) associated with elevated SCZ risk
- **Maternal immune activation (MIA)** disrupts normal brain development: increased GABA receptor 2 immunoreactivity, dopamine hyperfunction, delayed hippocampal maturation, decreased NMDA receptor expression in offspring
- **Microglial activation** and cytokine elevation (IL-6, TNF-alpha) are consistently found in SCZ brains
- **Anti-inflammatory treatments** targeting inflammation show some efficacy in SCZ; NSAIDs, minocycline, and anti-cytokine therapies have been explored
- The gut microbiota can influence the immune system and SCZ risk through microbial translocation, LPS-mediated inflammation, and tryptophan/kynurenine pathway modulation

# Methods & Findings

## Prenatal Infection Risk
- Danish study: hospitalization for infection increased SCZ risk
- Risk persisted whether infections occurred during or outside pregnancy
- Specific pathogen associations: T. gondii, CMV, influenza, HSV
- Polygenic risk score for SCZ found to be 2.30-fold higher in highest vs. lowest quartile

## Immune Mechanisms
- Neurodevelopmental hypothesis: infections during critical periods disrupt brain formation
- Progressive hypothesis: ongoing immune activation during illness causes neurotransmitter disruption
- MIA model: maternal cytokines cross placenta and disrupt fetal brain development
- Blood-brain barrier disruption allows brain-reactive antibodies to access CNS

## Treatment Implications
- COX-2 inhibitors (celecoxib) show modest benefit as adjunctive treatment
- Minocycline (tetracycline antibiotic with anti-inflammatory properties) shows promise
- Anti-TNF-alpha and anti-IL-6 therapies being explored
- Antibiotics can influence the gut microbiota, potentially affecting SCZ risk
- Steroids may actually lower SCZ risk in some cases
