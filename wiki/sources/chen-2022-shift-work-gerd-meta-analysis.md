# Shift work is significantly and positively associated with possible gastro-esophageal reflux disease: A meta-analysis study

## Key Claims

- Shift work is significantly associated with [[gerd]] with a pooled OR of 1.53 (95% CI 1.33-1.77, p=0.003)
- Night shift work showed OR 1.39 (95% CI 1.16-1.66) and rotating shift showed OR 1.83 (95% CI 1.44-2.33) for GERD risk
- Similar trends were observed in both shift working men (OR 1.28, 95% CI 1.03-1.60) and shift workers of both genders (OR 1.75, 95% CI 1.45-2.11)
- Shift work patterns are related to circadian rhythm disruption, which is associated with type 2 diabetes, obesity, cardiovascular disease, and cognitive vulnerability
- GERD prevalence worldwide is approximately 14%, with about 1 billion people affected; approximately 145 million US workers work non-daytime or shift schedules (16%)

## Methods/Findings

- **Study type**: Meta-analysis following PRISMA guidelines
- **Registration**: PROSPERO (CRD42022302196)
- **Search**: PubMed, Embase, Web of Science (no date limitation, searched April 12, 2022)
- **Included**: 4 cross-sectional studies (6,040 shipyard workers; 15,283 employed; 3,590 auto factory employees; 2,027 workers)
- **Analysis**: Fixed-effects model; I^2 for heterogeneity; funnel plot for publication bias
- **Subgroup analyses**: By shift type (night vs rotating), gender, and combined
