## Key Findings

- **Intratumoral fungi are cancer type-specific**: Different tumor types harbor distinct fungal communities, suggesting that fungal colonization of tumors is not random but reflects specific ecological niches created by different cancer microenvironments ([[cheng-2024-fungi-tumors-role-tumorigenesis-review|this study]], expert opinion).
- **Four mechanisms link fungi to tumorigenesis**: (1) Modulation of host immunity — fungi can both activate anti-tumor immune responses and promote immune evasion; (2) Production of bioactive metabolites — mycotoxins and fungal metabolites can be directly mutagenic or promote proliferation; (3) Interkingdom microbiome interactions — fungal-bacterial cooperation in biofilms and metabolic cross-feeding; (4) Epigenetic modifications — fungal metabolites can alter host DNA methylation and histone modification patterns.
- **Candida albicans is the most frequently detected intratumoral fungus**: [[candida-albicans]] is enriched in GI cancers, oral cancers, and potentially gynecological malignancies. Its morphological switching (yeast-to-hyphal transition) enables tissue invasion and immune evasion.
- **Fungal biofilms provide protected niches**: Fungi participate in polymicrobial biofilms with bacteria, creating microenvironments that shield both organisms from immune surveillance and therapeutic agents.
- **Therapeutic targeting of the mycobiome is feasible**: Antifungal agents, FMT, probiotics, and prebiotics are reviewed as potential interventions for mycobiome-mediated cancer progression.

## Detailed Summary

Cheng et al. provide a comprehensive framework for understanding fungal contributions to cancer biology. The review synthesizes the rapidly growing evidence that fungi are not merely bystanders in the tumor microenvironment but active participants in tumorigenesis and treatment response.

**Intratumoral mycobiome characterization.** Large-scale studies have identified cancer type-specific fungal signatures. Candida species predominate in GI and oral cancers, Malassezia in pancreatic cancer, and Aspergillus in lung cancers. The specificity of these associations suggests that tumors create distinct ecological niches that select for particular fungal communities — analogous to how metal availability selects for specific bacterial communities (Primitive 1, extended to the mycobiome).

**Immune modulation.** Fungi interact with the immune system through pattern recognition receptors (Dectin-1, TLR2, TLR4, mannose receptor). In the tumor context, this interaction is double-edged: fungal recognition can activate anti-tumor immune responses (through dendritic cell activation and Th1/Th17 polarization), but chronic fungal presence can also promote immune exhaustion and regulatory T cell expansion, facilitating immune evasion.

**Interkingdom interactions.** The review emphasizes fungal-bacterial cooperation in the tumor microenvironment (Primitive 6: interkingdom relationships and functional shielding). Candida albicans forms polymicrobial biofilms with Staphylococcus, Streptococcus, and other bacteria. Within these biofilms, bacteria can enhance Candida virulence (e.g., Streptococcus promotes Candida hyphal formation), and Candida provides physical protection for bacteria from immune cells and antibiotics.

**Metal ecology of intratumoral fungi.** The metal connection operates through several pathways: (1) Candida species require iron for growth and virulence factor expression — the iron-rich tumor microenvironment supports fungal colonization; (2) Fungal siderophores compete with bacterial siderophores and host iron-sequestration proteins for available iron; (3) Zinc-dependent fungal metalloproteinases contribute to tissue invasion; (4) Manganese is required for fungal superoxide dismutase (SOD), which protects fungi from oxidative killing by immune cells.
