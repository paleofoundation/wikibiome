# Key Claims

- [[nickel]] is a transition element extensively distributed in the environment that causes a variety of side effects including allergy, cardiovascular and kidney diseases, lung fibrosis, lung and nasal cancer
- Molecular mechanisms of nickel toxicity involve [[oxidative-stress|oxidative stress]], mitochondrial dysfunction, and [[epigenetic]] alterations (DNA methylation, histone modification, microRNA interference)
- Nickel is the most frequent cause of metal allergy, affecting 13-18% of females and 3-6% of males; approximately 10-15% of global population affected
- Insoluble nickel compounds (NiS, NiO) are more potent carcinogens than soluble ones (NiSO4, NiCl2)
- Nickel-induced apoptosis occurs through both intrinsic (mitochondrial/Cyt C release) and extrinsic (Fas/FasL) pathways
- Nickel substitutes for Fe2+ in non-heme iron dioxygenases involved in DNA and histone demethylation, representing a key epigenetic mechanism of carcinogenesis
- IARC classifies soluble and insoluble nickel compounds as Group 1 carcinogens, metallic nickel and alloys as Group 2B

# Methods/Findings

- **Study type**: Comprehensive narrative review
- **Chemical properties**: Ni is the 28th element; Ni2+ most common oxidation state; 24th most abundant in Earth's crust; atomic weight 58.6934; density 8908 kg/m3; melting point 1455 C
- **Exposure sources**: Occupational (stainless steel, electroplating, batteries, alloys), dietary (hazelnuts, cocoa, dark chocolate, grains, seafood, vegetables -- Table 1), environmental (fossil fuel combustion, cigarette smoke contains 1.1-3.1 ug Ni per cigarette), nanoparticles

 - Involves activation of antigen-presenting cells and T cells; hypersensitivity visible 48-72 hours after allergen exposure
 - EU Nickel Directive limits: 0.2 ug/cm2/week for pierced body parts, 0.5 ug/cm2/week for prolonged contact items
 - Quantitative test: EN 1811 artificial sweat method
 - Sources: jewelry, coins, orthodontic braces, medical devices, food items
- **Enzyme inhibition**: Nickel inhibits enzymes by binding to cysteine, histidine, glutamate, lysine residues; blocks Cob(I) alamin adenosyltransferase (vitamin B12 metabolism at 50% loss with 100 uM Ni2+); inhibits cobaltochelatase (B12 biosynthesis)

 - DNA hypermethylation and transcriptional repression of tumor suppressors
 - Histone hyperphosphorylation (H3S10), hypermethylation (H3K4), hyperubiquitination (H2A, H2B)
 - Intramuscular NiS injection in p53+/- mice induced malignant histiocytomas with hypermethylation of p16
 - Nickel condensed chromatin to greater extent than Mg2+, promoting heterochromatinization
 - Can interfere with microRNA networks
- **Teratogenicity**: Ni carbonyl, NiCl2, and Ni2S3 cause fetal malformations in rats and hamsters including hydrocephaly, microphthalmia, exophthalmia, skeletal defects; Saini et al. found lower implant sites and placental weight at 46-184 mg Ni/kg
- **Apoptosis (Figure 1)**: Ni2+ releases Cyt C from mitochondria, activates caspase-9 (intrinsic); promotes Fas/FasL interaction activating caspase-8/-10 (extrinsic); both converge on caspase-3, -6, -7 acting on PARP
- **Carcinogenicity**: Insoluble NiS particles remain in lungs long-term as primary carcinogenic concern; epidemiological studies show increased nasal and lung cancer in nickel refinery workers
- **Phytoremediation**: Hyperaccumulator plants (Alyssum murale, Sebertia acuminata, Rinorea niccolifera) accumulate >1000 mg Ni/kg dry weight
