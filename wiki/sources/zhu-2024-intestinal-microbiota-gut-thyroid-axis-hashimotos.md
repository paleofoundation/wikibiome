# Intestinal Microbiota Regulates the Gut-Thyroid Axis: The New Dawn of Improving Hashimoto Thyroiditis

## Key Findings

- **HT prevalence and microbiota**: [[hashimotos-thyroiditis]] prevalence has reached 10-12% globally with a 7-10:1 female-to-male ratio. Firmicutes and Bacteroidetes are dominant phyla in HT patients, with Bacteroides, [[roseburia]], [[ruminococcus]], [[dorea]], Fusicatenibacter, and [[eubacterium]] increased while [[bifidobacterium]] and [[lactobacillus]] decreased ([[zhu-2024-intestinal-microbiota-gut-thyroid-axis-hashimotos]], expert-opinion).
- **SCFA-NF-kappaB axis**: [[short-chain-fatty-acids]] produced by gut microbiota inhibit [[nf-kappa-b]] signaling and [[inflammation]], induce Treg cell function, and enhance intestinal barrier integrity relevant to thyroid autoimmunity ([[zhu-2024-intestinal-microbiota-gut-thyroid-axis-hashimotos]], expert-opinion).
- **Trace element dependencies**: [[selenium]] increases Treg proportion, suppresses pro-inflammatory cytokine secretion, and delays thyroid follicular cell apoptosis. [[iron]] deficiency associated with higher TSH and lower fT4; impairs thyroid hormone synthesis. [[zinc]] is cofactor for >300 metalloenzymes; mediates T lymphocyte differentiation. [[copper]] and selenium positively associated with FT4 levels ([[zhu-2024-intestinal-microbiota-gut-thyroid-axis-hashimotos]], expert-opinion).
- **Iodine U-shaped risk**: [[iodine]] excess can trigger thyroiditis and hypothyroidism through increased ROS production in thyroid follicular cells. The relationship between iodine and thyroid disease follows a U-shaped curve ([[zhu-2024-intestinal-microbiota-gut-thyroid-axis-hashimotos]], expert-opinion).
- **Intestinal permeability**: Gut [[dysbiosis]] damages intestinal barrier integrity, increases intestinal permeability, and promotes bacterial translocation that activates systemic immune responses against thyroid antigens via LPS-TLR4-NF-kappaB pathway ([[zhu-2024-intestinal-microbiota-gut-thyroid-axis-hashimotos]], expert-opinion).

## Detailed Summary

**Microbiota changes in HT** (Table 1): Brazil cohort: Bacteroides increased, Bifidobacterium decreased (real-time PCR). China cohorts (multiple): Blautia, Roseburia, Ruminococcus, Dorea increased; Faecalibacterium, Bacteroidetes, Prevotella_9 decreased (16S rRNA). Proteobacteria, Cyanobacteria, Actinobacteria increased; Firmicutes, Bacteroidetes decreased in some cohorts. Spanish cohort: Victivallaceae, Rikenellaceae reduced in GD ([[zhu-2024-intestinal-microbiota-gut-thyroid-axis-hashimotos]], expert-opinion).

**Immune mechanisms**: Microbiota regulates Th17/Treg balance; imbalance drives autoimmunity in HT. NLRP3 inflammasome activation by microbial-derived ATP promotes IL-1beta and IL-18 secretion. LPS from dysbiotic bacteria activates TLR4/NF-kappaB pathway. Molecular mimicry between bacterial proteins and thyroid antigens (TPO, Tg) may trigger autoantibody production. H. pylori CagA-positive strains show nucleotide sequence similarity to thyroid peroxidase (TPO) ([[zhu-2024-intestinal-microbiota-gut-thyroid-axis-hashimotos]], expert-opinion).

**Biomarkers** (Table 2): Zonulin/I-FABP/DAO reflect intestinal permeability. Claudin-2 expression increased, associated with autophagy inhibition. NLRP1/ASC and NLRP3 mediate pyroptosis and cytokine release (IL-1beta, IL-18). TMAO promotes inflammatory reaction. SCFAs inhibit HDAC, reduce IFN-gamma, regulate T cell polarization. DCA reduces bacterial overgrowth. LPS increases Tg and NIS expression via TLR-4 ([[zhu-2024-intestinal-microbiota-gut-thyroid-axis-hashimotos]], expert-opinion).

**Therapeutic strategies**: Rifaximin for small intestinal bacterial overgrowth (SIBO) in HT. AIP diet eliminates common triggers. Probiotics: L. reuteri modulated Tregs and suppressed thyroiditis in mice. FMT emerging but evidence limited. The review proposes a "10-step" approach: adjust diet → correct SIBO → supplement probiotics → restore barrier → modulate immunity → monitor biomarkers ([[zhu-2024-intestinal-microbiota-gut-thyroid-axis-hashimotos]], expert-opinion).

- Zonulin/I-FABP as monitoring biomarkers during gut-directed HT interventions

- SIBO screening and rifaximin as first-line gut intervention in HT patients with GI symptoms

## Contradictions / Tensions

- The direction of microbiota changes (increase vs. decrease of specific taxa) varies across cohorts and geographic populations
- The review notes that "although the intestinal microbiota of HT patients has changed, whether it is to help the host fight against or promote disease needs more animal experiment to prove"
- The therapeutic evidence is predominantly animal-model based; human clinical trial data for gut-directed HT interventions remains limited

## Open Questions

- Whether zonulin levels can predict HT disease activity or treatment response
- Whether SIBO prevalence in HT patients is higher than general population and whether SIBO treatment improves thyroid function
- The specific molecular mimicry epitopes between gut bacteria and thyroid antigens (TPO, Tg)
- Whether the proposed "10-step" approach improves clinical outcomes in controlled trials
