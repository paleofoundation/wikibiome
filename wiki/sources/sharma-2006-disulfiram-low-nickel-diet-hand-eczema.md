## Key Claims

- Combined [[pharmacomicrobiomics|disulfiram]] (nickel-chelating agent) and [[low-nickel-diet]] therapy achieved complete healing in 90.9% (10/11) of nickel-sensitive patients with chronic vesicular hand eczema after 4 weeks
- Only 10% (1/10) of the placebo/normal diet control group showed improvement at 4 weeks
- The improvement was statistically significant (P<0.001) and sustained during the 12-week follow-up with only 2 patients requiring a repeat one-week disulfiram course
- Disulfiram (tetraethyl thiuram disulphide) chelates nickel in the body, and its metabolite diethyldithiocarbamate causes excretion through urine, bile, and sweat

## Methods/Findings

- **Design**: Comparative single-blind trial in Bongaigaon, Assam, India
- **Population**: 21 patients (15 female, 6 male) with chronic, recurring vesicular hand eczema allergic to nickel alone on patch testing
- **Protocol**: Study group (n=11) received low-nickel diet for 4 weeks plus disulfiram 125 mg/day for 2 weeks then 250 mg/day for 2 more weeks; Control group (n=10) received placebo with normal diet for 4 weeks; 12-week post-treatment follow-up
- **Low-nickel diet**: Permitted foods included milk, flattened rice, rice, paneer, fish, meat, eggs; partially restricted foods included green gram, potato, green leafy vegetables; cooking in mustard oil, butter, ghee allowed
- **Results**: Study group - 10/11 complete clearance at 4 weeks; Control group - 1/10 improved (no itching/scaling); post-treatment follow-up showed only 2/11 needed repeat treatment
- **Side effects of disulfiram**: Metallic taste (3), mild drowsiness (2), anorexia; mild liver enzyme elevation in 3/11 (27.3%); all resolved
- **Nickel intake context**: Average human diet supplies 300-600 ug/day nickel; only 1-10% absorbed from gut; Indian diet rich in plant food has higher nickel than Western diets
