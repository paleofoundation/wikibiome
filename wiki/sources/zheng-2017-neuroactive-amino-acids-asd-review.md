# Key Claims

- **Results are inconclusive and contradictory** across studies due to variation in age, gender, sample size, medications, and methodology
- **Glutamate** levels generally increased in plasma but decreased in urine of ASD patients
- **GABA** levels generally increased in plasma but decreased in brain (neuroimaging) of ASD patients
- **Tryptophan** generally decreased in plasma; serotonin elevated in blood of ~25% of ASD children
- **Future studies should use saliva sampling** as non-invasive alternative with more frequent monitoring

# Methods & Findings

**Invited review** comprehensively tabulating all reported amino acid levels across body fluids in ASD (Table 1 - extensive).

**Glutamate (GLU):**
- Plasma: mostly increased (Moreno-Fuenmayor 1996; Aldred 2003; Shimmura 2011; Tu 2012); one study decreased (El-Ansary 2016)
- Brain (neuroimaging): mixed - increased (Page 2006; Joshi 2012) and decreased (van Elst 2014)
- GLU is major excitatory neurotransmitter; imbalance with GABA proposed as key ASD mechanism

**Glutamine (GLN):**
- Plasma: mostly decreased (Moreno-Fuenmayor 1996; Aldred 2003; Shimmura 2011)
- Urine: increased and decreased results reported

**GABA:**
- Plasma: generally increased (Dhossche 2002; El-Ansary 2014)
- Brain: decreased across neuroimaging studies (Kubas 2012; Gaetz 2014; Rojas 2014; Omura 2015)
- Excitation/inhibition imbalance central to ASD pathophysiology

**Tryptophan (TRP):**
- Plasma: generally decreased (Tirouvanziam 2011; Tu 2012; Naushad 2013)
- 5-HT elevated in blood of ~25% of ASD children
- Dietary TRP reduction impairs social behavior in mice

**D-Serine:**
- Serum: no difference (Shinohe 2006)
- D-serine is NMDA receptor co-agonist; potentially relevant to ASD glutamatergic dysfunction

**Other amino acids (Table 1 - comprehensive):**
- Lysine, citrulline, valine, isoleucine, threonine, proline, methionine, aspartate, asparagine, phenylalanine, tyrosine, histidine all with mixed/contradictory findings across studies

# Relevance to Metallomic Framework

- **Glutamate-zinc link**: NMDA receptors contain zinc-binding sites; zinc modulates glutamate excitotoxicity. ASD glutamate excess may reflect impaired zinc-dependent NMDA regulation
- **GABA-metal axis**: Glutamic acid decarboxylase (GAD) converting glutamate to GABA requires pyridoxal phosphate (vitamin B6) and is modulated by zinc; brain GABA depletion may reflect central zinc deficiency
- **Tryptophan-iron**: Tryptophan hydroxylase is iron-dependent; reduced plasma tryptophan with elevated peripheral serotonin suggests compartmentalized iron maldistribution
- **D-serine-zinc**: Serine racemase converting L-serine to D-serine is a zinc/magnesium-dependent enzyme; D-serine availability at NMDA receptors may be metal-limited
