# Overview

Systematic review and meta-analysis of **11 observational studies** examining associations between early-life (pre- and postnatal) antibiotic exposure and subsequent ASD diagnosis. Current data are **conflicting and do not conclusively support** the hypothesis that early-life antibiotic exposure is associated with ASD development.

# Key Findings

## Prenatal Antibiotic Exposure

- Two large Danish cohort studies on overlapping populations (96,736 and 780,547 children) found **borderline significant** increased risk of ASD after prenatal antibiotics (aHR 1.2, 95% CI 1.0-1.4)
- Stronger associations for **sulfonamides** during pregnancy (aHR 1.5; 95% CI 1.0-2.2) and **penicillins** in second trimester (aHR 1.4; CI 1.1-1.8) and third trimester (aHR 1.4; CI 1.0-1.8)
- Strongest association for **macrolides** anytime during pregnancy (aHR 2.2; 95% CI 1.1-4.4)
- Meta-analysis of case-control studies: pooled OR 1.24 (95% CI 0.53-2.92) -- **not significant**

## Early Childhood Antibiotic Exposure

- One Canadian cohort (214,834 children): trend toward **reduced** ASD risk after childhood antibiotics (aHR 0.91; CI 0.84-0.99)
- Danish study (677,403 children): slightly increased risk after postnatal penicillin (aHR 1.11, CI 1.04-1.19) and broad-spectrum antibiotics (aHR 1.10, CI 1.04-1.16)
- Meta-analysis of postnatal cohort studies: **no significant association** (HR 1.00, CI 0.83-1.21; sibling model HR 1.05, CI 0.96-1.14)

## Quality and Bias

- Three of four cohort studies earned maximum NOS stars; case-control studies had considerable bias risk
- Major confounding factors: demographics, pregnancy complications, maternal psychiatric history, healthcare use
