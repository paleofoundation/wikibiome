# Key Claims

- [[mercury]], [[lead]], [[chromium]], [[cadmium]], and [[arsenic]] share similar pathways to induce toxicity: ROS generation, weakening of antioxidant defense, enzyme inactivation, and [[oxidative-stress|oxidative stress]]
- Some metals have selective binding to specific macromolecules: lead interacts with aminolevulinic acid dehydratase (ALAD) and ferrochelatase (disrupting heme biosynthesis); chromium, cadmium, and arsenic cause genomic instability
- Defects in DNA repair following oxidative stress and DNA damage by these metals are considered carcinogenic mechanisms
- Bioaccumulation leads to diverse toxic effects across body tissues and organs, disrupting growth, proliferation, differentiation, and apoptosis
- Chelation therapy remains primary treatment but has significant side effects; specific biomarkers for monitoring are a priority

# Methods/Findings

- **Study type**: Narrative review of animal and human studies

 - CNS injuries, renal dysfunction, GI ulceration, hepatotoxicity
 - Mechanisms: thiol binding (GSH conjugation), enzyme inhibition, ROS production, aquaporin mRNA reduction, glutathione peroxidase inhibition
 - MeHg and ethylHg more toxic than inorganic Hg; crosses BBB
 - Hg vapor (550 ug/m3) deposits in brain; causes cognitive impairment and hippocampal damage in rats
 - Hepatotoxicity shown by elevated ALT, AST, ALP

 - CNS injury, lung damage, anemia, cardiovascular dysfunction, liver/kidney damage
 - Inhibits ferrochelatase and ALAD disrupting heme synthesis (Figure 1)
 - Reduces GSH, SOD, CAT, GPx; increases lipid peroxidation (MDA) and H2O2
 - PbA (500 mg/L) increases lipid peroxide and reduces GSH, SOD, CAT, GPx in liver and kidney
 - Immunological effects: inhibits lymphocyte proliferation to PHA; IFN-gamma significantly increased in stimulated PBMCs
 - Lead workers showed respiratory symptoms, reduced PFT values, elevated BLL and serum IgE

 - DNA damage, genomic instability, oxidative stress
 - Cr(VI) classified as Group 1 carcinogen (IARC); causes cancers of lung, larynx, bladder, kidneys, bone
 - Cr(VI) reduced to Cr(III) by GSH generating hydroxyl radicals; SSBs and DSBs in DNA
 - Cr(VI) causes cell cycle arrest via p53 activation; inhibits DNA ligase and DNA polymerase beta

 - Kidney dysfunction (primary target), liver damage, bone disease, cancer; 17-30 year biological half-life
 - Mechanisms: mRNA dysregulation, apoptosis, ER stress, Cd-MT absorption by kidneys, disrupted Ca/Zn/Fe homeostasis
 - Cd produces dispositional tolerance via metallothionein (MT) binding
 - Beta-2 microglobulin in urine as biomarker for Cd-induced tubular toxicity (as low as 360 ug/L)
 - Cd induces renal dysfunction through overexpression of p-cadherin leading to carcinogenesis

 - GI discomfort, skin/hair changes, CNS injury, liver damage
 - Inhibits pyruvate dehydrogenase (blocks Krebs cycle), uncouples oxidative phosphorylation
 - Activates p38 and JNK3 MAP kinases inducing neuronal apoptosis; inorganic As more toxic than DMA
 - Proinflammatory cytokines (TNF-alpha, IL-6, IL-8, IL-12) elevated in exposed populations; IL-10 decreased
 - DNA adduct 8-OHdG elevated in exposed populations
