# Key Findings

- **Molybdenum (Mo) was significantly lower** in whole blood (P=0.024) and serum (P=0.011) of PCOS women compared to controls — a novel finding for PCOS ([[smovrsnik-2025-trace-elements-pcos-case-control]], case-control, n=70).
- **Serum copper (Cu) was significantly higher** in PCOS women (P=0.026), consistent with prior literature linking elevated Cu to insulin resistance.
- Cu levels positively correlated with **leukocyte count** and negatively with **luteinizing hormone levels**, suggesting inflammatory and endocrine disruption pathways.
- Mo negatively correlated with urinary bilirubin; positive association with alanine and aspartate aminotransferase suggests liver function involvement.
- **Dietary predictors**: beef consumption predicted Cu exposure; cereal and boiled vegetable consumption predicted Mo levels.

# Methods

- Retrospective case-control study at University Medical Centre Ljubljana (Oct 2022–Jul 2023).
- 35 PCOS women (Rotterdam criteria) and 35 age/BMI-matched controls (BMI 18.5–29.9 kg/m²).
- Trace elements measured in urine, whole blood, and serum using multiple linear regression models.
- Extensive exclusion criteria including pregnancy, hormonal treatment, chronic diseases.

# Detailed Summary

This study comprehensively evaluated 9 trace elements across three biological matrices (urine, blood, serum) in PCOS women. The novel Mo finding is particularly interesting — Mo forms active sites of molybdoenzymes involved in obesity, insulin resistance, and diabetes pathogenesis. The Cu elevation aligns with the broader PCOS metallomic signature showing disrupted metal homeostasis. The dietary predictors provide actionable information for environmental exposure assessment.

# Contradictions / Tensions

- No significant differences found for Zn, Mn, Se, As, Cd, Hg, or Pb — contrasts with some studies reporting altered Zn and Se in PCOS.
- Small sample size (n=70) limits generalizability.

# Open Questions

- Is the Mo depletion finding replicable in larger multi-ethnic cohorts?
- What is the mechanism linking Mo to PCOS pathogenesis — via molybdoenzyme activity?
- Does Mo supplementation have therapeutic potential in PCOS?
