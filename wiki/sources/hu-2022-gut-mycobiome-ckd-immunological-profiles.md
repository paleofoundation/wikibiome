# Gut Mycobiome in CKD Patients and Immunological Profiles

## Key Findings

- **[[chronic-kidney-disease]] patients had significantly higher gut mycobiome richness and diversity** compared to BMI-matched healthy controls — a pattern opposite to bacterial microbiome findings where CKD typically shows reduced diversity.
- **[[candida-albicans]], Bjerkandera, Rhodotorula, and Ganoderma were depleted** in the CKD cohort, with Candida depletion being a counterintuitive finding given its typical enrichment in other dysbiotic conditions.
- **[[saccharomyces-cerevisiae]] was enriched in CKD**, potentially reflecting a compensatory or opportunistic expansion in the altered CKD gut environment.
- **Kappa immunoglobulin light chain levels positively correlated with Saccharomyces** and negatively with Ganoderma, linking fungal community composition to B-cell immune activation.
- The findings establish that fungal [[dysbiosis]] in CKD follows different ecological rules than bacterial dysbiosis, with increased rather than decreased fungal diversity.

## Methods

- **Design**: Cross-sectional case-control study.
- **Subjects**: 92 CKD patients matched by BMI with healthy controls.
- **Mycobiome profiling**: ITS (Internal Transcribed Spacer) sequencing of fecal samples for fungal community characterization.
- **Immunological assessment**: Immunoturbidimetric assays for serum immunoglobulin levels, including free light chains (kappa and lambda).
- **Analysis**: Alpha/beta diversity, differential abundance, correlation analysis between fungal taxa and immunological markers.

## Detailed Summary

This study is one of the first comprehensive characterizations of the gut mycobiome in CKD, revealing that fungal communities undergo distinct changes from bacterial communities in the uremic environment. The increased fungal diversity in CKD is unexpected — most disease-associated gut dysbiosis shows reduced diversity — and may reflect the disruption of bacterial-fungal ecological competition. When bacterial communities are disrupted by uremia, fungal species may expand into vacated ecological niches. The depletion of [[candida-albicans]] in CKD contrasts sharply with its enrichment in conditions like inflammatory bowel disease and colorectal cancer, suggesting that the CKD gut environment (high urea, altered pH, uremic toxins) creates selective pressures unfavorable to Candida. The immunoglobulin light chain correlations provide a mechanistic link: fungal cell wall components (beta-glucan, mannan) are potent activators of B-cell responses, and the altered fungal landscape in CKD may drive the B-cell hyperactivation and light chain overproduction observed in CKD patients.

## Open Questions

- Whether the increased fungal diversity in CKD is protective, pathological, or neutral.
- How fungal-bacterial ecological interactions shift across CKD stages.
- Whether the Saccharomyces enrichment represents a therapeutic opportunity (Saccharomyces boulardii as probiotic) or a warning signal of ecological disruption.
