# Metformin Effects on Gut Microbiome and Epigenetics in Type 2 Diabetes Patients and Healthy Individuals

## Summary

This doctoral thesis from the University of Latvia investigates the effects of [[metformin]] on the [[gut-microbiome]] and host epigenetics (DNA methylation) in both type 2 diabetes (T2D) patients and healthy individuals. It uses massive parallel sequencing and shotgun metagenomics to identify taxonomic and functional gut microbiome biomarkers for metformin pharmacodynamics.

## Key Findings

- Metformin therapy caused significant and immediate reduction of inner diversity in the healthy cohort
- Changes in taxonomic profile observed in both newly diagnosed T2D patients and healthy individuals
- Baseline sample microbiome composition could predict metformin therapy efficacy and tolerance using shotgun metagenomics
- Global DNA methylation profiling showed changes during metformin use in peripheral blood cells of healthy individuals (first longitudinal study of its kind)
- Top-ranked differentially methylated genes corresponded to main functional groups of metformin therapy targets: energy homeostasis, inflammatory responses, tumorigenesis, and neurodegeneration

## Relevance to Gut-Metal-Microbiome Axis

Metformin is being explored as a potential [[drug-repurposing]] candidate for [[multiple-sclerosis]] due to its anti-inflammatory and neuroprotective properties. This thesis demonstrates that metformin's therapeutic effects are partially mediated through [[gut-microbiome]] modulation, providing a mechanistic basis for its potential benefit in MS. The finding that metformin affects DNA methylation in genes related to neurodegeneration and inflammation is directly relevant to MS pathogenesis. The observation that baseline microbiome composition predicts metformin response suggests that [[gut-microbiome]] profiling could guide personalized use of metformin in MS patients, particularly those with metabolic comorbidities and [[heavy-metals]]-associated [[dysbiosis]].

## Limitations

- Doctoral thesis, not a single peer-reviewed paper
- Focused on T2D rather than MS or neurological conditions
- Healthy cohort may not represent MS patients with pre-existing dysbiosis
