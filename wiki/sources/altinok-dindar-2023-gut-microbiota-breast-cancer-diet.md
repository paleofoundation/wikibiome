# Association between Gut Microbiota and Breast Cancer: Diet as a Potential Modulating Factor

## Key Claims

- Breast cancer (BCa) patients have significantly reduced gut microbiome alpha-diversity (Shannon, Observed, Inverse Simpson, Pielou indices) compared to age-matched cancer-free controls
- Three specific genera -- *Acidaminococcus*, *Hungatella*, and *Tyzzerella* -- are enriched in the fecal microbiome of BCa patients
- These BCa-enriched genera are associated with distinct dietary intake patterns (whole fruits for Acidaminococcus; vegetables and dairy for Hungatella)
- Higher BMI, lower physical activity, and greater emotional stress were observed in BCa cases

## Methods/Findings

- **Design**: Case-control study at Oregon Health & Science University
- **Participants**: 42 newly diagnosed, treatment-naive female BCa patients + 44 age-matched cancer-free controls (March 2020 - October 2021)
- **Methods**: 16S rRNA V4 amplicon sequencing (Illumina MiSeq, 130,000 reads/sample), dietary assessment via NCI Diet History Questionnaire (DHQ), HEI-2015 scoring
- **Tumor Characteristics**: 35 invasive, 7 DCIS; 36 ER+, 30 PR+, 3 HER2+
- **Alpha Diversity** (BMI-adjusted):
  - Shannon: 3.91 vs. 4.13 (p = 0.012)
  - Observed: p = 0.025
  - Inverse Simpson: p = 0.005
  - Pielou: p = 0.028
- **Beta Diversity**: Significant difference by Jaccard distance (p = 0.04) but not Bray-Curtis (p = 0.23)
- **LEfSe Analysis**:
  - BCa-enriched (LDA >2): *Acidaminococcus* (24% cases vs. 9% controls), *Hungatella* (38% vs. 9%), *Tyzzerella* (38% vs. 20%)
  - Control-enriched: Christensenellaceae, UCG-005, Oscillospirales, NK4A14 group, Dialister, Gastranaerophilales, Coriobacteriales, Anaerofilum, Flavobacterials
- **Diet-Microbiome Associations**:
  - *Acidaminococcus*-positive participants had lower whole fruit HEI-2015 scores (p = 0.005)
  - *Hungatella*-positive participants had lower dairy intake (p = 0.029) and higher total vegetable intake (p = 0.024)
  - *Tyzzerella* presence was not associated with dietary intake scores
- **Lifestyle Factors**: BCa cases had significantly higher BMI (28.1 vs. 25.1, p = 0.02), lower physical activity (p = 0.02), and higher emotional stress (p = 0.02)

## Connections

- The reduced alpha-diversity in BCa patients parallels dysbiosis patterns seen in other immune-mediated conditions including [[multiple-sclerosis]] -- suggesting a common theme of microbiome disruption in disease
- *Hungatella* is associated with trimethylamine N-oxide (TMAO) and choline metabolism, connecting to metabolic pathways also relevant in [[metal-toxicity]] and cardiovascular risk
- Diet as a modulator of the microbiome composition connects to [[libbey-2018-diet-microbiota-eae]] which showed diet-driven microbiome changes alter MS disease severity
- The concept of using gut microbiome signatures as biomarkers has parallels in the MS probiotic literature: [[tankou-2018-probiotic-microbiome-ms]]
- The role of short-chain fatty acids (SCFAs) from gut bacteria in cancer connects to the anti-inflammatory mechanisms of [[probiotics]] studied in MS trials
- Higher BMI as a BCa risk factor may relate to altered gut metal handling and [[metal-homeostasis]], since adipose tissue accumulates metals including [[nickel]] and [[cadmium]]
