## Key Findings

- **HIV-associated dysbiosis overlaps with CVD-related dysbiosis profiles**, featuring depletion of [[lachnospiraceae]] and [[ruminococcaceae]] and enrichment of [[gammaproteobacteria]] and Desulfovibrionaceae, resulting in reduced short-chain fatty acid (SCFA) production capacity ([[troseid-2024-gut-microbiome-cardiometabolic-hiv]], expert-opinion).
- **Microbial translocation** — the passage of bacterial products (LPS, peptidoglycan) across a compromised gut barrier — is a chronic feature of HIV infection that triggers systemic inflammation and is linked to CVD incidence in multiple PLWH cohorts ([[troseid-2024-gut-microbiome-cardiometabolic-hiv]], expert-opinion, synthesizing prospective cohort data).
- **TMAO** (trimethylamine N-oxide) predicts cardiovascular events in PLWH, though its levels are influenced by diet, renal function, and hepatic oxidation, making it a complex biomarker ([[troseid-2024-gut-microbiome-cardiometabolic-hiv]], expert-opinion).
- **Imidazole propionate (ImP)**, a histidine-derived microbial metabolite, emerged as the most promising multi-omics biomarker in the COCOMO cohort, correlating with dysbiosis and carotid atherosclerosis in PLWH ([[troseid-2024-gut-microbiome-cardiometabolic-hiv]], expert-opinion).
- **No current microbiota-targeted intervention** (probiotics, prebiotics, synbiotics, or FMT) has demonstrated clinical benefit for cardiometabolic outcomes in PLWH; most trials are underpowered and lack clear primary endpoints ([[troseid-2024-gut-microbiome-cardiometabolic-hiv]], expert-opinion).

## Methods

This is a **narrative review** synthesizing published literature on the gut microbiome, circulating metabolites, and cardiometabolic comorbidities in people living with HIV (PLWH). The authors draw on data from several large cohorts:

- **American Gut Project** — community-based microbiome characterization across 20 diseases
- **AGEhIV cohort** (Amsterdam) — >10 years of biological samples with comorbidity follow-up in PLWH aged >45
- **COCOMO cohort** (Copenhagen) — prospective cohort of PLWH with microbiome, metabolomics, and carotid artery imaging
- **REPRIEVE trial** — RCT of pitavastatin for CVD prevention in PLWH

No new datasets were generated; the review integrates existing published findings.

## Detailed Summary

### HIV-Associated Dysbiosis and Confounders

The advent of 16S rRNA-based microbiome characterization revealed consistent differences between PLWH and controls. However, major confounders include **sexual practice** (men who have sex with men, MSM, show enrichment of [[prevotella]] independent of HIV status), **diet**, **alcohol intake**, and **ART toxicity**. After controlling for MSM status, HIV-related dysbiosis is characterized by depletion of [[lachnospiraceae]] and [[ruminococcaceae]] and enrichment of [[gammaproteobacteria]] and Desulfovibrionaceae.

In the AGEhIV cohort, HIV-associated dysbiosis was significantly greater in PLWH who went on to develop CVD compared to matched PLWH who did not, suggesting the dysbiosis itself may be a risk factor for cardiometabolic events. Associations between nadir CD4 count and dysbiosis severity were consistent across cohorts.

### Microbial Translocation

A shared feature of dysbiosis in PLWH is reduced production of [[short-chain-fatty-acids]] (SCFAs), particularly butyrate, which normally maintains gut epithelial barrier integrity. Loss of butyrate-producing bacteria leads to increased gut permeability and **microbial translocation** — passage of bacterial products (LPS, peptidoglycan) into systemic circulation. This triggers chronic immune activation via TLR4 and other innate immune receptors.

Markers of microbial translocation include:
- **LPS** (lipopolysaccharide) — directly pro-inflammatory
- **sCD14** (soluble CD14) — marker of monocyte activation by LPS
- **LBP** (LPS-binding protein) — acute-phase reactant
- **suPAR** (soluble urokinase plasminogen activator receptor) — inversely correlated with microbial diversity in PLWH and associated with CVD incidence

Increased LBP was linked to CAD in the HIV cohort. The authors found that penta-acylated LPS variants differ from hexa-acylated LPS in their immunostimulatory potency, adding complexity to LPS measurement interpretation.

### Candidate Microbiota-Related Biomarkers

#### TMAO (Trimethylamine N-oxide)

TMAO is produced by gut microbial metabolism of dietary choline, L-carnitine, and phosphatidylcholine, followed by hepatic oxidation via FMO3. TMAO predicts cardiovascular events in PLWH and the general population. However, limitations include:
- Levels influenced by diet, renal function, and hepatic oxidation
- Circulating TMAO weakly linked to dysbiosis
- Low circulating butyrate levels make SCFA a poor soluble biomarker
- TMAO precursors (carnitine, choline, TML) have also been associated with atherosclerosis

#### Short-Chain Fatty Acids (SCFAs)

SCFAs, particularly propionate and butyrate, have antihypertensive and anti-inflammatory properties. Circulating propionate should be investigated as a CVD biomarker in PLWH. However, SCFA production from gut microbes is difficult to quantify in vivo because butyrate is rapidly consumed by colonocytes. Low circulating levels in PLWH are consistently reported.

#### Tryptophan Catabolism (Kynurenine Pathway)

The kynurenine pathway converts tryptophan via IDO1, which is induced by inflammation. HIV infection drives chronic IDO1 upregulation. The kynurenine-to-tryptophan ratio (KT ratio) is a surrogate marker for activity and is associated with mortality and Th17 cell depletion in PLWH. Kynurenine metabolites are associated with carotid artery plaque and atherosclerosis. The metabolite pathway is relevant to both CVD and non-AIDS comorbidities including aging and inflammation.

#### Bile Acids

Secondary bile acids produced by gut microbiota have signaling functions via FXR and TGR5 receptors. Circulating bile acid pools and dysbiosis should be investigated in PLWH, but data remain sparse.

#### Uremic Toxins (PAGln)

Phenylacetylglutamine (PAGln), a microbiota-generated uremic toxin, accumulates with reduced renal clearance. PAGln predicts CVD in the general population. Its role in PLWH has not been well studied.

#### Imidazole Propionate (ImP)

ImP is produced by microbial histidine metabolism. In the COCOMO cohort, ImP emerged as the most promising unbiased multi-omics biomarker, correlating with dysbiosis, carotid atherosclerosis, and insulin resistance. ImP production has been linked to [[ruminococcus-gnavus]] and *Streptococcus mutans*. The authors highlight ImP as potentially the strongest microbiota-related CVD biomarker in PLWH.

### Therapeutic Landscape

- **Probiotics/prebiotics/synbiotics**: A comprehensive review found no evidence that any of these interventions are clinically helpful in PLWH. Trials are underpowered, biomarkers differ between trials, and primary endpoints are unclear.
- **FMT**: Limited evidence; engraftment is transient even with repeated inoculations.
- **Targeting enzymatic pathways**: Approaches targeting TMAO or ImP production ("drug the bug" strategy) are proposed but require identifying suitable biomarkers first.
- **Statins (pitavastatin)**: The REPRIEVE trial showed pitavastatin reduced major cardiovascular events by about one-third in PLWH. Anti-inflammatory mechanisms beyond LDL reduction may be relevant.

### Precision Medicine Considerations

The tremendous inter-individual variation in gut microbiome composition is a barrier to biomarker-based risk stratification but could also be leveraged for precision medicine. The authors note that machine learning models integrating microbiota profiles and metadata have personalized glycemic responses in the general population, and similar approaches could tailor CVD prevention in PLWH.

## Contradictions / Tensions

- **TMAO as a CVD biomarker**: The review notes conflicting and negative results — circulating TMAO is weakly linked to gut dysbiosis in PLWH, and its levels are confounded by diet, renal function, and hepatic oxidation. This contrasts with the strong predictive signal seen in general-population CVD cohorts. Existing WikiBiome content on TMAO may need a nuance qualifier for HIV-specific contexts.
- **Prevotella enrichment**: The MSM confounding issue means that many earlier studies attributing Prevotella enrichment to HIV infection were likely detecting a sexual-behavior signal. This is a supersession-relevant finding for any entity or signature page citing Prevotella enrichment as an HIV-specific feature.
- **Statin mechanism**: The REPRIEVE trial benefit may involve anti-inflammatory effects beyond LDL lowering, which is in tension with purely lipid-centric models of CVD prevention.

## Open Questions

1. **Can ImP serve as a clinically actionable biomarker for CVD risk in PLWH?** The COCOMO cohort data are promising but need independent replication in larger, multi-ethnic cohorts.
2. **What is the causal direction between HIV-associated dysbiosis and CVD?** The AGEhIV data showing dysbiosis precedes CVD events is suggestive but not definitive.
3. **Can "drug the bug" strategies targeting ImP or TMAO production reduce CVD events in PLWH?** No interventional trials exist yet.
4. **What role do the virome and mycobiome play in HIV-associated cardiometabolic risk?** The review explicitly flags these as underexplored.
5. **How should MSM status be controlled for in future microbiome-CVD studies in PLWH?** The confounding is well-documented but no consensus methodology exists.
6. **Does the iron ecology of the HIV gut — with chronic hepcidin elevation and siderophore-producing Gammaproteobacteria enrichment — directly drive CVD risk through microbial translocation?** This connection is mechanistically plausible but not directly tested in any cited study.
