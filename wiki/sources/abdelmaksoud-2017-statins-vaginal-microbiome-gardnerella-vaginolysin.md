## Key Findings

- **Statin use was associated with significantly lower proportions of *Gardnerella vaginalis*** in the vaginal microbiome (8.3% in statin users vs. 15.6% in normal cholesterol non-users, Wilcoxon p = 0.026; and vs. 16.7% in high cholesterol non-users, p = 0.047) ([[abdelmaksoud-2017-statins-vaginal-microbiome-gardnerella-vaginolysin]], cross-sectional, n=601).
- **Statin users were significantly more likely to exhibit CST I** (*L. crispatus*-dominant; Fisher test p = 0.0042 for normal cholesterol, p = 0.0297 for high cholesterol) and **less likely to exhibit CST IV** (high diversity/BV-associated; p = 0.0068 for normal cholesterol, p = 0.0099 for high cholesterol) ([[abdelmaksoud-2017-statins-vaginal-microbiome-gardnerella-vaginolysin]], cross-sectional).
- **Simvastatin pretreatment protected vaginal epithelial cells (VK2/E6E7) from vaginolysin (VLY)-mediated cytotoxicity** as measured by both trypan blue staining and LDH release assay. This protective effect was reversed by addition of cholesterol or mevalonate, confirming the mechanism is cholesterol depletion from cell membranes ([[abdelmaksoud-2017-statins-vaginal-microbiome-gardnerella-vaginolysin]], in-vitro component).
- **Statin users had significantly greater proportions of *L. jensenii*** relative to high-cholesterol non-users (p = 0.011) and greater proportions of **[[lactobacillus-crispatus]]** relative to normal-cholesterol non-users (p = 0.013) ([[abdelmaksoud-2017-statins-vaginal-microbiome-gardnerella-vaginolysin]], cross-sectional).
- The association between statin use and reduced *G. vaginalis* was consistent across both African American and European ancestry women, though the effect was stronger among AA women ([[abdelmaksoud-2017-statins-vaginal-microbiome-gardnerella-vaginolysin]], cross-sectional).

## Methods

**Study design:** Cross-sectional analysis with an in-vitro mechanistic component. Subjects selected from 4,306 women enrolled in the Vaginal Human Microbiome Project (VaHMP) at Virginia Commonwealth University, recruited 2009-2013.

**Groups:** 133 women taking statins at sampling; 152 women with high cholesterol not taking statins; 316 women with normal cholesterol and no statin use. Control groups were matched for age and ethnicity (proportional within 5-year bins among African American and European ancestry groups).

**Microbiome analysis:** 16S rRNA gene taxonomic survey (V1-V3 hypervariable regions) via 454 GS FLX Titanium sequencing. Median 24,030 reads/sample. Classified using RDP Classifier (0.8 cutoff) and STIRRUPS with a curated vaginal 16S rRNA database. Community state types (CSTs) assigned based on predominant taxon at >30% threshold.

**Statistical analysis:** LEfSe (Linear discriminant analysis Effect Size) for differential abundance. Wilcoxon rank sum test with continuity correction for species proportions. Fisher test for CST distribution. Kruskal-Wallis for effect size estimation.

**In-vitro component:** VK2/E6E7 vaginal keratinocytes pretreated 48h with 1 ug/mL simvastatin, then challenged with purified recombinant VLY (5 or 10 ug/mL) for 60 min at 37C. Cytotoxicity measured by trypan blue staining and LDH release assay. Cholesterol (5 ug/mL) or mevalonate (1 mM) added as rescue controls. Experiments performed 3 times with technical triplicates. One-way ANOVA with Tukey post-test.

## Detailed Summary

**Bacterial vaginosis (BV)** is the leading dysbiosis of the vaginal microbiome, characterized by depletion of protective *Lactobacillus* species and overgrowth of diverse anaerobes including [[gardnerella-vaginalis]]. *G. vaginalis* produces the cholesterol-dependent cytolysin (CDC) **vaginolysin (VLY)**, which lyses human cells by forming pores in cholesterol-rich cell membranes. VLY requires high membrane cholesterol (approximately 50 mol%) for pore formation.

**Statins** (HMG-CoA reductase inhibitors) reduce not only serum cholesterol but also plasma membrane cholesterol levels. The authors hypothesized that by depleting membrane cholesterol, statins could (1) inhibit VLY-mediated cytotoxicity and (2) thereby reduce *G. vaginalis* colonization fitness.

**Microbiome findings:** Among the 601 women analyzed, statin users showed a distinct vaginal community profile. CST I (*L. crispatus*-dominant) was significantly more common among statin users regardless of cholesterol status, while CST IV (high diversity, BV-associated) was significantly less common. LEfSe analysis identified *G. vaginalis* as significantly lower in statin users and *L. crispatus* and *L. jensenii* as significantly higher. The effect was observed in both African American and European ancestry women.

**Mechanistic findings (in vitro):** Simvastatin pretreatment significantly reduced VLY-induced LDH release from vaginal epithelial cells (p<0.005 at 10 ug/mL VLY, p<0.005 at 5 ug/mL VLY). This protection was reversed by co-treatment with cholesterol or mevalonate, confirming the effect operates through the mevalonate/cholesterol biosynthesis pathway. The authors propose that VLY function is essential for *G. vaginalis* colonization because: (1) VLY is highly conserved across *G. vaginalis* strains despite considerable genetic diversity, and (2) VLY-mediated damage to vaginal epithelial cells depletes glycogen stores that support beneficial lactobacilli, creating conditions favorable for BV-associated bacteria.

**Ethnic disparities:** AA women are twice as likely to have BV and more likely to have CST IV. Statin users of both ethnicities showed similar CST profiles, suggesting statins may reduce ethnic disparities in BV rates. Statins are less effective at reducing LDL in AA women, suggesting that cellular membrane cholesterol changes rather than serum LDL changes may be the relevant mechanism.

**Limitations:** Cross-sectional design cannot establish causality. Membrane cholesterol was not directly measured. No animal model validation. Retrospective self-reported statin use. Menopausal status predicted by age (51) rather than clinically confirmed in some cases.

## Relevance to WikiBiome

This study provides a mechanistic bridge between pharmaceutical cholesterol modulation and vaginal microbiome composition. Key contributions to entity pages:

- **[[gardnerella-vaginalis]]** — Establishes VLY as a cholesterol-dependent virulence factor essential for colonization; adds cholesterol as a key environmental dependency for this pathogen. VLY's dependence on membrane cholesterol is analogous to metal-dependent virulence enzymes in the gut — an environmental dependency that creates an Achilles' heel.
- **[[lactobacillus-crispatus]]** — Evidence that reduced *G. vaginalis* virulence activity correlates with increased *L. crispatus* dominance, supporting the competitive exclusion model.
- **Cholesterol-dependent cytolysins (CDCs)** — A class of virulence factors dependent on host membrane composition rather than metal cofactors, expanding the ecological engineering framework beyond metals.

## Relevance to Cureva

- **Drug repurposing signal:** Statins as a potential intervention for BV via VLY inhibition — a mechanism distinct from their cardiovascular indication. This could inform an intervention page for BV with triangle evidence: I_to_f (statin depletes membrane cholesterol, disabling VLY), I_to_D (statin users have lower *G. vaginalis* and less CST IV), f_to_D (VLY-mediated epithelial damage drives BV pathogenesis).
- **STOP signal potential:** If *G. vaginalis* is enriched in any condition where cholesterol supplementation is considered, this study provides mechanistic grounds for a STOP — exogenous cholesterol could enhance VLY activity.
- **Primitive 4 (Microbial Dependencies as Achilles' Heels):** VLY's absolute dependence on membrane cholesterol for pore formation is a targetable vulnerability, paralleling metal-dependent enzyme dependencies in gut pathogens.
- **Primitive 5 (Two-Sided Ecological Engineering):** Reducing *G. vaginalis* virulence simultaneously allows expansion of beneficial lactobacilli — the intervention has both suppressive and restorative effects.

## Contradictions / Tensions

- This study is filed under the **erectile dysfunction** condition library, but its content addresses the **vaginal microbiome and BV**. The connection to ED is not immediately apparent from the paper itself — the relevance may be through statin use as a shared pharmaceutical exposure affecting microbiome composition across body sites, or through cholesterol metabolism as a shared mechanism. <!-- NEEDS VERIFICATION: clarify the intended ED connection -->
- The study acknowledges that statins have **anti-inflammatory effects** independent of cholesterol lowering, which could confound the proposed VLY-cholesterol mechanism. The in-vitro data supports the cholesterol mechanism specifically, but the in-vivo effect may be multifactorial.
- The **cross-sectional design** cannot distinguish whether statins cause the microbiome shift or whether confounders (healthcare-seeking behavior, other medications) explain the association. The authors note this limitation explicitly.

## Open Questions

1. **Does the statin-VLY mechanism generalize to other body sites?** *G. vaginalis* bacteremia and CDCs from other organisms (pneumolysin, listeriolysin O) also depend on membrane cholesterol. Do statins affect CDC-producing pathogens systemically?
2. **What is the direct connection to erectile dysfunction?** The paper addresses vaginal microbiome — is the relevance through shared statin pharmacology, cholesterol-dependent virulence mechanisms in the urogenital tract, or cross-condition microbiome patterns?
3. **Would a prospective study confirm causality?** A before/after 16S analysis around statin initiation would be definitive. The authors suggest this as a future direction.
4. **Do different statins have different effects?** The study grouped all statin types together. Lipophilic vs. hydrophilic statins have different tissue penetration and could differentially affect mucosal membrane cholesterol.
5. **Does membrane cholesterol depletion affect other CDC-producing organisms** relevant to WikiBiome conditions (e.g., *Clostridium perfringens* perfringolysin, *Streptococcus* streptolysin O)?
