# Short-chain fatty acids in patients with schizophrenia and ultra-high risk population

## Summary

This study recruited 59 schizophrenia patients (including 15 converted from ultra-high-risk), 51 UHR participants, and 40 healthy controls and measured serum [[short-chain-fatty-acids]] concentrations. It investigated the association of SCFA alterations with cognitive functions in both SCZ and UHR populations over a 2-year follow-up.

## Key Findings

- Valeric acid concentrations were lower in both baseline UHR individuals who later converted to SCZ (p = 0.046) and SCZ patients (p = 0.036) compared to HC
- Caproic acid concentrations were lower at baseline in UHR individuals who later transitioned to SCZ (p = 0.019) and the UHR group (p = 0.016) compared to HC
- Caproic acid levels in UHR group significantly positively correlated with immediate memory (r = 0.355, p = 0.011)
- Caproic acid negatively correlated with TMT-B (r = -0.366, p = 0.009), indicating association with executive function
- No significant differences in acetic acid, butyric acid, or isovaleric acid between groups
- 15 UHR participants converted to SCZ within the 2-year follow-up

## Relevance to Gut-Metal-Microbiome Axis

This study identifies specific [[short-chain-fatty-acids]] -- valeric and caproic acids -- as potential early biomarkers for schizophrenia risk. The depletion of these branched-chain fatty acids at the UHR stage, before psychosis onset, suggests that [[dysbiosis]]-driven metabolic changes precede clinical disease. SCFAs are known to regulate histone deacetylase (HDAC) activity, modulate [[neuroinflammation]], and maintain gut barrier integrity. Their depletion could facilitate the translocation of metals and endotoxins across a compromised gut barrier. The correlation with cognitive function supports the role of SCFAs in brain health via the [[gut-brain-axis]]. Heavy metal exposure may selectively inhibit butyrate-producing bacteria, further depleting these protective metabolites.

## Limitations

- Relatively small sample size per group
- Chinese Han population limits generalizability
- Serum SCFAs may not reflect gut luminal concentrations
- Medication status in SCZ group not fully controlled
