# Oral and Gut Microbiome Profiles in People with Early Idiopathic Parkinson's Disease

## Summary

This large-scale study (445 PD cases, 221 controls for stool; 438 PD, 220 controls for saliva) from 23andMe examined both oral and gut [[microbiome]] signatures in recently diagnosed [[parkinsons-disease]] patients. Using matched case-control shotgun metagenomics, the study found that subspecies-level oral microbiome abundances best distinguish PD with high accuracy (AUC 0.758), and that microbial networks are disrupted in PD cases with reduced connectivity between [[short-chain-fatty-acids]]-producing bacteria.

## Key Findings

- The microbial signature of PD includes overabundance of 16 OTUs including *[[Streptococcus]] mutans* and *[[Bifidobacterium]] dentium*, and depletion of 28 OTUs
- Machine learning models using subspecies-level oral microbiome abundances distinguished PD with AUC of 0.758
- Stool alpha-diversity (Chao1) was significantly increased in PD cases (W = 41,384; p = 0.00173)
- Constipation symptoms in PD correlated with increased stool microbiome alpha-diversity
- Microbiome diversity metrics associated with non-motor autonomic symptom severity (orthostatic hypotension, constipation)
- Microbial network analysis revealed reduced connectivity between SCFA-producing bacteria in PD
- Saliva microbiome composition showed significant differences between PD and controls, with saliva proportions best at distinguishing cases from controls
- *Akkermansia*, *Alistipes*, *Bifidobacterium*, and *Streptococcus* were consistently overabundant in PD

## Relevance to Gut-Metal-Microbiome Axis

This study strengthens the [[gut-brain-axis]] framework by demonstrating that both oral and gut microbiome signatures are altered in early PD, supporting the hypothesis that [[dysbiosis]] begins in the oral cavity and spreads to the gut. The overabundance of *Streptococcus mutans* -- a [[manganese]]-dependent organism that uses Mn instead of [[iron]] for its metalloenzymes -- implicates metal-microbe interactions in early PD pathogenesis. The disrupted SCFA-producing networks connect to reduced [[butyrate]] production and compromised gut barrier integrity, which may facilitate translocation of metals and endotoxins. The finding that autonomic symptom severity correlates with microbiome diversity supports the peripheral-to-central progression model of PD via the [[gut-brain-axis]]. The oral microbiome findings are particularly relevant to metal exposure, as the oral cavity is a primary entry point for dietary [[nickel]], [[lead]], and other metals.

## Limitations

- Cross-sectional design; cannot establish causality
- Self-reported PD diagnosis via 23andMe survey
- Geographically restricted to the US
- Recently diagnosed patients only -- may miss later-stage changes
- Medications (especially dopaminergic agents) may influence results
