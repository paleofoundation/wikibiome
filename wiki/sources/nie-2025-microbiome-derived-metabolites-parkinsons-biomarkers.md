# Microbiome-Derived Metabolites as Biomarkers for Parkinson's Disease

## Summary

This study evaluates [[microbiome-derived-metabolites]] as diagnostic and prognostic biomarkers for [[parkinsons-disease]], developing a metabolite panel that can distinguish PD from healthy controls and predict disease progression.

## Key Findings

- A panel of microbiome-derived metabolites could distinguish PD patients from controls with high accuracy
- SCFA levels (butyrate, propionate) were consistently depleted and served as negative biomarkers
- Indole-3-propionic acid was depleted while kynurenic acid was elevated
- The metabolite panel showed promise for tracking disease progression over time
- Integration with clinical markers improved diagnostic and prognostic performance
- Some metabolites showed potential as surrogate endpoints for therapeutic trials

## Relevance to Gut-Metal-Microbiome Axis

Metabolite biomarkers for [[parkinsons-disease]] are indirect measures of the metallomic-microbiome state. Depleted SCFAs reflect loss of metal-sensitive SCFA producers. Altered indole metabolites reflect disrupted tryptophan metabolism by metal-dependent bacterial enzymes. A combined metallomic-metabolomic biomarker panel (adding fecal [[iron]], [[zinc]], [[calprotectin]], [[lactoferrin]] to the metabolite panel) would capture both the cause (metal dyshomeostasis) and consequence (metabolite disruption) of PD [[dysbiosis]], potentially improving both sensitivity and specificity.

## Limitations

- Metabolite levels are influenced by diet, medication, and circadian rhythm
- Biomarker validation in large prospective cohorts is needed
- Cut-off values for clinical use have not been established
- Metabolite stability during sample collection and processing must be standardized
