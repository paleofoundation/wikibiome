# Altered Gut Mycobiome in ESRD Patients

## Key Findings

- **ESRD patients had significantly higher gut mycobiome diversity** than healthy controls, paralleling findings from the Hu 2022 CKD mycobiome study and reinforcing the pattern of increased fungal diversity in kidney disease.
- **[[saccharomyces-cerevisiae]] was decreased in ESRD**, while opportunistic pathogens were enriched — a pattern that contrasts with the Hu 2022 finding of increased Saccharomyces in earlier CKD stages, suggesting stage-dependent mycobiome dynamics.
- **[[aspergillus]] fumigatus and Exophiala spinifera were enriched in ESRD**, both being opportunistic fungal pathogens that can cause invasive infections in immunocompromised hosts.
- **Opportunistic fungal pathogen abundance correlated with creatinine and homocysteine levels**, linking fungal [[dysbiosis]] to CKD severity markers and cardiovascular risk factors.
- Multi-omics integration revealed metabolomic signatures associated with the altered mycobiome, connecting fungal ecology to host metabolic disturbances.

## Methods

- **Design**: Cross-sectional multi-omics study.
- **Subjects**: 223 ESRD patients and 69 healthy controls — one of the largest gut mycobiome studies in kidney disease.
- **Sequencing**: Shotgun metagenomic sequencing for comprehensive fungal and bacterial community characterization.
- **Metabolomics**: Untargeted metabolomic profiling to correlate mycobiome changes with metabolite patterns.
- **Analysis**: Differential abundance, correlation with clinical parameters, multi-omics integration.

## Detailed Summary

This study represents one of the largest and most comprehensive characterizations of the gut mycobiome in end-stage renal disease. Using shotgun metagenomics (which provides higher taxonomic resolution than ITS sequencing), the authors identified significant fungal community restructuring in ESRD. The increased diversity parallels findings from earlier CKD stages but the specific taxa changes differ: while [[saccharomyces-cerevisiae]] was enriched in moderate CKD (Hu 2022), it was depleted in ESRD, suggesting a stage-dependent progression of mycobiome dysfunction. The enrichment of [[aspergillus]] fumigatus is clinically concerning because ESRD patients on immunosuppressive therapy (particularly post-transplant) are at risk for invasive aspergillosis. Exophiala spinifera, a melanized (dematiaceous) fungus, is an emerging pathogen in immunocompromised populations. The correlation of these pathogens with creatinine (kidney function) and homocysteine (cardiovascular risk) suggests that the mycobiome may influence both renal and cardiovascular outcomes in ESRD. The metabolomic integration adds functional context by showing which host metabolic pathways are disrupted in association with specific fungal shifts.

## Open Questions

- What drives the reversal of Saccharomyces dynamics from enrichment in moderate CKD to depletion in ESRD.
- Whether the Aspergillus fumigatus gut enrichment predicts subsequent invasive aspergillosis risk in ESRD patients undergoing transplantation.
- How dialysis modality (HD vs. PD) influences the gut mycobiome in ESRD.
