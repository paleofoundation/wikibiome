## Key Claims

- [[Systemic contact dermatitis]] (SCD) may affect up to 10% of nickel-sensitized patients due to dietary nickel found in normal diets
- A targeted, points-based dietary approach is most meaningful for patients with SCD, as the immunologic pathways are complex involving both Th2 and Th1 responses
- Only 37% of patients in a self-reporting nickel allergy registry recognized that nickel could be found in foods, highlighting the need for patient education
- Foods highest in nickel per serving include mussels (154 ug), spirulina (151 ug), canned refried beans (131 ug), oatmeal (83 ug), white beans (82 ug), wheat germ (82 ug)

## Methods/Findings

- **Design**: Short communication / narrative review synthesizing dietary nickel data and clinical evidence
- **Food nickel pyramid**: Developed a "Low Nickel Diet Food Pyramid" with point values to guide patients, based on the scoring system of [[mislankar-2013-low-nickel-diet-scoring]]
- **Nickel content table**: Comprehensive list of foods ranked by nickel content per standard serving using FDA data (10 ug = 1 point)
- **Immunology summary**: SNAS involves both Th1 (IFN-gamma, IL-2, TNF-alpha) and Th2 responses; IL-5 shows a statistically significant rise within 24 hours of oral challenge; the interplay between Type IV (cellular) and Type III (humoral) hypersensitivity is observed
- **Clinical evidence reviewed**: Retrospective case series showed low-nickel diet improved dermatitis in nickel-sensitive patients (14); a points-based diet proposed limiting daily nickel to <150 ug/day; oral hyposensitization (NiOHT) showed promise at 1.5 ug/week cumulative dose
