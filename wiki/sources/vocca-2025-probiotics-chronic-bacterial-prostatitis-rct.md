## Key Findings

- **Faster symptom recovery**: Patients receiving *Lactobacillus casei* DG after ciprofloxacin therapy recovered from prostatitis symptoms in a mean of 2 days vs. 8 days for placebo (*p* < 0.01), with symptom-free intervals of 86 days vs. 42 days ([[vocca-2025-probiotics-chronic-bacterial-prostatitis-rct]], RCT, n=24).
- **Gut microbiota restoration**: At baseline (T0), Bacteroides, Parabacteroides, and *Lactobacillus* GG were absent or negligible in both groups post-antibiotic therapy, indicating dysbiosis. In the probiotic group, these taxa reappeared by T1 (30 days); in placebo, *Lactobacillus* recovery required 90 days (T2) ([[vocca-2025-probiotics-chronic-bacterial-prostatitis-rct]], RCT, n=24).
- **Seminal microbiota normalization**: All CBP patients post-ciprofloxacin had seminal microbiota dominated by *Corynebacterium*, *Peptoniphilus*, and *Veillonella*. The probiotic group showed significantly faster *Lactobacillus* appearance in seminal fluid at T1 (*p* < 0.01) and significant reductions in pathogenic taxa ([[vocca-2025-probiotics-chronic-bacterial-prostatitis-rct]], RCT, n=24).
- **Erectile function improvement**: IIEF-5 scores improved in the probiotic group — at T3, severe ED cases dropped from 2 to 0 and normal scores rose from 1 to 4, compared with minimal change in placebo ([[vocca-2025-probiotics-chronic-bacterial-prostatitis-rct]], RCT, n=24).
- **Quality of life and psychological benefits**: SF-36 scores significantly improved in the probiotic group from T1 onward (*p* < 0.01). Zung SAS and SDS anxiety/depression scores significantly decreased by T3 (*p* < 0.01), suggesting a gut-brain axis component ([[vocca-2025-probiotics-chronic-bacterial-prostatitis-rct]], RCT, n=24).

## Methods

- **Design**: Randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled, multicenter clinical trial (March 2022 – April 2024).
- **Population**: 24 men aged 18–55 with clinical, instrumental, and microbiological diagnosis of chronic bacterial prostatitis (NIH category II) for >6 months. 82 screened, 24 enrolled.
- **Protocol**: All patients received ciprofloxacin 1000 mg daily for 4 weeks. After antibiotic completion, patients were randomized 1:1 to either *Lactobacillus casei* DG (Enterolactis Plus, 24 billion live cells/day) or identical placebo for 3 months.
- **Follow-up**: T0 (admission), T1 (30 days), T2 (90 days), T3 (180 days).
- **Outcomes**: Primary — number of prostatitis exacerbations. Secondary — gut and seminal microbiota composition, adverse events (Naranjo Scale), symptom severity (NIH-CPSI, IPSS), erectile function (IIEF-5), quality of life (SF-36), anxiety/depression (Zung SAS/SDS).
- **Microbiota assessment**: QIAstat-Dx Gastrointestinal Panel (gut pathogens), culture isolation tests with Gram staining, and Meares-Stamey test (seminal/prostatic fluid).
- **Statistical analysis**: Parametric *t*-test, Pearson correlation, Shapiro-Wilk normality, ANOVA, Tukey and Benjamini-Hochberg corrections. Significance threshold *p* < 0.01.
- **Ethics**: Approved by Local Ethical Committee of Calabria Centro (ID 258, September 2019).

## Detailed Summary

**Chronic bacterial prostatitis** (CBP) is a category II chronic prostatitis affecting 7–14% of men with chronic prostatitis, typically treated with prolonged fluoroquinolone courses that increase antibiotic resistance risk. The pathophysiology involves bacterial biofilm formation and increased intestinal permeability, suggesting a gut-prostate axis where intestinal bacterial reservoirs may seed prostatic infections.

**Baseline dysbiosis**: Post-ciprofloxacin therapy, all 24 patients showed profound gut dysbiosis — Bacteroides, Parabacteroides, and *Lactobacillus* GG were absent or negligible at T0. The QIAstat-Dx panel confirmed absence of acute gut pathogens at baseline. Seminal fluid at T0 was dominated by *Corynebacterium*, *Peptoniphilus*, and *Veillonella* in all patients.

**Gut microbiota trajectory**: In the probiotic group (n=12), Bacteroides, Parabacteroides, and *Lactobacillus* GG reappeared at T1 (30 days) with significant increases (*p* < 0.01 at each time point). In the placebo group (n=12), *Lactobacillus* recovery was delayed until T2 (90 days), though all participants showed normalization by T3 (180 days). At T3, the probiotic group had higher *Lactobacillus* CFU/g than placebo.

**Seminal microbiota trajectory**: *Lactobacillus* appeared in seminal fluid at T1 in the probiotic group (*p* < 0.01), while *Corynebacterium*, *Peptoniphilus*, and *Veillonella* declined. The placebo group also showed normalization but slower, with significant *Lactobacillus* increases only by T2. The significant reduction of pathogenic seminal taxa (including *Pseudomonas*, *Staphylococcus*, *Streptococcus*) suggests competitive exclusion by the restored commensal flora.

**Clinical outcomes**: NIH-CPSI and IPSS symptom scores decreased significantly in both groups over time (*p* < 0.01 vs. T0), but the between-group difference was not statistically significant (NIH-CPSI: *p* = 0.187 at T3; IPSS: *p* = 0.546 at T3). However, the probiotic group showed significantly faster recovery from exacerbations (2 vs. 8 days) and longer symptom-free intervals (86 vs. 42 days).

**Erectile function**: IIEF-5 data showed improvement in the probiotic group: at T0, 2 patients had severe ED (IIEF-5 0–7) and 1 had normal function (22–25); at T3, 0 had severe ED and 4 had normal function. The placebo group showed minimal change (2 severe at T0, 1 at T3; 2 normal at T0, 2 at T3).

**Psychological outcomes**: SF-36 quality of life scores improved significantly in the probiotic group from T1 (*p* < 0.01). Zung SAS anxiety scores were significantly lower in the probiotic group at T2 (*p* < 0.05) and T3 (*p* < 0.01). Zung SDS depression scores were significantly lower in the probiotic group at T2 and T3 (*p* < 0.01).

**Safety**: No adverse drug reactions or drug interactions were recorded in either group. The Naranjo Scale assessment confirmed safety throughout the trial.

## Contradictions / Tensions

- **NIH-CPSI and IPSS not significantly different between groups**: While the probiotic group showed faster recovery and longer symptom-free intervals, the overall NIH-CPSI (*p* = 0.187) and IPSS (*p* = 0.546) scores at T3 were not significantly different between groups. This tension between faster recovery kinetics and similar endpoint scores suggests the probiotic may primarily affect relapse dynamics rather than steady-state symptom severity.
- **Small sample size**: n=24 total (12 per arm) limits statistical power and generalizability. The *t*-test power was only 0.65, well below the conventional 0.80 threshold, meaning the study may have been underpowered to detect real between-group differences in some measures.
- **No direct mechanism demonstrated**: The study documents microbiota changes and clinical outcomes but does not establish a causal mechanism linking gut restoration to prostatic symptom relief. The gut-prostate transmigration hypothesis is cited from prior literature but not tested.

## Open Questions

1. **What is the mechanism of bacterial transmigration from gut to prostate?** The study hypothesizes increased intestinal permeability as the route but does not measure barrier integrity markers (zonulin, LPS, etc.).
2. **Would longer probiotic treatment extend the symptom-free interval further?** The 86-day symptom-free interval in the probiotic group is promising but treatment was only 3 months — does the effect persist beyond 6 months?
3. **What is the metal dependency profile of the pathogenic seminal taxa?** *Corynebacterium*, *Peptoniphilus*, and *Veillonella* dominance post-antibiotic is unexplored from a metallomics perspective — do these organisms exploit specific metal niches vacated by commensal depletion?
4. **Does the erectile function improvement persist?** IIEF-5 data at T3 are encouraging but longer follow-up is needed to determine durability.
5. **Would a larger RCT confirm the trend in NIH-CPSI and IPSS?** The underpowered design (power = 0.65) leaves open whether a larger trial would find significant between-group differences in these primary symptom measures.
6. **Is the gut-brain axis improvement (Zung SAS/SDS) mediated by microbiota restoration or by reduced prostatitis symptoms?** Disentangling the psychological benefit of reduced pain/urinary symptoms from a direct microbial-neurological effect would require a more mechanistic study design.
