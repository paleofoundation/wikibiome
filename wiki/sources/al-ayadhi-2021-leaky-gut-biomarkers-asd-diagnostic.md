# Leaky Gut Biomarkers for Early ASD Diagnosis

## Key Findings

- **Altered gut microbiota observed frequently in autistic patients** may trigger increased intestinal permeability ("leaky gut"), allowing bacterial products to enter systemic circulation ([[al-ayadhi-2021-leaky-gut-biomarkers-asd-diagnostic]], cross-sectional)
- **Bacterial metabolites** (SCFAs, LPS, beta-cresol) and **intestinal proteins** ([[calprotectin]], zonulin, lysozyme) serve as measurable biomarkers for leaky gut detection in ASD
- **Disrupted gut-blood-brain barriers** can explain how bacterial metabolites reach the brain and contribute to neuroinflammation
- **Probiotics** may represent a non-invasive tool to reestablish healthy gut microbiota and restore barrier integrity
- **Calprotectin and zonulin** are particularly promising as non-invasive fecal and serum biomarkers for gut barrier dysfunction in ASD <!-- NEEDS VERIFICATION: specific sensitivity/specificity values -->

## Detailed Summary

This study examines the "leaky gut" hypothesis in autism spectrum disorder through the lens of measurable biomarkers. The hypothesis proposes that ASD-associated gut dysbiosis disrupts the intestinal epithelial barrier, allowing bacterial metabolites and endotoxins to enter systemic circulation and cross the blood-brain barrier, contributing to neuroinflammation and behavioral symptoms.

The authors evaluate several categories of biomarkers: (1) Intestinal barrier proteins — zonulin (a regulator of tight junction permeability), calprotectin (a neutrophil-derived marker of gut inflammation), and lysozyme (an innate immune antimicrobial protein); (2) Bacterial metabolites — altered SCFA profiles (indicating dysbiosis), elevated LPS (indicating gram-negative bacterial translocation), and beta-cresol (a product of tyrosine fermentation by Clostridioides and other bacteria).

Elevated fecal calprotectin in ASD children indicates intestinal inflammation, while elevated serum zonulin indicates compromised tight junction integrity. Together, these markers provide objective evidence for the gut barrier disruption that may link [[dysbiosis]] to neurological symptoms. The review proposes that these biomarkers could enable early detection of gut-mediated ASD risk, potentially before behavioral symptoms manifest.

## Open Questions

- Can calprotectin or zonulin levels predict which children at genetic risk for ASD will develop symptoms?
- How do heavy metal exposures (lead, mercury) interact with gut barrier integrity in ASD?
- Does restoring gut barrier function (via probiotics, zinc supplementation, or other interventions) improve ASD behavioral symptoms?
- What is the temporal relationship — does dysbiosis precede leaky gut, or do they develop simultaneously?
