# Overview

First two-sample MR study to investigate the **causal relationship between 10 human intestinal metabolites and ASD risk**. Uses data from the Framingham Heart Study metabolome (2,076 European participants) and the Psychiatric Genomics Consortium ASD GWAS (61,220 participants).

# Key Findings

## Serotonin Increases ASD Risk

- **Serotonin** may increase ASD risk (IVW OR = 1.060, 95% CI: 1.006-1.118)
- Consistent across MR-PRESSO and maximum likelihood methods
- Serotonin is widely studied for its role in neurodevelopment and is produced by enterochromaffin cells regulated by gut microbiota

## Choline Decreases ASD Risk

- **Choline** could decrease ASD risk (IVW OR = 0.925, 95% CI: 0.868-0.988)
- Some prior reports suggested choline increases ASD risk, creating ambiguity
- The specific mechanism by which choline acts on ASD remains to be explored

## No Causality for Other Metabolites

- No definite causality observed for trimethylamine-N-oxide, betaine, carnitine, glutamate, kynurenine, phenylalanine, tryptophan, or tyrosine
- Funnel plots and MR-PRESSO test found no outliers
- Cochran's Q test showed no significant heterogeneity

## Clinical Implications

- Managing children's diet to control serotonin should be essential component to reduce ASD risk
- Antibiotic administration and FMT could help alter serotonin and choline concentrations
