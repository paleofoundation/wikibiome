## Key Findings

- **Nickel is the most abundant metal contaminant** in recombinant Abeta40 at 72.5 mmol/mol, and enhances aggregation 5.7-fold ([[benoit-2021-nickel-chelator-inhibits-amyloid-beta]], in-vitro).
- **Zinc drives the strongest aggregation** (14-fold enhancement), but nickel is a previously unrecognized contributor to the aggregation cascade.
- **DMG (dimethylglyoxime)**, a nickel-specific chelator, inhibited Abeta40 aggregation by 40-85%, suggesting nickel removal as a therapeutic target distinct from the well-studied copper and zinc chelation approaches.
- **Multiple metals promote aggregation**: Nickel (5.7-fold), zinc (14-fold), copper, and [[aluminum]] all enhanced Abeta40 aggregation, but with different potencies and potentially different binding sites.
- **Nickel-specific chelation is feasible**: DMG's selectivity for nickel over other biologically relevant metals demonstrates that targeted, metal-specific chelation can modulate amyloid aggregation without broad-spectrum metal disruption.

## Methods

- **Study design**: In-vitro biophysical study
- **Target**: Recombinant Abeta40 peptide (amyloid-beta 1-40)
- **Key measurements**: ICP-MS metal content analysis of recombinant Abeta40; Thioflavin T (ThT) fluorescence aggregation kinetics; transmission electron microscopy (TEM) of amyloid fibrils; chelator dose-response curves
- **Chelator tested**: Dimethylglyoxime (DMG) — a well-characterized nickel-specific chelator
- **Controls**: Metal-free conditions, individual metal additions (Ni, Zn, Cu, Al), chelator alone without peptide
- **Sample size**: N/A (in-vitro)

## Detailed Summary

This Scientific Reports study from the Maier laboratory addresses a previously overlooked contributor to amyloid-beta aggregation: **[[nickel]]**. Using ICP-MS analysis, the authors first demonstrated that commercially produced recombinant Abeta40 contains significant metal contamination, with nickel being the most abundant at 72.5 mmol/mol — a finding that raises questions about how many previous Abeta aggregation studies were unknowingly influenced by trace nickel. Systematic metal addition experiments showed that nickel enhanced Abeta40 aggregation 5.7-fold, while zinc (the most potent) drove 14-fold enhancement. The aggregation enhancement by nickel likely occurs through coordination at histidine residues (His6, His13, His14) on Abeta40, the same residues that bind copper and zinc. This represents a classic **[[mis-metallation]]** scenario: nickel occupying metal-binding sites normally occupied by physiological metals, but driving pathological folding patterns. The therapeutic proof-of-concept came from DMG (dimethylglyoxime), a well-characterized chelator with high selectivity for Ni²⁺. DMG inhibited Abeta40 aggregation by 40-85% in a dose-dependent manner, demonstrating that removing nickel alone — without chelating the copper and zinc that have other essential functions — is sufficient to substantially reduce aggregation. This selectivity is clinically important: broad-spectrum metal chelators (like EDTA or DTPA) could cause dangerous systemic metal depletion, but nickel-specific chelation avoids this problem because nickel has no known essential function in human physiology.
