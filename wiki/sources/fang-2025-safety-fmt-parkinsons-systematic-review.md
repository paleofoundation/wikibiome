# Safety and Efficacy of Fecal Microbiota Transplantation in the Treatment of Parkinson's Disease: A Systematic Review

## Summary

This systematic review evaluates the safety and efficacy evidence for [[fecal-microbiota-transplant]] (FMT) in [[parkinsons-disease]], aggregating data from published clinical studies and case reports.

## Key Findings

- FMT appears safe in PD patients, with most adverse events being mild GI symptoms
- GI outcomes (constipation, bowel function) consistently improve after FMT
- Motor symptom improvements are reported but inconsistent across studies
- Microbiome engraftment is achievable but variable in duration and completeness
- Multiple dosing schedules may improve engraftment durability
- Both colonic and capsule-based delivery methods have been used
- Larger, well-controlled RCTs are needed to establish efficacy

## Relevance to Gut-Metal-Microbiome Axis

This systematic review provides the clinical safety evidence needed to advance [[fecal-microbiota-transplant]] as an intervention for the gut-metal-microbiome axis in [[parkinsons-disease]]. The consistent improvement in constipation is directly relevant to reducing metal absorption (shorter colonic transit time = less [[iron]], [[nickel]], [[lead]], [[manganese]] absorption). The safety profile supports the feasibility of FMT trials that incorporate metallomic endpoints -- measuring whether FMT normalizes serum metal panels alongside microbiome composition. Future FMT trials in PD should include: (1) metallomic profiling pre/post FMT; (2) SCFA measurements as proxy for [[iron]] regulatory capacity; (3) gut permeability markers indicating barrier restoration; (4) donor screening for metal-handling microbiome capacity. This review establishes FMT as a viable platform for testing the full gut-metal-microbiome intervention hypothesis.

## Limitations

- Small number of published clinical studies/case series
- Heterogeneous study designs and FMT protocols
- Short follow-up periods in most studies
- Donor selection criteria not standardized for PD
- Publication bias toward positive results likely
