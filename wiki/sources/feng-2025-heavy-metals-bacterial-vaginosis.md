## Key Findings

- **Serum lead was positively associated with bacterial vaginosis risk.** In the fully adjusted model (Model II), the highest tertile of serum lead (1.3-23.9 ug/dl) showed a 35% increased risk of BV compared to the lowest tertile (OR = 1.35, 95% CI: 1.06-1.72, p = 0.016). The dose-response trend was significant across all three models (p for trend = 0.015) ([[feng-2025-heavy-metals-bacterial-vaginosis]], cross-sectional, n=2,493).
- **Serum cadmium was positively associated with bacterial vaginosis risk.** Overall cadmium concentration showed a 48% increase in BV risk in the fully adjusted model (OR = 1.48, 95% CI: 1.25-1.76, p < 0.001). The highest cadmium tertile (0.5-7.4 ug/l) showed a 41% increase in BV susceptibility (OR = 1.41, 95% CI: 1.01-1.98, p = 0.047) ([[feng-2025-heavy-metals-bacterial-vaginosis]], cross-sectional, n=2,493).
- **Serum total mercury did not show a statistically significant association with BV** overall (p > 0.05), though a negative correlation was observed in a subgroup of females aged 25-36 (OR = 0.86, 95% CI: 0.76-0.98, p = 0.022) ([[feng-2025-heavy-metals-bacterial-vaginosis]], cross-sectional, n=2,493).
- **Stratification analysis confirmed the stability** of lead and cadmium associations across most subgroups defined by age, race, education, marital status, BMI, HDL-cholesterol, total calcium, and uric acid ([[feng-2025-heavy-metals-bacterial-vaginosis]]).
- **The authors propose that heavy metals act as endocrine-disrupting compounds (EDCs)**, interfering with hormone receptors and disrupting the hypothalamic-pituitary-gonadal axis, which may alter the vaginal microbiota and increase BV susceptibility ([[feng-2025-heavy-metals-bacterial-vaginosis]]).

## Methods

**Design:** Cross-sectional analysis of NHANES data (2001-2002 and 2003-2004 cycles).

**Population:** 2,493 adult female participants aged 18-49 years with complete data on bacterial vaginosis status and serum heavy metal levels. Starting pool was 21,161 participants; 18,355 excluded for missing BV data, 99 for missing heavy metal data, 214 for missing covariates.

**Exposure measurement:** Serum [[lead]] and [[cadmium]] measured by PerkinElmer SIMAA 6000 simultaneous multi-element atomic absorption spectrometer (2001-2002) and ICP-MS (2003-2004). Serum total [[mercury]] measured by flow injection cold vapor atomic absorption analysis (2001-2002) and ICP-MS (2003-2004). Results below the lower limit of detection were substituted with LOD / sqrt(2).

**Outcome:** [[Bacterial-vaginosis]] defined by Nugent score on Gram-stained vaginal swabs. Nugent score 0-6 = negative; 7-10 = positive for BV. BV prevalence was 32.2% (802/2,493).

**Statistical approach:** Multivariable logistic regression with three models: unadjusted; Model I (age, race, BMI, education); Model II (all covariates including PIR, marital status, HDL-cholesterol, total cholesterol, total calcium, uric acid, household size, physical activity). Three smooth curve fittings assessed nonlinearity. Stratified analyses performed across multiple demographic and clinical variables.

**Covariates:** Age, race, BMI, education, PIR, marital status, HDL-cholesterol, total cholesterol, total calcium, uric acid, household size, moderate and vigorous physical activity over the past 30 days.

## Detailed Summary

The study analyzed the association between serum concentrations of three heavy metals ([[lead]], [[cadmium]], [[mercury]]) and [[bacterial-vaginosis]] prevalence in a nationally representative cohort of American women of reproductive age.

**Baseline characteristics:** Women with BV had significantly higher mean serum lead (1.42 +/- 1.41 vs 1.16 +/- 1.07 ug/dl, p < 0.001) and cadmium (0.60 +/- 0.61 vs 0.46 +/- 0.50 ug/l, p < 0.001) compared to BV-negative women. Mercury did not differ significantly between groups (1.26 +/- 1.70 vs 1.31 +/- 1.78, p = 0.366). BV-positive women were more likely to be Non-Hispanic Black, have lower education, lower PIR, higher BMI, higher uric acid, and lower HDL-cholesterol.

**Lead findings:** The dose-response relationship between serum lead and BV was consistent across all three regression models. Smooth curve fitting confirmed a monotonic positive relationship (p = 0.020). The association was particularly pronounced in women aged 37-49 (OR = 1.20, 95% CI: 1.05-1.37, p = 0.008), those who were married (OR = 1.20, p = 0.008) or separated (OR = 2.47, p = 0.043), and those with BMI 29.91-65.41 (OR = 1.27, p = 0.010).

**Cadmium findings:** The cadmium-BV association was the strongest of the three metals tested. The smooth curve fitting showed a nonlinear relationship (p < 0.001) with risk increasing steeply up to approximately 3 ug/l before plateauing. The association was especially strong in younger women aged 18-24 (OR = 2.03, 95% CI: 1.38-2.99, p < 0.001), Non-Hispanic Black women (OR = 1.63, 95% CI: 1.07-2.47, p = 0.022), never married women (OR = 2.03, 95% CI: 1.36-3.02, p < 0.001), and women with less than high school education (OR = 1.87, 95% CI: 1.34-2.59, p < 0.001).

**Mercury findings:** No significant overall association. A protective effect of mercury was observed only in women aged 25-36 (OR = 0.86, 95% CI: 0.76-0.98, p = 0.022), but the authors note this was unstable across subgroups and may be a stratification artifact.

**Proposed mechanisms:** The authors discuss several pathways by which [[lead]] and [[cadmium]] could increase BV risk: (1) acting as endocrine-disrupting compounds that interfere with hormone receptors and the HPG axis, disrupting hormonal balance that maintains vaginal [[lactobacillus]] dominance; (2) acting as metalloestrogens that contribute to estrogen-related pathology; (3) damaging mucosal immunity through immunotoxic effects on vaginal epithelial cells and local lymphoid tissue; (4) disrupting barrier defense involving epithelial and endothelial cells. BV itself is characterized by loss of [[lactobacillus]] dominance and overgrowth of at least 35 bacterial phylotypes including anaerobes such as [[gardnerella]] ([[feng-2025-heavy-metals-bacterial-vaginosis]]).

**Sensitivity analysis:** Results remained consistent when missing covariate data were imputed via multiple interpolation.

## Contradictions / Tensions

- **Mercury null finding:** The lack of a significant mercury-BV association may appear to conflict with studies showing mercury's immunotoxic and endocrine-disrupting effects in other reproductive contexts. However, this may reflect tissue-specific differences in mercury accumulation and the vaginal niche's relative exposure.
- **Cross-sectional limitation:** The authors themselves acknowledge that causality cannot be established. The association between serum metals and BV could be confounded by socioeconomic factors that drive both metal exposure and BV risk, despite covariate adjustment.
- **Historical data:** NHANES 2001-2004 data may not reflect current exposure patterns. Lead exposure has declined significantly in the U.S. since this period, which may affect generalizability to contemporary populations.
- **Vaginal vs. serum metals:** The study measured serum metal levels, not vaginal tissue or fluid concentrations. The local metal environment in the vaginal niche — which directly contacts the microbiota — may differ substantially from systemic serum levels.

## Open Questions

1. **Mechanism specificity:** Do lead and cadmium directly alter the vaginal microbiome composition (e.g., selectively favoring BV-associated anaerobes over [[lactobacillus]]), or do they act indirectly through endocrine and immune disruption? Animal models or in vitro studies with vaginal bacteria are needed.
2. **Dose-response threshold:** The cadmium smooth curve suggests a plateau effect above ~3 ug/l. Is there a threshold below which cadmium exposure does not increase BV risk?
3. **Temporal relationship:** Does metal exposure precede BV onset, or does BV-associated inflammation alter metal metabolism? Prospective cohort data are needed.
4. **Metal-microbe interaction:** Do BV-associated anaerobes like [[gardnerella]] have metal dependencies that give them a competitive advantage in lead/cadmium-enriched environments? This is a key gap for WikiBiome's ecological model.
5. **Intervention evidence:** Does reducing heavy metal body burden (through chelation, dietary modification, or exposure reduction) reduce BV recurrence rates? No clinical trials exist on this question.
