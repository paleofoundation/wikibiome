# Gastroesophageal disease risk and inhalational exposure: a systematic review and meta-analysis

## Key Claims

- Inhalational exposures (cigarette smoking, waterpipe smoking, and [[particulate matter]]) showed significant associations with upper gastrointestinal diseases in a meta-analysis of 111 studies
- The pooled estimate for [[esophagitis]] risk with inhalational exposure was OR 1.32 (95% CI 1.06-1.65; I^2=86%)
- [[gerd]] risk was OR 1.71 (95% CI 1.14-2.55; I^2=94%), representing a 71% increased risk with inhalational exposures
- Peptic ulcer disease risk was OR 1.21 (95% CI 1.03-1.43; I^2=93%), esophageal cancer OR 1.83 (95% CI 1.54-2.18; I^2=73%), and gastric cancer OR 1.71 (95% CI 1.39-2.10; I^2=73%)
- No significant association was found for [[Barretts esophagus]] (OR 0.93, 95% CI 0.65-1.34; I^2=76%)
- WTC-PM exposure was associated with obstructive airway disease (OAD) and gastroesophageal diseases including GERD and BE; 44% of WTC rescue workers developed GERD symptoms by 2005

## Methods/Findings

- **Study type**: Systematic review and meta-analysis following PRISMA guidelines
- **Search**: PubMed (N=764) and Web of Science (N=1,036); 111 studies met eligibility criteria
- **Exposures**: PM2.5, PM10, cigarette/tobacco smoke, waterpipe tobacco, vape/e-cigarette aerosols, marijuana/cannabinoid inhalation
- **Outcomes**: GERD, BE, PUD, esophagitis, gastritis, esophageal cancer (EAC), gastric cancer (GCa)
- **Risk of bias**: NOS assessment; N=35 good quality cohort studies, N=23 good quality case-control studies
- **Smoking-GERD**: Former smoking significantly associated with GERD risk; waterpipe smoking associated with "severe and frequent" reflux; gender-specific differences noted
- **Sensitivity analyses**: Confirmed findings; excluding high-heterogeneity studies did not change direction of effects
