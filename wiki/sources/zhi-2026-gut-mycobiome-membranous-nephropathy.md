# Gut Mycobiome in Membranous Nephropathy Patients

## Key Findings

- **Gut mycobiome composition was significantly altered** in patients with membranous nephropathy (MN) compared to healthy controls, establishing that fungal [[dysbiosis]] extends to autoimmune kidney diseases.
- **Membranous nephropathy** is a leading cause of nephrotic syndrome in adults, characterized by immune complex deposition along the glomerular basement membrane. The study extends the gut-kidney axis concept to the fungal domain.
- **Fungal community shifts** may contribute to the immune dysregulation underlying MN, given that fungal antigens are potent activators of innate and adaptive immunity.
- This is among the first studies to characterize the gut mycobiome specifically in membranous nephropathy, complementing bacterial microbiome studies in [[chronic-kidney-disease]].

## Methods

- **Design**: Cross-sectional observational study comparing gut mycobiome profiles between MN patients and healthy controls.
- **Sequencing**: Fungal community profiling (likely ITS-based sequencing) of fecal samples.
- **Analysis**: Taxonomic composition, alpha and beta diversity comparisons, differential abundance testing.
- **Journal**: Scientific Reports (2026, early access).

## Detailed Summary

Membranous nephropathy is an autoimmune glomerular disease where anti-PLA2R antibodies target podocyte antigens, leading to proteinuria and progressive kidney damage. While the gut bacterial microbiome has been increasingly linked to CKD pathogenesis, the fungal component (mycobiome) has received far less attention. This study characterized the gut mycobiome in MN patients, revealing significant compositional differences from healthy individuals. The findings suggest that fungal dysbiosis may participate in the immune activation cascade that drives MN pathology — either through direct fungal antigen-driven immune stimulation or through disruption of fungal-bacterial ecological balance in the gut. The identification of specific fungal taxa altered in MN could inform biomarker development and mycobiome-targeted interventions.

## Open Questions

- Which specific fungal taxa are enriched or depleted in MN, and do they differ from patterns seen in other CKD subtypes.
- Whether mycobiome shifts precede MN diagnosis or are a consequence of immune-mediated kidney damage.
- Whether antifungal or probiotic yeast interventions could modify MN disease course.
