## Key Findings

- **Gut microbiome dysbiosis is consistently observed in MS patients**, with enrichment of pro-inflammatory taxa and depletion of SCFA-producing commensals including [[faecalibacterium-prausnitzii]]
- **The gut-brain axis** mediates microbiome effects on CNS inflammation and demyelination through three main pathways: immune modulation (Th17/Treg balance), neural signaling (vagus nerve), and metabolite production (SCFAs, tryptophan derivatives)
- **[[akkermansia-muciniphila]]** is frequently enriched in MS, a counterintuitive finding since this organism is typically considered beneficial; in MS, its mucin-degrading activity may compromise gut barrier function under inflammatory conditions
- **Therapeutic strategies** including [[probiotics]], [[fecal-microbiota-transplant]], and dietary interventions show preliminary promise for modulating MS disease activity through microbiome restoration
- **Tryptophan metabolism** through the microbiome-AhR axis is a key pathway linking gut dysbiosis to CNS inflammation in MS

## Detailed Summary

This master's thesis from the University of Lisbon synthesizes the current understanding of how gut microbiome composition relates to multiple sclerosis pathogenesis. MS is a chronic autoimmune demyelinating disease of the central nervous system, and growing evidence implicates the gut microbiome as a modulator of CNS autoimmunity through the gut-brain axis.

The thesis reviews the three main communication pathways of the gut-brain axis in the context of MS. First, the immune pathway: gut microbiota shape the balance between pro-inflammatory Th17 cells (which drive MS pathology) and anti-inflammatory regulatory T cells (Tregs). Dysbiosis with increased pro-inflammatory organisms shifts this balance toward Th17 dominance. Second, the neural pathway: vagus nerve signaling transmits information about gut microbial composition and metabolites to the CNS, modulating neuroinflammation. Third, the metabolic pathway: microbial metabolites including SCFAs and tryptophan derivatives cross the blood-brain barrier and directly modulate glial cell function and neuroinflammation.

The MS-associated microbiome signature shows several consistent features across studies: depletion of SCFA producers (particularly [[faecalibacterium-prausnitzii]] and Clostridia cluster XIV), enrichment of [[akkermansia-muciniphila]] (paradoxically, given its association with health in other contexts), and altered [[prevotella]] abundance (typically depleted). The Akkermansia enrichment may reflect a compensatory mucin-turnover response to gut barrier dysfunction rather than a pathogenic role per se.

Therapeutic approaches reviewed include multi-strain probiotics (primarily Lactobacillus and Bifidobacterium species), FMT, and dietary interventions (Mediterranean diet, high-fiber diets). Early-phase clinical studies show some promise for reducing inflammatory markers and modifying disease activity, though large-scale RCTs are lacking.
