# The Cerebrospinal Fluid Virome in People with HIV: Links to Neuroinflammation and Cognition

## Summary

This cross-sectional study analyzed 85 CSF samples (74 from people with HIV [PWH] on antiretroviral therapy, 11 controls without HIV) through shotgun metagenomics for DNA and RNA viruses to characterize the CSF virome and its relationship to [[neuroinflammation]], cognitive function, and depressive symptoms.

## Key Findings

- Of 46 read-positive CSF samples: 93.5% contained prokaryotic virus (PV) sequences, 47.8% human eukaryotic virus (hEV), 45.6% non-human eukaryotic virus (nhEV)
- HIV status explained 3.4% of variation in viral composition (p=0.016)
- Higher relative abundance of PV correlated with higher CSF S100beta (p=0.002) and beta-Amyloid 1-42 fragment
- Higher RA of hEV correlated with **better cognition** (rho -0.38, p=0.003) and lower beta-A-42
- Higher RA of nhEV correlated with **poorer cognitive performance** (rho 0.26, p=0.022)
- Three CSF clusters identified: differences in astrocytosis, beta-A-42, tau protein, and cognitive functions
- Participants with hEV-enriched CSF showed better cognitive performance vs virus-devoid or nhEV-enriched CSF

## Relevance to Gut-Metal-Microbiome Axis

This study demonstrates that the CSF virome is not merely a passive bystander but actively associated with [[neuroinflammation]] and cognition. The finding that prokaryotic viruses (bacteriophages) in CSF correlate with neuroinflammatory markers links gut-derived phages to CNS health. Bacteriophages from the [[gut-microbiome]] can cross the [[blood-brain-barrier]] and influence neuroimmune processes. For [[multiple-sclerosis]], this suggests that virome [[dysbiosis]] -- potentially driven by [[heavy-metals]]-induced disruption of bacterial communities and their associated phages -- could contribute to [[neuroinflammation]] through translocation of gut phages to the CNS.

## Limitations

- HIV population, not MS
- Cross-sectional design
- Small control group (n=11)
- Cannot determine directionality of virome-cognition associations
