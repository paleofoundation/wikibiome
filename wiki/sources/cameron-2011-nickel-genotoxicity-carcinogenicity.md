# Exploring the Molecular Mechanisms of Nickel-Induced Genotoxicity and Carcinogenicity

## Why This Paper Matters

This NIH-funded review provides the most detailed mechanistic examination of [[nickel]]-induced genotoxicity and carcinogenicity available, bridging the gap between Costa's foundational 1982 work on nickel particle phagocytosis and the modern understanding of nickel's molecular carcinogenic mechanisms. It systematically covers the full spectrum from environmental exposure and body deposition through oxidative stress, DNA damage types, cell surface receptor interactions, hypoxia-inducible signaling, 8-hydroxydeoxyguanosine formation, and epigenetic mechanisms. The paper's integration of NTP (National Toxicology Program) animal inhalation studies with human occupational epidemiology makes it an essential bridge reference for nickel carcinogenesis.

## Key Claims

- Nickel toxicity is influenced by route of exposure, dose, and solubility of the nickel compound
- Lung inhalation is the major route of exposure for nickel-induced toxicity; primary target organs are kidneys and lungs
- The most common health effect of nickel is allergic reaction (affecting ~10% of women and ~2% of men)
- Nickel refinery dust and Ni3S2 are classified as Group A/Group 1 human carcinogens by US EPA, IARC, and DHHS
- Nickel carbonyl (Ni(CO)4) is classified as Group B2 (probable human carcinogen) by US EPA
- Oxidative stress, DNA damage (strand breaks, crosslinks, base modifications), and epigenetic alterations are the three primary genotoxic mechanisms
- The carcinogenic potential of nickel is directly proportional to the metal's ability to enter cells and increase intracellular nickel ion levels
- Nickel activates the hypoxia-inducible factor 1 (HIF-1) signaling pathway, contributing to carcinogenesis through iron homeostasis disruption

## Methods/Findings

- **Study type**: Comprehensive literature review of nickel genotoxicity and carcinogenicity
- **Affiliation**: NIH RCMI Center for Environmental Health, Jackson State University

### Environmental Exposure
- Average dietary nickel intake: ~170 micrograms per day (no known effect on human/ecosystem health)
- Inhalation: 0.1-1 ug nickel daily (excluding tobacco smoke) for general population
- Occupational: 0.1-0.25 mg nickel/day inhaled; nickel refining operations 0.3-0.8 mg nickel/day
- Nickel in soil near nickel-producing industries: 4-80 ppm (can increase up to 9,000 ppm)
- Foods high in nickel: tea, coffee, chocolate, soybeans, oatmeal, cabbage, spinach, potatoes
- Children more likely to ingest soil particles containing nickel

### Nickel Absorption and Deposition
- Particles <30 um are inhaled; <10 um deposited in lower lung regions
- NiCl2 and NiSO4 button compounds penetrate occluded skin with 77% absorption within one day
- Following exposure to NiS particles in A549 lung cells: most nickel contained in nucleus; soluble NiCl2 shows ions in cytoplasm

### Nickel Toxicity Mechanisms
- **Oxidative stress**: Ni(II) induces oxidative stress in human lymphocytes; order of increasing toxicity: Ni(II) < Cr(VI) < Cr(II)
- Ni(II) does not directly cause efficient free radical generation but reactivity with oxygen derivatives can be modulated by chelation with histidine and cysteine-containing ligands
- **Hematotoxicity**: dose-related decrease in bone marrow cellularity, granulocyte-macrophage and pluri-potent stem-cell proliferative responses; NTP inhalation studies show increased hemoglobin and erythrocyte counts (consistent with erythropoietin response to tissue hypoxia)
- **Immunotoxicity**: most common effect is allergic skin reaction; nickel evokes dual immunomodulatory and immunotoxic effects; skin allergies, lung fibrosis, respiratory tract cancer

### Nickel-Induced Carcinogenicity
- Close relationship between nickel exposure and increased lung/nasal cancer in nickel refinery workers
- **Norwegian nickel refinery** (Grimsrud et al.): correlation between total exposure to water-soluble nickel and lung cancer risk (nickel exposure was sole risk factor after controlling for arsenic, cobalt, asbestos)
- **Finnish refinery** (Harabayata): 1155 workers, 25 years exposure -- increased risk for nasal cancer (SIR 41.1, 95% CI 4.97-148) and excess risk for lung cancer (SIR 2.61, 95% CI 0.96-5.67)
- **Nigerian pediatric cough suppressants**: nickel present at 4.13 and 0.79 mg/L -- public health concern for children

### NTP Animal Inhalation Studies
- **NiO 2-year study**: 65 male and 65 female mice exposed to 0, 0.62, 1.25, 2.5 mg NiO/m3
  - Increased alveolar/bronchiolar adenoma or carcinoma in male F344/N rats
  - Pigmentation of bronchial and mediastinal lymph nodes at >=2.5 mg/m3
- **Ni3S2**: Clear evidence of carcinogenic activity in male and female F344/N rats exposed to 0.6-1.2 mg/m3
  - No evidence in B6C3F1 mice at 1.25, 2.5, or 5 mg/m3
- **NiSO4**: 13-week study -- non-neoplastic lesions in lungs at >=2.5 mg/m3; lymphoid hyperplasia of bronchial/mediastinal lymph nodes

### Nickel Genotoxicity -- DNA Damage
- Nickel disrupts DNA strands, DNA crosslinks, and DNA repair
- In HL-60 leukemia cells: long-term Ni2+ exposure leads to DNA fragmentation, cell death, and ROS (prevented by ascorbic acid or N-acetyl-cysteine)
- Nickel(II) genotoxicity linked to generation of DNA-damaging ROS and inhibition of DNA repair
- Kelly et al.: HPLC-UV-EC evidence of 8-oxoguanine (8-oxoG) formation from free guanine and guanine in DNA mediated by Nickel(II)
- Nickel was the only metal to induce DNA damage at concentrations that induced >50% apoptosis (i.e., <0.05 mM) compared to vanadium
- Wu et al.: DNA damage increased with increasing doses of nickel-refining dusts (SCGE technique); strand breaks peaked at 2-4h exposure
- Nickel stimulates L1 retrotransposition ~2.5-fold (genotoxic stress via transposon mobilization)

### Molecular and Cellular Interactions
- **Cell surface receptors**: Nickel activates extracellular Ca2+-sensing receptor (CaSR) triggering intracellular Ca2+ mobilization
- CaSR activation induces calcium and hypoxia-inducible factor pathway, enabling cells to survive in anaerobic environments
- **HIF-1 pathway**: Nickel strongly activates HIF-1 and tumor marker NDRG1 (Cap43); nickel-induced genes coding for glycolytic enzymes and glucose transporters are regulated by HIF-1
- Sanikov et al.: nickel sulfate can induce hypoxia-inducible genes and IL-8 production in human lung cells with or without interference with iron metabolism

### 8-Hydroxydeoxyguanosine Formation
- In cultured HeLa cells, Ni3S2 induced significant increase in 8-OH-dG formation
- NiO, NiO, NiSO4 did not produce this effect on intratracheal instillation in rats
- Ni3S2 also significantly increased testicular lipid peroxide levels and decreased antioxidant enzyme activities and glutathione levels

### Epigenetic Mechanisms
- Nickel induces DNA methylation, histone modification, RNA-dependent regulation
- Broday et al.: nickel induces a loss of histone H4 and H3 acetylation and DNA hypermethylation
- Nickel causes phosphorylation of histones at serine 10 via JNK/SAPK-dependent manner
- Gene silencing leads to neoplastic transformation
