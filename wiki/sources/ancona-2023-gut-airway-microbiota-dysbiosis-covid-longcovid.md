## Key Findings

- **Dysbiosis pattern in acute COVID-19**: Reduced beneficial taxa (Faecalibacterium prausnitzii, Roseburia) with increased potentially pathogenic bacteria (Streptococcus, Veillonella, Clostridium), correlating with disease severity (review of cohort studies)
- **Persistent dysbiosis in Long COVID**: Evidence suggests dysbiosis may continue after acute infection, potentially maintaining immune dysregulation and symptom persistence
- **Gut-lung axis dysfunction**: Dysbiosis reduces short-chain fatty acid (SCFA)-producing organisms, impairing mucosal barrier function and immune homeostasis in respiratory tract
- **Gut-brain axis implications**: Dysbiosis may drive neurological symptoms of Long COVID through altered microbial metabolite production and intestinal barrier dysfunction

## Methods

Narrative mini-review synthesizing recent literature on microbiota dysbiosis in COVID-19 and Long COVID. Analysis of published studies on both gut and airway dysbiosis patterns, their association with disease severity, and proposed mechanistic links to Long COVID through systemic axes.

## Detailed Summary

This mini-review establishes microbiota dysbiosis as a hallmark of both acute COVID-19 and potentially Long COVID. Key observations include:

**Acute COVID-19 dysbiosis pattern:**
- Significant reduction in beneficial commensals, particularly Faecalibacterium prausnitzii and Roseburia species (short-chain fatty acid producers)
- Relative enrichment of Streptococcus, Veillonella, and Clostridium species
- Dysbiosis severity correlates with COVID-19 disease severity
- Dysbiosis associated with SARS-CoV-2 RNA detection in fecal samples (gastrointestinal replication)

**Proposed mechanisms linking dysbiosis to Long COVID:**

1. **Gut-lung axis dysfunction**: Loss of SCFA-producing bacteria → impaired intestinal barrier → reduced regulatory T cell development → immune dysregulation affecting both gut and airways
2. **Immune hyperactivation**: Dysbiosis promotes pro-inflammatory pathobiont dominance, maintaining elevated systemic inflammation
3. **Gut-brain axis**: Dysbiosis alters production of neurologically active metabolites (SCFAs, tryptophan metabolites), potentially driving cognitive and neuropsychiatric Long COVID symptoms
4. **Persistent viral antigen or reactivation**: Dysbiotic microbiota may be unable to mount effective immune responses against persistent viral elements

The review documents that dysbiosis correlates with immune inflammation markers (IL-6, TNF-α, CRP) even during recovery phase, suggesting dysbiosis contributes to persistent inflammation in Long COVID.

## Relevance to WikiBiome

This paper establishes microbiota dysbiosis as the primary mechanistic bridge between acute SARS-CoV-2 infection and Long COVID symptom persistence. It identifies specific taxa loss (Faecalibacterium, Roseburia) and pathobiont enrichment patterns that should be documented in Long COVID signatures.

## Relevance to Cureva

For Cureva, this paper directly supports dysbiosis-targeted intervention strategies:
- Probiotic/prebiotic restoration of SCFA-producing taxa
- Dietary support for beneficial commensals
- Immune support through microbiota restoration
- Targeting of pathobionts where enriched

## Contradictions / Tensions

The review notes variable dysbiosis patterns across studies, likely due to differences in sampling timing, patient severity, antibiotic exposure, and methodology. The persistence vs. resolution of dysbiosis in Long COVID remains incompletely characterized.

## Open Questions

- How long does dysbiosis persist in Long COVID patients?
- Does dysbiosis resolution correlate with symptom improvement?
- Are specific dysbiosis patterns predictive of Long COVID severity or phenotype?
- Can microbiota-directed interventions reverse dysbiosis and improve outcomes?
- What is the contribution of dysbiosis to Long COVID's neurological symptoms?
