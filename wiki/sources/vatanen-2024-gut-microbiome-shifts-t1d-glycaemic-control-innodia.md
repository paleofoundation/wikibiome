# Key Claims

- **Longitudinal increase in 21 bacterial species** in newly diagnosed T1D cohort but not in unaffected family members
- **Faecalibacterium prausnitzii abundance inversely associated** with HbA1c levels at diagnosis (p=0.0019)
- **Rate of subsequent disease progression** associated with abundance of several bacterial species
- **Rapid decrease in C-peptide levels** associated with lowest gut microbiome diversity at T1D diagnosis
- **Sutterella sp. KLE1602** had increased abundance in newly diagnosed T1D vs unaffected family members (p=1.2x10^-4)

# Methods & Findings

INNODIA study: 98 newly diagnosed T1D + 194 autoantibody-positive unaffected family members:

- Metagenomic sequencing used for microbiome characterization
- Newly diagnosed cohort sampled within 6 weeks and at 3, 6, 12, 24 months
- Family members sampled at baseline and every 6 months for 36 months
- 18 bacterial species + fecal metabolites provided excellent discriminatory power for T1D

# Relevance to Metallomic Framework

- **[[zinc]]**: Faecalibacterium prausnitzii (key finding) produces butyrate via zinc-dependent enzymes -- zinc deficiency may deplete this critical species
- **[[iron]]**: Iron competition in the gut may determine Sutterella abundance -- Sutterella species are iron-dependent
- **[[selenium]]**: Selenium status may modulate the oxidative stress response that shapes post-diagnosis microbiome shifts
- **[[gut-metal-microbiome]]**: The 21 species showing longitudinal change may reflect post-diagnosis metal homeostasis disruption
- **[[dysbiosis]]**: Progressive [[dysbiosis]] after T1D diagnosis correlates with worsening glycemic control
- **[[immune-balance]]**: Microbiome shifts post-diagnosis reflect ongoing autoimmune destruction and metabolic decompensation
