## Key Findings

- **H. pylori inversely associated with erosive reflux disease (ERD)** — H. pylori prevalence was lowest in ERD patients (5.2%) compared to NERD and functional dyspepsia, supporting the hypothesis that H. pylori may be protective against erosive esophageal damage ([[sugihartono-2022-gastric-microbiota-h-pylori-gerd]], cross-sectional, n=197).
- **Anaerobacillus found exclusively in ERD patients** — a novel finding suggesting this genus as a potential biomarker for erosive disease.
- **ERD had significantly higher alpha diversity than NERD** — counterintuitively, the more severe phenotype harbored a more diverse gastric microbial community.
- **[[streptococcus]], [[rothia]], and [[alloprevotella]]** were among the dominant gastric genera across all GERD subtypes.

## Methods

**Cross-sectional study** of 197 patients undergoing upper GI endoscopy, stratified into erosive reflux disease (ERD), non-erosive reflux disease (NERD), and functional dyspepsia groups based on endoscopic and clinical findings. **16S rRNA gene sequencing** of gastric mucosal biopsy samples was used to characterize the gastric microbiota. H. pylori status was determined by both molecular (16S sequencing) and conventional methods. Alpha and beta diversity were compared between groups, and differential abundance analysis identified taxa enriched or depleted in each disease phenotype.

## Detailed Summary

Sugihartono et al. characterized the gastric microbiota across GERD subtypes, revealing important ecological patterns that challenge conventional understanding of H. pylori's role in upper GI disease. The study's key contribution is demonstrating that the gastric microbial ecosystem in GERD is far more complex than the H. pylori-centric model suggests.

The **inverse H. pylori-ERD relationship** (only 5.2% H. pylori prevalence in ERD vs. higher rates in NERD and functional dyspepsia) adds to growing evidence that H. pylori may serve a protective function in the gastroesophageal tract. The proposed mechanism is that H. pylori colonization reduces gastric acid output (through CagA-mediated effects on parietal cells and induction of atrophic gastritis in the corpus), thereby reducing acid reflux and erosive damage. From an ecological perspective, H. pylori's [[nickel]]-dependent urease generates ammonia that locally alkalinizes its microenvironment, which may buffer acid exposure at the gastroesophageal junction.

The **exclusive presence of Anaerobacillus in ERD** is a novel finding with potential as a disease biomarker. Anaerobacillus is an alkaliphilic, facultatively anaerobic genus — its presence specifically in erosive disease may reflect the altered pH environment created by acid-damaged tissue (damaged tissue may paradoxically have higher local pH due to impaired acid secretion from destroyed mucosa and bicarbonate secretion from regenerating epithelium).

The **higher alpha diversity in ERD vs. NERD** is counterintuitive because more severe disease is often assumed to reduce diversity. However, in the gastric environment, H. pylori dominance (more common in NERD) actually suppresses diversity by monopolizing the ecological niche — its loss in ERD opens ecological space for a diverse community. This parallels ecological disturbance theory, where moderate disturbance (but not total ecosystem collapse) can increase diversity.

The dominance of **[[streptococcus]], [[rothia]], and [[alloprevotella]]** across GERD subtypes identifies these as core gastric community members that persist regardless of H. pylori status or disease severity. These genera have different metabolic profiles and oxygen tolerances, suggesting a functionally diverse gastric ecosystem.

## Relevance to WikiBiome

This study enriches the [[helicobacter-pylori]] entity page with evidence for its paradoxical protective role in GERD — a key narrative for WikiBiome's nuanced view of pathogens. The inverse relationship challenges the simplistic "eradicate all pathogens" approach and supports the two-sided ecological engineering concept. H. pylori's nickel-dependent urease and NiFe-hydrogenase enzymes make it a central example of metal dependency as both virulence factor and Achilles' heel (Primitive 4).

## Open Questions

- Does H. pylori eradication in GERD patients worsen erosive disease — and should this inform STOP recommendations?
- What metabolic functions does Anaerobacillus perform in the ERD gastric environment, and does it contribute to or protect against mucosal damage?
- How does proton pump inhibitor (PPI) use — the standard GERD treatment — interact with the gastric microbiome findings, given that PPIs profoundly alter gastric pH and microbial ecology?
- Whether nickel availability in the gastric environment influences H. pylori colonization density and its protective vs. pathogenic behavior.
