## Key Findings

- **Camouflaging** is defined as the use of strategies to mask or compensate for autistic traits in social situations, encompassing masking (hiding autistic behaviors), compensation (using learned strategies to appear neurotypical), and assimilation (attempting to fit in with neurotypical peers) ([[cook-2021-camouflaging-autism-systematic-review|this study]], systematic-review-meta-analysis).
- **Diagnostic delays** are a major consequence of camouflaging — individuals who camouflage effectively may not meet diagnostic thresholds on standard assessments, leading to late or missed diagnoses, particularly in females and those with higher cognitive abilities.
- **Mental health consequences** of sustained camouflaging include exhaustion, burnout, anxiety, depression, suicidal ideation, and identity confusion. The effort required to maintain a neurotypical presentation exacts a psychological toll.
- **Gender differences** in camouflaging are significant — females with ASD camouflage more frequently and more effectively than males, contributing to the historical underdiagnosis of ASD in women and girls.
- **Measurement challenges** persist — the review identified multiple approaches to operationalizing and measuring camouflaging, with no consensus measure established.

## Methods

- **Study type**: Systematic review of the camouflaging literature in ASD.
- **Scope**: Synthesized qualitative, quantitative, and mixed-methods studies on camouflaging behaviors, motivations, consequences, and measurement approaches.
- **Databases**: Comprehensive search across major psychological and medical databases.
- **Focus areas**: Definition and conceptualization, motivations for camouflaging, consequences, gender differences, measurement approaches.

## Detailed Summary

This systematic review provides a comprehensive overview of the camouflaging phenomenon in ASD. Camouflaging represents a significant challenge for ASD research and clinical practice because it can fundamentally alter how autism presents, leading to systematic biases in diagnosis.

The review identifies three key components of camouflaging: masking (suppressing autistic behaviors like stimming), compensation (developing cognitive strategies to navigate social situations), and assimilation (actively learning and performing neurotypical social scripts). These strategies are cognitively demanding and often require conscious effort, distinguishing them from natural social learning.

The gender dimension is critical. The higher prevalence of camouflaging in females may partly explain the historical male-to-female diagnostic ratio of approximately 4:1, which recent studies suggest may be closer to 3:1 or even 2:1 when camouflaging is accounted for. This has direct implications for prevalence estimates, research sampling, and clinical service provision.

The mental health consequences are clinically significant. Chronic camouflaging has been likened to sustained performance of a role, and the associated stress, exhaustion, and identity loss are associated with elevated rates of anxiety, depression, and suicidality in autistic adults who camouflage heavily.
