# Metformin Modulates Gut Microbiome in Broiler Breeder Hens

## Key Findings

- **Metformin significantly altered cecal microbial community composition** at the 75 mg/kg dose in broiler breeder hens, with dose-dependent effects observed across treatment groups.
- **[[ruminococcus]] and members of Clostridia family XIII** were differentially abundant between metformin-treated and control groups, suggesting these taxa are particularly responsive to metformin's gut-level effects.
- **Broiler breeder hens serve as a natural model for PCOS** due to their spontaneous development of ovarian dysfunction, hyperandrogenism, and metabolic features paralleling human PCOS.
- Metformin did not significantly alter alpha diversity metrics, suggesting its effects are on community structure rather than overall richness.
- The findings support the hypothesis that metformin's therapeutic effects in reproductive endocrine disorders may be partly mediated through gut microbiome modulation.

## Methods

- **Design**: Randomized controlled animal trial with 4 dose groups.
- **Subjects**: 48 broiler breeder hens divided into control, low-dose (25 mg/kg), mid-dose (50 mg/kg), and high-dose (75 mg/kg) metformin groups.
- **Duration**: Treatment period with cecal content sampling at study endpoint.
- **Sequencing**: 16S rRNA gene amplicon sequencing of cecal microbiota.
- **Analysis**: Alpha and beta diversity metrics, differential abundance testing using LEfSe and DESeq2.

## Detailed Summary

This study investigated how metformin — a first-line drug for type 2 diabetes increasingly studied for its effects on reproductive endocrine conditions — modulates the cecal gut microbiome in broiler breeder hens. These hens naturally develop a PCOS-like phenotype including ovarian cysts, anovulation, and metabolic dysfunction, making them a valuable translational model. Metformin was administered at three doses (25, 50, 75 mg/kg body weight) over a treatment period. At the highest dose, significant shifts in microbial beta diversity were observed, driven primarily by changes in [[ruminococcus]] and Clostridia family XIII. Alpha diversity remained relatively stable, indicating that metformin reshapes microbial composition without dramatically reducing community richness. The dose-dependent nature of the response suggests that metformin's microbiome-modulating effects have a threshold, with clinical relevance depending on achieving sufficient gut lumen concentrations.

## Open Questions

- Whether metformin-induced microbiome shifts in hens translate to similar patterns in human PCOS patients receiving metformin therapy.
- Whether the Ruminococcus and Clostridia changes mediate any of metformin's clinical benefits on ovarian function or metabolic parameters.
- How metformin's microbiome effects interact with its known inhibition of mitochondrial complex I and AMPK activation.
