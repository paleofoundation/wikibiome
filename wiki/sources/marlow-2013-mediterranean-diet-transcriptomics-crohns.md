# Key Claims

- A 6-week Mediterranean-inspired anti-inflammatory diet significantly altered gene expression in CD patients, with 3,551 genes differentially expressed (1,902 up-regulated, 1,649 down-regulated)
- Traditional biomarkers (CRP, micronuclei) showed non-significant reductions, but transcriptomics detected changes undetectable by established markers, highlighting its superior sensitivity
- Key canonical pathways affected include EIF2 signalling, B cell development, and T helper cell differentiation
- The upstream regulator network converged on IRF2 (interferon regulatory factor 2) and STAT3, both critical in NF-kappaB regulation and IBD pathogenesis
- The diet trended toward normalizing the intestinal microbiota -- increased Bacteroidetes, increased Clostridium clusters IV and XIVa, decreased Proteobacteria and Bacillaceae

# Methods/Findings

- **Study type**: Pilot dietary intervention study; 8 CD patients consumed anti-inflammatory Mediterranean diet for 6 weeks
- **Diet**: Salmon, organic avocados, kumara, vegetables, gluten-free bread, olive oil, green tea, honey, fish oil capsules; reduced red meat, white flour, high-fat foods, sugar
- **Transcriptomics**: Affymetrix GeneChip PrimeView microarray on PBMCs; 3,551 differentially expressed genes (p<0.05)
- **Top functions affected** (Table 2): Neurological disease (122 genes), cancer (358 genes), infectious disease (126 genes), cellular growth (218 genes), immune cell trafficking (136 genes)
- **Microbiota changes** (Figure 5): Bacteroidetes increased (17.89% to 21.86%), Clostridium cl IV (19.2% to 21.6%), Clostridium cl XIVa (26.78% to 28.97%); Proteobacteria decreased (5.93% to 5.48%)
- **CRP**: Trend toward reduction (p=0.39); micronuclei decreased (p=0.15)
