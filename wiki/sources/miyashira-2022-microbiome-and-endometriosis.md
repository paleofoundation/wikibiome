## Key Findings

- **Pseudomonas was overrepresented in peritoneal fluid** of women with endometriosis across multiple studies, and was also observed to be increased in vaginal, endometrial, and intra-lesional samples ([[miyashira-2022-microbiome-and-endometriosis]], systematic review).
- **Gardnerella was increased in cervical and endometrial samples** but decreased in fecal and vaginal samples of patients with endometriosis, illustrating site-specific variation ([[miyashira-2022-microbiome-and-endometriosis]], systematic review).
- **Streptococcus was increased in peritoneal, endometrial, and cervical samples** from women with endometriosis ([[miyashira-2022-microbiome-and-endometriosis]], systematic review).
- **Atopobium was decreased in vaginal and cervical samples** but increased in vaginal samples in one contradictory study ([[miyashira-2022-microbiome-and-endometriosis]], systematic review).
- **GnRHa treatment significantly altered the vaginal microbiome**, decreasing *Lactobacillaceae* (P < 0.01) and increasing *Streptococcaceae*, *Staphylococcaceae*, and *Enterobacteriaceae* (P < 0.05 each) in endometriosis patients compared to untreated women ([[miyashira-2022-microbiome-and-endometriosis]], systematic review, citing Khan et al. 2016).

## Methods

- **Study design:** Systematic review of human studies assessing the microbiome in association with endometriosis.
- **Search strategy:** PubMed/Medline, Cochrane, and Embase databases searched for literature published from 1986 to August 2, 2021.
- **Inclusion criteria:** All human studies using 16S rRNA sequencing or shotgun sequencing to assess the microbiome in women with endometriosis. Only case-control studies were included.
- **Exclusion criteria:** Case reports, reviews, conference abstracts, animal studies, and unpublished studies.
- **Quality assessment:** National Institutes of Health (NIH) Study Quality Assessment Tools for case-control and prospective non-randomized studies. Studies fulfilling 70%+ criteria rated good, 30-70% fair, <30% poor.
- **Yield:** 209 studies identified; after excluding 65 duplicates and 122 by title/abstract, 23 underwent full-text screening, yielding **12 case-control studies** for qualitative synthesis.
- **No meta-analysis performed** due to heterogeneity of included studies.

## Detailed Summary

### Study Characteristics

All 12 included studies were case-control designs. Sample sizes ranged from 10 (Xu et al. 2017) to 264 (Svensson et al. 2021). Eight studies were rated fair quality, three good, and one poor. Studies assessed microbiome samples from fecal, vaginal, cervical, peritoneal, endometrial, and intra-lesional sources. Two NGS techniques were used: microbiome shotgun sequencing and Ion Torrent Personal Genome Machine. Researchers were not blinded in any included study.

### Female Reproductive Tract Microbiome

Seven studies evaluated vaginal and cervical samples:

- **Vaginal findings were inconsistent.** *Lactobacillus* species were predominant in both endometriosis and control groups, with the cervical microbiota being similar across groups. *Enterobacteriaceae* and *Streptococcus* were more prevalent in women with endometriosis (P < 0.05).
- **Chen et al. (2020)** found *Lactobacillus* was the most prevalent genus in the vagina across all groups, but the genus *Atopobium* was more commonly identified in women with both endometriosis and adenomyosis. *Campylobacter*, *Ezakiella*, and *Faecalibacterium* were also enriched.
- **Ata et al. (2019)** found decreased *Gardnerella*, *Atopobium*, *Megasphaera*, *Prevotella*, *Snethia* in cervical samples and increased *Escherichia/Shigella* and *Dialister*.

### Peritoneal Fluid Microbiome

Three studies examined peritoneal fluid:

- **Lee et al. (2021)** compared 45 endometriosis patients (stages III-IV) with 45 controls and found increased *Acinetobacter*, *Pseudomonas*, *Streptococcus*, and *Erysipelothrix* with significant reductions in *Propionibacterium*, *Actinomyces*, and *Rothia* in the endometriosis group (P < 0.05).
- **Wei et al. (2020)** found significantly increased *Pseudomonas* and *Sphingobium* in peritoneal fluid of women with endometriosis.
- **Wang et al. (2018)** found no statistically significant difference between endometriosis and control groups (P > 0.05), though the main bacteria detected were Proteobacteria and Firmicutes.

### Fecal Microbiome

Two studies compared fecal microbiomes:

- **Svensson et al. (2021)** (n=264) found only three bacteria with significantly different abundance: *Lachnospira*, *Oscillospira*, and a genus in the order *Bacteroidales* (P < 0.05).
- **Ata et al. (2019)** found decreased *Barnesella*, *Gardnerella*, and *Snethia* in fecal samples.

### Endometrial and Lesion Microbiome

- **Hernandes et al. (2020)** found *Gardnerella* and *Prevotella* decreased in endometrium; *Alishewanella*, *Enterococcus*, and *Pseudomonas* identified in endometriotic lesions.
- **Khan et al. (2016)** found *Moraxellaceae* and *Streptococcaceae* enriched in endometrium.

### Hormonal Influence on Microbiome

GnRHa treatment (Khan et al. 2016) significantly altered vaginal microbiome: *Lactobacillaceae* decreased (P < 0.01) while *Streptococcaceae*, *Staphylococcaceae*, and *Enterobacteriaceae* increased (P < 0.05). Svensson et al. (2021) found higher abundance of *Blautia*, *Ruminococcus*, and *Butyricimonas* among those taking hormonal medications in the fecal microbiome.

### Endometriosis Symptoms and Microbiome

- Svensson et al. (2021) reported no significant association between stool microbiome and pain symptoms or digestive complaints in 66 endometriosis patients.
- Xu et al. (2017) found decreased *Paraprevotella*, *Odoribacter*, *Veillonella*, and *Ruminococcus* with increased *Prevotella* in chronically stressed endometriosis patients.
- Chao et al. (2021) found decreased *Lactobacillus jensenii* and *Shuttleworthia* with increased *Clostridium butyricum* and *Alloscardovia* in endometriosis patients with chronic pelvic pain.

### Bacterial Contamination Hypothesis

The review references Khan et al. (2018) who proposed a "bacterial contamination hypothesis" for endometriosis, whereby bacterial endotoxins activate a peritoneal pro-inflammatory response, increase cell-to-cell adhesion, and facilitate the growth of ectopic endometrial implants. This connects gut dysbiosis to the estrobolome and enhanced estrogen recirculation via beta-glucuronidase activity ([[bacterial-contamination-hypothesis]]).

The finding that *Pseudomonas* is consistently enriched in peritoneal fluid provides a strong candidate for the taxonomic signature. The hormonal modulation data (GnRHa effects on vaginal microbiome) is relevant to understanding how hormonal interventions alter the microbial ecosystem.
- **Estrobolome connection** (Karen's Brain Primitive 7): The review notes that gut dysbiosis may alter the estrobolome, leading to enhanced estrogen deconjugation and increased free circulating levels, potentially contributing to endometriosis progression. This supports the beta-glucuronidase / estrogen recirculation pathway in the signature.
- **Intervention hypotheses:** The consistent *Lactobacillus* depletion in reproductive tract sites, combined with *Enterobacteriaceae* enrichment, suggests ecological disruption amenable to probiotic intervention targeting competitive exclusion (Primitive 5).
- **STOP signal:** The GnRHa-induced shift from *Lactobacillaceae* to *Enterobacteriaceae* and *Staphylococcaceae* suggests that GnRHa treatment may inadvertently worsen microbial dysbiosis even while reducing hormonal drivers — a potential STOP or at minimum a consideration for the signature page.

## Contradictions / Tensions

- ***Atopobium* direction is contradictory:** Ata et al. (2019) found it decreased in vaginal/cervical samples, while Chen et al. (2020) found it increased in vaginal samples. The review notes this without resolution.
- ***Lactobacillus* role is complex:** Remains dominant in vaginal microbiome of both cases and controls in most studies, yet specific species (*L. jensenii*) are depleted in endometriosis with CPP, and *Lactobacillaceae* overall decreases with GnRHa. This suggests species-level resolution is needed rather than genus-level generalizations.
- **Peritoneal fluid findings partially conflict:** Wang et al. (2018) found no significant difference between endometriosis and controls in peritoneal fluid, while Wei et al. (2020) and Lee et al. (2021) found significant differences. The authors attribute this to methodological heterogeneity and small sample sizes.
- **Fecal microbiome evidence is weak:** Only two studies examined fecal samples, and the one larger study (Svensson et al. 2021, n=264) found only three taxa with significant differences — a surprisingly modest signal given the estrobolome hypothesis.

## Open Questions

1. **Causality vs. consequence:** Does the altered microbiome cause or contribute to endometriosis, or is it a consequence of the disease and its inflammatory environment? No included study could establish directionality.
2. **Site-specific colonization mechanisms:** How do bacteria colonize supposedly sterile sites like the peritoneal cavity and endometrium? The review mentions bloodstream, mesenteric lymph nodes, and retrograde progression but this remains unresolved.
3. **Species-level resolution:** Most findings are at the genus or family level. Species-level and strain-level characterization is needed to identify specific pathogenic vs. commensal roles.
4. **Menstrual cycle confounding:** Only 2 of 12 studies addressed menstrual phase as a variable, despite evidence that the microbiome varies across the cycle. Future studies need standardized timing.
5. **Hormonal treatment confounding:** Many studies included women on hormonal medications without controlling for this variable. The Khan et al. (2016) GnRHa data shows substantial hormonal effects on the microbiome, suggesting this is a critical confounder.
6. **Metal dependencies of enriched taxa:** None of the 12 studies examined metal concentrations alongside microbiome data. The metal dependencies of consistently enriched taxa like *Pseudomonas* (iron-dependent siderophores) and *Escherichia* (iron, zinc, nickel enzymes) are unexplored in the endometriosis context — a critical gap for WikiBiome's metallomic framework.
7. **Beta-glucuronidase activity:** The estrobolome hypothesis is mentioned but no included study directly measured beta-glucuronidase activity or estrogen metabolite levels alongside microbiome data.
