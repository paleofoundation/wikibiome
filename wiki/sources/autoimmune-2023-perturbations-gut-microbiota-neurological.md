# Perturbations in Gut Microbiota Composition in Patients with Autoimmune Neurological Diseases

## Summary

This systematic review and meta-analysis examines gut [[gut-microbiome|microbiome]] perturbations across autoimmune neurological diseases (including [[multiple-sclerosis]], myasthenia gravis, and autoimmune aspects of [[parkinsons-disease]]), identifying shared and disease-specific dysbiotic features.

## Key Findings

- Autoimmune neurological diseases share a core dysbiotic signature: depleted SCFA producers, enriched Proteobacteria
- PD shares more microbiome features with autoimmune neurological conditions than previously recognized
- Alpha diversity is consistently reduced across autoimmune neurological diseases
- Functional pathway analysis reveals shared metabolic disruptions (SCFA, tryptophan, bile acid metabolism)
- The shared dysbiotic features suggest common immune-microbiome mechanisms across diseases
- Disease-specific microbial features may explain differential clinical presentations

## Relevance to Gut-Metal-Microbiome Axis

The shared [[dysbiosis]] pattern across autoimmune neurological diseases and [[parkinsons-disease]] supports the metallomic-microbiome framework. Autoimmune diseases feature prominent [[nutritional-immunity]] responses: [[calprotectin]] elevation (sequestering [[zinc]]/[[manganese]]), [[lactoferrin]] elevation (sequestering [[iron]]), and [[hepcidin]]-mediated iron trapping. These metal-sequestering responses create the same gut metal environment that drives [[dysbiosis]] in PD. The overlapping microbiome signatures may reflect a common metal-immune disruption that manifests differently depending on genetic susceptibility and tissue-specific vulnerabilities.

## Limitations

- Heterogeneity across included studies (different sequencing methods, populations, disease stages)
- Meta-analysis of microbiome data has methodological challenges
- PD's autoimmune component is debated
- Medication differences across diseases confound comparisons
