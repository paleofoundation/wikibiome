## Key Claims

- In 44 of 112 (39.28%) nickel-sensitive patients with chronic allergic-like dermatopathies, a [[low-nickel-diet]] was effective in controlling symptoms
- The oral double-blind placebo-controlled nickel challenge was positive in all patients who had favorably responded to the elimination diet (except one)
- No serum-specific IgE antibodies against nickel were found, and skin-prick tests were negative in patients with anaphylactoid reactions, suggesting that ingested nickel activates cell-mediated rather than IgE-mediated pathways
- Dietary nickel is the real cause of chronic allergic-type cutaneous manifestations in a substantial subgroup of nickel-sensitized patients

## Methods/Findings

- **Design**: Open clinical study evaluating elimination diet and oral nickel challenge at Asola Hospital, Mantova, Italy
- **Population**: 112 adult patients (106 women, 6 men; age 16-58, mean 29) with widespread allergic-like dermatopathies and contact sensitization to nickel; included patients with urticaria, angioedema, atopic dermatitis-like lesions, and pruritus
- **Protocol**: Patients prescribed a low-nickel diet for 4 weeks; those who improved underwent double-blind placebo-controlled oral nickel challenge (10 mg and 20 mg nickel sulphate hexahydrate = 2.23 and 4.47 mg elemental nickel)
- **Results**: 44/112 (39.28%) improved on diet; oral DBPC challenge positive in 43/44; challenge induced widespread cutaneous manifestations (symmetrically-localized rash, flare-up at previous patch test site); reactions occurred 3-6 hours post-challenge
- **Challenge-induced symptoms**: Moderate itching, urticaria, flare-up, rash, severe itching; reactions at doses of 10-20 mg nickel sulphate
- **Key observation**: Almost half of selected patients recovered with low-nickel diet; in nearly all cases, nickel challenge was positive, confirming the etiological role of dietary nickel
- **Limitations**: No standardized lower/intermediate challenge doses available at the time; some severe reactions observed
