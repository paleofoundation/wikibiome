# Plasma inflammatory cytokines and short-chain fatty acid in depression and schizophrenia

## Summary

This cross-sectional study investigated differences in plasma inflammatory cytokines and [[short-chain-fatty-acids]] among patients with MDD (n=24), schizophrenia (n=24), and gender/age-matched healthy controls (n=27). It identified potential plasma biomarker panels for discriminating between conditions.

## Key Findings

- Pro-inflammatory cytokines were increased and anti-inflammatory cytokines were decreased in both MDD and SCH
- Most SCFAs were reduced in both MDD and SCH, except 4-methylvaleric acid which was increased
- Biomarker panels could discriminate: MDD from HC (AUC = 0.997), SCH from HC (AUC = 0.999), and MDD from SCH (AUC = 0.983)
- Alterations in plasma cytokines and SCFAs may serve as distinguishing biomarkers between depression and schizophrenia
- The pattern of SCFA depletion differed between MDD and SCH, suggesting disease-specific metabolic signatures

## Relevance to Gut-Metal-Microbiome Axis

The near-perfect AUC values for discriminating schizophrenia from healthy controls using combined cytokine and [[short-chain-fatty-acids]] panels demonstrates the diagnostic power of [[dysbiosis]]-related biomarkers. The simultaneous elevation of pro-inflammatory cytokines and depletion of anti-inflammatory SCFAs creates conditions favoring [[neuroinflammation]] and metal-mediated oxidative damage. The disease-specific patterns suggest that different psychiatric conditions may involve distinct [[dysbiosis]] signatures, relevant to understanding how [[immune-balance]] disruption via the [[gut-brain-axis]] manifests differently across disorders.

## Limitations

- Small sample size per group
- Cross-sectional design
- Chinese population limits generalizability
- Medication effects not fully controlled
