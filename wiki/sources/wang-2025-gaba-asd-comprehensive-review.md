# GABA in Autism Spectrum Disorders: Comprehensive Review

## Key Findings

- **GABA system dysfunction is closely related to ASD pathogenesis** — GABA is the primary inhibitory neurotransmitter and its dysfunction creates excitatory/inhibitory (E/I) imbalance ([[wang-2025-gaba-asd-comprehensive-review]], expert-opinion)
- **Abnormalities in GABAergic neurons, signaling pathways, and related genes** (GABRA, GABRB, GAD1/GAD2) play important roles in ASD
- **Abnormal GABA levels** found in both blood and brain tissue of ASD individuals, though directionality varies across studies
- **GABAergic drugs** (arbaclofen, bumetanide) have shown potential to improve ASD symptoms in animal models and some clinical trials
- **ASD prevalence in the US has reached 27.6 per 1,000** (1 in every 36 children), underscoring the urgent need for targeted interventions

## Detailed Summary

This comprehensive review synthesizes current understanding of GABA system dysfunction in autism spectrum disorder. The GABAergic system mediates inhibitory neurotransmission throughout the brain, and the balance between excitation (glutamate) and inhibition (GABA) — the E/I balance — is increasingly recognized as a core feature disrupted in ASD.

The review covers multiple levels of evidence: (1) Genetic — numerous ASD-associated genes encode GABA receptor subunits (GABRA, GABRB, GABRG families) and GABA-synthesizing enzymes (GAD1, GAD2); (2) Molecular — GABA levels are altered in blood and brain tissue, though findings are inconsistent regarding direction; (3) Cellular — GABAergic interneuron dysfunction affects cortical circuit maturation; (4) Pharmacological — drugs targeting the GABA system show promise but have yielded mixed clinical trial results.

Importantly, GABA's developmental role shifts from excitatory in early postnatal life to inhibitory as the brain matures, mediated by the chloride transporter KCC2. Disruption of this "GABA switch" has been proposed as a mechanism contributing to ASD, and bumetanide (a chloride transporter modulator) has been tested as an intervention based on this hypothesis.

The review also touches on the gut-brain connection: gut bacteria (particularly [[lactobacillus]] species) produce GABA, and alterations in gut microbial GABA production may contribute to systemic and potentially central GABA imbalances. Zinc is a co-factor in GABAergic signaling as a modulator of GABA-A receptors.
