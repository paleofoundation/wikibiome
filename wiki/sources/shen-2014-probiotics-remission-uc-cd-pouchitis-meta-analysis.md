# Key Claims

- [[probiotics]] significantly increase remission rates in active UC (p=0.01, RR=1.51) but not in CD (p=0.94, RR=0.89) or pouchitis
- Only VSL#3 significantly increased remission/response rates in active UC (p=0.004, RR=1.74)
- For maintenance therapy, probiotics significantly reduced clinical relapse rates (p=0.04, RR=0.73) but with moderate-to-high heterogeneity
- VSL#3 was the only probiotic with significant benefit for maintaining UC remission (p<0.0001, RR=0.18)
- No significant different adverse events were detected between probiotics and controls

# Methods/Findings

- **Study type**: Meta-analysis of 23 RCTs; 1,763 total participants
- **Databases**: MEDLINE, EMBASE, Cochrane, OVID, BIOSIS, Chinese Biomedical Database (1966-2013)
- **Probiotics tested**: VSL#3, E. coli Nissle 1917, Lactobacillus rhamnosus GG, Bifidobacterium, Saccharomyces boulardii
- **UC induction**: 12 trials; pooled RR 1.28 (1.00-1.64); VSL#3 subgroup showed strongest effect
- **CD induction**: Only 3 trials included patients with CD; no significant benefit (p=0.35)
- **Maintenance**: 16 trials; overall significant benefit for relapse prevention; disease-specific subanalyses showed UC benefit but not CD

# Connections

- Supports disease-specific [[probiotics]] approaches: VSL#3 for UC but limited evidence for [[crohns-disease]]
- E. coli Nissle 1917 as a probiotic contrasts with pathogenic [[escherichia-coli]] strains in CD
- [[faecalibacterium-prausnitzii]] is not represented in commercial probiotics despite its importance in CD [[dysbiosis]]
- Probiotic modulation of [[inflammation]] may work through SCFA production, barrier function, and immune regulation
- Results suggest that CD may require different microbiome-targeted approaches than UC, potentially including [[gut-metal-microbiome]] interventions
