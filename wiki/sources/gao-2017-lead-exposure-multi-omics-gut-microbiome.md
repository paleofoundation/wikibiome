# Key Claims

- [[lead]] exposure disturbed gut microbiome development trajectories and inhibited phylogenetic diversity in C57BL/6 mice over a 13-week period
- Lead exposure significantly reduced key metabolites: vitamin E (alpha- and gamma-tocopherol), primary bile acids (cholic acid, ursodeoxycholic acid), cholesterol, and coprostanol
- The gut microbiome's nitrogen metabolism, energy metabolism, and oxidative stress/defense/detoxification mechanisms were significantly perturbed by lead
- Lead induced oxidative stress and activated the defense/detoxification mechanism in gut bacteria (increased CoADR, disulfide bond regulator, MutT protein)
- Gluconeogenesis may be inhibited after lead exposure based on carbon storage regulator changes
- The functional impact of lead on the gut microbiome extends beyond composition to metabolic pathways

# Methods/Findings

- **Study type**: Original animal experiment using multi-omics approach
- **Design**: C57BL/6 female mice (~7 weeks old), n=5 per group; 10 ppm Pb chloride in drinking water for 13 weeks
- **Methods**: 16S rRNA gene sequencing (V4 region, Illumina MiSeq), whole genome metagenomics sequencing, GC-MS metabolomics
- **16S results**: PCoA showed distinct separation of Pb-treated vs. control at genus level by week 4; alpha diversity increase was severely inhibited in Pb-treated mice; Ruminococcus, Oscillospira decreased; Clostridiaceae genera significantly changed
- **Metabolomics**: 1314 molecular features with p<0.05 and fold change >1.5; PLS-DA clearly separated groups; vitamin E, bile acids, cholesterol significantly reduced
- **Metagenomics**: Nitrogen metabolism altered (urease accessory protein UreE increased, creatinine amidohydrolase increased, urea reduced, hydroxylamine decreased); energy metabolism perturbed (glucose transporter, xylose ABC transporter significantly changed); oxidative stress genes activated (CoADR, MutT, tyrosine recombinase XerC increased)
- **Detoxification**: Heavy-metal-translocating P-type ATPase significantly increased; phosphate ABC transporter increased indicating lead-resistance mechanisms
