# Discovery and Inhibition of an Interspecies Gut Bacterial Pathway for Levodopa Metabolism

## Summary

This landmark study identifies the specific gut bacterial enzymes responsible for metabolizing levodopa (L-DOPA) in the GI tract before it reaches the brain. It discovers that *Enterococcus faecalis* encodes a tyrosine decarboxylase (TyrDC) that converts L-DOPA to dopamine in the gut, followed by *Eggerthella lenta* dehydroxylation, significantly reducing levodopa bioavailability.

## Key Findings

- *Enterococcus faecalis* TyrDC converts L-DOPA to dopamine in the gut, reducing brain bioavailability
- *Eggerthella lenta* further converts dopamine to m-tyramine through dehydroxylation
- This interspecies pathway explains variable levodopa responses among PD patients
- AFMT (alpha-fluoromethyltyrosine) can inhibit bacterial TyrDC without affecting host DOPA decarboxylase
- Gut microbial L-DOPA metabolism accounts for significant drug loss before systemic absorption
- The abundance of TyrDC-encoding bacteria varies across PD patients
- Targeting bacterial drug metabolism could improve levodopa efficacy

## Relevance to Gut-Metal-Microbiome Axis

This study has direct implications for the metal-microbiome axis because levodopa transport and metabolism are metal-dependent. Levodopa is absorbed through the LAT1 transporter but also interacts with DMT1, the [[iron]] and [[manganese]] transporter. Bacterial TyrDC is a pyridoxal phosphate (PLP)-dependent enzyme that also requires divalent metal cofactors. The PD [[dysbiosis]] that enriches *Enterococcus* and *Eggerthella* simultaneously increases levodopa degradation and alters metal handling in the gut. [[iron]] availability in the gut may modulate TyrDC activity, as metalloenzyme function depends on local metal concentrations. This creates a direct link between gut [[iron]] status, microbiome composition, and PD medication efficacy -- a key consideration for the gut-metal-microbiome therapeutic framework.

## Limitations

- In vitro and gnotobiotic mouse models; human clinical validation ongoing
- Individual variation in TyrDC-encoding bacteria abundance is high
- The AFMT inhibitor has not been tested in PD clinical trials
- Other bacterial pathways for levodopa metabolism may exist
