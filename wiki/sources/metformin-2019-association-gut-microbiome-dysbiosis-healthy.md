# Metformin-Induced Gut Microbiome Dysbiosis in Healthy Volunteers

## Key Findings

- **Metformin administration in healthy volunteers** causes measurable gut microbiome changes independent of any disease state ([[metformin-2019-association-gut-microbiome-dysbiosis-healthy]], expert-opinion)
- **Drug-induced microbiome changes** occur even in the absence of metabolic disease, demonstrating that metformin directly reshapes microbial communities
- **Metformin's GI side effects** (diarrhea, nausea, abdominal discomfort) may be mediated through microbiome disruption rather than direct drug toxicity

## Detailed Summary

This study addressed a critical confounding issue in microbiome research: whether microbiome signatures attributed to type 2 diabetes are actually caused by disease or by metformin treatment. By administering metformin to healthy volunteers without metabolic disease, the authors could isolate drug-specific effects on the microbiome.

The results demonstrated that metformin alone can produce significant shifts in gut microbial composition, including changes that had previously been attributed to T2D pathophysiology. This finding has profound implications for the field, as many T2D microbiome studies included participants on metformin without adequately controlling for this confounder.

The GI side effects of metformin (experienced by approximately 20-30% of patients) may be partly explained by these microbiome perturbations, including shifts in gas-producing bacteria, altered bile acid metabolism, and changes in short-chain fatty acid production profiles.
