# Role of Nickel in Microbial Pathogenesis

## Overview

Comprehensive review of how **[[nickel]]** functions as an essential cofactor for pathogen virulence. Covers **Ni-enzymes** (urease, hydrogenase, GloI, ARD, SOD) across **at least 40 prokaryotic and 9 eukaryotic pathogens**. Central insight: mammals do not synthesize known Ni-requiring proteins, making **[[nutritional-immunity]]** (nickel sequestration) a viable antimicrobial strategy — but one that is underexplored compared to iron/zinc restriction.

## Key Ni-Enzymes in Pathogenesis

### Urease
- Catalyzes hydrolysis of urea → bicarbonate + ammonia.
- **Dual role**: (i) nitrogen source for microorganisms, (ii) acid neutralization via ammonia/bicarbonate — critical for survival in acidic environments.
- Found in the widest range of pathogens among Ni-enzymes.
- Most extensively studied in **[[helicobacter-pylori]]**:
  - Comprises up to 10% of total proteome.
  - Essential for in vivo survival — buffering cytoplasmic pH.
  - Ammonia production can cause hyperammonemia (minimal hepatic encephalopathy in cirrhosis patients).
  - **Many non-acid-neutralizing roles discovered**: urease is needed for persistence even at neutral pH, promotes angiogenesis, stimulates pro-inflammatory cytokines, induces apoptosis in gastric epithelial cells via Class II MHC binding, disrupts tight junctions, activates blood platelets.
  - **Novel dual activity**: Holo-urease (Ni-bound) has both catalytic (urea hydrolysis) and non-catalytic (oxidant quenching via Met/Met-sulfoxide cycle) activity. Apo-urease (Ni-free) retains only the antioxidant function.
- **[[staphylococcus-aureus]]**: urease important for skin survival (sweat contains 22 mM urea), kidney colonization, biofilm formation. Urease genes upregulated in biofilm-embedded cells.
- **[[proteus-mirabilis]]**: urease critical for crystalline biofilm formation on catheters, urinary stone formation, and UTI pathogenesis.
- **Ureaplasma spp.**: urease is genus-defining; ammonia contributes to PMF-driven ATP synthesis and struvite stone formation.
- **Eukaryotic pathogens**: *Cryptococcus neoformans* (brain invasion), *C. posadasii* (pulmonary infection severity), *C. gattii*.

### Hydrogenase
- [[nife-hydrogenase]] enzymes catalyze H₂ oxidation, providing energy (PMF) for the pathogen.
- **[[helicobacter-pylori]]**: single H₂-uptake hydrogenase (*hydABCDE* operon).
  - H₂ is chronically available in the stomach (~80 μM in human stomachs, well above the enzyme Km of ~1.8 μM).
  - Powers **CagA translocation** — the carcinogenic effector protein. Hydrogenase deletion mutants cannot translocate CagA or induce gastric cancer in gerbils.
  - H₂-stimulated CO₂ fixation ("mixotrophy") — a growth mode never before described in a human pathogen.
  - **Correlation with cancer**: higher hydrogenase activity in strains from cancer patients vs. gastritis patients.
- **[[helicobacter-hepaticus]]**: hydrogenase supports amino acid uptake and cell growth; linked to liver lesions in mice.
- **[[salmonella-typhimurium]]**: 4 distinct [NiFe] hydrogenases (Hya, Hyb, Hyc, Hyd). Hyb is most important for virulence. Triple mutant (ΔhyaΔhybΔhyd) is avirulent (100% survival in typhoid fever mouse model).
- **[[campylobacter-jejuni]]**: membrane-bound [NiFe] hydrogenase essential for growth and chicken colonization.
- **[[shigella-flexneri]]**: H₂-uptake hydrogenases combat acid stress in phagolysosomes.

### Other Ni-Enzymes
- **Glyoxalase I (GloI)**: detoxifies methylglyoxal. Ni-dependent form in prokaryotic pathogens (*P. aeruginosa*, *N. meningitidis*, *Y. pestis*, *Clostridia*). GloI is essential in *L. donovani* — proposed drug target.
- **Acireductone dioxygenase (ARD)**: methionine salvage pathway. Ni-form produces different products than Fe-form. Found in all pathogenic γ-proteobacteriaceae.
- **Ni-Superoxide dismutase (Ni-SOD)**: rare; found in *Streptomyces* spp. Important against oxidative stress in plant hosts.

## Nickel Availability and Host-Mediated Influences

- Nickel is **scarce in mammalian hosts**: <5 ppm in most organs, <0.1% of zinc levels in liver/kidney.
- **[[nutritional-immunity]]**: hosts sequester metals to starve pathogens.
  - **Calprotectin**: coordinates Ni(II) preferentially over Zn(II) at the hexahistidine site; sequesters nickel from *S. aureus* and *K. pneumoniae*, inhibiting urease.
  - **Lactoferrin**: can bind nickel via histidine/tyrosine ligands — nickel-sequestering effect plausible but unexplored.
  - **Hepcidin**: well-established for iron; role in nickel starvation unknown but likely.
  - **NRAMP1**: exports Ni(II) from macrophage phagolysosomes, restricting availability to engulfed pathogens.
- **Counter-strategy**: targeting nickel trafficking in pathogens (e.g., blocking H₂-uptake hydrogenase and urease maturation in *H. pylori*) proposed as therapeutic approach.
- **Complication**: disrupting nickel in host microbiota could cause dysbiosis.

## Nickel Transport in Pathogens

- **ABC-type transporters**: NikABCDE (*E. coli*), NiuBDE (*H. pylori*) — high-affinity, can operate at acidic pH.
- **NiCoT-type transporters**: NixA (*H. pylori*) — secondary, Ni-only transport.
- **Metallophores** ("nickelophores" by analogy to siderophores):
  - **Staphylopine** (*S. aureus*): nicotianamine-like; originally thought zinc-specific but also binds nickel.
  - **Pseudopaline** (*P. aeruginosa*): primary mechanism for nickel uptake in chelating environments.
  - **Yersiniabactin** (*Yersinia*, *Klebsiella*, *E. coli*): originally characterized for iron; also binds extracellular nickel in UPEC.
- **Nickel export**: CznABC pump in *H. pylori* (cadmium, zinc, nickel efflux); PMI1518 in *P. mirabilis* (essential for CAUTI).

## Nickel Storage and Toxicity

- Best characterized in **[[helicobacter-pylori]]**:
  - **Hpn**: small His-rich protein (47% His), 20-mer binding 5 Ni(II) per monomer. Present in all gastric *Helicobacter*.
  - **HpnI** (Hpn-like): 25% His, binds 2 Ni(II) per monomer. Restricted to *H. pylori* and *H. acinonychis*.
  - Both compete for nickel under low-nickel conditions; Hpn is the primary sequestering reservoir.
  - Recent work: storage proteins interact with a **much wider array** of proteins than expected — urease/hydrogenase maturation, AmiE (aliphatic amidase), PepA (aminopeptidase). Suggests they function as central nickel hubs.
  - **HspA**: GroES homolog with unique His-rich C-terminus for nickel binding. Candidate for anti-*H. pylori* vaccine.

## Two-Kingdom Conundrum

A key evolutionary observation: **plants use nickel** (in Ni-urease) while **mammals do not** synthesize Ni-requiring proteins. This means:
- Nickel sequestration by hosts naturally occurs (plants compete for nickel with pathogens; mammals simply don't use it).
- Very few nickel-utilizing plant pathogens exist (*Streptomyces scabies* and related species only).
- This asymmetry may explain why nickel restriction is a viable immune strategy against mammalian pathogens.
