# Cadmium Exposure and Gut Microbiome Disruption in Neurodegeneration

## Summary

This study examines how cadmium exposure disrupts the gut [[microbiome]] and contributes to neurodegenerative processes relevant to [[parkinsons-disease]]. Cadmium is a toxic heavy metal with no biological function that accumulates in tissues and is associated with increased neurodegeneration risk.

## Key Findings

- Cadmium exposure significantly alters gut microbiome composition
- Cadmium-driven [[dysbiosis]] includes enrichment of Proteobacteria and depletion of Firmicutes
- Intestinal barrier function is compromised by cadmium, increasing gut permeability
- Cadmium accumulation in the gut lumen directly damages epithelial cells
- Systemic inflammation increases with cadmium-induced gut barrier disruption
- Cadmium and other heavy metals synergize in producing microbiome damage
- The gut microbiome modulates cadmium bioavailability and toxicity

## Relevance to Gut-Metal-Microbiome Axis

Cadmium exposure exemplifies how environmental metals drive PD-relevant [[dysbiosis]] through the gut-metal-microbiome axis. Cadmium competes with [[iron]] and zinc for DMT1 and ZIP transporter uptake, creating competition that dysregulates [[iron]] homeostasis. Cadmium-driven enrichment of Proteobacteria (which includes [[iron]]-scavenging Enterobacteriaceae) mirrors the PD microbiome signature. Cadmium exposure through food (rice, leafy vegetables, tobacco smoke) represents a chronic environmental input to the metal-microbiome axis. The co-exposure of cadmium with [[nickel]], [[lead]], and [[manganese]] in contaminated environments creates compound effects on the gut microbiome, with each metal selecting for different resistant organisms and collectively reshaping the microbial ecosystem toward the PD-associated pattern.

## Limitations

- Animal model; human cadmium-PD gut microbiome studies limited
- Cadmium doses used may exceed typical human exposure
- Interaction with other co-occurring metal exposures not fully characterized
- Long-term chronic exposure effects may differ from acute models
