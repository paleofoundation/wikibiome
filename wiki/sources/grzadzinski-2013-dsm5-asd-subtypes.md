# Overview

Review summarizing revisions to ASD diagnostic criteria in **DSM-5** and how its dimensional approach may help identify meaningful subtypes within ASD. DSM-5 replaced the multi-categorical DSM-IV system (autistic disorder, Asperger's, PDD-NOS) with a single **autism spectrum disorder** diagnosis.

# Key Findings

## Key DSM-5 Changes

- Two-domain model: (1) social-communication deficits and (2) restricted/repetitive behaviors (RRBs) -- replaces DSM-IV triad
- DSM-5 includes **unusual sensory responses** in the RRB domain -- not previously required
- Stereotyped language reassigned from communication to RRB domain
- Dimensional severity levels (1-3) based on support needs
- Over 90% of children with DSM-IV PDD diagnoses met DSM-5 criteria

## Opportunities for Subtyping

- **Cognitive and adaptive functioning**: ASD displays wide range from severe intellectual disability to above-average IQ
- **Language skills**: structural language spectrum from no spoken words to verbal fluency with pragmatic deficits
- **Pattern of onset**: acquisition, regression, or plateau -- important for identifying biological trajectories
- **Comorbid symptoms**: ADHD, anxiety, mood disorders overlap differently across ASD subtypes
- Approximately **30% of ASD individuals** display regressive onset profiles around 18 months

## Research Implications

- Researchers should assess and report: cognitive functioning, language level, onset pattern, comorbid symptoms, other medical conditions
- DSM-5 dimensional approach helps increase homogeneity of research samples
- Better sample characterization improves interpretability and replicability of studies
