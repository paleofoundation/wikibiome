# Alterations in Circulating Fatty Acid Are Associated With Gut Microbiota Dysbiosis and Inflammation in Multiple Sclerosis

## Summary

This study measured serum butyric acid (BA) and caproic acid (CA) concentrations alongside immune parameters, intestinal barrier integrity markers, and gut microbiota composition in 38 MS patients compared with 38 age- and sex-matched healthy controls from Milan, Italy.

## Key Findings

### Serum Fatty Acid Alterations
- Butyric acid (BA) was significantly reduced in MS (median: HC=907 ng/ml; MS=752 ng/ml; p<0.0001)
- Caproic acid (CA) was significantly increased in MS (median: HC=181 ng/ml; MS=863 ng/ml; p<0.0001)
- BA/CA ratio was dramatically reduced in MS (ratio: 0.9) compared to HC (ratio: 5; p<0.0001)

### Immune Correlations
- CA positively correlated with CD4+/IFN-gamma+ T lymphocytes (Th1)
- BA/CA ratio positively correlated with CD4+/CD25high/Foxp3+ T lymphocytes (Tregs)
- BA/CA ratio negatively correlated with CD4+/IFN-gamma+ T lymphocytes
- Tregs (CD4+/CD25++FOXP3+) were dramatically decreased in MS (0.3%) vs HC (1%; p<0.0001)
- Th1 and Th17 cells were significantly increased in MS

### Gut Barrier Permeability
- LPS (lipopolysaccharide) was significantly elevated in MS plasma (median: HC=0.3 Eu/ml; MS=0.7 Eu/ml; p=0.001)
- I-FABP (intestinal fatty acid-binding protein) was dramatically increased in MS (HC=336 pg/ml; MS=715 pg/ml; p<0.0001)
- These markers indicate significant gut barrier dysfunction and microbial translocation in MS

### Gut Microbiota Alterations
- Dramatic depletion of *Lachnospiraceae* family in MS
- SCFA producers *Roseburia*, *Coprococcus*, and *Blautia* reduced by 4.5, 3.4, and 2 times respectively in MS
- Increased *Akkermansia* and *Collinsella* (7.4-fold and 2.4-fold increases)
- Increased *Streptococcus* in MS
- SPMS patients showed greater relative abundance of *Akkermansia* and *Collinsella*, and decreased *Dorea*
- LPS and I-FABP negatively correlated with *Bifidobacterium* abundance

## Relevance to Gut-Metal-Microbiome Axis

This study provides direct mechanistic links for the [[gut-metal-microbiome]] model:

1. **SCFA-producing bacteria depletion** - *Roseburia*, *Coprococcus*, *Blautia* are precisely the taxa most sensitive to [[heavy-metals]] toxicity, particularly [[cadmium]] and [[lead]]
2. **Gut barrier breakdown** - elevated LPS and I-FABP demonstrate the "leaky gut" that allows both metals and bacterial products to access systemic circulation and the CNS via the [[gut-brain-axis]]
3. **Butyrate reduction** - BA maintains GI epithelial barrier integrity; its depletion creates a vicious cycle where metal-induced [[dysbiosis]] reduces butyrate, increases permeability, and allows more metal absorption
4. **Caproic acid increase** - medium-chain fatty acids promote Th1/Th17 differentiation, the same pro-[[inflammation]] pathways activated by [[nf-kappa-b]] signaling from metal exposure
5. **Akkermansia paradox** - increased in MS (especially SPMS), consistent with [[cadmium]]-induced *Akkermansia* expansion

## Limitations

- Cross-sectional design
- Italian cohort; dietary patterns may influence results
- Did not measure metal levels or environmental exposures
- BA and CA measured in serum, not feces (reflects systemic rather than colonic levels)
