# Insights into Oral Microbiome and Colorectal Cancer - On the Way of Searching New Perspectives

## Key Claims

- Oral pathogenic bacteria from the red and orange complex (periodontal disease classification), particularly *Porphyromonas gingivalis* and *Fusobacterium nucleatum*, possess significant cancerogenic virulence factors that can promote CRC development after migration to the colon
- *F. nucleatum* virulence factors include Fad2 (adhesin/invasin essential for binding to both healthy and cancerous colon cells), FadA (adhesin promoting E-cadherin disruption), Fap2 (autotransporter protein activating immune evasion), and LPS (triggering inflammation)
- *P. gingivalis* virulence involves gingipains (cysteine proteinases cleaving host complement factors, immunoglobulins, and collagens), fimbriae (minor and major types enabling host cell binding and MAPK/ERK pathway activation), and LPS
- Oral microbiome composition and diversity were significantly different in CRC patients vs. healthy volunteers, with highest diversity observed in patients with colorectal adenoma, suggesting potential as non-invasive [[microbial-biomarkers]]
- Buccal *F. nucleatum* may migrate to CRC locus and impair radiotherapy efficiency; metronidazole administration reduced this effect in mouse models

## Methods/Findings

- **Design**: Narrative review covering oral microbiome in healthy individuals, alterations in CRC, virulence factors, biomarker potential, and treatment interactions
- **Key virulence mechanisms**: Detailed molecular pathways for *F. nucleatum* (Fad2, FadA, Fap2, LPS) and *P. gingivalis* (gingipains, fimbriae, LPS) in promoting CRC
- **Biofilm significance**: Dental biofilms develop in three stages; *Candida albicans* also discussed as a cancer-promoting oral fungal organism
- **Treatment implications**: *F. nucleatum* induced PD-L1 expression via STING signaling in mice, potentially enhancing PD-L1 blockade efficacy; periodontal disease management reduced *F. nucleatum* in stool of successfully treated CRC patients
