# Toxic Air Pollutants and Their Effect on Multiple Sclerosis: A Review Study

## Summary

This narrative review examined the relationship between toxic air pollutants and [[multiple-sclerosis]] (MS) incidence, covering literature from 2000 to 2022. Of 360 articles retrieved, 9 were selected for analysis. The review covers polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs), [[heavy-metals]], volatile organic compounds (VOCs), particulate matter (PM), and gases.

## Key Findings

- Toxic air pollutants including PAHs, heavy metals, VOCs, PM, and gases are significantly associated with increased MS incidence and severity
- Air pollutants can cross the blood-brain barrier and directly damage neurons, or enter through the olfactory bulb to cause [[inflammation]] in the brain
- [[cadmium]] specifically has been linked to increased MS susceptibility through cigarette smoking
- Microglia activation by air pollutants leads to chronic [[neuroinflammation]] and destruction of nerve cells
- Swallowed particles can have indirect neurological effects through the intestine, linking [[gut-brain-axis]] disruption to air pollution exposure
- Urban areas with high pollution concentrations have significantly higher MS prevalence than rural areas

## Mechanistic Pathways

- Air pollutants disrupt microglia regulation, leading to chronic brain [[inflammation]]
- Pollutants absorbed through the respiratory tract cause systemic pulmonary inflammation and activate proinflammatory cytokines in the brain
- Indirect effects via the gut: swallowed particles affect the intestinal microbiome, and studies show delivering small particles to the gut can cause systemic inflammation
- [[oxidative-stress]], atherosclerosis, and thrombosis are downstream consequences
- Genetic mutations caused by toxic air pollutants may affect MS onset

## Relevance to Gut-Metal-Microbiome Axis

The review explicitly notes the [[gut-brain-axis]] connection: air pollutants entering the gastrointestinal tract can alter the gut microbiome and trigger systemic [[inflammation]]. This supports the [[gut-metal-microbiome]] model where inhaled and ingested [[heavy-metals]] and pollutants disrupt intestinal barrier function, promote [[dysbiosis]], and amplify [[neuroinflammation]] characteristic of MS.

## Limitations

- Narrative review with only 9 studies included
- Limited mechanistic depth on specific pollutant-microbiome interactions
- Did not systematically quantify dose-response relationships
