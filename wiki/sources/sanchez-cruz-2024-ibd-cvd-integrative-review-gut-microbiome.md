# Inflammatory Bowel Disease and Cardiovascular Disease: An Integrative Review With a Focus on the Gut Microbiome

## Summary

Integrative review examining the association between IBD (Crohn's disease and ulcerative colitis) and [[cardiovascular-disease]], emphasizing the role of the gut microbiome in this relationship. Covers the shared pathophysiology of chronic [[inflammation]], endothelial dysfunction, and [[dysbiosis]] linking the two conditions.

## Key Findings

### Epidemiology of IBD and CVD
- Approximately 6 million IBD patients worldwide; approximately half of US cases
- IBD patients have increased risk of coronary artery disease, heart failure, cerebrovascular accidents, and atrial fibrillation
- After adjusting for traditional CV risk factors, IBD patients have two times increase in heart failure risk
- Patients with IBD have a 19% increase in risk of developing heart failure up to 20 years after diagnosis
- IBD patients who were pregnant had a 2-3 times increased risk of VTE

### Pathophysiology Linking IBD and CVD
- Endothelial dysfunction is central: chronic inflammatory state alters vasodilator/vasoconstrictor balance
- NO decreased in IBD patients due to TNF-alpha increase and arginase enzyme competition
- IBD promotes angiogenesis via CD40-CD40 ligand pathway
- CRP, SAA, TNF-alpha, IL-1-beta, IL-6, IL-8, IL-12, and calprotectin all elevated in IBD and predict CVD risk
- Genetic risk factors: NOD2 mutation, ATG16L1 deficiency, IL-10 mutation, RAG2 deficiency, NEMO deletion

### Dysbiosis as Shared Mechanism
- Firmicutes/Bacteroidetes ratio alterations and Enterobacteriaceae enrichment (*Escherichia coli*) observed in both IBD and CVD
- Dysbiosis increases intestinal permeability leading to LPS absorption
- LPS induces macrophage activation, vascular endothelialitis, and increased CRP
- Indole and phenyl derivatives exacerbate atherosclerosis and cause hypertension
- [[tmao]] promotes foam cell formation, endothelial dysfunction, platelet activation, and LDL deposition

### Therapeutic Implications
- Probiotics: OR 2.45 reduction in CRP (95% CI: -5.16 to -1.73) in IBD patients; increase *Bifidobacterium* and *Lactobacillus* in UC
- FMT: Shown to induce changes in microbiota and mucosal gene expression in UC, maintaining remission
- Dietary interventions: High-quality carbohydrates can positively influence fecal microbiota and improve cardiometabolic health
- No established CVD risk guidelines specific to IBD patients; European Society of Cardiology 2019 guidelines recognize IBD for rigorous screening

## Relevance to the Wiki

Bridges the IBD-CVD comorbidity literature with a microbiome focus, establishing [[dysbiosis]] and [[inflammation]] as shared pathogenic mechanisms. Relevant to understanding how chronic intestinal inflammation contributes to systemic [[cardiovascular-disease]] risk through metabolite and immune-mediated pathways.
