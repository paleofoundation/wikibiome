# Environmental Cadmium Exposure Alters the Internal Microbiota and Metabolome of Sprague-Dawley Rats

## Summary

This study investigated the effects of [[cadmium]] (CdCl2) exposure on the gut and blood microbiome and serum metabolome in Sprague-Dawley rats over 30 days. The results demonstrate that Cd exposure disrupts the balance between blood and intestinal flora, promotes bacterial translocation from gut to blood, and alters serum metabolic profiles relevant to [[cardiovascular-disease]] risk.

## Key Findings

### Intestinal Barrier Disruption
- Cd exposure significantly reduced ZO-1 tight junction protein expression in the intestine (p<0.05)
- Inflammatory factors TNF-alpha and IL-6 were significantly increased in both intestine and blood (p<0.05)
- Positive correlation between *Corynebacterium*, *Muribaculaceae*, *Atopostipes* and TNF-alpha in the intestinal tract

### Gut Microbiome Changes
- Bacteroidota and Firmicutes were the dominant phyla in the intestinal tract
- At genus level: *Muribaculaceae* (22.84%), *Prevotella* (21.54%), *Lactobacillus* (16.17%), *Alloprevotella* (4.67%)
- After Cd exposure, *Clostridia_UCG_014*, *NK4A214_group*, *Lachnospiraceae_NK4B4_group*, and *Christensenellaceae_R_7_group* became dominant in the CdCl2 group
- Acinetobacteriota significantly decreased in gut and increased in blood

### Blood Microbiome Changes
- Proteobacteria and Bacteroidota were main phyla in blood
- *Sphingomonas* (21.82%), *Ralstonia* (13.69%), *Sediminibacterium* (12.30%), *Escherichia* (8.23%) were primary blood bacteria
- Cd increased Actinobacteriota in blood microbiome (p<0.05)
- *Muribaculaceae* was not detected in normal blood but significantly increased in Cd-exposed group, indicating bacterial translocation from gut

### Metabolomic Alterations
- 233 differential metabolites identified; 22 potential metabolic biomarkers (VIP >1.0, p<0.05)
- 12 biomarkers upregulated and 10 downregulated
- Key upregulated metabolites: indoxyl sulfate, phenol sulfate, p-cresol sulfate (arylsulfates), sphingosine, indole-3-acetylglycine, L-glutamine
- Key downregulated metabolites: delta-tocopherol, 7-ketocholesterol
- Affected pathways: alpha-linolenic acid metabolism, glycerophospholipid metabolism, sphingolipid metabolism, necroptosis, choline metabolism

### Microbiome-Metabolite Correlations
- Sphingosine positively associated with blood *Corynebacterium*, *Muribaculaceae*, *Atopostipes* and gut *Clostridia_UCG_014* and *NK4A214_group*
- 7-ketocholesterol and 9S,10S,13R-trihydroxyoctadec-11E-enoic acid negatively associated with these changes

## Relevance to Gut-Metal-Microbiome Axis

This study contributes to the [[gut-metal-microbiome]] model by:

1. **Bacterial translocation evidence** - Cd exposure causes gut bacteria (especially *Muribaculaceae*) to translocate into the blood, directly demonstrating how [[cadmium]]-induced intestinal barrier damage enables microbial invasion of the circulatory system
2. **Uremic toxin accumulation** - Upregulation of indoxyl sulfate and p-cresol sulfate (known uremic toxins and CVD risk factors) links Cd exposure to [[cardiovascular-disease]] risk via microbiome-mediated metabolite production
3. **[[Inflammation]] cascade** - Cd-induced increase of *Clostridia_UCG_014* (a proinflammatory bacterium positively correlated with blood glucose) establishes a pathway from metal exposure through [[dysbiosis]] to systemic [[inflammation]]
4. **[[Oxidative-stress]] markers** - Downregulation of delta-tocopherol (antioxidant) with concurrent upregulation of inflammatory metabolites suggests Cd-driven oxidative damage
5. **Sphingolipid disruption** - Altered sphingolipid metabolism connects to [[metabolic-syndrome]] pathways relevant to CVD
