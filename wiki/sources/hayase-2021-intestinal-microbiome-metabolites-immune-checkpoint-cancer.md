## Key Findings

- **Intestinal microbiome modulates ICI responses** via the host immune system, with specific taxa associated with treatment response vs. resistance across multiple cancer types
- **FMT from ICI responders to non-responders** can convert refractory patients to responders, providing the strongest causal evidence that the microbiome determines immunotherapy efficacy
- **[[bacteroides-fragilis]]** was among the first taxa associated with anti-CTLA-4 (ipilimumab) response, with its polysaccharide capsule activating dendritic cells and promoting anti-tumor Th1 responses
- **[[bifidobacterium]]**, [[faecalibacterium-prausnitzii]], and [[akkermansia-muciniphila]] are associated with favorable responses to anti-PD-1/PD-L1 therapy across melanoma, NSCLC, and other cancers
- **Microbial-derived metabolites** including SCFAs, inosine, and bile acid derivatives directly modulate anti-tumor immunity by enhancing T cell function, dendritic cell maturation, and reducing immunosuppressive regulatory T cell populations

## Detailed Summary

This review from Genome Medicine provides a comprehensive synthesis of the rapidly growing evidence linking intestinal microbiome composition to immune checkpoint blockade immunotherapy outcomes. Immune checkpoint inhibitors (anti-PD-1, anti-PD-L1, anti-CTLA-4) have transformed cancer treatment, but response rates vary widely and a significant fraction of patients fail to respond. The microbiome has emerged as a major determinant of this variability.

The evidence progresses from association to causation. Observational studies first identified that ICI responders harbor different gut microbiome compositions than non-responders. Antibiotic use before or during ICI therapy consistently worsens outcomes, providing indirect evidence of microbiome importance. Most compellingly, FMT trials have shown that transferring fecal material from ICI responders to non-responders can overcome resistance, demonstrating a causal relationship.

Multiple taxa have been associated with favorable ICI responses, though the specific taxa vary across cancer types and ICI agents. [[bacteroides-fragilis]] was identified as important for anti-CTLA-4 response through its activation of innate immune pathways. [[bifidobacterium]] species enhance dendritic cell function and CD8+ T cell priming. [[faecalibacterium-prausnitzii]] and other SCFA producers support anti-tumor immunity through butyrate-mediated T cell differentiation. [[akkermansia-muciniphila]] has been associated with anti-PD-1 response, potentially through enhancing intestinal immune surveillance.

The mechanistic pathways involve microbial metabolites that cross the gut epithelium and enter systemic circulation. SCFAs (particularly butyrate) enhance CD8+ T cell anti-tumor activity. Inosine (produced by certain Bifidobacterium species) activates adenosine receptors on T cells. Bile acid metabolites modulate dendritic cell and macrophage function. These metabolites act as intermediaries between gut microbiome composition and systemic anti-tumor immunity.

Iron ecology is relevant because many of the response-associated organisms have specific iron acquisition strategies. [[bacteroides-fragilis]] possesses sophisticated iron uptake systems, and competition for iron in the gut may determine the relative abundance of response-promoting vs. resistance-promoting organisms.

## Open Questions

- Can a standardized microbiome-based biomarker panel predict ICI response across cancer types?
- What is the optimal timing and composition of FMT or consortium interventions relative to ICI treatment?
- Does iron status or iron supplementation affect the abundance of ICI response-associated taxa?
- How do antibiotics differ in their impact on ICI-relevant taxa, and can narrow-spectrum approaches preserve response-associated organisms?
- What role does the mycobiome play in ICI response modulation?
