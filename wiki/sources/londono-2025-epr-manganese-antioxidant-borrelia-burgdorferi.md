# Why This Paper Matters

This study uses electron paramagnetic resonance (EPR) and electron-nuclear double resonance (ENDOR) spectroscopy to directly observe [[manganese]] speciation in living Borrelia burgdorferi cells, revealing a dual antioxidant defense system where Mn-superoxide dismutase (MnSOD) at the cell surface and low-molecular-weight Mn-metabolite complexes (H-Mn) in the cytoplasm each protect against different sources of superoxide. For the [[mis-metallation]] framework, B. burgdorferi is a uniquely informative organism because it has eliminated [[iron]] from its biology entirely -- it does not accumulate iron and lacks iron-dependent enzymes. This evolutionary strategy prevents [[Fenton-chemistry]] (Fe2+ + H2O2) and eliminates host [[nutritional-immunity]] targeting iron as an anti-pathogen weapon. Instead, B. burgdorferi has built its antioxidant defense entirely on manganese, making it a natural experiment in what happens when an organism eliminates one major transition metal cofactor. However, this Mn-dependence creates a new vulnerability: when metabolite pools deplete (stationary phase), excess Mn2+ that cannot form protective H-Mn complexes becomes cytotoxic -- representing a form of manganese-mediated self-poisoning.

# Key Claims

- **B. burgdorferi lacks iron and iron-dependent enzymes**, eliminating Fenton chemistry and Fe-dependent oxidative damage entirely
- **Two complementary Mn-based antioxidant systems**: MnSOD (L-Mn, enzyme-bound, low-symmetry EPR) at the cell surface for extracellular O2--, and H-Mn (metabolite-bound, high-symmetry EPR) in the cytoplasm for intracellular O2--
- **B. burgdorferi has very low H-Mn content** (~10% of total Mn as H-Mn) compared to radiation-resistant organisms like Deinococcus radiodurans (>90% H-Mn), explaining its radiosensitivity despite Mn accumulation
- **MnSOD provides the primary antioxidant defense** in B. burgdorferi, unlike in Deinococcus where H-Mn dominates
- **MnCl2 supplementation restores radioresistance** in the delta-MnSOD mutant by increasing H-Mn, but in metabolite-depleted (stationary phase) cells, the same supplementation becomes toxic
- **Mn toxicity emerges when metabolites are depleted**: excess Mn2+ without metabolite binding partners causes off-target binding (mis-metallation) effects
- **The Mn-binding affinity of enzymes (SodA) exceeds that of metabolites**, creating competition where abundant SodA outcompetes metabolites for Mn2+

# Methods & Findings

## EPR-Based Mn Speciation in Living Cells

EPR spectroscopy non-invasively distinguishes two Mn pools in intact cells:

| Mn Pool | EPR Signature | Identity | Function | Localization |
|---------|-------------|----------|----------|-------------|
| L-Mn | Broad, featureless | Enzyme-bound (mainly MnSOD) | Catalytic O2-- dismutation | Cell surface |
| H-Mn | Narrow, 6-peak | Mn2+-metabolite complexes (Pi, carboxylates, amino acids, peptides) | Non-enzymatic O2-- scavenging | Cytoplasm |

## Mn Partitioning in B. burgdorferi vs. Radiation-Resistant Organisms

| Organism | L-Mn (enzyme) | H-Mn (metabolite) | Radioresistance |
|----------|--------------|-------------------|----------------|
| B. burgdorferi (WT) | ~90% | ~10% | Low (<4 kGy) |
| D. radiodurans | ~10% | >90% | Very high (>70 kGy) |
| B. subtilis spores | Low | >90% | High |
| L. plantarum | Low | >90% | High |
| E. coli | ~90% | ~10% | Low |

## Growth Phase-Dependent Mn Dynamics

- **Exponential phase** (metabolite-replete): H-Mn complexes accumulate, providing cytoplasmic antioxidant protection
- **Early stationary phase**: Peak H-Mn accumulation; highest radioresistance
- **Late stationary phase** (metabolite-depleted): H-Mn pool shrinks; Mn2+ released from H-Mn complexes stored in polyphosphate/DPS granules
- **Post-depletion Mn supplementation**: Excess Mn2+ cannot form H-Mn (no metabolites available) and becomes toxic through off-target binding

## Mn Toxicity in Metabolite-Depleted Cells

Delta-MnSOD mutants supplemented with 0.5 uM MnCl2 at day-6 (metabolite-depleted) showed acute Mn toxicity even in non-irradiated controls. This demonstrates that free Mn2+ without metabolite or protein binding partners causes cellular damage, likely through mis-metallation of non-Mn enzymes or disruption of other divalent metal-dependent processes.
