# Developmental Toxicity of Cadmium in Infants and Children: A Review

## Why This Paper Matters

This systematic review is the most comprehensive examination of [[cadmium]] developmental toxicity specifically focused on the pediatric population -- from in utero exposure through childhood. It compiles evidence from multiple international cohort studies (Table 1) demonstrating that cadmium crosses the placental barrier, is present in cord blood and breast milk, and produces measurable adverse effects on pregnancy outcomes, neurobehavioral development, renal function, and cancer risk in children. The paper is critical for the developmental vulnerability thread because it establishes that children are disproportionately susceptible to cadmium toxicity due to immature organ systems, particularly the developing kidney and brain.

## Key Claims

- Cadmium is both a carcinogenesis toxicant and documented developmental toxicant
- Cadmium crosses the placental barrier and exposure may occur from the beginning of life
- In utero or early life exposure can have serious health consequences reflected either in childhood or later stages of life
- Prenatal cadmium exposure influences anthropometric development and growth; fetal DNA methylation linked to cord blood cadmium levels
- Low-level cadmium exposure is associated with lower IQ, attention deficit hyperactivity disorder ([[ADHD]]), learning disabilities, and behavioral problems in children
- Cadmium may act as an endocrine disruptor, influencing estrogens, thyroid, and growth hormones
- The developing kidney is more at risk than the adult kidney: glomerular and tubular functions continue to develop until 2-3 years of age
- Smoking is the main factor influencing cadmium toxicity during pregnancy

## Methods/Findings

- **Study type**: Systematic review using PubMed and Google Scholar; literature search on cadmium exposure in utero, early life, gestational, lactational, and prenatal periods
- Articles in English only; focus on human and animal exposure studies

### Neurobehavioral and Cognitive Dysfunctions
- Cadmium has been categorized as potential developmental neurotoxicant due to its ability to cross the placental and blood-brain barrier
- Bao et al. (2009): higher hair cadmium in children aged 7-16 associated with withdrawal, social problems, attention problems
- Cadmium exposure associated with lower verbal and full-scale IQ (inverse statistically significant relationship stronger in girls than boys)
- Few clinical studies prove cadmium may act as endocrine disruptor influencing estrogens, thyroid, and growth hormones during in utero or early life
- Cadmium alters gabaergic and serotonergic neurotransmission in rat pups exposed during gestation
- Prenatal cadmium exposure leads to delays in development of righting reflex and cliff aversion
- Zhang et al. (2009): enhanced oxidative stress markers (SOD, catalase, GSH-Px decreased; MDA increased) in brain of rat pups after cadmium chloride administration

### Renal Dysfunctions
- Kidney is main site of cadmium accumulation due to long biological half-life
- Urine cadmium (U-Cd) is well-accepted biomarker for long-term exposure
- Developing kidney more susceptible: glomerular and tubular functions not fully mature until age 2-3
- High cadmium in urine of infants demonstrates lower kidney accumulation capacity compared to adults
- Biomarkers: urinary beta-2-microglobulin (UB2M), retinol-binding protein (URBP), N-acetyl-beta-D-glucose aminidase (UNAG), albumin, Cystatin C
- Several cross-sectional studies in cadmium-contaminated areas report associations between cadmium exposure and impaired verbal IQ and visual-motor cognitive tasks in children

### Carcinogenesis and Cancer-Related Mortality
- Cadmium is an established human carcinogen (IARC Group 1)
- Chronic cadmium exposure can induce cancer in breast, lung, pancreas, and renal tissues
- Children have a higher risk of leukemia, renal cancer from cadmium pollution
- Several occupational studies found high risk of cancer in adults with cadmium exposure, and less evidence in children specifically

### Mechanisms
- [[oxidative-stress]]: enhanced ROS production, DNA repair inhibition with altered DNA methylation
- Cadmium may decrease transport of essential trace elements (Fe, Mn, Zn, Mo, Cu) to fetus via placental interactions
- Epigenetic modifications: altered DNA methylation in cord blood, sex-specific methylation changes
- Antagonistic interaction between cadmium and [[selenium]] in pregnant women is understudied
