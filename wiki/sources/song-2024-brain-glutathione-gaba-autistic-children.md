# Brain Glutathione and GABA Levels in Autistic Children

## Key Findings

- **No significant differences in cerebral GSH or GABA+ levels** between ASD (n=52) and typically developing children (TDC, n=49) across all examined brain regions ([[song-2024-brain-glutathione-gaba-autistic-children]], cross-sectional, n=101)
- **HERMES MR spectroscopy at 3T** enabled simultaneous measurement of both GSH and GABA+ in a single acquisition, reducing scan time and improving reliability
- **Four brain regions examined**: primary motor cortex (SM1), thalamus, medial prefrontal cortex (MPFC), and supplementary motor area (SMA) — all showed no group differences
- **Important null finding** challenging widely held assumptions about cerebral oxidative stress and GABAergic dysfunction in ASD
- **Children aged 8-12 years** were studied, suggesting that any cerebral GSH/GABA differences may be age-dependent or confined to earlier developmental stages

## Methods

Cross-sectional neuroimaging study comparing brain GSH (glutathione) and GABA+ (GABA plus macromolecules) levels between children with ASD (n=52) and typically developing controls (n=49), aged 8-12 years. HERMES (Hadamard Encoding and Reconstruction of MEGA-Edited Spectroscopy) at 3T MRI was used for simultaneous GSH and GABA+ quantification. Voxels placed in SM1, thalamus, MPFC, and SMA. Statistical analysis compared metabolite concentrations between groups. Published in Autism Research.

## Detailed Summary

This well-powered neuroimaging study used advanced MR spectroscopy to simultaneously measure two metabolites of high interest in ASD research: glutathione (GSH, the brain's primary antioxidant) and GABA (the primary inhibitory neurotransmitter). Both have been hypothesized to be altered in ASD based on peripheral (blood/urine) measurements and smaller imaging studies.

The HERMES technique represents a methodological advance, allowing simultaneous editing and quantification of both metabolites in a single scan, which reduces motion artifacts and session duration — critical advantages when studying children. The study examined four brain regions selected for their relevance to ASD: motor cortex (motor stereotypies), thalamus (sensory processing), prefrontal cortex (executive function), and supplementary motor area (motor planning).

The null finding across all regions and both metabolites is significant. While peripheral GSH deficiency and systemic oxidative stress markers are well-documented in ASD, this study suggests that cerebral GSH levels may be maintained through compensatory mechanisms (the brain prioritizes antioxidant defense). Similarly, while peripheral GABA levels and GABAergic gene expression are altered in ASD, brain GABA+ concentrations in this age range appear normal, suggesting that GABAergic dysfunction may manifest at the receptor or circuit level rather than as reduced neurotransmitter concentration.

## Open Questions

- Would younger children (under 8) or adults show different cerebral GSH/GABA patterns?
- Is the null finding explained by compensatory upregulation of GSH synthesis in the brain?
- Does GABAergic dysfunction in ASD manifest at the receptor density or signaling level rather than neurotransmitter concentration?
- How do peripheral oxidative stress markers (from gut dysbiosis, metal exposure) relate to cerebral antioxidant status?
- Would functional MRS (task-related GABA changes) reveal differences not detectable at rest?
