# Synbiotic Ameliorates Quality of Life in PCOS

## Key Findings

- **Synbiotic supplementation significantly improved PCOSQ-26 scores** across emotional, body hair, weight, and infertility domains compared to placebo ([[hariri-2024-synbiotic-pcos-quality-of-life-rct]], randomized-controlled-trial, n=72)
- **SF-12 physical component score** was significantly enhanced in the synbiotic group, indicating improved physical health-related quality of life
- **Probiotic formulation included Bacillus coagulans and Lactobacillus rhamnosus** combined with prebiotic fiber
- **Perceived stress scores** improved in the synbiotic group, suggesting gut-brain axis modulation <!-- NEEDS VERIFICATION: exact stress scale used and p-values -->
- **Intervention duration** was sufficient to demonstrate clinically meaningful quality of life improvements

## Methods

Randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled clinical trial in women diagnosed with PCOS. 72 participants were randomized to receive either synbiotic (containing Bacillus coagulans, Lactobacillus rhamnosus, and prebiotic) or placebo. Outcomes measured using PCOSQ-26 (PCOS-specific quality of life questionnaire covering emotional, body hair, weight, infertility, and menstrual domains) and SF-12 (general health-related quality of life). <!-- NEEDS VERIFICATION: intervention duration and exact dosing -->

## Detailed Summary

Polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS) significantly impairs quality of life through symptoms including hirsutism, weight gain, infertility, menstrual irregularity, and psychological distress. This RCT evaluated whether synbiotic supplementation could ameliorate these quality of life dimensions, reasoning that gut microbiome modulation may influence the hormonal and metabolic disturbances underlying PCOS symptoms.

The synbiotic formulation combined spore-forming [[bacillus]] coagulans (which survives gastric acid) with [[lactobacillus]] rhamnosus and prebiotic fiber to support colonization. The significant improvements across multiple PCOSQ-26 domains suggest that the synbiotic affected multiple pathophysiological axes: the gut-endocrine axis (hormonal symptoms), the gut-brain axis (emotional well-being), and systemic inflammation (physical symptoms).

The improvement in perceived stress is particularly noteworthy, as it suggests that gut microbiome modulation can influence psychological outcomes in PCOS, consistent with emerging evidence on the microbiome-stress axis.
