# The Nasal Microbiome in Patients Suffering from Non-Steroidal Anti-Inflammatory Drugs-Exacerbated Respiratory Disease in Absence of Corticosteroids

## Summary

This study examines the nasal microbiome in patients with NSAID-exacerbated respiratory disease (N-ERD), chronic rhinosinusitis with nasal polyps (CRSwNP), CRSsNP, and disease controls (n=20 per group) using 16S rRNA gene amplicon sequencing and 33 inflammatory cytokines.

## Key Findings

- Across all 4 groups, *Corynebacteria* and *Staphylococci* showed the highest relative abundances
- Higher relative abundance of *Staphylococci* in middle meatus in N-ERD patients vs CRSwNP (p<0.001), CRSsNP (p<0.01), and disease controls (p<0.05)
- Decreased *Lawsonella* in CRSwNP patients
- Positive correlation of *Staphylococci* with IL-5 (Pearson r=0.548)
- Negative correlation of *Corynebacteria* with Eotaxin-3 (r=-0.540)
- N-ERD and CRSwNP patients showed significantly higher type 2 cytokines (IL-5, IL-9, Eotaxin, CCL17)
- No significant difference in alpha or beta diversity between CRS entities and controls

## Relevance to Gut-Metal-Microbiome Axis

While focused on the nasal rather than gut microbiome, this study illustrates how NSAIDs interact with mucosal microbiomes to drive inflammatory disease. The type 2 inflammatory response identified (IL-5, Eotaxin) differs from the Th1/Th17-dominated inflammation in [[multiple-sclerosis]], but the principle that drug-microbiome interactions shape immune outcomes is directly applicable. The nasal microbiome is increasingly recognized as part of the broader mucosal immune network that includes the gut, and oral/nasal [[dysbiosis]] has been documented in MS patients.

## Limitations

- Small sample size (20 per group)
- Cross-sectional design
- Nasal, not gut, microbiome focus
- Does not address MS specifically
