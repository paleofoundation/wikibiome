## Key Findings

- **Perioperative gut dysbiosis drives neuroinflammation through multiple gut-brain axis pathways**, including gut barrier disruption, blood-brain barrier (BBB) compromise, HPA axis dysregulation, vagus nerve signaling, immune cell activation, and microbial metabolite alterations ([[zhou-2025-gut-microbiota-neuroinflammation-perioperative-neurocognitive-disorders]], expert-opinion, narrative review).
- **Fecal microbiota transplantation from PND patients to germ-free mice reproduces cognitive impairment** — learning disabilities, anxiety-like behavior, and reduced social skills — establishing a causal role for the gut microbiome in perioperative cognitive dysfunction ([[zhou-2025-gut-microbiota-neuroinflammation-perioperative-neurocognitive-disorders]], citing Kundu et al. 2022; Luczynski et al. 2016).
- **Surgery and anesthesia induce characteristic dysbiosis patterns**: enrichment of pathogenic taxa (*Proteobacteria*, *Escherichia-Shigella*, *Fusobacteria*, *Gammaproteobacteria*, *Pseudomonas*, *Staphylococcus*) and depletion of beneficial taxa (*Firmicutes*, *Actinobacteria*, *Lachnospiraceae*, *Butyrivibrio*, *Eubacterium*, *Lactobacillus*, *Bifidobacterium*) ([[zhou-2025-gut-microbiota-neuroinflammation-perioperative-neurocognitive-disorders]]).
- **SCFAs (butyrate, propionate, acetate) are key mediators**: they regulate microglial activation, inhibit NF-kB signaling in astrocytes, modulate HPA axis cortisol secretion, reinforce BBB tight junctions, and suppress neuroinflammatory cytokine release ([[zhou-2025-gut-microbiota-neuroinflammation-perioperative-neurocognitive-disorders]]).
- **Therapeutic interventions targeting the gut microbiota** — probiotics (VSL#3, *Lactobacillus*, *Bifidobacterium*), prebiotics (B-GOS), FMT, dietary modifications, and vagus nerve stimulation — show promise for PND prevention and treatment ([[zhou-2025-gut-microbiota-neuroinflammation-perioperative-neurocognitive-disorders]]).

## Methods

This is a **narrative review** synthesizing current knowledge on the mechanistic interplay between gut microbiota dysbiosis and neuroinflammation in perioperative neurocognitive disorders (PND). The authors reviewed literature on neuroinflammatory pathways (microglia, astrocytes, oligodendrocytes), gut-brain axis communication routes (gut barrier, BBB, HPA axis, vagus nerve, immune system, metabolites), and therapeutic strategies (probiotics, prebiotics, FMT, dietary interventions). A summary table (Table 1) collates 14 human and animal studies on gut microbiota and PND. No systematic search methodology or inclusion/exclusion criteria are reported, classifying this as expert-opinion level evidence.

## Detailed Summary

### Neuroinflammation and PND

**Perioperative neurocognitive disorders (PND)** encompass postoperative delirium, preoperative cognitive impairment, postoperative cognitive dysfunction, and delayed neurocognitive recovery. Incidence is approximately 23.8%, rising to 50% in elderly cohorts. PND leads to prolonged hospitalization, reduced quality of life, and increased mortality.

**Neuroinflammatory cells involved:**
- **Microglia**: Surgery and anesthesia trigger microglial activation, producing inflammatory cascades (cytokines, eicosanoids, complement factors, ROS, nitric oxide). Colony-stimulating factor 1 receptor inhibitors reduce neuroinflammation in PND mouse models.
- **Astrocytes**: Transition to neurotoxic A1 phenotype during perioperative inflammation, releasing pro-inflammatory mediators and promoting neuronal death. The A1/A2 balance is critical.
- **Oligodendrocytes**: Anesthesia directly acts on surface receptors, impairing proliferation and myelination. Propofol selectively causes apoptosis in oligodendrocytes in fetal/neonatal primate brains.

### Gut Microbiota and PND

The perioperative period disrupts gut microbiota through multiple mechanisms:

1. **Preoperative factors**: Mechanical bowel preparation alters microbiota composition (recovery takes up to 14 days). Fasting and fluid restriction reshape community structure. Psychological stress weakens the gut barrier via the sympathetic nervous system and HPA axis.
2. **Intraoperative factors**: Anesthesia disrupts 5-HT and GABA production by gut bacteria. Surgical interventions (especially abdominal) directly alter the gut microbiota environment. Isoflurane and sevoflurane specifically contribute to dysbiosis.
3. **Postoperative factors**: Infection risk, antibiotics (reduce diversity, increase *Pseudomonas*, *Staphylococcus*, *Enterococcus*, decrease *Lactobacillus*, *Bifidobacterium*), pain/opioids damage intestinal epithelial barrier and promote bacterial translocation.

### Gut-Brain Axis Pathways to Neuroinflammation

**Six routes** by which gut dysbiosis drives neuroinflammation in PND:

1. **Gut barrier disruption**: Dysbiosis compromises the mucus layer, epithelial barrier, and vascular barrier. Bacterial translocation and LPS entry into systemic circulation trigger mucosal and systemic inflammation. SCFAs (especially butyrate) normally maintain barrier integrity via MUC-2 upregulation and tight junction protein modulation.

2. **BBB compromise**: Dysbiosis-induced systemic inflammation damages BBB tight junctions (claudins, occludins, JAMs, ZO-1, ZO-2). Antibiotic-treated and germ-free mice show increased BBB permeability; fecal transplantation from normal mice restores tight junction integrity. SCFAs enhance BBB function via G-protein-coupled receptors and NF-kB inhibition. LPS disrupts BBB via TLR4/NF-kB signaling.

3. **HPA axis dysregulation**: Gut microbiota influences HPA axis development and reactivity. GF mice show exaggerated HPA responses normalized by fecal colonization. Higher *Bifidobacterium* and *Streptococcus* abundance with lower *Bacteroides* in early infancy correlates with enhanced cortisol reactivity. Chronic excess cortisol promotes inflammation via glucocorticoid receptor downregulation.

4. **Vagus nerve signaling**: The vagus nerve transmits microbiota-mediated inflammatory signals to the brain. Dysbiosis activates pro-inflammatory cascades via NR2B (NMDA receptor subtype) binding on vagal afferents, triggering BBB disruption and neuroinflammation. *Lactobacillus rhamnosus* and *Bifidobacterium longum* reduce stress-induced corticosterone via vagotomy-dependent pathways. Vagus nerve stimulation attenuates neuroinflammation by suppressing alpha7nAChR/JAK2/STAT3/NF-kB signaling.

5. **Immune cell activation**: Surgery and anesthesia disrupt mucosal barrier, facilitating bacterial translocation and immune cell (macrophages, neutrophils, T cells) infiltration into CNS. Th17 cells are crucial for mucosal immunity; surgery/anesthesia-induced dysbiosis leads to Th17 dysregulation and IL-17 secretion. Tregs depletion enhances beta-amyloid clearance but impairs neuroinflammation resolution.

6. **Gut microbiota metabolites**: SCFAs (acetate, propionate, butyrate) inhibit microglial activation and NF-kB signaling. Gut bacteria produce ~60% of colonic 5-HT; surgical dysbiosis disrupts 5-HT metabolism causing hippocampal deficiency. *Lactobacillus* and *Bifidobacterium* synthesize GABA; anesthesia impairs GABA-ergic signaling via p38 MAPK pathway. Bile acid pool alterations (ursodeoxycholic acid) and tryptophan-derived indoles further modulate neuroinflammation.

### Therapeutic Strategies

1. **Probiotics and prebiotics**: *Lactobacillus* and *Bifidobacterium* protect the gut barrier and exert anti-inflammatory effects. VSL#3 mitigated anesthesia/surgery-induced memory impairment in mice. B-GOS (galactooligosaccharide prebiotic) alleviated surgery-induced PND in rats by modulating gut microbiota and inhibiting M1 microglia. Acetate exhibited anti-neuroinflammatory activity by suppressing microglial activation.

2. **Fecal microbiota transplantation (FMT)**: Demonstrated efficacy in *Clostridium difficile*, IBD, insulin resistance, and obesity. FMT from healthy mice to PND models improved cognitive deficits. FMT from human amyloid precursor protein knock-in models to GF mice induced cognitive deficits, implicating gut microbiota in beta-amyloid deposition. In APPswe/PS1dE9 transgenic AD mice, FMT attenuated cognitive decline with reduced cerebral amyloid-beta plaque formation.

3. **Dietary interventions**: Omega-3 fatty acids and vitamins A/D attenuated LPS-induced neuroinflammatory markers in BV-2 microglial cells. Omega-3 supplementation reversed high-fat diet-induced neuroinflammation in rats. Dietary restriction improved perioperative neurocognitive disorders by inhibiting neuroinflammation and gut dysbiosis. Paradoxically, prolonged preoperative fasting exacerbates gut dysbiosis and stress activation.

## Relevance to Cureva

- **Potential intervention hypotheses for PND**: Probiotics (*Lactobacillus*, *Bifidobacterium*, VSL#3), prebiotics (B-GOS/galactooligosaccharides), FMT, omega-3 supplementation, dietary modification, and vagus nerve stimulation all warrant triangle validation against PND.

## Contradictions / Tensions

- **FMT evidence is preclinical only for PND**: While FMT shows promise in animal models, no human clinical trials for PND are cited. The review acknowledges "rigorous clinical evaluation is warranted."
- **Fasting paradox**: Preoperative fasting is standard for aspiration risk but exacerbates the very gut dysbiosis that contributes to PND. The review does not resolve this clinical tension.
- **Probiotics timing and strain specificity**: The review discusses multiple probiotic interventions but does not address optimal timing relative to surgery, strain-specific effects, or dosing — a gap for clinical translation.

## Open Questions

1. What is the optimal timing for probiotic/prebiotic administration relative to surgery to maximize PND prevention — preoperative, perioperative, or postoperative?
2. Can specific dysbiosis patterns (e.g., *Proteobacteria* enrichment, *Lachnospiraceae* depletion) serve as predictive biomarkers for PND risk before surgery?
3. Does the type of anesthesia (propofol vs. sevoflurane vs. isoflurane) produce distinct dysbiosis signatures with different neuroinflammatory consequences?
4. Are there metal-dependent mechanisms in PND pathogenesis? The review does not discuss metals, but iron sequestration by gut pathogens during post-surgical dysbiosis and zinc-dependent tight junction maintenance could be relevant pathways to investigate.
5. What role do fungal commensals play in perioperative gut-brain axis dysfunction? The review is entirely bacteria-focused.
