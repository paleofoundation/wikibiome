# Feeding the Gut Microbiome: Impact on Multiple Sclerosis

## Summary

This comprehensive review describes how diet impacts the gut microbiota and the development and course of [[multiple-sclerosis]] (MS). It covers preclinical studies on experimental autoimmune encephalomyelitis (EAE) and clinical dietary intervention studies in MS, with particular attention to gut metabolite-immune system interactions. The review also evaluates [[probiotics]], prebiotics, and postbiotics as microbiome-targeted therapies.

## Key Microbiome Alterations in MS

The review provides a detailed table of microorganisms altered in MS:

**Increased in MS:**
- *Methanobrevibacter* (Archaea) - recruits inflammatory cells and DCs
- *Akkermansia* - associated with SCFA production and pro-inflammatory effects
- *Streptococcus thermophilus*, *S. salivarius*, *S. oralis*, *S. mitis* - promotes Th17 differentiation
- *Mycoplasma*, *Pseudomonas*, *Blautia*, *Eggerthella lenta*
- Caudovirales (phage)

**Decreased in MS:**
- *Butyricimonas* - involved in [[short-chain-fatty-acids]] production
- *Prevotella* - negative relationship with Th17 frequency
- *Faecalibacterium* - positively associated with SCFA production, negatively with TNF-alpha
- *Bacteroides*, *Parabacteroides*, *Sutterella*
- *Clostridium* cluster IV and XIVa - affects SCFAs, Tregs, and anti-inflammatory cytokine production

## Dietary Interventions in EAE and MS

- **High-fat diet**: increases EAE severity via Th1/Th17 differentiation through p38/MAPK pathway
- **High-salt diet**: increases CNS infiltration of Th17; mitigated by *Lactobacillus murinus* supplementation
- **Dietary fibers**: increase [[short-chain-fatty-acids]] production; cellulose-rich diet expands Th2 cell population and protects against EAE
- **Dietary tryptophan**: restriction alters microbiome composition; microbially derived AHR ligand indoxyl-3-sulfate mitigates disease (relevant to [[indoles]])
- **Isoflavones**: increase *Parabacteroides distasonis* and *Adlercreutzia equolifaciens*, protecting against EAE
- **Mediterranean diet**: associated with reduced fatigue severity and lower EDSS scores in MS

## Probiotics, Prebiotics, and Postbiotics

- [[probiotics]] show promise in MS: *L. murinus* mitigates high-salt diet effects in EAE
- Dietary restriction regimens have protective effects via the gut microbiota
- The gut microbiota mediates the effects of intermittent fasting, which enriches Lactobacillaceae, Bacteroidaceae, and Prevotellaceae
- Fecal microbiome transplantation from IF mice to immunized mice ameliorated EAE

## Relevance to Gut-Metal-Microbiome Axis

This review highlights that diet is the primary determinant of gut microbiota composition. The taxa depleted in MS (e.g., *Faecalibacterium*, *Prevotella*, *Butyricimonas*) are precisely those known to be sensitive to [[heavy-metals]] exposure. The protective dietary factors (fiber, vegetables, polyphenols) are also those that promote metal chelation and support metal-resistant beneficial bacteria, reinforcing the [[gut-metal-microbiome]] connection. The [[gut-brain-axis]] mechanisms described - involving [[short-chain-fatty-acids]], [[indoles]], Treg/Th17 balance, and [[nf-kappa-b]] signaling - represent the downstream pathways through which metal-induced [[dysbiosis]] could drive [[neuroinflammation]] in MS.

## Limitations

- Review primarily covers EAE models, which imperfectly model human MS
- Limited discussion of environmental toxicant effects on the microbiome
- Clinical dietary intervention studies in MS remain small and heterogeneous
