## Key Findings

- **Gut microbiota composition is significantly altered in men with prostatic diseases** including chronic prostatitis/chronic pelvic pain syndrome (CP/CPPS), benign prostatic hyperplasia (BPH), and prostate cancer (PCa), with consistent reductions in microbial diversity compared to healthy controls ([[cao-2024-gut-microbiome-preventive-therapeutic-prostatic-disease]], expert-opinion, narrative review).
- **Four primary mechanisms link GM to prostatic disease**: (1) direct bacterial dissemination from gut to prostate via urethra or bloodstream, (2) inflammation and immune dysregulation through cytokine cascades (IL-17, IL-23, TNF-alpha, IFN-gamma), (3) metabolite production — particularly SCFAs with dual roles in immune modulation and tumor progression, and (4) androgen metabolism by gut bacteria including *Clostridium scindens* which converts androgen precursors into potent androgens.
- **Androgen deprivation therapy (ADT) alters the gut microbiome**, reducing diversity and shifting taxonomic composition, which may in turn influence treatment efficacy and castration resistance in prostate cancer.
- **Mendelian randomization studies provide causal evidence** supporting a link between specific gut microbiota compositions and prostatic disease risk, strengthening the case beyond observational associations.
- **Six therapeutic strategies targeting the GM** — diet modification, traditional Chinese medicine, antibiotics, probiotics/prebiotics, fecal microbiota transplantation, and natural compounds — show preliminary promise for preventing and treating prostatic disease.

## Methods

This is a **narrative review** synthesizing observational, experimental (animal model), and clinical studies on the relationship between gut microbiota and prostatic diseases. The authors systematically surveyed literature on three prostatic conditions — CP/CPPS, BPH, and PCa — covering human cohort studies, animal models, and Mendelian randomization analyses. The review includes a comprehensive summary table (Table 1) cataloging 15+ individual studies with sample sizes ranging from 6 to 152 participants across human and animal models, organized by prostatic disease type.

## Detailed Summary

### GM and CP/CPPS

Patients with CP/CPPS show **decreased alpha diversity** (p=0.001) with overrepresentation of 3 taxa and underrepresentation of 12 taxa, including significantly fewer **Prevotella** bacteria. A Chinese study of 41 CP/CPPS patients and 43 controls found notable variations in gut microbiota composition, with potential for non-invasive diagnostic markers. Comprehensive analysis in rats showed strong associations between gut microbiota, gene expression, and DNA methylation affecting intestinal immunity, metabolism, and epithelial barrier function.

### GM and BPH

In men with prostate enlargement (n=66 with PE, n=62 controls), the BPH group had a **higher percentage of *Bacillota* (*Firmicutes*) and *Actinomycetota* (*Actinobacteria*)** and lower *Bacteroidota* (*Bacteroidetes*), with a significantly higher Firmicutes/Bacteroidetes ratio. In rat models, BPH was associated with altered abundance of **[[lactiplantibacillus]]**, *Flavonifractor*, *Acetatifactor*, *Oscillibacter*, *Pseudoflavonifractor*, *Intestinimonas*, and *Butyricimonas*. Finasteride treatment reversed some of these changes. LC-MS/MS analysis revealed differential metabolites enriched in steroid hormone biosynthesis, ovarian steroidogenesis, and biosynthesis of unsaturated fatty acids.

### GM and Prostate Cancer

Multiple studies document altered microbiota in PCa patients. Key findings include:
- **[[bacteroides]]** *massiliensis* was more significant in men with BPH or intermediate/high-risk PCa
- **[[akkermansia-muciniphila]]**, *Bifidobacteriaceae*, and *Enterococcaceae* were enriched at different stages of PCa progression
- **Pseudomonadota** (*Proteobacteria*) was enriched in metastatic PCa (mPCa), with positive association to plasma IL-6 levels, regional lymph node metastasis, and distant metastasis status
- **[[ruminococcus]]** and *Phascolarctobacterium* were increased in castration-resistant prostate cancer (CRPC)
- **[[lachnospira]]**, **[[alistipes]]**, and *Rikenellaceae* — all SCFA-producing bacteria — were significantly increased in high-risk PCa

### Mechanisms of GM Influence on Prostatic Disease

**Direct infection:** Intestinal bacteria can enter the prostate through the urinary tract or bloodstream, with **[[escherichia-coli]]** (80%) and **Enterococcus faecalis** identified as primary pathogens from intestinal origin. Various research has found and recognized bacteria in prostate tissues.

**Inflammation and immune dysregulation:** Dysbiosis leads to production of inflammatory cytokines (IL-17, IL-23, TNF-alpha, IFN-gamma) that travel via bloodstream to the prostate. SCFAs have complex immunomodulatory roles — butyrate inhibits IFN-gamma-producing cells while enhancing Treg formation; propionate inhibits M2 macrophage polarization. SCFAs boost HIF-1 activation through histone deacetylase inhibition, enhancing antimicrobial agent synthesis.

**Metabolites:** SCFAs (acetate, propionate, butyrate) play critical roles. In PCa, SCFAs increased movement and penetration of PCa cells by stimulating autophagy through TLR3 activation and triggering NF-kB and MAPK pathways. Urolithins derived from gut microbiota showed effects on suppressing cell growth, PSA release, and arginase function.

**Androgens and ADT:** **[[clostridium-scindens]]** can transform cortisol into 11-beta-hydroxy androstenedione (11beta-OHA), a precursor of androgens. ADT significantly alters gut microbiota composition — a study of 23 PCa patients showed significant alterations in *Pseudomonadota*, *Gammaproteobacteria*, *Pseudomonadales*, *Pseudomonas* levels after 24 weeks. PCa reduced relative abundance of [[akkermansia-muciniphila]], and ADT reversed this effect. *Thauera* sp. strain GDN1 decreased serum androgen levels by approximately 50% when administered orally.

### Therapeutic Strategies

**Diet:** High-fat diet increases BPH/PCa risk via gut microbiota changes. Weight-loss diet in 40 PCa patients showed *Pseudomonadota* and *Clostridium*/*Blautia* correlations. Indole-3-carbinol (I3C) from cruciferous vegetables showed protective effects.

**Traditional Chinese medicine:** *Poria cocos* polysaccharides (PPs) and finasteride both improved CP/CPPS in rats, with PPs specifically affecting *Oscillospiraceae NK4A214* group. Rapeseed bee pollen reduced CP/CPPS symptoms by controlling gut microbiota, suppressing *Bacillota*-to-*Bacteroidota* ratio and increasing *Prevotella*.

**Probiotics/prebiotics:** Live probiotics potentially postpone aggressive PCa development. A combination of diverse bacterial strains appears more effective than single-strain probiotics. Partially hydrolyzed guar gum increased *Bifidobacterium* counts in patients.

**FMT:** Fecal microbiota transplantation delayed castration resistance in CRPC mice. Fecal filtrate from healthy mice increased GPER expression, suppressed cell proliferation, and triggered apoptosis in BPH-1 cells.

**Natural compounds:** Astaxanthin increased [[akkermansia-muciniphila]] proportion and SCFA levels. Phytoestrogens (lignans) suppress NF-kB pathway. Polyphenols from berries hinder harmful bacteria growth. Green tea catechins (GTCs) and ellagitannins from pomegranate juice showed anti-PCa potential via gut microbiota interactions.

## Contradictions / Tensions

- **PCa microbiome inconsistency:** One study (Alanee et al., 2019, n=30) found **no significant GM difference** between PCa patients and controls, directly contradicting the majority of studies reviewed. The authors note this may reflect sample size limitations or methodological differences (16S rRNA gene sequencing in all cases, but variable regions and analysis pipelines differ).
- **SCFA dual role:** SCFAs are presented as both protective (immune modulation, anti-inflammatory) and harmful (stimulating PCa cell autophagy and invasion via TLR3/NF-kB/MAPK pathways). This tension is unresolved and likely context-dependent (healthy tissue vs. tumor microenvironment).
- **Antibiotic paradox:** Antibiotics are listed as a therapeutic strategy for prostatic disease while simultaneously being flagged as a risk factor for PCa progression — the review acknowledges but does not fully resolve this contradiction.
- **Condition scope note:** This source is filed under "erectile dysfunction" per library organization, but the paper itself focuses on CP/CPPS, BPH, and prostate cancer — not erectile dysfunction directly. The relevance to ED is indirect, through shared prostatic inflammation pathways and the gut-prostate axis.

## Open Questions

1. **What is the causal direction?** Most evidence is correlational. Do prostatic diseases alter the GM, or does an altered GM drive prostatic disease? Mendelian randomization studies suggest causality in at least some cases, but more are needed.
2. **Which specific bacterial species are consistently altered across prostatic diseases?** Current studies show high heterogeneity in reported taxa — no single "prostatic disease microbiome signature" has emerged.
3. **Can FMT or targeted probiotics meaningfully alter prostate cancer outcomes in humans?** All FMT evidence is preclinical (mouse models). No human RCTs exist for FMT in prostatic disease.
4. **How does the SCFA paradox resolve?** Under what conditions do SCFAs protect versus promote prostatic disease? Is it dose-dependent, taxa-specific, or tumor-stage-dependent?
5. **What is the interaction between ADT-induced microbiome changes and castration resistance?** Can microbiome-targeted interventions delay or prevent CRPC development?
6. **How do metal exposures interact with the gut-prostate axis?** This review does not address metals, but given that cadmium is a known prostate carcinogen and zinc is essential for prostate function, the metallomic layer remains unexplored in this context.
