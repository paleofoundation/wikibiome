# Role of the Intestinal Microbiome in Health and Disease: From Correlation to Causation

## Key Claims

- The intestinal microbiome can be linked to over 25 diseases or syndromes, but associative or correlative support currently dominates the evidence base; only a handful of cause-and-effect studies have been performed
- The common core microbiota of over 50 healthy subjects comprises approximately 20 known genera/species including *Akkermansia muciniphila*, *Bacteroides* spp., *Bifidobacterium* spp., *Faecalibacterium prausnitzii*, and *Ruminococcus* spp., along with 387 uncultured phylotypes
- For CRC specifically, the strongest microbiome association involves variation in *Bacteroides* spp. and increased *Fusobacterium*, supported by multiple independent studies
- FMT studies in *C. difficile* infection and metabolic syndrome provide some of the strongest causal evidence for microbiome-disease relationships, with CDI cure rates reportedly approaching 90%
- The temporal stability of the healthy microbiome is high but can be disrupted by antibiotics, travel, and diet changes, with recovery requiring weeks to months

## Methods/Findings

- **Design**: Comprehensive review differentiating associative, correlative, and causal evidence linking microbiota to disease
- **Scope**: Covers IBD (Crohn's disease and ulcerative colitis), CRC, allergy/atopy, celiac disease, T1D, T2D, obesity, IBS, and CDI
- **Key framework**: Evidence is classified as (1) associative (altered composition), (2) correlative (specific functional correlations), or (3) causal (Koch's postulates or intervention-based)
- **Methodological cautions**: Significant heterogeneity in rRNA gene copies, bioinformatics databases, and analysis platforms means reproducibility requires standardization
