# Effects of Synbiotic Supplementation on Gut Microbiome and Clinical Outcomes in Parkinson's Disease

## Summary

This study investigates the effects of synbiotic supplementation on gut microbiome composition and clinical parameters in [[parkinsons-disease]] patients. The intervention combined probiotic strains with prebiotic fiber to target the PD-associated [[dysbiosis]].

## Key Findings

- Synbiotic supplementation shifted gut microbiome composition toward healthier profiles
- Abundances of beneficial bacteria including *Faecalibacterium* and *Roseburia* increased
- Pro-inflammatory Enterobacteriaceae decreased after synbiotic treatment
- Gastrointestinal symptoms, especially constipation, improved significantly
- Fecal [[short-chain-fatty-acids]] (butyrate, propionate) concentrations increased
- Systemic inflammatory markers showed decreasing trends
- Bowel movement frequency and stool consistency improved

## Relevance to Gut-Metal-Microbiome Axis

The reduction of Enterobacteriaceae (high-affinity [[iron]] scavengers with siderophore systems) and increase in SCFA-producing commensals represents a direct improvement in the gut metal-handling ecosystem. By suppressing organisms that aggressively compete for [[iron]] via enterobactin and aerobactin siderophores, synbiotics may reduce the selective pressure that drives [[iron]] piracy in the PD gut. The simultaneous increase in [[butyrate]]-producing taxa restores the metabolic framework for appropriate [[iron]] absorption regulation. This dual action -- suppressing metal-scavenging pathogens while rebuilding metal-regulatory commensals -- makes synbiotics a promising intervention for the gut-metal-microbiome axis in [[parkinsons-disease]].

## Limitations

- Moderate sample size
- Open-label design in some study arms
- Short intervention period
- Specific microbiome changes may vary with different synbiotic formulations
