## Key Findings

- **179 out of 288 measured metabolites** were significantly different between platinum-sensitive and platinum-resistant ovarian cancer cell lines, revealing a comprehensive metabolic rewiring in resistant cells ([[poisson-2015-metabolomic-platinum-resistance-ovarian-cancer|this study]], in-vitro).
- **Methionine degradation and cysteine biosynthesis** were the top canonical pathways differentially regulated, reflecting the central role of sulfur amino acid metabolism in platinum detoxification.
- **Glutathione synthesis** was significantly upregulated in platinum-resistant cells — glutathione directly conjugates platinum compounds, facilitating their inactivation and export from the cell.
- **Polyamine biosynthesis** was also significantly altered, suggesting changes in cell proliferation and survival signaling pathways.
- **KEGG pathway analysis** confirmed enrichment of pathways related to amino acid metabolism, lipid metabolism, and energy metabolism in resistant cells.

## Methods

- **Study type**: In vitro metabolomic profiling study.
- **Cell lines**: Platinum-sensitive and platinum-resistant ovarian cancer cell line pairs (isogenic models of acquired resistance).
- **Platform**: Global metabolomic profiling using mass spectrometry-based approaches; 288 metabolites quantified.
- **Analysis**: Pathway enrichment using KEGG and Ingenuity Pathway Analysis (IPA); canonical pathway identification; individual metabolite comparison between sensitive and resistant lines.

## Detailed Summary

This study used an unbiased metabolomic approach to identify metabolic differences between platinum-sensitive and platinum-resistant ovarian cancer cells. The scale of metabolic rewiring is striking — over 60% of all measured metabolites were significantly altered, indicating that platinum resistance is not a single-pathway phenomenon but a systemic metabolic reprogramming.

The centrality of sulfur amino acid metabolism (methionine and cysteine pathways) is mechanistically coherent. Platinum drugs (cisplatin, carboplatin) exert cytotoxicity by forming DNA adducts, but they also react avidly with sulfur-containing molecules. Glutathione (synthesized from cysteine, glutamate, and glycine) is the primary intracellular thiol that conjugates and neutralizes platinum compounds. Resistant cells upregulate the transsulfuration pathway (methionine to cysteine) and glutathione synthesis, effectively building a biochemical shield against platinum drugs.

This connects directly to WikiBiome's metallomics framework: platinum resistance is fundamentally a metal-handling problem. The cancer cell solves it by upregulating the same sulfur metabolism pathways that organisms use to handle toxic metal exposure more broadly. The cysteine-glutathione axis is also central to [[ferroptosis]] resistance — cells with high glutathione levels resist iron-dependent lipid peroxidation.

The polyamine pathway alteration adds another dimension. Polyamines (putrescine, spermidine, spermine) are involved in cell proliferation, and their biosynthesis requires S-adenosylmethionine — connecting back to the methionine pathway. This suggests a metabolic network where sulfur amino acid metabolism simultaneously serves platinum detoxification and pro-survival signaling.
