# No Evidence for Viral Etiology in CKDu in Sri Lanka

## Key Findings

- **No evidence** was found for viral etiology in symptomatic CKDu in Sri Lanka, despite comprehensive screening against a broad panel of known pathogenic viruses.
- **Serum screening** using VirScan technology (a phage display immunoprecipitation method that tests for antibodies against the entire human virome) was negative for CKDu-specific viral signatures.
- **Next-generation sequencing (NGS)** of kidney biopsy tissue showed no viral nucleic acid material, ruling out latent or low-level viral infections that might be missed by serology alone.
- Specifically, **Hantavirus, CMV, and EBV** — viruses previously hypothesized to contribute to CKDu — were unlikely causative agents based on the combined serological and molecular evidence.
- The exclusion of viral etiology narrows the search for CKDu causation toward **environmental factors** — heavy metals, agrochemicals, cyanotoxins, and potentially the gut microbiome.

## Methods

**Prospective observational cohort** of 151 symptomatic CKDu patients in CKDu-endemic areas of Sri Lanka. The study employed two complementary virological approaches: (1) **VirScan** — a high-throughput serological platform that screens for antibodies against peptides from over 1,000 viral species, enabling comprehensive virome-wide screening; and (2) **next-generation sequencing (NGS)** — performed on kidney biopsy specimens to detect viral nucleic acids directly in affected tissue. Serology for specific viruses (Hantavirus, CMV, EBV) was also performed. Comparisons were made against controls from non-endemic areas.

## Detailed Summary

Pushpakumara et al. conducted one of the most comprehensive assessments to date of the viral etiology hypothesis for CKDu in Sri Lanka. CKDu predominantly affects farming communities in the dry zone provinces, with prevalence rates that suggest an environmental rather than infectious etiology — but viral causes had not been definitively excluded.

The study employed VirScan, a sophisticated serological technology that can detect prior exposure to virtually all known human viruses in a single assay, coupled with tissue-level NGS to detect active or latent viral infections. This dual approach provides both historical (antibody-based) and current (nucleic acid-based) assessment of viral involvement.

The negative results across both platforms and multiple specific viral targets provide strong evidence against a viral etiology for CKDu. While it remains theoretically possible that an entirely novel virus not represented in VirScan's peptide library could be involved, this is unlikely given the breadth of coverage.

The importance of this study is primarily in what it excludes: by ruling out viral causation with high confidence, it focuses attention on environmental and toxicological etiologies — particularly chronic heavy metal exposure (cadmium, lead, arsenic from agrochemicals and water sources), cyanotoxin exposure from contaminated water, and potentially the gut microbiome as a mediator of environmental toxin effects on the kidney.

## Relevance to WikiBiome

This study is a critical **exclusion study** that strengthens the environmental/metallomics hypothesis for CKDu. By comprehensively ruling out viral etiologies, the study leaves environmental exposures — including heavy metals ([[cadmium]], [[lead]], [[arsenic]]) and waterborne toxins — as the primary candidates for CKDu causation. This is directly relevant to WikiBiome's core thesis that metal exposures drive disease through ecological disruption.

The study also indirectly supports the gut-kidney axis model for CKDu: if viruses are not the cause, then the pathogenic mechanisms likely involve chronic environmental exposures that alter both renal function directly and the gut microbiome (which further damages the kidneys through uremic toxin production and systemic inflammation).

## Open Questions

- With viral etiology excluded, what is the relative contribution of specific environmental exposures (cadmium vs. arsenic vs. agrochemicals vs. cyanotoxins) to CKDu?
- Does the gut microbiome of CKDu patients show distinctive features compared to CKD patients with known etiologies (diabetic, hypertensive)?
- Could chronic low-level multi-metal exposure alter the gut microbiome in ways that synergize with direct nephrotoxicity to produce CKDu?
