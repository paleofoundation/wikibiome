# Gut Mycobiome in Cardiometabolic Disease Progression: Current Evidence and Future Directions

## Summary

Comprehensive review consolidating evidence on the gut mycobiome's role in cardiometabolic disease (CMD) progression, including obesity, diabetes, atherosclerosis, hypertension, heart failure, and [[metabolic-syndrome]]. Covers fungal metabolites, cross-kingdom interactions, and therapeutic implications.

## Key Findings

### Obesity and Mycobiome
- *Candida*, *Saccharomyces cerevisiae*, *Penicillium*, *Rhodotorula mucilaginosa*, *Malassezia restricta* increased in obesity
- *Mortierellomycota* and *Mucor* spp. may confer protective roles
- *C. albicans* and *C. parapsilosis* contribute to obesity via elevated intestinal free fatty acids

### Diabetes and Mycobiome
- *Candida* consistently elevated in T2DM across multiple studies
- *C. dubliniensis* early-life exposure confers protection against diabetes
- *Aspergillus dublinensis* cell wall components induce islet-resident macrophage infiltration

### Atherosclerosis and Mycobiome
- *C. albicans* increased in CAD patients
- *Exophiala*, *Malassezia*, *Penicillium*, *Wallemia* increased in ACVD
- *Mucor racemosus* and *Fusarium* decreased in CHD; protective taxa
- *Candida*, *Thermomyces*, *Saccharomyces* may be key drivers of AS progression

### Hypertension and Mycobiome
- *Malassezia* significantly enriched in hypertensive cohorts, positively correlated with immunoglobulin light chains
- *Mortierella* prevalent in normotensive populations, suggesting protective function
- *Exophiala*, *Nakaseomyces*, *Saccharomyces* correlated with blood pressure

### Heart Failure and Mycobiome
- *Candida* spp. proliferation in CHF patients with elevated intestinal permeability
- Heightened inflammatory cytokines increase susceptibility to pathogenic fungal overgrowth
- *Saccharomyces boulardii* supplementation failed to improve cardiac function in GutHeart trial

## Relevance to Gut-Metal-Microbiome Axis

- [[cardiovascular-disease]]

1. **Cross-kingdom [[dysbiosis]]** - Heavy metals affect both bacterial and fungal communities; this review demonstrates that both kingdoms contribute to CMD progression
2. **Fungal metabolites** - Enzymatic derivatives, alcohols, and other fungal metabolites may interact with metal toxicity pathways to amplify [[oxidative-stress]] and [[inflammation]]
3. **[[Cadmium]] and fungal resistance** - Some fungi like *Candida* have metal-binding capabilities that could shift competitive dynamics in the gut under metal exposure
4. **Barrier disruption cascade** - Fungal overgrowth (especially *Candida*) can directly damage epithelial cells, compounding [[lead]] and [[cadmium]]-induced barrier disruption
