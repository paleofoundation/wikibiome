# Effect of Chronic Administration of Nickel on Affective and Cognitive Behavior in Male and Female Rats

Lamtai et al. (2018). *Brain Sci* 8:141.

## Overview

An experimental study examining the neurobehavioral effects of chronic low-dose [[nickel]] exposure in male and female Wistar rats, demonstrating dose-dependent increases in anxiety and depression-like behaviors, impaired spatial memory (males only), and hippocampal [[oxidative-stress]]. This was the first study to use doses as low as 0.25 mg/kg to model environmental trace-level exposure, establishing a foundation for the concept of [[nickel-neurotoxicity]].

## Study Design

- **Animals:** Adult Wistar rats, both male and female
- **Route:** Chronic intraperitoneal (IP) injection of nickel chloride (NiCl2)
- **Doses:** 0.25, 0.5, and 1.0 mg/kg body weight
- **Duration:** 8 weeks of daily administration
- **Behavioral tests:** Open Field Test (OFT), Elevated Plus Maze (EPM), Forced Swim Test (FST), Morris Water Maze (MWM)
- **Biochemical assays:** Hippocampal lipid peroxidation (TBARS), nitric oxide (NO), superoxide dismutase (SOD), catalase (CAT)

## Key Findings

### Anxiety

Nickel produced dose-dependent anxiogenic effects measured by two independent tests:

- **Open Field Test:** Decreased time spent in the central area (TCA), indicating increased anxiety. Effects observed at all three doses in both males and females
- **Elevated Plus Maze:** Decreased time spent in open arms (TOA), confirming the anxiogenic effect. Again dose-dependent and present in both sexes

### Depression

Nickel induced depression-like behavior as measured by the Forced Swim Test:

- Increased immobility time and decreased struggling/climbing time
- **Sex difference in sensitivity:** Males showed significant effects only at the highest dose (1 mg/kg), while females were affected at both 0.5 and 1 mg/kg, suggesting females may have a lower threshold for nickel-induced depressive behavior

### Spatial Memory

Morris Water Maze testing revealed impaired spatial learning and memory:

- **Males:** Significant impairment at 1 mg/kg only, with increased escape latency and reduced time in the target quadrant during probe trials
- **Females:** No significant memory impairment at any dose tested
- This sex difference suggests that the neural circuits underlying spatial memory may be differentially vulnerable to nickel, or that female-specific protective factors (possibly hormonal) exist

### Hippocampal Oxidative Stress

Biochemical analysis of hippocampal tissue revealed a consistent pattern of oxidative damage:

- **Lipid peroxidation (TBARS):** Dose-dependent increase, indicating membrane damage from reactive oxygen species
- **Nitric oxide (NO):** Dose-dependent increase, suggesting nitrosative stress and possible excitotoxicity
- **Superoxide dismutase (SOD):** Dose-dependent decrease in activity, indicating depletion of antioxidant defenses
- **Catalase (CAT):** Dose-dependent decrease in activity, further confirming overwhelmed antioxidant capacity

All four markers showed dose-response relationships, and the oxidative stress parameters correlated with the severity of behavioral deficits.

### Sex Differences Summary

- Males appeared more sensitive for anxiety measures and spatial memory impairment
- Females showed depression-like effects at lower doses than males
- Both sexes showed comparable hippocampal oxidative stress profiles
- The authors suggest these differences may reflect sex-specific neurotransmitter system vulnerabilities

## Proposed Mechanism

The authors propose the following mechanistic pathway:

1. Nickel crosses the blood-brain barrier and accumulates in the cerebral cortex and hippocampus
2. Ni2+ ions catalyze Fenton-like reactions and disrupt mitochondrial electron transport, generating reactive oxygen species
3. ROS overwhelm endogenous antioxidant defenses (SOD, CAT), leading to lipid peroxidation and protein oxidation
4. Oxidative damage to hippocampal neurons impairs synaptic function and neuronal survival
5. These cellular-level effects manifest as anxiety, depression, and cognitive deficits

### Neurotransmitter Interference

Nickel is known to interfere with multiple neurotransmitter systems relevant to the observed behavioral effects:

- **Dopamine:** Disrupted synthesis and release (relevant to motivation and reward)
- **Norepinephrine:** Altered levels (relevant to anxiety and arousal)
- **Serotonin (5-HT):** Decreased levels (relevant to depression and anxiety)
- **Acetylcholine:** Impaired cholinergic transmission (relevant to memory)
- **GABA:** Altered GABAergic function (relevant to anxiety)
- **NMDA receptors:** Nickel blocks NMDA receptor channels in a voltage-dependent manner, which could impair long-term potentiation and spatial memory formation

## Significance

This study is notable as the first to examine neurobehavioral effects at doses as low as 0.25 mg/kg, which the authors argue are more representative of environmental trace exposure levels than the higher doses used in most prior nickel neurotoxicity research. The finding that even the lowest dose produced measurable anxiogenic effects raises concerns about subclinical neurological impacts of chronic low-level nickel exposure in human populations.
