# Key Claims

- MS patients show markedly reduced colonic microbiome concentrations and diversity compared to healthy controls, with essential bacteria (Roseburia, Bacteroides, [[faecalibacterium-prausnitzii]]) consistently reduced
- A 6-month ketogenic diet restored total bacterial concentrations in MS patients to levels indistinguishable from healthy controls (83 vs 85.4 x 10^9 bacteria/ml, P=0.7)
- The microbiome recovery was biphasic: initial further decline at 2 weeks (similar to antibiotic effects), followed by gradual recovery exceeding baseline by weeks 23-24
- The ketogenic diet's microbiome restoration was fundamentally different from antibiotic recovery -- antibiotics cause pioneer bacterial overshoot while KD showed gradual essential bacteria recovery
- Colonic biofermentation function is markedly impaired in MS and was normalized by the ketogenic diet after 6 months

# Methods/Findings

- **Study type**: Prospective observational study; 25 MS patients + 14 healthy controls; 10 MS patients received ketogenic diet for 6 months
- **FISH quantification**: 162 ribosomal RNA-derived bacterial FISH probes; 35 substantial bacterial groups tracked
- **MS vs healthy** (Table 2): Total bacteria 65 vs 85.4 x10^9/ml (P<0.001); essential bacteria 24.6 vs 36.2 (P<0.001); F. prausnitzii 8.8 vs 11.6 (P<0.05)
- **KD timeline**: Week 2 -- bacterial concentrations dropped to 25.1 x10^9/ml; Week 12 -- 36.4; Week 23/24 -- 83 x10^9/ml (fully restored)
- **Essential bacteria recovery**: Roseburia, Bacteroides, F. prausnitzii all recovered to near-normal levels by 6 months
- **Pioneer bacteria**: Remained reduced throughout KD (P<0.05-0.008), indicating true microbiome restoration rather than dysbiotic overshoot
