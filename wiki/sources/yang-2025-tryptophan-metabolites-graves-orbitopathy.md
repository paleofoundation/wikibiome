# Tryptophan metabolites exert potential therapeutic activity in Graves' orbitopathy by ameliorating orbital fibroblasts inflammation and proliferation

## Key Findings

- **Tryptophan metabolism-associated gut flora** (phylum Firmicutes and genus [[anaerostipes]]) were significantly down-regulated in Graves' orbitopathy (GO) patients compared to healthy controls, as shown by 16S rRNA sequencing of 33 GO patients and 32 controls ([[yang-2025-tryptophan-metabolites-graves-orbitopathy]], in vitro + case-control)
- **Serum levels of three key indole metabolites were significantly decreased** in both GD and GO: indolepropionic acid (IPA), indole-3-lactate (ILA), and indoleacetic acid (IAA); IAA was further reduced in GO compared to GD alone, establishing a stepwise depletion pattern ([[yang-2025-tryptophan-metabolites-graves-orbitopathy]], in vitro + case-control)
- **IAA levels were negatively correlated with clinical activity score (CAS) and serum TRAb**, and active GO patients had significantly lower IAA than inactive GO patients, suggesting IAA as a potential biomarker for GO disease activity and progression ([[yang-2025-tryptophan-metabolites-graves-orbitopathy]], in vitro + case-control)
- **In vitro, IPA, ILA, and IAA suppressed TNF-alpha-induced inflammation and proliferation** in orbital fibroblasts isolated from GO patients, through inhibition of the Akt signaling pathway: IPA tested at 10, 50, 100 uM; ILA at 0.1, 0.5, 1 mM; IAA at 25, 100, 400 uM ([[yang-2025-tryptophan-metabolites-graves-orbitopathy]], in vitro)
- **KEGG pathway enrichment analysis** confirmed the tryptophan metabolic pathway was significantly enriched in both GO and GD groups, connecting gut microbial metabolic changes directly to orbital pathology ([[yang-2025-tryptophan-metabolites-graves-orbitopathy]], in vitro + case-control)

## Methods

- **Study type**: Case-control study with in vitro functional experiments
- **Clinical cohort**: Beijing Tongren Hospital, January 2020 - March 2022; 100 healthy volunteers (controls), 145 GD patients, 156 GO patients
- **16S rRNA sequencing substudy**: 33 patients with severe and active GO and 32 healthy volunteers; analyzed for tryptophan metabolism-associated gut flora
- **Serum metabolomics substudy**: March 2017 - March 2018; 16 healthy volunteers, 16 GD patients, 31 severe and active GO patients; targeted metabolomics for indole derivatives
- **In vitro experiments**: Orbital fibroblasts (OFs) isolated from GO patients during orbital decompression surgery; treated with TNF-alpha (20 ng/ml) to model inflammation; IPA, ILA, and IAA applied at multiple concentrations
- **Proliferation assay**: Cell Counting Kit-8 (CCK8) over 24 hours
- **Signaling analysis**: Akt pathway activation/inhibition assessed

## Detailed Summary

**Gut microbiota changes in GO.** 16S rRNA sequencing of 33 GO patients vs. 32 controls revealed that tryptophan metabolism-associated gut flora were significantly altered in GO. At the phylum level, Firmicutes was significantly decreased. At the genus level, Anaerostipes was significantly down-regulated. KEGG pathway enrichment confirmed the tryptophan metabolic pathway as significantly enriched in both GO and GD groups, indicating a functional shift in microbial tryptophan processing.

**Serum metabolite changes.** Targeted metabolomics identified three key indole metabolites — IPA, ILA, and IAA — as significantly decreased in both GD and GO patients compared to controls. Among these, IAA showed the most clinically relevant pattern: it was the most significantly decreased in GO; active GO patients had significantly lower IAA levels than inactive GO patients; IAA levels negatively correlated with CAS (clinical activity score) and serum TRAb levels. This establishes IAA as a potential biomarker for disease activity in GO.

**In vitro functional validation.** Orbital fibroblasts from GO patients were cultured and challenged with TNF-alpha (20 ng/ml) to model the inflammatory microenvironment. All three indole metabolites — IPA, ILA, and IAA — mitigated TNF-alpha-induced inflammation and proliferation in a dose-dependent manner. The anti-proliferative effect was quantified by CCK8 assay over 24 hours. Mechanistic analysis identified suppression of the Akt signaling pathway as the primary mechanism of action. This provides a direct functional link between gut microbiota-derived tryptophan metabolites and orbital tissue pathology.

**Tryptophan metabolism axis.** The study establishes a gut microbiota → tryptophan metabolites → orbital fibroblast inflammation axis in GO pathogenesis. Decreased Firmicutes and Anaerostipes → reduced IPA, ILA, IAA production → loss of Akt pathway suppression → unopposed TNF-alpha-driven orbital fibroblast inflammation and proliferation → GO progression.

## Contradictions / Tensions

- The in vitro concentrations used (IPA up to 100 uM, IAA up to 400 uM) may not be achievable through oral supplementation or dietary modification — translational relevance needs validation
- The 16S and metabolomics substudies used different patient cohorts from different time periods (2020-2022 vs. 2017-2018), potentially introducing confounders
- Akt pathway inhibition is the proposed mechanism, but other pathways (NF-kappaB, JAK/STAT) may also contribute and were not fully explored
- The study does not address whether the indole metabolite depletion is a cause or consequence of GO

## Open Questions

- Can oral IPA/ILA/IAA supplementation achieve therapeutically relevant serum concentrations in GO patients?
- Does IAA predict GO progression or response to existing treatments (corticosteroids, orbital radiation)?
- Which specific Anaerostipes species are responsible for indole metabolite production, and can they be delivered as a targeted probiotic?
- Does the tryptophan metabolite depletion precede GO onset, or is it a consequence of systemic inflammation?
- Is the Akt pathway the sole mediator, or do these indole metabolites also act through AhR (aryl hydrocarbon receptor) activation?
