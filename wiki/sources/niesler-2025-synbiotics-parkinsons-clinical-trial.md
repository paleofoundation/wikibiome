# Synbiotic Supplementation in Parkinson's Disease

## Summary

This randomized controlled trial evaluates the effects of synbiotic supplementation (combined [[probiotics]] and prebiotics) on gastrointestinal symptoms, microbiome composition, and inflammatory markers in [[parkinsons-disease]] patients.

## Key Findings

- Synbiotic supplementation significantly improved constipation severity scores in PD patients
- Gut microbiome alpha-diversity increased in the synbiotic group versus placebo
- SCFA-producing bacteria (Faecalibacterium, Roseburia) increased after synbiotic treatment
- Fecal [[butyrate]] levels increased significantly in the treatment group
- Inflammatory markers (calprotectin, hs-CRP) decreased with synbiotic supplementation
- Quality of life measures showed modest improvement
- The intervention was safe and well-tolerated over the study period

## Relevance to Gut-Metal-Microbiome Axis

The synbiotic approach directly addresses the [[butyrate]] deficit that disrupts [[iron]] homeostasis in PD. By combining probiotic organisms with prebiotic substrates, synbiotics ensure that transplanted bacteria have the nutritional support to produce [[short-chain-fatty-acids]]. The documented increase in fecal butyrate would restore HIF-2alpha signaling, normalizing [[iron]] absorption in the colon. The reduction in calprotectin indicates less neutrophil infiltration, which reduces lactoferrin-mediated [[iron]] redistribution in the gut mucosa. Synbiotics may represent a more practical, scalable alternative to [[fecal-microbiota-transplant]] for correcting the metal-microbiome imbalance in PD, with the advantage of standardized, reproducible formulations.

## Limitations

- Moderate sample size
- Relatively short treatment duration
- Motor symptom outcomes not primary endpoints
- Specific bacterial strains and prebiotic substrates may not be optimal for all PD patients
