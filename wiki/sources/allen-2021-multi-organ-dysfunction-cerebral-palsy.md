## Key Findings

- **Iron deficiency is highly prevalent in CP (13-48%)**, raising the critical question of whether this represents true deficiency or nutritional immunity (host-mediated sequestration).
- **Constipation affects 26-74% of CP patients**, making it one of the most common comorbidities and a major driver of quality-of-life impairment.
- **CP is a multi-organ condition**, not merely a neurological one: respiratory, cardiac, renal, and gastrointestinal systems are all affected, creating compounding dysfunction.
- **Vitamin D deficiency** is common due to reduced mobility, sun exposure, and anticonvulsant use — with implications for immune regulation and bone health.

## Relevance to WikiBiome

- Informs entity pages for [[cerebral-palsy]] and [[iron]], and concept pages on [[nutritional-immunity]].
- **Primitive 1** (metals as selective pressures): the high prevalence of iron deficiency in CP must be interpreted through the lens of whether iron supplementation would feed pathogenic iron-dependent organisms in an already disrupted gut.
- **Primitive 2** (nutritional immunity as interpretive constraint): iron deficiency in CP may be functional rather than absolute — hepcidin and inflammatory markers should be assessed before supplementation. This is a classic nutritional immunity question.

## Relevance to Cureva

- Critical for a cerebral palsy STOP page on iron supplementation: the 13-48% iron deficiency rate will drive clinical reflex to supplement, but if this is nutritional immunity, supplementation could fuel siderophore-producing pathogens.
- Constipation prevalence data supports prioritizing GI interventions in CP management.
- Multi-organ framing argues for a systems-level signature rather than isolated neurological treatment.

## Open Questions

- Does iron supplementation in CP children alter gut microbiome composition?
- Is there a relationship between constipation severity and iron status in CP?
