# Tryptamine Attenuates Experimental Multiple Sclerosis Through Activation of Aryl Hydrocarbon Receptor

## Summary

This study demonstrated that tryptamine, a microbially-produced monoamine alkaloid and AHR agonist, attenuates EAE through AHR-dependent suppression of encephalitogenic T cells, increased Treg induction, and promotion of butyrate production by the gut microbiota.

## Key Findings

### Tryptamine Ameliorates EAE
- Tryptamine treatment (12.5 mg/kg every 48h) significantly reduced paralysis symptoms in chronic progressive EAE mice
- Decreased cumulative disease scores and prevented body weight loss
- Reduced CD4+ T cell infiltration into the CNS
- Suppressed IL-17a secretion while maintaining IL-10 levels

### Immune Modulation
- Increased FoxP3+ Tregs in spleens of tryptamine-treated mice (both proportion and total number)
- Decreased ROR-gamma-t+ T cells (Th17 progenitors)
- In vitro, tryptamine inhibited proliferation of MOG-specific encephalitogenic CD4+ T cells and inhibited ROR-gamma-t expression

### AHR-Dependent Mechanism
- Tryptamine's effects were confirmed as AHR-dependent using Lck-Cre AHR-flox/flox mice (lacking AHR in T cells)
- Transfer of tryptamine-treated wild-type encephalitogenic T cells into naive mice caused marked attenuation of EAE
- Transfer of AHR-deficient T cells treated with tryptamine showed no reduction in encephalitogenic capacity
- Confirmed that tryptamine acts through AHR on T cells

### Gut Microbiome and Butyrate Production
- Tryptamine treatment altered the cecal microbiota composition (PCoA clustering)
- Increased *Dehalobacterium* and decreased *Bacteroidetes* and *Peptostreptococcaceae*
- **Significantly increased [[short-chain-fatty-acids]] content** in cecal content of tryptamine-treated mice
- The microbiota-modifying effect of tryptamine contributes to its therapeutic efficacy

### Tryptamine Production by Gut Bacteria
- Tryptamine is produced by commensal microorganisms, primarily *Clostridium sporogenes* and *Ruminococcus gnavus*
- Clinical studies show *Clostridium* species are decreased in MS patients
- Hindered tryptamine production in the GI tract may affect MS severity

## Relevance to Gut-Metal-Microbiome Axis

This study is highly significant for the [[gut-metal-microbiome]] model:

1. **Tryptamine is produced by metal-sensitive Clostridia** - [[cadmium]], [[lead]], and [[nickel]] exposure depletes these bacteria, removing a critical source of AHR-activating [[indoles]]
2. **AHR activation is protective in MS** - metal-induced loss of tryptamine production removes this protective pathway
3. **Dual mechanism** - tryptamine both directly suppresses encephalitogenic T cells AND promotes butyrate-producing bacteria, creating a positive feedback loop disrupted by metal exposure
4. **Connects [[dysbiosis]] to [[neuroinflammation]]** - demonstrates that a single microbial metabolite can bridge gut [[dysbiosis]] to CNS autoimmunity through the [[gut-brain-axis]]

## Limitations

- Mouse EAE model
- High-dose tryptamine administration may not reflect physiological levels
- Limited microbiome analysis (16S, not shotgun metagenomics)
