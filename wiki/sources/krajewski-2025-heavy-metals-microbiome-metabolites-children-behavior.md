# Heavy metals, noradrenaline/adrenaline ratio, and microbiome-associated hormone precursor metabolites

## Summary

This study investigates the associations between urinary heavy metal load (arsenic, cadmium, lead, mercury), microbiome-associated catecholamine precursor metabolites (phenylalanine, tyrosine, L-dopa derivatives), and the noradrenaline/adrenaline ratio with social behaviour, ADHD symptoms, and executive function in 87 primary school children. The findings link [[dysbiosis]]-related metabolic disruption with behavioral outcomes relevant to psychiatric conditions including schizophrenia.

## Key Findings

- Heavy metal load, microbiome-associated metabolites, and noradrenaline/adrenaline ratio accounted for 32% of variance in social behaviours
- Microbiome-associated metabolites predicted 11% of variance in executive functions and 17% in ADHD symptoms
- Executive functions shared 55% of variance with ADHD symptoms and 17% with social behaviours
- Children with the lowest social behaviours had a sixfold increase in odds of high heavy metal loads and 3.4-fold increase in odds of elevated microbiome-associated metabolites
- Arsenic (r = -0.43, p < 0.001) and cadmium (r = -0.38, p = 0.003) showed strongest negative correlations with social behaviour
- Microbiome-associated metabolites were positively correlated with heavy metal load (r = 0.33, p = 0.004)
- The gut microbiome can alter metabolic outcomes of [[heavy metals]], and heavy metals influence microbiome viability and metabolism

## Relevance to Gut-Metal-Microbiome Axis

This study provides direct evidence linking [[heavy metals]] exposure to disrupted microbiome-associated metabolite production in children, with downstream effects on behavior and cognition. The correlation between heavy metal burden and catecholamine precursor metabolites produced by gut bacteria supports the model where [[mis-metallation]] of microbial enzymes alters [[tryptophan-metabolism]] and catecholamine pathways. The findings are relevant to schizophrenia pathogenesis, as disrupted catecholamine and [[tryptophan-metabolism]] are central features of the disorder. Exposure to [[arsenic]], [[cadmium]], [[lead]], and [[mercury]] may contribute to [[dysbiosis]] and altered [[gut-brain-axis]] signaling, potentially increasing vulnerability to psychiatric disorders. The role of [[zinc]], [[copper]], and [[iron]] displacement by toxic metals in microbial enzyme function may underlie the observed metabolic disruptions.

## Limitations

- Cross-sectional design limits causal inference
- Small sample size (n = 87)
- Focused on children, not psychiatric populations
- Urine metabolites may not reflect gut mucosal concentrations
