# Key Claims

- Pooled analysis of 9 studies (1,168 PCOS patients and 1,106 controls) confirmed that **serum copper levels are significantly higher in PCOS** (SMD = 0.51, 95% CI [0.30, 0.72], p < 0.0001)
- Significant heterogeneity existed among studies (I2 = 78%, p < 0.01)
- When the one contradictory study (Kirmizi et al. 2020) was removed, heterogeneity dropped to I2 = 43% (p = 0.09) and SMD increased to 0.59 [0.45, 0.73]
- **Subgroup analysis by country** showed no significant discrepancy: China SMD = 0.55 [0.38, 0.73]; Western SMD = 0.63 [0.39, 0.87]
- No evidence of publication bias by Begg's and Egger's tests (p = 0.765)
- The association between elevated Cu and PCOS may operate through **ROS production** and reactive nitrogen species, causing damage to membrane lipids, proteins, and DNA
- Cu may have **estrogen-like activity**, potentially contributing to endocrine disruption in PCOS
- High Cu concentrations could contribute to the release of [[hyperandrogenism|luteinizing hormone]] and adrenocorticotropic hormone via pituitary effects

# Methods/Findings

- **Design**: Meta-analysis following PRISMA guidelines
- **Search**: PubMed, Google Scholar, Scopus up to June 30, 2020
- **Inclusion**: Studies with normal controls, available Cu concentration data, human observational cohorts on PCOS
- **Quality assessment**: Newcastle-Ottawa Scale; all 9 studies rated 7-9 stars (high quality)
- **Studies included**: 9 cross-sectional studies published 2012-2020 from Turkey, USA, India, Iran, China, Sudan
- **Statistical methods**: Random-effects model; SMDs with 95% CIs; I2 for heterogeneity; sensitivity analysis; Begg's rank correlation and Egger's regression for publication bias
- **All included studies**: Kurdoglu 2012, Celik 2013, Chakraborty 2013, Zheng 2015, Li 2016, Sharif 2017, Kanafchian 2019, Sun 2019, Kirmizi 2020
- **Sensitivity analysis**: Sequentially omitting each study confirmed robustness (SMDs ranged 0.46-0.59)
- **Limitations**: All included studies were cross-sectional; serum Cu measurement methods varied (atomic absorption vs ICP-MS); could not assess dose-response
