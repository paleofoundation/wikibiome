# The Faecal Metabolome and Mycobiome in Parkinson's Disease

## Summary

This study investigates both the faecal [[mycobiome]] and [[shotgun-metagenomics|metabolome]] in [[parkinsons-disease]] patients compared to healthy controls. By combining ITS sequencing for fungal profiling with untargeted metabolomics, the authors provide an integrated view of fungal community alterations and their metabolic consequences in PD.

## Key Findings

- PD patients showed significant alterations in gut fungal community composition compared to controls
- *[[candida-albicans]]* species were enriched in PD faecal samples, while *[[saccharomyces]]* was relatively depleted
- Metabolomic profiling revealed alterations in tryptophan metabolism, bile acid pathways, and [[short-chain-fatty-acids]] (SCFAs)
- Fungal dysbiosis correlated with specific metabolite signatures, suggesting functional consequences of mycobiome shifts
- The faecal metabolome distinguished PD patients from controls with reasonable accuracy
- Integration of mycobiome and metabolome data revealed coordinated fungal-metabolic disruption patterns

## Relevance to Gut-Metal-Microbiome Axis

This study bridges the gap between [[mycobiome]] composition and functional [[metabolomics]] in [[parkinsons-disease]]. The enrichment of *Candida* -- a genus with extensive metal-dependent virulence mechanisms including [[iron]] acquisition via siderophores and [[zinc]]-dependent secreted aspartyl proteases -- connects fungal [[dysbiosis]] to the metallomic framework. Altered [[bile-acid-metabolism]] metabolism affects metal solubility and absorption in the gut, creating feedback loops between fungal overgrowth and metal [[dyshomeostasis]].

## Limitations

- Cross-sectional design -- cannot determine causality
- Relatively small sample size
- Author Accepted Manuscript -- may differ from final published version
- ITS sequencing provides limited species-level resolution for some fungal taxa
