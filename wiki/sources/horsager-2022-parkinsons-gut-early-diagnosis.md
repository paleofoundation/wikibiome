# Parkinson's Disease and the Gut: Future Perspectives for Early Diagnosis

## Summary

This review examines future perspectives for early [[parkinsons-disease]] diagnosis through the [[gut-brain-axis]], focusing on the body-first vs. brain-first PD subtypes and how gut-based biomarkers could enable prodromal detection.

## Key Findings

- PD may have two distinct subtypes: "body-first" (gut-origin) and "brain-first" (olfactory bulb/amygdala origin)
- Body-first PD begins with [[alpha-synuclein]] aggregation in the enteric nervous system, preceded by gut dysfunction
- Body-first PD shows prodromal constipation, REM sleep behavior disorder, and autonomic dysfunction
- Gut-based biomarkers (microbiome composition, fecal alpha-synuclein, calprotectin) could enable early detection
- Colonic biopsies can detect alpha-synuclein pathology years before motor symptom onset
- The "body-first" subtype may represent the majority (>60%) of PD cases

## Relevance to Gut-Metal-Microbiome Axis

The body-first PD subtype aligns perfectly with the metallomic-microbiome framework. If PD originates in the gut, then gut metal [[dyshomeostasis]] and [[dysbiosis]] represent the earliest pathogenic events -- preceding even alpha-synuclein aggregation. This implies that metallomic-microbiome biomarkers could detect PD risk even earlier than alpha-synuclein-based tests. Fecal [[calprotectin]] (already proposed as a gut biomarker) is itself a zinc/manganese-sequestering protein of [[nutritional-immunity]], directly connecting the gut immune-metal response to PD pathogenesis. Combining fecal metal profiles with microbiome signatures and alpha-synuclein could create a multi-layered early detection panel.

## Limitations

- The body-first/brain-first distinction is still debated
- Prodromal biomarkers have not been prospectively validated at scale
- Body-first PD may not fully explain sporadic PD
- Gut-based diagnostic procedures may have limited patient acceptance
