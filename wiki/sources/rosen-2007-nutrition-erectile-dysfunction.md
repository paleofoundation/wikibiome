## Key Findings

- **Over 90% of surveyed men had unhealthy diets** according to the study's Nutrition Index (max 150 points; healthy threshold = 99), with an average score of 75.78 (classified as "very unhealthy"). Only 9.2% of men met the healthy diet threshold.
- **Men with very unhealthy diets had more erectile dysfunction** than those with merely unhealthy diets: 28.0% occasional ED + 15.4% consistent ED in the very unhealthy group versus 16.2% occasional + 8.1% consistent ED in the unhealthy group.
- **72.1% of men believed they ate a healthy diet**, despite the Nutrition Index indicating otherwise. Simultaneously, 85.7% wished to eat healthier and 62.2% acknowledged eating insufficient vegetables.
- **Higher BMI correlated with greater incidence and severity of ED.** The sample averaged BMI 27.3, with 72.5% classified as overweight or obese (versus 64.0% nationally at the time).
- **Strong associations existed between ED and comorbid conditions**: 60.8% of men diagnosed with cardiovascular disease had ED; 55.5% of those diagnosed with depression had ED. All respondents with 3+ diagnosed diseases had ED.
- **ED incidence rose sharply with age**: 19.0% of 30-39 year-olds had some ED, rising to 42.9% at 50-59, 63.6% at 60-69, and 87.5% at 70-79.
- **Lifestyle Index showed only a weak association with ED**, unlike the stronger Nutrition Index association.

## Methods

**Design:** Cross-sectional survey study using an internet-based self-administered questionnaire. The survey was developed by the author and included a modified version of the International Index of Erectile Function (IIEF) reduced from 15 to 7 questions.

**Population:** 251 males aged 30-78 (mean 47.7), recruited via convenience sampling through personal contacts and the Mankind Project email list. Predominantly Caucasian (91.6%), with 49.4% from Wisconsin.

**Three composite indices:**
1. **Nutrition Index** (max 150): Scored dietary patterns across eating out, beverages, grains, fruits/vegetables, dairy, main meals, sweets/snacks, and organic food consumption. Healthy threshold = 99.
2. **Lifestyle Index** (max 35): Scored sedentary behavior, exercise, smoking, alcohol, and drug use. Healthy threshold = 25.
3. **Erectile Function Index** (max 35): 7 IIEF-derived questions on erectile function, satisfaction, desire, and confidence. Adjusted for men on ED medications (n=6) and those with no sexual activity (n=8), yielding n=239 for ED analysis.

**Analysis:** Primarily descriptive statistics (frequencies, means, modes) and cross-tabulations. Data did not follow normal distribution, limiting advanced statistical procedures. Chi-square testing used where cell sizes permitted.

## Detailed Summary

This doctoral dissertation examined the relationship between dietary quality and erectile dysfunction through a comprehensive literature review and original survey data. The author hypothesized that (1) men with poor nutrition are more likely to suffer ED across all age groups, and (2) satisfactory erectile function can be restored through dietary changes and supplementation.

**Survey findings on diet quality:** The foods considered most nutritious -- whole grains, vegetables, fruits, fermented dairy, vegetable main meals, fish/seafood, and organic products -- had the lowest consumption patterns. Conversely, the least healthy options -- refined grains, processed foods, red meat, cheese, sweets, and salty snacks -- were dietary staples. Only 35.8% of men ate fruit daily. Whole grains were consumed less than once per week by the mode (36.7%). Processed/refined grains were consumed daily or more by 54.6% of respondents.

**ED prevalence in the sample:** After adjustment, 64.4% had no dysfunction, 22.6% had occasional ED, and 13.0% had consistent ED. This broadly aligned with the Massachusetts Male Aging Study (MMAS) finding of 52% prevalence between ages 40-70.

**Nutrition-ED cross-tabulation:** The very unhealthy diet group showed markedly higher ED rates than the unhealthy diet group (43.4% any ED vs. 24.3%). Age appeared independent of diet quality -- the Nutrition Index classification was evenly distributed across age groups, suggesting diet and age contribute independently to ED risk.

**BMI and ED:** Higher BMI consistently associated with greater incidence and severity of ED, consistent with the literature finding that men with BMI > 28.7 have 30% greater risk.

**Comorbidity patterns:** ED was identified as the second most commonly diagnosed condition after hypertension. The author emphasized ED as an early warning signal for cardiovascular disease, citing the Rancho Bernardo Study. The number of diagnosed diseases correlated directly with ED severity -- all men with 3+ diseases had ED.

**Physiological mechanism reviewed:** The dissertation reviewed the nitric oxide / cGMP pathway underlying erection. L-arginine conversion to nitric oxide via nitric oxide synthase, activation of guanylate cyclase, production of cGMP causing smooth muscle relaxation and blood inflow. PDE-5 inhibitors (Viagra, Cialis, Levitra) work by preventing cGMP breakdown but do not address underlying vascular causes.

**Literature review on dietary interventions:** The dissertation reviewed evidence on L-arginine, omega-3 fatty acids (DHA/EPA), antioxidants (vitamins C, E, selenium), B vitamins, zinc, DHEA, ginseng, Ginkgo biloba, Tribulus terrestris, and the Mediterranean diet pattern in relation to ED. It also reviewed herbal therapies and the Esposito et al. (2006) study finding that Mediterranean diet improved erectile function in men with metabolic syndrome.

## Contradictions / Tensions

- **No direct microbiome data.** This dissertation predates the gut-ED connection research and does not examine gut microbiome composition, TMAO, or microbial metabolites relevant to vascular health. More recent literature (post-2015) has identified gut dysbiosis patterns in ED.
- **Convenience sample bias acknowledged by author.** The sample skews Caucasian (91.6%), Wisconsin-based (49.4%), and likely higher income/education. Generalizability is limited.
- **Non-normal data distribution** prevented the use of ANOVA, regression, and other standard statistical tests. Findings are primarily descriptive cross-tabulations, not statistically controlled associations.
- **Unaccredited institution.** Clayton College of Natural Health lost its accreditation and closed in 2010. This affects the weight of this source relative to peer-reviewed publications. <!-- NEEDS VERIFICATION: verify Clayton College accreditation status -->
- **No blinding or control group** in the survey design. The proposed intervention study (Chapter 5) was not conducted within this dissertation.

## Open Questions

1. How would the Nutrition Index findings change with a nationally representative random sample and validated dietary assessment tool (e.g., 24-hour recall)?
2. What is the gut microbiome composition profile in men with ED who have poor versus healthy diets? Does the diet-ED association operate partly through microbiome-mediated mechanisms (TMAO, SCFAs, bile acid metabolism)?
3. Does the dietary intervention proposed in Chapter 5 (which was not executed) produce measurable improvements in ED when controlled for age, BMI, and comorbidities?
4. What is the role of specific metal exposures (lead, cadmium) in endothelial dysfunction contributing to ED -- an angle this dissertation does not address but which would connect to WikiBiome's metallomics framework?
5. Does the gut microbiome mediate the BMI-ED connection through inflammatory pathways and endotoxemia?
