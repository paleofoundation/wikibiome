# Dietary Composition Modulates Gut Microbiota in CKD

## Key Findings

- [[chronic-kidney-disease]] patients showed a **distinct shift in gut microbiota** with significant reduction of SCFA-producing bacteria compared to healthy controls, confirming the CKD dysbiosis signature.
- Patients consuming a **low-protein, high-fiber (LP-HF) diet** displayed greater abundance of SCFA-producing taxa and lower levels of inflammatory markers compared to those on high-protein diets.
- Patients on a **high-protein, low-fiber (HP-LF) diet** showed enrichment of **proteolytic genera including Klebsiella**, along with elevated TMAO, IL-18, and MCP-1 — markers of gut-derived inflammation and cardiovascular risk.
- The study demonstrates that **dietary macronutrient composition** directly modulates both the gut microbial community and the production of clinically relevant metabolites and cytokines in CKD patients.
- SCFA levels (particularly butyrate and propionate) correlated positively with SCFA-producing bacterial abundance and inversely with inflammatory markers.

## Methods

**Cross-sectional study** of 135 CKD patients and 19 healthy controls from Thailand. Gut microbiota composition was assessed by **16S rRNA gene sequencing**. Patients were categorized by dietary pattern (low-protein/high-fiber vs. high-protein/low-fiber) based on dietary recall and food frequency assessment. Serum TMAO, fecal SCFAs, and serum cytokines (IL-18, MCP-1, TNF-alpha) were measured. Statistical comparisons were made between dietary groups and between CKD patients and controls.

## Detailed Summary

Udomkarnjananun et al. examined how dietary macronutrient composition shapes the gut microbiome and related biomarkers in CKD patients, providing one of the more comprehensive assessments of the diet-microbiome-metabolite-inflammation axis in a CKD cohort.

The CKD-associated dysbiosis signature confirmed by this study — depletion of SCFA-producing bacteria — is consistent with findings from Chinese, European, and North American cohorts, suggesting a disease-driven (rather than population-specific) pattern.

The most clinically actionable finding was the differential effect of dietary patterns: LP-HF diets supported a more favorable microbiome composition (enriched in SCFA producers) and lower inflammatory markers, while HP-LF diets promoted expansion of proteolytic taxa, particularly Klebsiella. This is concerning because Klebsiella species are known opportunistic pathogens with robust siderophore systems for iron acquisition, LPS-driven inflammatory potential, and the capacity to produce uremic toxin precursors through protein fermentation.

The elevation of TMAO in HP-LF diet patients is directly relevant to cardiovascular risk — TMAO is an independent predictor of adverse cardiovascular events in CKD, and its gut microbial origin (from choline and carnitine metabolism) makes it a modifiable risk factor through dietary and microbiome interventions.

IL-18 and MCP-1 elevation in the HP-LF group connects the dietary-microbiome axis to systemic inflammation. IL-18 is a pro-inflammatory cytokine with established roles in renal fibrosis and CKD progression, while MCP-1 (monocyte chemoattractant protein-1) drives monocyte/macrophage recruitment to both the gut and the kidney.

## Relevance to WikiBiome

This study provides direct evidence that **dietary macronutrient composition** modulates the gut-kidney axis in CKD patients, with measurable effects on microbial community structure, metabolite production, and systemic inflammation. The enrichment of Klebsiella in HP-LF diet CKD patients is relevant to WikiBiome's metals framework — Klebsiella species express siderophore systems (enterobactin, aerobactin) and are iron-dependent organisms whose expansion in the CKD gut may be facilitated by both protein substrate availability and iron supplementation practices common in CKD management.

The study strengthens the case that dietary interventions targeting the gut microbiome could be a therapeutic strategy in CKD — supporting the two-sided ecological engineering approach.

## Open Questions

- Does switching from HP-LF to LP-HF diet in CKD patients reverse the Klebsiella enrichment and reduce TMAO levels?
- How does iron supplementation (common in CKD) interact with dietary protein intake to select for siderophore-producing Klebsiella?
- Can personalized dietary recommendations based on individual gut microbiome profiles optimize the LP-HF approach for each patient?
