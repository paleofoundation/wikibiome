# The Microbiome-Gut-Brain Axis in Multiple Sclerosis

## Key Claims

- The microbiome-gut-brain axis (MGBA) represents bidirectional communication between gut and CNS mediated by the immune system, enteric nervous system, vagus nerve, and gut microbiome
- Gut microbiota dysbiosis is implicated in [[multiple-sclerosis|MS]] onset and severity through multiple pathways: modulation of immune system, neurotransmitter production, gut barrier permeability, neuroendocrine (HPA) axis, and neural (vagal/ENS) signaling
- Key genetic risk factors: HLA-DRB1*15:01 (OR ~3 for MS susceptibility) vs HLA-A*02 (OR ~0.6, protective); non-MHC genes include IL2RA, CD58, CD226, CYP27B1, TNFRSF1A, IRF8, IL7R, TYK2
- Environmental risk factors converging on the MGBA include vitamin D deficiency, EBV infection, obesity (especially adolescent), smoking (dose-response), and gut microbiome dysbiosis
- MS gut microbiota shows decreased Bacteroidetes (*Bacteroides*, *Parabacteroides*), lower *Prevotella*, and increased Firmicutes (*Dorea*, *Blautia*) during relapse vs remission
- Gut mycobiome is underappreciated: MS patients show higher fungal alpha diversity, increased *Saccharomyces* and *Aspergillus*, anti-*Candida* CSF antibodies, and increased fungal-to-bacterial ratio
- EBV and HHV-6 both linked to MS development; HHV-6 reactivation during clinical relapses
- Helminths may be protective in MS through immune modulation

## Methods/Findings

- **Study type**: Narrative review (2023) from Institute of Molecular Genetics and Genetic Engineering, Belgrade
- **MS epidemiology**: 2.8 million people globally; 3:1 female-to-male ratio; peak onset ages 20-40
- **MS subtypes**: RRMS (most common), SPMS, PPMS, PRMS
- **Immune pathology**: Th1 cells (IFN-gamma) and Th17 cells (IL-17) are key drivers; CD8+ T cells correlate with axonal damage; CD27+ GM-CSF+ B cells drive abnormal Th1/Th17 responses; Tregs show decreased Foxp3 expression
- **Neurodegeneration**: Gray matter damage begins early; remyelination impaired by inhibitory molecules and insufficient oligodendrocyte precursor recruitment
- **Gut microbiota biomarkers**: Bacteria, fungi, parasites, and viruses all contribute; mostly focused on bacterial changes with limited attention to fungi and viruses
- **Metabolite pathways discussed**: SCFAs (anti-inflammatory), tryptophan/kynurenine (neurotoxic quinolinic acid), serotonin (5-HT), GABA
- **Limitations**: Narrative review without systematic methodology; primarily synthesizes existing literature
