# Tissue-Associated Bacterial Alterations in Rectal Carcinoma Patients Revealed by 16S rRNA Community Profiling

## Key Claims

- Rectal cancer tissue samples showed significantly higher species richness and diversity compared to non-cancer controls (Shannon index P = 0.002, Simpson P < 0.001), contradicting the common assumption that cancer reduces microbial diversity
- Enterotyping analysis classified samples into two distinct community types: Enterotype I (enriched for *Bacteroides*, *Clostridiales*, *Dorea*) was significantly enriched in rectal-cancer samples (P = 0.0001, Fisher's exact test)
- *Bacteroides fragilis* was among the taxa with the highest fold change between cancer and control groups, confirmed by digital droplet PCR (ddPCR) with R-squared = 0.78 correlation between sequencing and ddPCR results
- The phylum *Parcubacteria* (candidate phylum OD1) was detected in 14/18 rectal-cancer samples but only 1/19 non-cancer samples (P < 0.001), representing the first evidence for a possible role of this phylum in rectal cancer
- This was the first study to present evidence for tissue-associated bacteria specific to rectal (not combined colon) tumors, emphasizing the need to study anatomical regions separately

## Methods/Findings

- **Design**: Tissue-associated microbiota comparison between 18 rectal adenocarcinoma patients and 18 non-cancer controls
- **Participants**: Recruited from AC Camargo Cancer Center, Sao Paulo, Brazil; all rectal biopsies; no significant demographic differences between groups
- **Methods**: 16S rRNA V4-V5 amplicon sequencing on Ion Torrent PGM; QIIME analysis; enterotyping via Dirichlet multinomial mixture model; ddPCR validation for *B. fragilis*; immunohistochemistry confirmation
- **Alpha diversity**: 3222 OTUs total; cancer samples had higher OTU counts (280-366) vs. controls (236)
- **Key genera**: *Bacteroides*, *Phascolarcobacterium*, *Odoribacter*, *Parabacteroides*, *Desulfovibrio* more abundant in cancer; *Pseudomonas*, *Bacillus*, *Escherichia*, *Acinetobacter* more abundant in non-cancer
