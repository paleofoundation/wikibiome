# The Role of Statins in Breast and Colorectal Cancer Regulation

## Key Findings

- **Statins inhibit the mevalonate pathway** by blocking HMG-CoA reductase, reducing cholesterol biosynthesis and downstream isoprenoid production essential for cancer cell signaling ([[dang-2025-statins-breast-colorectal-cancer-regulation]], expert-opinion)
- **Multiple anticancer mechanisms identified**: inhibition of Ras/Rho prenylation, induction of apoptosis, cell cycle arrest, anti-angiogenic effects, and immune modulation
- **Lipophilic statins** (simvastatin, atorvastatin) demonstrate stronger anticancer activity compared to hydrophilic statins, likely due to enhanced cellular uptake
- **Combination therapies** with chemotherapy (e.g., cisplatin, doxorubicin) and immunotherapy (e.g., PD-1/PD-L1 inhibitors) show synergistic effects in preclinical and some clinical studies
- **Epidemiological evidence** suggests statin users have reduced incidence and improved outcomes in breast and colorectal cancers <!-- NEEDS VERIFICATION: specific risk reduction magnitudes -->

## Detailed Summary

This review examines the emerging evidence for statins as anticancer agents in breast and colorectal cancer. The mevalonate pathway, the primary target of statins, produces cholesterol and isoprenoid intermediates (farnesyl pyrophosphate, geranylgeranyl pyrophosphate) that are essential for post-translational modification of small GTPases (Ras, Rho, Rac) involved in cancer cell proliferation, migration, and survival. By inhibiting HMG-CoA reductase, statins deplete these intermediates and disrupt oncogenic signaling.

The review highlights that statins can induce ferroptosis in cancer cells by depleting CoQ10 (ubiquinone), a product of the mevalonate pathway that serves as an endogenous lipid peroxidation inhibitor. This connection to ferroptosis is mechanistically significant, as it links cholesterol metabolism to iron-dependent cell death pathways. Additionally, statins modulate the tumor immune microenvironment by affecting macrophage polarization and T-cell function, potentially enhancing immunotherapy efficacy.
