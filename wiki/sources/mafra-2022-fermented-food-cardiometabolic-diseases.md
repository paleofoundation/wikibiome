# Fermented Food and Cardiometabolic Diseases

## Key Findings

- **Fermented foods provide both live microorganisms and bioactive metabolites** that may benefit patients with cardiometabolic diseases including [[chronic-kidney-disease]], diabetes, and cardiovascular disease (Mafra et al. 2022, expert opinion)
- **Historical dietary patterns from the early Neolithic period included extensive fermentation**, suggesting that the human gut microbiome co-evolved with regular fermented food exposure that modern diets have largely eliminated
- **Fermented dairy, vegetables, and grains contain strain-specific probiotic organisms** and postbiotic metabolites (organic acids, bacteriocins, bioactive peptides) that modulate gut ecology and systemic inflammation
- **CKD patients may particularly benefit from fermented foods** because the uremic gut environment is characterized by loss of saccharolytic organisms and overgrowth of proteolytic organisms that fermented foods could help rebalance
- **The review argues for a return to fermentation-rich dietary patterns** as a microbiome-supportive strategy, contrasting with the processed food-dominated modern diet that promotes dysbiosis

## Methods

- **Design**: Expert opinion / narrative review
- **Published in**: Critical Reviews in Food Science and Nutrition (2022)
- **Scope**: Historical, evolutionary, and clinical perspectives on fermented food consumption and cardiometabolic health
- **Evidence base**: Synthesizes anthropological dietary data, microbiome research, and clinical studies of fermented food interventions
- **Disease focus**: Cardiometabolic diseases broadly, with specific attention to CKD, cardiovascular disease, and metabolic syndrome

## Detailed Summary

Mafra et al. take an evolutionary perspective on the relationship between fermented foods and human health, arguing that the dramatic reduction in fermented food consumption over the past century has contributed to the epidemic of cardiometabolic diseases. Early Neolithic diets relied heavily on fermentation for food preservation and processing, and the human gut microbiome likely adapted to this regular influx of dietary microorganisms and fermentation-derived metabolites. Modern industrialized diets have replaced fermented foods with processed alternatives that are sterile or contain only industrial starter cultures, depriving the gut of the microbial diversity that characterized ancestral diets. The review catalogs the mechanisms by which fermented foods support cardiometabolic health: live organisms from fermented products can transiently colonize the gut and compete with pathobionts; fermentation-derived organic acids (lactic acid, acetic acid) lower intestinal pH and inhibit urease-producing organisms relevant to CKD; bioactive peptides from fermented dairy have ACE-inhibitory effects relevant to hypertension; and fermentation can reduce anti-nutritional factors in grains and legumes, improving mineral bioavailability. For CKD specifically, the authors note that fermented foods could address both the [[dysbiosis]] (by introducing beneficial organisms) and the uremic toxin burden (by providing substrates that promote saccharolytic over proteolytic fermentation). The fermented food approach is distinguished from probiotic supplementation by the delivery of organisms in a food matrix with additional bioactive components.

## Open Questions

- Which specific fermented foods (kefir, kimchi, sauerkraut, tempeh) have the strongest evidence for cardiometabolic benefit in CKD patients?
- Does the food matrix of fermented products provide advantages over isolated probiotic supplementation for gut colonization and persistence?
- How do the potassium and phosphorus content of fermented foods interact with CKD dietary restrictions?
- Can fermented food consumption measurably reduce uremic toxin levels in CKD patients?
