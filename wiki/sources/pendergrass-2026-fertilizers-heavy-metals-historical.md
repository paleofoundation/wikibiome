## Summary

This historical integrative review synthesizes six decades (1960-2025) of evidence on heavy metal contamination from synthetic fertilizer production and use, focusing on cadmium, lead, chromium, nickel, and related metals in phosphate and urea fertilizers. Using longitudinal production data, regulatory records, and environmental monitoring studies, the review reconstructs temporal contamination trends from the Green Revolution through the modern regulatory era.

## Key Findings

- Phosphate fertilizers are the dominant source of cadmium in agricultural soils; cadmium concentrations peaked at 12-14 mg/kg in the 1990s
- Urea fertilizers contributed a distinct nickel burden linked to industrial scaling; nickel content peaked at 3.5-4.2 mg/kg in urea during the 1990s-2000s
- Nickel in urea fertilizers plays a unique dual role: it is an essential cofactor for the urease enzyme (catalyzing urea hydrolysis) while being toxic at elevated concentrations
- Optimal nickel concentrations (0.25-0.5 ppm) increase plant growth and yield, but higher levels (above 1-2 ppm) produce toxicity
- Cadmium residence time in soil is 20-40 years; lead persists 100-200 years
- Soil nickel concentrations in intensive urea-fertilizer regions reached 35-85 mg/kg vs. background of 20-30 mg/kg
- Post-2000 regulatory frameworks reduced metal concentrations in newly manufactured fertilizers, but legacy contamination persists
- South Asian agricultural systems (India, Bangladesh, Pakistan) face the highest contamination burden with 51-72% of soils moderately to highly contaminated

## Temporal Trends by Decade

- 1960s-1970s: Industrial expansion without regulation
- 1980s-1990s: Peak contamination recognition
- 2000s: Regulatory frameworks and monitoring programs emerge
- 2010s-2020s: Mitigation, alternative technologies, persistent legacy concerns

## Relevance to Wiki

Establishes that fertilizer-derived nickel and cadmium contamination of agricultural soils is a primary pathway for dietary heavy metal exposure, directly linking industrial agricultural practices to food chain contamination and human health risk.
