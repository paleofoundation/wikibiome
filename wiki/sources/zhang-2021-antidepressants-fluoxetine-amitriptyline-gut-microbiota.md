# Key Claims

- Both fluoxetine (SSRI) and amitriptyline (TCA) significantly altered overall gut microbiota composition in depression-model rats, as assessed by PERMANOVA of weighted/unweighted UniFrac distances
- At the phylum level, 6-week antidepressant treatment led to decreased Firmicutes/Bacteroidetes ratio due to enhanced Bacteroidetes and reduced Firmicutes; fluoxetine was more potent than amitriptyline at altering these ratios
- Both antidepressants significantly increased *Porphyromonadaceae* abundance; at the genus level, *Parabacteroides*, *Butyricimonas*, and *Alistipes* increased following both treatments
- Shotgun metagenomics revealed both antidepressants modulated metabolic pathways involved in carbohydrate metabolism, membrane transport, and signal transduction
- Both antidepressants affected antibiotic resistome genes including aminoglycoside (*aph3iiiA*), multidrug (*mdtK*, *mdtP*, *mdtH*, *mdtG*, *acrA*), and tetracycline (*tetM*) resistance in CUMS rats
- Amitriptyline enhanced Shannon diversity, while fluoxetine increased observed OTU richness; bacterial community structure in CUMS group clustered separately from healthy controls
- Fecal microbiota of CUMS rats showed *Bacteroidetes* were overrepresented while *Lachnospiraceae* was underrepresented compared to healthy controls, consistent with depression-associated dysbiosis

# Methods/Findings

- **Study type**: Animal model study (Sprague-Dawley rats) with 16S rRNA and shotgun metagenomic sequencing
- **Design**: 4 groups (HC, CUMS, Ami-treated, Flu-treated); 8-week CUMS induction followed by 6-week treatment
- **Methods**: 16S rRNA V3-V4 (Illumina HiSeq 2500); shotgun metagenomics (HiSeq 4000); KEGG pathway analysis; ARGs analysis
- **Behavioral outcomes**: Both antidepressants reversed sucrose preference, open field, and light/dark test deficits
