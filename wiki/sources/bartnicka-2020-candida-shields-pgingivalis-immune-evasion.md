# Candida albicans Shields the Periodontal Killer Porphyromonas gingivalis from Recognition by the Host Immune System and Supports the Bacterial Infection of Gingival Tissue

## Key Findings

- **C. albicans biofilm attenuates macrophage immune recognition of P. gingivalis**: THP-1 macrophages exposed to mixed-species (C. albicans + P. gingivalis) biofilm supernatants showed decreased cytokine and chemokine production compared to bacterial mono-species biofilm. The mixed biofilm reduced TNF-alpha production and nearly eliminated IL-8, while IL-1beta showed a 25-fold increase with mixed biofilm (vs. 4-fold lower with bacteria alone) — suggesting engagement of different IL-1beta release mechanisms (microvesicle/exosome-mediated, protected from gingipain degradation) [[bartnicka-2020-candida-shields-pgingivalis-immune-evasion]], animal-model.

- **Gingipain activity amplified by fungal co-culture**: Arginine-specific gingipain (Rgp) activity increased up to **130% under anoxic conditions and up to 10-fold under normoxic conditions** in the presence of C. albicans, compared to P. gingivalis mono-culture. The fungal biofilm both protects bacteria from immune detection AND enhances their virulence enzyme production [[bartnicka-2020-candida-shields-pgingivalis-immune-evasion]], animal-model.

- **In vivo mouse subcutaneous chamber model**: Pre-infection with C. albicans before P. gingivalis inoculation **reduced mouse mortality from 21% to 7%**. Bacterial persistence was dramatically enhanced — incidence of bacteria in chambers remained at **85% in dual-species infection vs. only 24% in mono-infection by day 4**. Fungal cell counts also increased during co-infection, suggesting mutual benefit [[bartnicka-2020-candida-shields-pgingivalis-immune-evasion]], animal-model.

- **Neutrophil enzyme activity suppressed**: Neutrophil elastase (NE) activity showed a 4-fold increase during mono-species bacterial infection but remained at baseline during dual-species infection — the fungal biofilm prevented the host from detecting and responding to the bacterial invasion [[bartnicka-2020-candida-shields-pgingivalis-immune-evasion]], animal-model.

- **Periodontitis patient fibroblasts showed reduced immune responsiveness**: Gingival fibroblasts from periodontitis patients (PP) produced 4-fold less IL-8 and 3-fold less [[interleukin-6|IL-6]] compared to healthy donor (HD) fibroblasts when challenged with C. albicans virulence factors. Prolonged P. gingivalis contact had already reduced the host cells' ability to recognize and respond to fungal infection [[bartnicka-2020-candida-shields-pgingivalis-immune-evasion]], animal-model.

- **Co-aggregation mechanism**: Mutual contact between organisms stabilized by specific binding between **fungal adhesin Als3 and the adhesive domain of gingipain RgpA**. The bacterial enzyme **PPAD (peptidylarginine deiminase)** modifies C. albicans surface proteins, facilitating adhesion. PPAD-deficient P. gingivalis mutants showed significantly reduced binding to fungal cells [[bartnicka-2020-candida-shields-pgingivalis-immune-evasion]], animal-model.

- **Systemic dissemination limited by local shielding**: C. albicans promoted local P. gingivalis infection while actually reducing systemic bacterial spread to spleen and kidney. The fungal biofilm creates a protected niche for chronic local persistence rather than acute systemic invasion [[bartnicka-2020-candida-shields-pgingivalis-immune-evasion]], animal-model.

## Methods

- **In vitro**: THP-1 monocyte-derived macrophages and gingival fibroblasts (from healthy donors and periodontitis patients) exposed to supernatants from mono- and dual-species biofilms. Cytokine quantification by ELISA. Gingipain activity measured by BAPNA assay. Gene expression by qRT-PCR.
- **In vivo**: Murine subcutaneous chamber model (C57BL/6 mice, n=8/group). Titanium chambers implanted; sequential infection with C. albicans (day -1) then P. gingivalis. CFU counts, neutrophil enzyme activity, and organ infiltration tracked over 96 hours.
- **Strains**: P. gingivalis W83 (wild-type); C. albicans ATCC 10231.

## Relevance to WikiBiome

- **[[functional-shielding]]** — The most mechanistically detailed study of C. albicans shielding a bacterial pathogen from immune recognition. Demonstrates that shielding is not passive co-occurrence but active immune subversion: the mixed biofilm simultaneously amplifies bacterial virulence (gingipain upregulation) while suppressing host detection (reduced neutrophil enzyme activity, attenuated cytokine production).
- **[[porphyromonas-gingivalis]]** — Gingipain activity is zinc-dependent; the amplification of Rgp in fungal co-culture means the zinc-dependent virulence axis is enhanced by interkingdom cooperation.
- **[[candida-albicans]]** — Als3 adhesin mediates specific binding to P. gingivalis RgpA; the fungus actively recruits the bacterium, not just passively sheltering it.
- **[[co-aggregation]]** — Als3-RgpA and PPAD-mediated binding provide the molecular basis for cross-kingdom co-aggregation.

## Relevance to Cureva

- **Primitive 6 (Interkingdom Relationships)**: Strongest in vivo evidence that C. albicans + P. gingivalis co-infection creates chronic persistent infection with reduced mortality but enhanced bacterial persistence — the "chronic disease" phenotype rather than the "acute infection" phenotype.
- **STOP signal**: Treating periodontitis with antibiotics alone without addressing the Candida biofilm scaffold will fail, because the bacterial partner is physically and immunologically protected. Sequential antifungal → antibiotic therapy is mechanistically justified.
- **Intervention hypothesis**: Disrupting Als3-RgpA co-aggregation (e.g., via Als3 peptide mimetics) could collapse the interkingdom biofilm and expose P. gingivalis to immune clearance.

## Contradictions / Tensions

- Small in vivo sample size (n=8 per group).
- Subcutaneous chamber model may not fully recapitulate the oral periodontal environment.
- The paradox of reduced mortality + increased persistence raises questions about clinical interpretation — is the mixed infection "better" or "worse" for the host?

## Open Questions

1. Does the Als3-RgpA co-aggregation axis operate in human periodontal pockets with the same efficiency as in vitro?
2. Can antifungal pre-treatment (fluconazole, nystatin) before scaling and root planing improve periodontitis outcomes by collapsing the Candida scaffold?
3. Does this shielding mechanism operate in other anatomical sites where C. albicans and P. gingivalis co-occur (e.g., esophagus, gut)?
4. What is the role of zinc availability in the gingival pocket in modulating gingipain amplification during co-culture?
