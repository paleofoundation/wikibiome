## Key Findings

- **Community state types (CST) with Lactobacillus dominance** (CST II-V) characterized successful pregnancies following artificial insemination, while all CST IV-B profiles (dominated by [[gardnerella]], [[prevotella]], [[atopobium]] rather than [[lactobacillus]]) resulted in implantation failure
- **Mediterranean diet adherence explained the greatest proportion of variation** in vaginal microbiota community structure, demonstrating a diet-vaginal microbiome connection
- **The diet-vaginal microbiota axis** represents a modifiable determinant of reproductive success that can be targeted through nutritional counseling
- **Lactobacillus species** produce lactic acid that maintains vaginal pH below 4.5, creating a hostile environment for pathogenic organisms and establishing conditions favorable for embryo implantation
- **Non-Lactobacillus dominant profiles (CST IV-B)** are associated with pro-inflammatory cytokine production, disrupted cervical mucus, and compromised endometrial receptivity

## Methods

- **Study design**: Prospective cohort study of women undergoing artificial insemination
- **Dietary assessment**: Mediterranean diet adherence scoring (likely via validated questionnaire such as PREDIMED or similar)
- **Microbiome assessment**: Vaginal microbiota profiling with community state type (CST) classification
- **Outcome**: Pregnancy success (implantation and clinical pregnancy) following artificial insemination
- **Statistical analysis**: Variance partitioning to determine dietary and microbiome contributions to reproductive outcomes

## Detailed Summary

This prospective cohort study investigates the relationship between dietary patterns, vaginal microbiota composition, and pregnancy success in women undergoing artificial insemination. The study bridges two fields -- nutritional science and reproductive microbiome research -- by demonstrating that dietary patterns shape vaginal microbial communities, which in turn influence reproductive outcomes.

The community state type (CST) classification system divides vaginal microbiomes into distinct profiles based on dominant organisms. CSTs dominated by different [[lactobacillus]] species (L. crispatus, L. gasseri, L. iners, L. jensenii) are generally associated with vaginal health, while CST IV-B (diverse non-Lactobacillus communities) is associated with bacterial vaginosis and adverse reproductive outcomes. This study found that all CST IV-B profiles failed to achieve pregnancy, while Lactobacillus-dominant profiles were associated with success.

The Mediterranean diet -- characterized by high intake of vegetables, fruits, legumes, nuts, whole grains, fish, and olive oil -- showed the strongest association with vaginal microbiota variation. This likely reflects the diet's anti-inflammatory properties, its provision of substrates that support Lactobacillus-favorable systemic metabolic states, and its micronutrient density (including zinc, selenium, and iron).

The mechanistic connection between diet and vaginal microbiota is not fully elucidated but may involve systemic metabolic and immune effects. Diet shapes gut microbiota, which modulates systemic inflammation and immune function, which in turn affects vaginal immune environment and microbial colonization resistance. Additionally, dietary glycogen and nutrient availability affect vaginal epithelial glycogen stores, which serve as substrate for Lactobacillus metabolism.
