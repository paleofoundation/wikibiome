# Migraine and gastroesophageal reflux disease: Disentangling the complex connection with depression as a mediator

## Key Claims

- Bidirectional [[Mendelian randomization]] revealed a positive causal effect of [[gerd]] on [[migraine]] (IVW: OR=1.49, 95% CI 1.34-1.66, p=3.70E-13), but migraine did not increase GERD risk (OR=1.07, P=0.1139)
- [[depression]] mediated 28.72% of GERD's effect on migraine, identifying it as a key mechanistic pathway
- The positive causal effect of GERD on migraine remained after adjusting for confounders including smoking, alcohol consumption, obesity, type 2 diabetes, and depression via multivariable MR
- GERD-induced esophageal inflammation can promote release of inflammatory mediators such as interleukins and tumor necrosis factor, which may affect the [[gut-brain axis]]
- Enteroendocrine cells in the gut can release [[neuropeptides]] including [[serotonin]], [[dopamine]], and gastrin, which affect neural transmission and regulation in the brain
- Clinical studies support the association: OR=1.55 (95%CI 1.45-1.66) for GERD among migraine patients in a retrospective study of 22,444 patients

## Methods/Findings

- **Study type**: Bidirectional two-sample MR with multivariable MR and mediation MR
- **Data sources**: GERD GWAS from meta-analysis (UK Biobank + QSKIN); migraine discovery from FinnGen; migraine validation from multi-ethnic GWAS; depression, smoking, alcohol, BMI, T2D from respective GWAS consortia
- **Methods**: IVW primary; MR-Egger, weighted median, leave-one-out; MVMR adjusting for smoking, alcohol, obesity, T2D, depression
- **Mediation analysis**: Depression as mediator between GERD and migraine; mediation proportion 28.72%
- **Reverse MR**: Migraine did not significantly increase GERD risk, establishing unidirectional causation
- **Sensitivity**: All results supported by Cochran's Q test, MR-Egger intercept, and MR-PRESSO; validated in independent dataset
