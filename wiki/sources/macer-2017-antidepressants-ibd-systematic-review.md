# Key Claims

- Twelve of fifteen studies reviewed suggested antidepressants have a positive impact on IBD disease course
- Nine studies reported anxiety and depression as outcomes; eight of these reported beneficial antidepressant effects
- Anxiety rates in IBD are 66.4% (active) vs 28.2% (inactive); depression rates 34.7% vs 19.9%
- 28.9% of IBD patients in one audit had used antidepressants at some point
- Evidence remains limited due to lack of large RCTs; further trials with objective IBD activity measures are needed

# Methods/Findings

- **Study type**: Systematic review; MEDLINE, EMBASE, Cochrane, CINAHL, AMED, PsycINFO, Open Grey searched from 1990 onwards
- **Included studies**: 15 eligible (1 RCT, 2 cohorts, 1 case-control, 1 cross-sectional, 1 qualitative, 2 audits, 1 case-series, 6 case reports)
- **Antidepressant classes**: SSRIs, TCAs, and others examined
- **Quality assessment**: Most studies at low risk of bias except case reports
- **Animal evidence**: Desipramine and fluoxetine reduced colitis risk and improved inflammatory markers

# Connections

- Antidepressants may modulate [[inflammation]] through serotonergic pathways in the gut-brain axis, relevant to [[crohns-disease]]
- Gut microbiota produce and respond to serotonin, connecting antidepressant effects to [[dysbiosis]] correction
- Depression and anxiety in IBD may partly reflect [[inflammation]]-driven neuropsychiatric effects
- Drug-microbiome interactions of antidepressants may affect [[gut-metal-microbiome]] through altered motility and secretion
