# Bengi 2025 — PMDD/PMS Comorbidity with Mood Disorders (Systematic Review & Meta-Analysis)

## Key Findings

- **42% (95%CI 30–55%)** of individuals with mood disorders have comorbid PMDD/PMS (MD diagnosed first, n=4,687) ([[bengi-2025-pmdd-pms-mood-disorder-comorbidity-meta-analysis]], systematic review/meta-analysis, 39 studies).
- **45% (95%CI 26–63%)** of PMDD/PMS populations have comorbid mood disorders (PMDD/PMS diagnosed first, n=9,451) ([[bengi-2025-pmdd-pms-mood-disorder-comorbidity-meta-analysis]]).
- Subgroup analyses: **PMDD with MDD 49%** (26–73%), **PMDD with BPD 39%** (24–57%), **PMDD with BDII 57%** (41–72%) ([[bengi-2025-pmdd-pms-mood-disorder-comorbidity-meta-analysis]]).
- Comorbidity estimates remained stable across sensitivity analyses excluding poor-quality studies.
- **High heterogeneity** (I²=94–99%) across all analyses — likely reflects variation in diagnostic criteria, sampling methods, and populations.
- Concurrent diagnostic sampling (both PMDD and MD assessed simultaneously): **42% (25–61%)** comorbidity rate.

## Methods

PRISMA systematic review and meta-analysis (PROSPERO CRD42021246796). Searched Embase, MEDLINE, PsycINFO from inception to January 2024. Included studies on women with PMDD/PMS and mood disorders (MDD, BPD, BDII). Random-effects pooled-prevalence meta-analyses via Comprehensive Meta-Analysis software. Risk of bias via NIH quality tools. Stratified by diagnostic order (MD first vs. PMDD first) and concurrent sampling.

## Detailed Summary

This is the **largest meta-analysis to date** on PMDD/PMS–mood disorder comorbidity. The ~42–49% bidirectional comorbidity rate is clinically transformative: it means nearly half of PMDD patients have a concurrent mood disorder, and nearly half of mood disorder patients have comorbid PMDD/PMS. This has direct implications for the shared neurobiological substrate — both conditions involve serotonergic dysfunction, neuroinflammation, and GABAergic sensitivity changes. The BDII finding (57%) is particularly striking and suggests PMDD may share more biological overlap with bipolar spectrum than unipolar depression.
