# Nickel Chelation Therapy as an Approach to Combat Multi-Drug Resistant Enteric Pathogens

## Key Findings

- **DMG inhibits growth of MDR Enterobacteriaceae**: Dimethylglyoxime (DMG), a nickel-specific chelator, has a **bacteriostatic effect** on MDR *Klebsiella pneumoniae* BAA-2472 (NDM-1 positive, carbapenem-resistant), MDR *Salmonella* Typhimurium 700408, and *S.* Typhimurium ATCC 14028 at millimolar concentrations (significant growth inhibition at 5-10 mM, P < 0.01). Bacteria are still detected at higher concentrations, confirming bacteriostatic rather than bactericidal activity [[benoit-2019-nickel-chelation-therapy-mdr-enteric-pathogens]], animal-model.

- **DMG abolishes Salmonella hydrogenase activity**: H₂-uptake [[nickel|NiFe-hydrogenase]] activity was completely abolished at 10 mM DMG. Addition of 50 µM NiCl₂ restored some hydrogenase activity at lower DMG concentrations, confirming the mechanism is nickel chelation. Hydrogenase is essential for *S.* Typhimurium colonization in murine models [[benoit-2019-nickel-chelation-therapy-mdr-enteric-pathogens]], animal-model.

- **DMG abolishes Klebsiella urease activity**: [[urease]] activity in MDR *K. pneumoniae* was significantly decreased at 1-2 mM DMG and **completely inhibited at 5 mM**. Urease is a key enzyme for nitrogen metabolism in *K. pneumoniae*; an isogenic urease mutant failed to colonize mouse intestines. This confirms that DMG-mediated Ni-chelation can efficiently block the in vivo colonization capacity of the pathogen [[benoit-2019-nickel-chelation-therapy-mdr-enteric-pathogens]], animal-model.

- **Oral DMG is non-toxic and orally bioavailable**: Mice receiving daily oral DMG doses (up to 6.08 mg/day, ~100 mM equivalent) for up to 6 days showed **no toxicity symptoms** — no changes in body weight, mobility, posture, or behavior. NMR confirmed DMG-specific signals in liver samples of orally treated mice, demonstrating intestinal absorption and systemic distribution. DMG is far less toxic than non-selective chelators (EDTA LD₅₀ ~400 mg/kg vs. DMG showing no toxicity at comparable doses) [[benoit-2019-nickel-chelation-therapy-mdr-enteric-pathogens]], animal-model.

- **Oral DMG reduces Salmonella virulence in vivo**: In the typhoid fever mouse model (100% mortality within 6 days without treatment), oral DMG for 7 days resulted in **37.5% survival** (n=8); 9-day treatment resulted in **50% survival** (n=4). Organ colonization (liver and spleen) was **10-fold lower** in DMG-treated mice (P < 0.01 for livers, P < 0.025 for spleens) [[benoit-2019-nickel-chelation-therapy-mdr-enteric-pathogens]], animal-model.

- **DMG reduces MDR pathogen virulence in Galleria mellonella**: Injection of DMG (250 mM) into wax moth larvae 5-10 minutes prior to MDR *K. pneumoniae* or MDR *S.* Typhimurium challenge led to **40% and 60% survival** respectively, compared to **100% mortality** without DMG [[benoit-2019-nickel-chelation-therapy-mdr-enteric-pathogens]], animal-model.

- **WHO priority pathogen coverage**: Among the 12 WHO MDR priority pathogens needing new antibiotics most urgently, **10 are urease-positive, 6 are hydrogenase-positive, and 4 have both enzymes**. Furthermore, 8 of 12 possess the Ni-containing glyoxalase I enzyme, and 5 contain the acireductone dioxygenase (another Ni enzyme). Thus DMG-mediated targeting of either or all Ni-requiring enzymes would be effective against the majority of WHO critical-priority MDR pathogens [[benoit-2019-nickel-chelation-therapy-mdr-enteric-pathogens]], animal-model.

- **Patent filed**: The in vivo use of DMG to inhibit pathogenic bacteria, including MDR strains, is protected by patent application #0235.000285US60.

## Methods

- **In vitro**: Growth inhibition assays (CFU/mL at 16h) for MDR K. pneumoniae BAA-2472, MDR S. Typhimurium 700408, and S. Typhimurium 14028 at DMG concentrations 0-12.5 mM. Hydrogenase activity via amperometric H₂ oxidation and methyl viologen spectrophotometry. Urease activity via phenol-hypochlorite NH₃ detection.
- **Toxicity**: BALB/c mice, oral DMG (0.1-200 mM solutions, ~3-6 mg/day) for 4-6 days. Weight monitoring, behavioral observation. NMR of liver extracts to confirm absorption.
- **Efficacy**: Typhoid fever mouse model (S. Typhimurium 14028 oral infection, 10⁸ CFU). DMG (3 mg oral) given 6h post-infection then daily for 7 or 9 days. Survival curves, organ colonization CFU counts.
- **Galleria mellonella**: Wax moth larvae injected with DMG (250 mM, 5 µL) into rear proleg, then MDR bacteria (5×10⁵ CFU) into left proleg. Survival monitored for 96h.

## Relevance to WikiBiome

This paper is **structurally essential** to WikiBiome's knowledge graph. It provides the first in vivo proof-of-concept that:

1. **Karen's Brain Primitive 4 (Metal Dependencies as Achilles' Heels) works in practice** — not just in theory. Nickel-dependent virulence enzymes (hydrogenase, urease) can be selectively disabled by a nickel-specific chelator, attenuating pathogen virulence without killing the organism.

2. **Selective chelation is real** — correcting the common misconception that chelation therapy is inherently non-selective. DMG binds nickel with high specificity (two DMG molecules coordinate one Ni²⁺ ion); it does not strip essential metals non-specifically like EDTA. This validates the [[dimethylglyoxime]] concept page.

3. **The approach works against MDR pathogens** — the entire raison d'être. When antibiotics fail (carbapenem-resistant Klebsiella, MDR Salmonella), metal-targeted interventions remain effective because metal dependency is not encoded on resistance plasmids. Pathogens cannot evolve around their need for nickel the way they evolve around antibiotic targets.

4. **Oral bioavailability + safety** — DMG can be delivered orally, absorbs into the liver, and is non-toxic at therapeutic doses. This is clinically translatable.

## Relevance to Cureva

- **Intervention — validated in animal model**: Oral DMG for enteric MDR infections. Triangle: I→f (DMG chelates Ni → disables hydrogenase/urease), I→D (reduces Salmonella mortality from 100% to 50%, 10-fold organ burden reduction), f→D (hydrogenase/urease are essential colonization factors).
- **Drug repurposing potential**: DMG is commercially available, inexpensive, and has industrial safety data (used for decades in nickel detection). Clinical trials in humans needed.
- **STOP signal for EDTA/non-selective chelation**: This paper explicitly contrasts DMG's safety with EDTA's toxicity — supporting the selective chelation paradigm over broad-spectrum metal stripping.
- **Combination potential**: DMG + antibiotic combinations could be synergistic — DMG weakens the pathogen's colonization machinery while the antibiotic targets growth. This could rescue antibiotics that have lost efficacy against MDR strains.

## Contradictions / Tensions

- DMG concentrations required for growth inhibition (5-10 mM) are relatively high — achieving these concentrations in the gut lumen via oral dosing needs further pharmacokinetic characterization.
- Bacteriostatic, not bactericidal — pathogen clearance depends on host immune function; may not work in severely immunocompromised patients.
- Mouse typhoid model uses high bacterial inoculum (10⁸ CFU) — the protective effect may be stronger at natural infection doses.
- No human safety or efficacy data yet.

## Open Questions

1. What is the optimal DMG dose and duration for human enteric infections?
2. Can DMG be combined with antibiotics to rescue resistant strains synergistically?
3. Does chronic DMG administration affect the commensal microbiome (most commensals are not nickel-dependent)?
4. Can DMG prevent NEC in preterm infants by targeting nickel-dependent Enterobacteriaceae (Klebsiella, Citrobacter, Enterobacter)?
5. Does DMG work against H. pylori urease in the gastric environment?
6. Can the approach be extended to other selective metal chelators targeting different Achilles' heel metals (zinc chelators for gingipain-dependent pathogens, iron chelators for siderophore-dependent pathogens)?
