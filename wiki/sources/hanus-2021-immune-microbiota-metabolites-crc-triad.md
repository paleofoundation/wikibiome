# Immune System, Microbiota, and Microbial Metabolites: The Unresolved Triad in Colorectal Cancer Microenvironment

## Key Claims

- The tumor microenvironment in CRC involves a complex triad of immune cells, microbiota, and microbial metabolites, with butyrate exerting dual roles: serving as an energy source in normal colonocytes (Krebs cycle) but acting as an HDAC inhibitor in tumor colonocytes due to the Warburg effect (anaerobic glycolysis)
- pks+ *E. coli* strains synthesize colibactin toxin that induces double-stranded DNA breaks and single-base substitution, deletion, and insertion mutations, directly contributing to genomic instability in CRC
- *F. nucleatum* virulence involves FadA (E-cadherin disruption, beta-catenin activation), Fap2 (NK cell inhibition via TIGIT receptor, Gal-GalNAc binding to tumor cells), and LPS-TLR4 interaction activating autophagy and chemotherapy resistance
- Table 1 comprehensively catalogs bacteria involved in colorectal carcinogenesis: *S. gallolyticus*, enterotoxigenic *B. fragilis* (ETBF), pks+ *E. coli*, *F. nucleatum*, *Peptostreptococcus anaerobius*, and *Enterococcus faecalis* with their specific pro-tumor mechanisms
- Polyamines (putrescine, spermidine, spermine, cadaverine) have dual roles in CRC: promoting tumor cell proliferation and immune suppression at high concentrations, while spermidine-modified pullulan reduces immunosuppressive TME

## Methods/Findings

- **Design**: Comprehensive review integrating immune system, microbiota, and metabolite interactions in CRC TME
- **Key metabolites reviewed**: SCFAs (butyrate, propionate, acetate) with receptor-specific effects (GPR41/FFAR3, GPR43/FFAR2, GPR109A/HCAR2); polyamines; colibactin
- **Immune mechanisms**: MHC I loss in CRC, NK cell evasion by *F. nucleatum* Fap2-TIGIT, MDSC/TAM recruitment, T cell exhaustion
- **Butyrate paradox**: Detailed mechanistic explanation of why butyrate promotes normal colonocyte proliferation but inhibits CRC cell growth through HDAC inhibition and p21/p27 upregulation (Figure 3)
