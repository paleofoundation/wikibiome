# Gut Microbiome and Its Role in Parkinson's Disease

## Summary

This review provides a comprehensive overview of the gut [[microbiome]] in [[parkinsons-disease]], synthesizing evidence for microbial involvement in PD pathogenesis through the [[gut-brain-axis]].

## Key Findings

- The gut microbiome is consistently altered in PD across multiple independent studies
- Core PD-associated changes: depleted *Prevotella*, *Faecalibacterium*; enriched *Akkermansia*, Enterobacteriaceae
- [[Alpha-synuclein]] pathology begins in the gut and spreads to the brain via the vagus nerve
- Vagotomy reduces PD risk, supporting the gut-origin hypothesis
- Gut microbiome composition affects levodopa metabolism and drug efficacy
- Multiple therapeutic strategies target the microbiome: probiotics, FMT, prebiotics, dietary interventions

## Relevance to Gut-Metal-Microbiome Axis

This review consolidates the evidence that gut [[dysbiosis]] is a consistent and early feature of [[parkinsons-disease]], supporting the metallomic-microbiome framework. The finding that gut [[microbiome]] composition affects levodopa metabolism is particularly relevant -- bacterial tyrosine decarboxylases (which are metal-dependent enzymes requiring pyridoxal phosphate and divalent cations) convert levodopa to dopamine in the gut before it reaches the brain. Metal [[dyshomeostasis]] in the gut could modulate this drug metabolism, potentially explaining variable levodopa response among patients.

## Limitations

- Review article synthesizing existing literature
- Rapid pace of research means some findings may be outdated
- Therapeutic strategies described are mostly in early stages
- Mechanistic understanding remains incomplete
