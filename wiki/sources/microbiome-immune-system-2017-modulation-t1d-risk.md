# Microbiome and Immune System — Modulation of T1D Risk

## Key Findings

- **Intestinal microbiome directly modulates immune system** through multiple pathways relevant to T1D, including SCFA-mediated Treg induction, TLR signaling, and metabolite-driven T-cell polarization (expert opinion, 2017).
- **Treg/Th17 balance** -- the critical immunological axis determining tolerance versus autoimmunity -- is shaped by gut microbiome composition, with specific taxa promoting tolerogenic (Treg) or inflammatory (Th17) programs.
- **Autoimmune tolerance breakdown** in T1D involves a cascade from dysbiosis through barrier dysfunction to immune activation against beta-cell antigens.
- **Microbiome-based immunomodulation** -- through probiotics, prebiotics, or dietary intervention -- could theoretically prevent or delay T1D onset by restoring immune balance before seroconversion.

## Detailed Summary

This review examines the mechanistic pathways by which the intestinal microbiome modulates immune system function in the context of T1D risk. The central thesis is that the Treg/Th17 balance -- which determines whether the immune system tolerates self-antigens or mounts an autoimmune attack -- is fundamentally shaped by the gut microbiome. The metallomic dimension is substantial: **[[zinc]]** is essential for thymic T-cell development and Treg function; **[[iron]]** availability influences T-cell polarization toward Th1/Th17 (pro-inflammatory) or Treg (tolerogenic) phenotypes; and **[[selenium]]** through selenoproteins regulates T-cell activation thresholds. Dysbiosis-driven malabsorption of these metals could therefore compound the immune dysregulation. The review positions the microbiome-immune axis as a modifiable risk factor in T1D, with a window of intervention during early immune education (consistent with Knip & Honkanen 2017).
