# Antioxidant Status in Relation to Heavy Metals Induced Oxidative Stress in PCOS

## Key Claims

- PCOS patients show significantly elevated serum levels of As, Cd, Pb, and Hg compared to controls (P < 0.001)
- Serum antioxidant markers (SOD and GSH) are significantly decreased in PCOS patients (P < 0.001)
- Strong negative correlations exist between heavy metals (As, Pb, Hg) and GSH levels (P < 0.01 for As, P < 0.05 for Pb, P < 0.01 for Hg)
- SOD was negatively correlated with As and Pb levels (P < 0.05)
- Heavy metals act as [[endocrine disruptors]] that can alter hormonal balance, menstrual cycle, ovulation, and fertility
- Significant positive intercorrelations among all four metals suggest co-exposure patterns
- Metal-induced [[oxidative-stress|oxidative stress]] is proposed as a paramount cause in PCOS aetiology

## Methods/Findings

- **Study design**: Prospective case-control study; 56 PCOS women and 50 controls, ages 19-35, Riyadh, Saudi Arabia (Oct 2018 - Dec 2020)
- **PCOS diagnosis**: Rotterdam criteria -- at least two of: oligo/amenorrhea, hyperandrogenism, polycystic ovaries
- **Metal analysis**: ICP-MS (Agilent 7700) for As, Cd, Pb, Hg in serum; detection range 0.05-100 ppb
- **PCOS group metal levels**: As 0.59 +/- 0.22, Cd 36.69 +/- 6.57, Pb 5.0 +/- 1.08, Hg 2.68 +/- 0.50 ppb (all significantly higher than controls)
- **Metabolic findings**: PCOS patients had significantly elevated FBS, HbA1c, TG, and LH; decreased SOD (9.30 vs 17.39 IU/ml) and GSH (6.24 vs 8.09 mg/ml)
- **Correlations**: Hg positively correlated with FBS (P < 0.001) and HbA1c (P < 0.05); Cd positively correlated with total cholesterol (P < 0.05)
- **Interelement correlations**: All metal pairs showed significant positive correlations (P < 0.001 for most), suggesting common environmental exposure sources
- **Limitations**: Did not evaluate residential environmental exposure; cross-sectional design limits causal inference
