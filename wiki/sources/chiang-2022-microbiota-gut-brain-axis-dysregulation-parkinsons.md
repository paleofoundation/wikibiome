# The Role of the Microbiota-Gut-Brain Axis and Intestinal Microbiome Dysregulation in Parkinson's Disease

## Summary

This review explores the microbiota-[[gut-brain-axis]] and intestinal microbiome dysregulation in [[parkinsons-disease]], integrating evidence from animal models and human studies. It covers the mechanisms by which gut [[dysbiosis]] may initiate or exacerbate PD pathology, including alpha-synuclein propagation, [[neuroinflammation]], and immune dysregulation.

## Key Findings

- Gut microbiome alterations in PD include enrichment of pro-inflammatory taxa and depletion of anti-inflammatory commensals
- Intestinal barrier dysfunction permits translocation of bacterial products (LPS, amyloid proteins) that trigger systemic inflammation
- Bacterial amyloid proteins (e.g., curli from *E. coli*) can cross-seed alpha-synuclein aggregation
- The vagus nerve serves as a conduit for pathological alpha-synuclein spread from gut to brain
- Gut-derived inflammatory cytokines access the brain through a compromised blood-brain barrier
- [[Probiotics]] and [[fecal-microbiota-transplant]] show potential as therapeutic interventions for PD
- Microbiome-targeted therapies could address prodromal GI symptoms and potentially slow neurodegeneration

## Relevance to Gut-Metal-Microbiome Axis

The bacterial amyloid cross-seeding mechanism is important for the metal-microbiome framework. Bacterial curli fibers from *E. coli* (enriched in PD gut) can template alpha-synuclein misfolding, and this process is accelerated by divalent metals like [[iron]] and [[manganese]]. The LPS translocation through a leaky gut activates [[hepcidin]] via IL-6 signaling, trapping [[iron]] in macrophages and potentially in microglia, promoting [[ferroptosis]]. The review's coverage of therapeutic interventions ([[probiotics]], [[fecal-microbiota-transplant]]) is relevant because these approaches could restore SCFA production and normalize [[iron]] homeostasis by rebuilding the [[butyrate]]-HIF-2alpha-iron regulatory axis.

## Limitations

- Review article without primary data
- Therapeutic evidence for microbiome interventions in PD remains preliminary
- Animal model findings may not fully translate to human PD
