# Drug Repurposing for Parkinson's Disease Through Microbiome Modulation

## Summary

This review examines the potential for repurposing existing drugs for [[parkinsons-disease]] based on their effects on the gut [[gut-microbiome|microbiome]]. It covers drugs originally developed for other conditions that show PD-relevant microbiome effects, including antibiotics, GLP-1 receptor agonists, metformin, and anti-inflammatory agents.

## Key Findings

- Several existing drugs have microbiome-modulating effects relevant to PD
- GLP-1 receptor agonists (used for diabetes) show neuroprotective effects and modify gut microbiome composition
- Metformin reshapes the gut microbiome and has epidemiological associations with reduced PD risk
- Specific antibiotics (rifaximin for SIBO) address secondary microbiome complications in PD
- Anti-inflammatory drugs (minocycline, NSAIDs) affect both neuroinflammation and gut microbiome
- Proton pump inhibitors, commonly used in PD patients, worsen dysbiosis
- Drug-microbiome interactions should be considered in PD treatment planning

## Relevance to Gut-Metal-Microbiome Axis

Drug repurposing for PD through the microbiome lens has direct implications for the metal axis. GLP-1 agonists regulate [[iron]] metabolism through their effects on hepcidin and pancreatic iron homeostasis. Metformin activates AMPK, which regulates [[iron]] regulatory protein activity and influences cellular [[iron]] handling. PPIs, by raising gastric pH, increase [[iron]] malabsorption while simultaneously promoting Enterobacteriaceae overgrowth -- a double hit to the metal-microbiome axis. Drug selection in PD should consider metal-microbiome impacts: avoiding PPIs where possible, considering GLP-1 agonists or metformin for diabetic PD patients, and using antibiotics judiciously to avoid further [[dysbiosis]] and disruption of metal-handling commensals.

## Limitations

- Most drug repurposing evidence is epidemiological or from preclinical models
- Microbiome effects of most drugs studied in non-PD populations
- Polypharmacy in PD patients creates complex drug-drug-microbiome interactions
- RCTs of repurposed drugs specifically for PD via microbiome mechanisms are lacking
