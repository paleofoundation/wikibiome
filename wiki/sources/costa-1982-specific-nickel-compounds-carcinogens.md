# Specific Nickel Compounds as Carcinogens

## Why This Paper Matters

This is a foundational 1982 paper by Max Costa -- one of the most influential researchers in nickel carcinogenesis -- that established the critical structure-activity relationships between specific nickel compounds and their carcinogenic potential. The key insight that **crystalline nickel sulfide (NiS) particles are selectively phagocytosed by cells based on their surface charge**, and that this phagocytosis is a **primary determinant of carcinogenic activity**, became a cornerstone of nickel carcinogenesis research for the next four decades. This paper introduced the concept that the physical form and solubility of [[nickel]] compounds determines their carcinogenic potency -- a principle that later informed IARC classification of insoluble nickel compounds as Group 1 carcinogens versus metallic nickel as Group 2B.

## Key Claims

- Epidemiological studies implicate nickel compounds in human respiratory cancers among nickel refinery workers
- The relationship between structure and carcinogenic activity is of fundamental importance: **metal compounds of low water solubility** that dissolve only in limited number of extracellular cationic species are the most potent carcinogens
- Crystalline NiS particles are considerably more toxic than amorphous NiS -- crystalline NiS induces morphological transformation at rates 100x higher (24% vs. essentially none for amorphous)
- **Phagocytosis is the primary determinant** of cell transformation by nickel particles
- Crystalline NiS has a negative surface charge and binds to positively-charged cell surfaces, facilitating phagocytosis; amorphous NiS has a positive charge and is repelled
- Water-soluble nickel compounds have very potent carcinogenic activity while others possess virtually no oncogenic activity
- NiCO3 and other very lipid soluble compounds readily enter cells, producing toxic and carcinogenic effects at relatively low concentrations
- Metallothionein is the most potent inducer of cell transformation; crystalline metals are the most potent inducers

## Methods/Findings

- **Study type**: Review with original experimental data on structure-activity relationships
- **Assay system**: In vitro morphological transformation assay using freshly isolated embryo fibroblast cultures incubated with test compounds, followed by quantification of morphological transformation (change from normal parallel arrays to disordered, cross-crossed growth patterns)

|----------|-----------------|-------------------|
| Crystalline NiS | 24% | 100% (relative to crystalline NiS standard) |
| Crystalline Ni3S2 | 22% | 115% |
| Amorphous NiS | 8% | 18% |
| Metallic Ni | -- | 48% |
| Amorphous CoS | 7% | 5.4% |
| Amorphous CuS | 4.5% | 5.5% |
| Latex beads | Nd | Nd |

- Crystalline metal sulfide compounds are the more potent inducers of morphological transformation
- Amorphous compounds have considerably less transforming activity (Table 1)
- Cells with a neoplastic state correlated with cells showing disordered growth; cells with normal growth pattern did not induce tumors in "nude" mice

### Surface Charge and Phagocytosis
- X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy revealed crystalline NiS differed from amorphous NiS in Ni/S ratio and sulfur oxidation state
- Crystalline NiS: negative surface charge (-27 mV Zeta potential), binds to positively-charged DEAE paper and cell membranes
- Amorphous NiS: positive surface charge (+9 mV Zeta potential), binds to negatively-charged phosphocellulose paper
- Chemical reduction of both forms with LiAlH4 to more negative state increased binding to DEAE paper and enhanced phagocytosis and transformation

### Intracellular Mechanisms
- Following phagocytosis, NiS particles undergo cytoplasmic dissolution as shown by video time-lapse microscopy
- Negatively-charged crystalline NiS particles bind irreversibly to cell membrane while positively-charged amorphous particles endocytose differently
- Particle dissolution may take ~2-3 days; mean particle half-life in cytoplasm is ~2-3 days
- Released nickel ions interact with DNA: decrease fidelity of DNA synthesis, promote crosslinking, induce single strand breaks, cause sister chromatid exchanges
- Ionic nickel may interact directly with phosphodiester backbone of DNA or compete for natural metal binding sites (Zn2+) in involved regulatory proteins
- Chronic exposure resulting in reduced nicotinamide can disrupt oxidative phosphorylation

### Metallothionein and Protection
- Protection against toxic and carcinogenic effects of heavy metals arises from metallothionein -- a 6800 mol wt protein containing 30% cysteine
- Metallothionein has extremely high affinity for a variety of toxic metals and is a natural chelator of Zn2+
- Certain metal ions such as Cd2+ are potent inducers of metallothionein
- Since nickel does not potently induce metallothionein, its carcinogenic effects may be greatly enhanced
