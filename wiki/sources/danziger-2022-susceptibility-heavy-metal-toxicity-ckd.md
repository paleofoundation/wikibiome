# Key Claims

- CKD patients have higher blood [[lead]] levels (+0.23 ug/dL) but simultaneously lower urinary lead excretion (-0.16 ng/ml), suggesting reduced elimination capacity
- CKD is associated with a 0.05 ug/dL higher adjusted blood lead and 0.02 ug/L higher cadmium per 10 ml/min per 1.73m2 lower eGFR
- Black race significantly modifies the association: 10 ml/min per 1.73m2 lower eGFR associated with 0.13 ug/dL more lead among Black participants vs 0.03 among White
- CKD may confer heightened susceptibility to even low levels of environmental metal exposure
- Blood [[cadmium]] was not significantly different with CKD status after adjustment
- Lower urinary lead excretion in CKD was consistent across racial strata

# Methods/Findings

- **Data**: NHANES 1999-2002 (primary); NHANES 2017-2020 (confirmatory); n = 5,638 participants with cystatin C measurements
- **CKD definition**: eGFR < 60 mL/min/1.73m2 by cystatin C (17%, n=989 had CKD)
- **Blood metals**: ICP-MS; mean Pb 2.3 ug/dL, Cd 0.41 ug/L
- CKD participants: higher blood Pb (2.1 vs 1.8 ug/dL) and Cd (0.65 vs 0.51 ug/L)
- Among 1,852 with urinary measures: CKD had higher blood but lower urinary lead levels
- Urinary lead/creatinine ratio significantly lower in CKD (-0.003, 95% CI -0.004 to -0.001)
- Black individuals: higher rates of iron deficiency, CKD, and lead susceptibility

# Connections

- Key concept: CKD creates a state of heightened susceptibility to [[lead]] toxicity by reducing excretion
- Racial disparities in [[lead]] susceptibility connect to [[environmental-metal-exposure|environmental justice]] and health equity
- Supports [[sabath-2012-renal-health-heavy-metal-nephrotoxicity]] on proximal tubule dysfunction reducing metal clearance
- Relevant to understanding reverse causality in cross-sectional metal-CKD studies
- Black individuals may have additional susceptibility via [[iron|iron deficiency]], vitamin D deficiency, and proximal tubular handling differences
