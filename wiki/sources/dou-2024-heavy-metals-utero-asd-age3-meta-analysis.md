## Key Findings

- **Prenatal cadmium concentration above detection level** during the first/second trimester was associated with 1.69 times higher ASD risk (95% CI: 1.08-2.64) and 1.29 times higher non-typical development (Non-TD) risk (95% CI: 0.95-1.75) ([[dou-2024-heavy-metals-utero-asd-age3-meta-analysis|this study]], prospective-cohort, n=401).
- **Doubling of cesium concentration** during the first/second trimester was marginally associated with 1.89 times higher ASD risk (95% CI: 0.94-3.80).
- **Third trimester cesium doubling** was associated with 1.69 times higher ASD risk (95% CI: 0.97-2.95), suggesting trimester-specific vulnerability windows.
- **Other metals examined** (arsenic, lead, mercury, manganese, selenium, tin, thallium) did not show consistent associations with ASD across both cohorts at the meta-analysis level.
- The enriched-risk design (siblings of children with ASD) increases the likelihood of detecting genetic and environmental contributions to ASD susceptibility.

## Methods

- **Design**: Meta-analysis of two prospective pregnancy cohorts — EARLI (Early Autism Risk Longitudinal Investigation, n=170) and MARBLES (Markers of Autism Risk in Babies — Learning Early Signs, n=231).
- **Population**: Pregnant women who already had a child diagnosed with ASD (enriched familial risk).
- **Exposure assessment**: Urinary metals measured by inductively coupled plasma mass spectrometry (ICP-MS) at two time points — first/second trimester and third trimester.
- **Metals measured**: Arsenic, cadmium, cesium, lead, manganese, mercury, selenium, thallium, tin (9 metals total).
- **Outcome classification**: Children assessed at age 3 using the Autism Diagnostic Observation Schedule (ADOS) and classified as ASD, Non-TD, or typically developing (TD).
- **Statistical approach**: Logistic regression with meta-analysis across the two cohorts using fixed-effects models; adjusted for maternal age, education, race/ethnicity, and child sex.

## Detailed Summary

This study represents one of the more methodologically rigorous examinations of prenatal metal exposure and ASD risk, leveraging two well-characterized enriched-risk pregnancy cohorts with prospective design, urinary biomarkers measured during pregnancy, and standardized developmental assessments at age 3.

The cadmium finding is biologically plausible given cadmium's known neurotoxicity, its ability to cross the placenta, and its documented effects on epigenetic regulation and oxidative stress — mechanisms implicated in ASD pathogenesis. Cadmium exposure occurs primarily through diet (leafy vegetables, grains, organ meats) and tobacco smoke, making it a modifiable risk factor.

The cesium finding is less expected and less established in the ASD literature. Cesium is a less-studied metal with limited prior evidence connecting it to neurodevelopment. The marginal significance suggests this finding needs replication.

The null findings for lead and mercury at the meta-analysis level are noteworthy, as these are the metals most commonly associated with neurodevelopmental harm. The authors note that the enriched-risk population may have had overall lower metal exposures due to health-conscious behaviors, potentially limiting the ability to detect effects at the lower end of the exposure distribution.

The evidence level is classified as prospective-cohort rather than systematic-review-meta-analysis because this is a meta-analysis of only two cohorts from the same research consortium, not a systematic review of all available evidence.

## Open Questions

- Whether the cesium finding replicates in larger, population-based cohorts.
- How prenatal cadmium exposure interacts with infant gut microbiome colonization — does in utero cadmium exposure alter the microbial environment the infant encounters?
- Whether cadmium's effects on ASD risk are mediated through epigenetic changes, oxidative stress, disrupted metal homeostasis, or direct neurotoxicity.
- Whether maternal cadmium body burden influences breast milk composition and postnatal microbial colonization.
