# Meta-analysis of the Age-Dependent Efficacy of Multiple Sclerosis Treatments

## Summary

This meta-analysis of 38 randomized, blinded clinical trials (>28,000 MS subjects) tests whether efficacy of immunomodulatory disease-modifying therapies (DMTs) on [[multiple-sclerosis]] disability progression is age-dependent. The key finding is that DMT efficacy strongly decreases with advancing age, with the model predicting zero efficacy beyond approximately age 53.

## Key Findings

- Efficacy of immunomodulatory DMTs on disability strongly decreased with advancing age (R-squared = 0.6757, p = 6.39e-09)
- The regression predicts zero efficacy beyond approximately age 53
- Inclusion of baseline EDSS did not significantly improve the model
- High-efficacy drugs outperform low-efficacy drugs in inhibiting disability only for patients younger than 40.5 years
- 13 categories of immunomodulatory drugs were included across 44 clinical trials

## Relevance to Gut-Metal-Microbiome Axis

The age-dependent decline in DMT efficacy has important implications for understanding MS through the [[gut-brain-axis]]. The aging [[gut-microbiome]] undergoes significant compositional shifts, with declining diversity and altered metabolite production. The progressive MS phenotype, which predominates in older patients and is resistant to immunomodulatory DMTs, may be driven more by neurodegeneration than by peripheral immune mechanisms -- suggesting that [[gut-microbiome]]-targeted therapies (which act through multiple pathways beyond immunomodulation) could be particularly valuable for older MS patients where conventional DMTs fail.

## Limitations

- Does not address microbiome or environmental factors
- Focused on disability progression, not relapse rates
- Individual patient data not available for most trials
