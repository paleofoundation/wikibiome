# Multiple Nutritional Factors and the Risk of Hashimoto's Thyroiditis

## Key Findings

- **Iodine**: Chronic excess induces autoimmune thyroiditis. Highly-iodinated thyroglobulin exposes neo-epitopes and is more immunogenic. Observational studies after universal salt iodization programs (China, Denmark, Italy) show transient HT incidence increases.
- **Selenium**: Essential for glutathione peroxidases (GPx1, GPx3, GPx4) that neutralize H2O2 generated by DUOX1/2 during thyroglobulin iodination. **Selenoprotein S** (SELENOS) genetic variants implicated in HT susceptibility via anti-inflammatory function. RCT evidence supports **selenomethionine 200 µg/day** reducing TPO-Ab, hypothyroidism progression, and postpartum thyroiditis incidence.
- **Iron**: TPO is a heme enzyme requiring iron for apical-membrane activation. Iron deficiency impairs T4/T3 synthesis. **Autoimmune gastritis** (atrophic, parietal-cell antibody positive) is a common HT comorbidity, impairing iron absorption. Iron + levothyroxine combination therapy more effective than either alone in iron-deficient women with hypothyroidism.
- **Vitamin D**: Inverse correlation between serum 25(OH)D and TPO/Tg antibodies in observational studies, but RCT evidence for supplementation effect on antibody titres remains weak. Low vitamin D status may be a *consequence* of autoimmune VDR dysfunction rather than a cause.

## Methods

Narrative review of PubMed and Cochrane Library through 2016 on iodine, iron, selenium, and vitamin D in relation to HT risk, pathogenesis, and treatment. 5,560 words; not a formal systematic review.

## Relevance to Cureva

- **Validated intervention**: Selenium (50–200 µg/day, preferably selenomethionine) in iodine-sufficient or iodine-excess regions — reduces TPO-Ab.
- **Validated intervention**: Iron replacement in iron-deficient HT patients — improves thyroid hormone levels and levothyroxine efficacy.
- **STOP candidate**: High-dose iodine supplementation (kelp, potassium iodide) in HT patients — may accelerate autoimmune progression.
- **Conditional intervention**: Vitamin D correction to >30 ng/mL in deficient HT patients — biological plausibility, weak clinical evidence.

## Open Questions

- Optimal selenium form (selenomethionine vs. yeast-bound vs. sodium selenite) for HT antibody reduction.
- Whether screening for autoimmune gastritis should be routine in newly-diagnosed HT.
- Whether gut microbiome mediates iodine–autoimmunity or iron–thyroid relationships.
