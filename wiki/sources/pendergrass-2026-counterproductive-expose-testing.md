## Summary

This preprint argues that the growing practice of unstructured public expose testing -- where independent actors privately test consumer food products for heavy metals and publicly disclose results by brand name -- is systematically counterproductive to reducing heavy metal contamination in the food supply. Drawing on compliance theory, game theory, chilling-effects research, and supply chain economics, the paper demonstrates that such testing creates perverse incentives that suppress transparency, discourage voluntary remediation, and concentrate reputational damage on brands that may be among the cleanest in their categories.

## Key Arguments

- Public expose testing lacks population-level benchmarking, measurement uncertainty disclosure, temporal/seasonal context, and distinction between detection and risk
- The chilling effect: brands that invest in testing and transparency become more vulnerable to expose actors, while brands generating no data cannot be targeted
- Supply chain misattribution: contamination originates from soil geochemistry, irrigation water, and agricultural land -- not from brand-level decisions
- Multiple brands source from the same supply pool, but expose testing targets only one
- Small and mid-sized brands investing in ISO 17025-accredited testing bear disproportionate reputational and legal costs
- Published test results serve as litigation scaffolding for plaintiffs' attorneys
- The expose cycle is self-reinforcing: testing generates data, publications trigger alarm, litigation imposes costs, brands reduce transparency, contamination persists
- Alternative framework proposed: population-level data infrastructure, supplier intelligence and collective action, confidential remediation pathways, measurement uncertainty and scientific rigor

## Relevance to Wiki

Critically examines the food safety disclosure landscape for heavy metals, arguing that structural approaches (certification programs, supply chain intelligence) are more effective than public shaming for actually driving contamination reduction in the food supply.
