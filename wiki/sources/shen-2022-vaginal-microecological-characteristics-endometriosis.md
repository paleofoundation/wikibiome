## Key Findings

- **Hormone-driven vaginal microbiome dynamics**: Estrogen and progesterone levels dictate vaginal microbiota composition across the female lifecycle. Estrogen promotes epithelial proliferation and glycogen storage; progesterone lyses epithelial cells to release glycogen. [[lactobacillus]] dominance depends on this glycogen availability and the resulting acidic pH (Shen et al., 2022, narrative review).
- **Iron as microbial nutrient during menstruation**: During menstruation, [[iron]] from damaged blood cells becomes a primary nutrient source for vaginal bacteria. [[streptococcus]] and [[gardnerella]] secrete iron chelator complexes to obtain iron deposited on the vaginal mucosa. Neutrophil gelatinase-associated lipocalin (NGAL) inhibits iron-dependent bacterial growth as part of host defense (Shen et al., 2022, citing Scholl et al., 2016; Witkin and Linhares, 2017).
- **Endometriosis microbial signature**: The microbiota of women with endometriosis (EMs) shows decreased [[lactobacillus]] dominance and increased numbers of bacteria associated with bacterial vaginosis and opportunistic pathogenic species, including [[gardnerella]], [[streptococcus]], and [[escherichia-coli]]. The cervical microbiota showed complete absence of the genus *Chironomus* in feces and significant decrease in genera *Gana* and *Ciliophora* (Shen et al., 2022, citing Ata et al., 2019).
- **Gut-reproductive tract axis in endometriosis**: Dysbiosis of gut flora disrupts immune function, increases pro-inflammatory cytokines, decreases immune surveillance, and creates a chronic inflammatory state conducive to increased adhesion and angiogenesis — driving EMs progression (Shen et al., 2022, citing Jiang et al., 2021; Ruan et al., 2020).
- **Beta-glucuronidase and estrogen recirculation**: Beta-glucuronidase produced by intestinal bacteria metabolizes estrogen, increasing the amount of estrogen delivered to the endometrium via the bloodstream, directly linking the [[estrobolome]] to endometriosis pathogenesis (Shen et al., 2022, citing the bacterial contamination hypothesis).

## Methods

This is a **narrative review** synthesizing national and international literature on vaginal microecology across physiological periods (puberty, menstruation, pregnancy, menopause) and pathological states (bacterial vaginosis, endometriosis, cervical cancer, ovarian cancer, endometrial cancer). The authors also summarize findings from the Human Microbiome Project (HMP) and its integrative phase (iHMP). No original data were generated; evidence is compiled from primary studies spanning case-control, cohort, cross-sectional, and animal model designs.

## Detailed Summary

### Physiological Periods

**Puberty**: In early adolescence, with low estrogen and progesterone, the vaginal microbiota is dominated by *Aspergillus*, *Bacteria*, and *Actinobacteria* with high alpha diversity. As sex hormones increase in late adolescence, [[lactobacillus]] (thick-walled bacteria) becomes dominant due to increased glycogen availability from estrogen-driven epithelial thickening.

**Menstruation**: The menstrual cycle creates a dynamic microbial environment. During the luteal phase (pre-menstruation), peak sex hormones drive glycogen accumulation. At menstruation onset, the endometrium sheds and menstrual blood enters the vagina, raising pH from the acidic range (~3.8–4.5) toward neutral (~7.2–7.4). This pH shift eliminates the protective effect of Lactobacillus-produced lactic acid. Critically, **iron in iron-containing heme from damaged blood cells becomes the primary nutrient source for bacteria** including [[streptococcus]] and [[gardnerella]], which secrete iron chelator complexes. The host counters with NGAL (lipocalin-2), which inhibits iron-dependent bacterial growth — a clear **nutritional immunity** response in the vaginal environment.

**Pregnancy**: Early pregnancy shows increased diversity with a new group of microbiota (Atopobium, Aerococcus, Gemella, Sneathia, Parvimonas, Gardnerella, Megasphaera). By mid-pregnancy, this diversity declines as [[lactobacillus]] re-dominates. Late pregnancy Lactobacillus stability inhibits harmful bacterial growth and reduces reproductive tract infections. Vaginal microbiota diversity is a significant predictor of preterm birth outcomes.

**Menopause (Climacteric)**: Reduced estrogen leads to decreased Lactobacillus and increased anaerobic bacteria (Bacteroides, Gardia vaginalis). Premenopausal and perimenopausal women share similar microbial profiles, while postmenopausal women show dominance of Aspergillus, Anaplasma, and Actinobacteria. Hormone replacement therapy (HRT) restores bacterial counts comparable to premenopausal women (Gliniewicz et al., 2019).

### Pathological States

**Bacterial Vaginosis (BV)**: The most common vaginal syndrome in women of childbearing age. Characterized by decreased or absent hydrogen peroxide-producing [[lactobacillus]] and increased anaerobic and pathogenic bacteria. BV-positive vaginal flora shows decreased Lactobacillus and high diversity of microbial complexes dominated by [[gardnerella]] and [[atopobium]].

**Endometriosis**: The review identifies multiple mechanisms linking the microbiome to EMs:
1. **Bacterial contamination theory**: Bacteria in the uterine environment cause endometriosis by refluxing lipopolysaccharides (LPS) into the peritoneal fluid and binding to pattern recognition receptors (PRRs).
2. **Estrobolome pathway**: Beta-glucuronidase from gut bacteria activates and binds estrogens, increasing estrogen delivery to endometrium.
3. **Gut dysbiosis → immune dysregulation → EMs progression**: Imbalanced gut flora leads to chronic inflammation, increased adhesion and angiogenesis.
4. **Stem cell theory**: Endometrial tissue contains stem cells that can migrate to ectopic sites via the bloodstream, promoted by microbial translocation.
5. The study by Ata et al. (2019) — the endobiota study — compared vaginal, cervical, and gut microbiome between women with stage III-IV endometriosis and healthy controls, finding increased pathogenic species ([[gardnerella]], [[streptococcus]], [[escherichia-coli]]) in the cervical microbiota of EMs patients.

**Cervical cancer**: Vaginal microecology with [[lactobacillus]] garciae or *L. iners* as dominant bacterium is associated with rapid HPV clearance. Low Lactobacillus and high [[atopobium]] abundance correlates with lowest HPV clearance rates.

**Endometrial cancer**: Associated with excessive estrogen use, obesity, and inflammation. Higher incidence in women with dysregulated vaginal microbiota. Enriched phyla include Thicket, Helicobacter, Actinomycetes, Bacteroides, and Anaplasma in endometrial cancer tissue.

**Ovarian cancer**: Potentially pathogenic intracellular microorganisms (Brucella, Mycoplasma, Chlamydia) found in 60–76% of ovarian tumors. Dysregulation of genital microbiota is a potential biomarker for ovarian cancer.

### Human Microbiome Project (HMP) Findings

The review summarizes HMP and iHMP findings relevant to the reproductive tract. The MOMS-PI study (part of HMP2) followed 1,527 pregnant women longitudinally, collecting 206,437 samples across multiple body sites. Key insight: classification of separate microbiome composition often does not correlate well with host phenotype — microbial molecular functions or personalized strain-specific analysis better predict outcomes.

## Contradictions / Tensions

- The review treats endometriosis microbiome data as broadly consistent, but the underlying primary studies (e.g., Ata et al., 2019) are small (n=34 in the endobiota study) and cross-sectional. The confidence level for the taxonomic signature should remain **preliminary** until larger cohorts confirm the pattern.
- The review describes the "bacterial contamination theory" of endometriosis as one of several hypotheses, alongside the estrobolome pathway and immune dysregulation. These are not mutually exclusive, but the relative contribution of each remains unresolved.
- The iron-during-menstruation mechanism is well-supported for vaginal ecology but has not been directly tested as a driver of endometriosis specifically — it is inferred from general vaginal microbiology.

## Open Questions

1. **Does cyclic menstrual iron availability directly drive expansion of pathogenic taxa in endometriotic lesions**, or is the iron effect limited to the vaginal environment?
2. **What is the directionality** of the gut-reproductive tract dysbiosis in endometriosis — does gut dysbiosis cause reproductive tract changes, or does retrograde menstruation seed gut dysbiosis?
3. **Can Lactobacillus-based probiotics or lactoferrin supplementation** reduce the cyclic pathogenic expansion during menstruation in endometriosis patients?
4. **How do the five community state types (CST I–V)** distribute across endometriosis patients compared to controls, and does CST type predict disease severity?
5. **Does HRT in postmenopausal women with a history of endometriosis** reactivate the estrobolome-driven disease pathway while restoring protective Lactobacillus dominance?
