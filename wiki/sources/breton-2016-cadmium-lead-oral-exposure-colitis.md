# Key Claims

- Subchronic oral exposure to [[cadmium]] (Cd) and [[lead]] (Pb) in drinking water can paradoxically mitigate symptoms of chemically induced colitis (DSS and TNBS models) in mice
- Short-term Cd exposure (1 week) exacerbates acute colitis, but subchronic (6-week) exposure has protective effects
- Cd reduces transepithelial electric resistance (TEER) in human intestinal cell lines in a concentration-dependent manner, while Pb does not significantly affect TEER
- Both metals modulate cytokine release from bacteria-stimulated PBMCs, with Cd reducing IL-10 and IL-12p70 at 68 uM
- Heavy metals may mediate immunosuppressive effects that could be either beneficial ("collateral benefit") or harmful depending on dose and duration

# Methods/Findings

- **Study type**: In vitro and in vivo experimental study
- **In vitro TEER**: Cd (20 and 68 uM CdCl2) reduced TEER in Caco-2 and T84 cells dose-dependently; higher dose abolished resistance; lower dose effects were reversible after washout; Pb (PbCl2 100 uM) did not affect TEER
- **PBMC immunomodulation**: Heavy metal salts alone did not modulate basal cytokine release, but during bacterial stimulation, Cd 68 uM significantly lowered IL-10 and IL-12p70; Pb 100 uM had smaller effects
- **Salmonella challenge**: Neither 1-week nor 6-week Cd or Pb exposure altered survival after oral Salmonella typhimurium infection
- **Short-term Cd + DSS**: 1-week Cd exposure worsened DSS-induced weight loss (p<0.01); high-dose (10 mg/kg) TNBS colitis also worsened
- **Subchronic Cd/Pb + DSS (6 weeks)**: No significant worsening of body weight loss or SAA levels; histologically, metal-exposed groups showed less epithelial damage; lower transcriptional activity of Il6, Il1b, Tnfa, Nos2, Cox2
- **Subchronic Cd/Pb + TNBS (6 weeks)**: Significant reduction in body weight loss, macroscopic and histological scores, and colonic inflammation; lower IL-6 and SAA; reduced inflammatory gene expression (Il6, Il1b, Nos2) and upregulation of metallothioneins (Mt1, Mt2) and Tgfb
- Cd induces metallothioneins in intestinal tissue, which may contribute to adaptive protective response
