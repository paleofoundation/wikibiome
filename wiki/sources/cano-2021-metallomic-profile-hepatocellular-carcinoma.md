# Metallomic Profile in Non-Cirrhotic Hepatocellular Carcinoma

Cano et al. (2021). *Scientific Reports* 11:14195.

## Overview

This study presents the first comparative analysis of liver metallomic profiles in hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC) and non-tumoral liver (NTL) tissue from patients with non-cirrhotic HCC (HCC-NC) across two geographically distinct cohorts: 38 Peruvian and 38 French patients. Twelve metals were quantified using ICP-MS: Mn, Fe, Cu, Co, Zn, As, Se, Rb, Mo, Cd, Pb, and Sn. The study reveals a striking phenomenon of metal depletion in tumoral tissue relative to non-tumoral tissue in both cohorts, despite dramatically different clinical and environmental contexts.

## Key Findings

### Cohort Differences
- **Peruvian cohort**: Mean age 40.6 years, 50% HBsAg+, larger tumors (14.3 cm mean), 90% HCC-NC
- **French cohort**: Mean age 67.5 years, 2.73% HBsAg+, smaller tumors (7.0 cm mean)
- Metal concentrations in NTL were significantly higher in Peruvian patients for 7 of 12 metals (Co, Mn, Cu, Zn, Rb, Se, Sn), likely reflecting environmental/dietary differences (Andean heavy metal contamination)

### Tumor Metal Depletion
- In both cohorts, metal concentrations were **lower in HCC compared to NTL** for the majority of analyzed elements
- This is the most important highlight -- cancer cells develop similar adaptive processes regarding metal metabolism regardless of geographic origin or etiological factors
- The single exception was **Cu**, which was slightly higher in HCC than NTL in the French cohort (median 12.35 vs NTL values), consistent with previous reports of Cu elevation in breast, cervical, ovarian, and lung cancers

### Copper Elevation in Tumors
- Cu increase in cancer explained by reduced catabolism of ceruloplasmin (Cp) in tumoral cells due to increased sialylation
- Cu acts as stabilizer of HIF-1a, mediating inhibition of prolyl-4-hydroxylation
- Cu serves as an angiogenic agent and promoter of endothelial cell migration

### Selenium and Survival
- Se concentration in tumor tissue was associated with extended survival **only in the Peruvian cohort** (p = 0.048)
- Peruvian patients with tumor Se above 1.48 ug/g had mean survival of 323 weeks vs 50 weeks for those below this threshold
- This relationship was not reproduced in the French cohort (p = 0.31), possibly because French patients received adjuvant treatments

### Iron Accumulation
- Fe was significantly higher in HCC of Peruvian patients (116.00 vs 216.95 ug/g in French), with both cohorts showing high absolute Fe levels
- Hepatic iron stores/siderosis previously linked to HCC-NC development

### Toxic Metal Exposure
- As and Cd elevated in NTL of Peruvian patients, consistent with Andean environmental contamination (Cd, Pb, As, Hg documented in Peruvian HCC blood samples)
- Cd-related injury involves thiol group interaction and inactivation of SOD family metalloenzymes

## Methods

- **Tissue**: Paraffin-embedded HCC and NTL samples from surgical hepatic resection
- **Measurement**: ICP-MS with collision cell technology (Thermo X Series II)
- **Elements**: 12 metals quantified; certified reference material (bovine liver ZC71001) for quality control
- **Statistics**: Mann-Whitney U-test, Kaplan-Meier survival analysis, multivariate Cox regression

## Limitations

- Cannot determine whether HCC-NC is directly caused by heavy metals vs. heavy metals serving as enhancing factors alongside HBV or alcohol
- Non-cirrhotic context limits generalizability to the majority of HCC cases that develop in cirrhotic liver
- Sample size (n = 38 per cohort) limits statistical power

## Relevance to Wiki

This paper provides direct evidence for the **tumor metal adaptation hypothesis** -- that cancer cells systematically modulate their metal metabolism, depleting most metals while selectively accumulating copper. The geographic comparison (Peru vs France) strengthens the finding by demonstrating this pattern is independent of environmental exposure levels, genetic background, or etiological factors. The selenium-survival association in the Peruvian cohort adds to the growing evidence for Se as a prognostic biomarker in cancer.
