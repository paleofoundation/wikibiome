# From Dysbiosis to Dyshomeostasis: Why Parkinson's Requires a Metallomic–Microbiome Lens

## Summary

This editorial, published as the inaugural issue of *Microbiome Medicine Journal* (Volume I, Issue 1, 2025), advances an integrative metallomic-microbiome framework for [[parkinsons-disease]]. It argues that many canonical features of PD — mitochondrial dysfunction, [[oxidative-stress]], neuroinflammation, [[alpha-synuclein]] aggregation, and gut [[dysbiosis]] — become more coherent when interpreted through the lens of metal dyshomeostasis, particularly [[iron]] mismanagement.

The central proposition: **metal dyshomeostasis initiates vulnerability, and the microbiome amplifies and operationalizes it.**

## Key Claims

- PD's major biological themes (mitochondrial dysfunction, oxidative stress, neuroinflammation, alpha-synuclein aggregation, gut dysbiosis) are too often treated as parallel explanations rather than coupled consequences of a shared upstream disturbance: metal dyshomeostasis
- Altered host metal handling increases labile metal pools, creating selection pressure for microbes capable of surviving and exploiting metal-rich, inflammatory environments
- [[nickel]]-dependent enzymes support microbial persistence under immune stress, while zinc-dependent metalloproteases contribute to barrier degradation and nutrient liberation from host tissues
- [[dysbiosis]] in this framework reflects functional enrichment for metal resistance and virulence, not a nonspecific imbalance
- Reframing dysbiosis as downstream of altered metal handling yields a testable, systems-level model linking [[ferroptosis]], barrier dysfunction, and neuroinflammation
- Iron-driven lipid peroxidation and [[ferroptosis]] susceptibility in dopaminergic neurons is a key domain connected through this metallomic lens
- Metal-induced protein misfolding and [[mis-metallation]] of antioxidant systems bridges the gap between metal exposure and neurodegeneration
- The paper also advances a complementary susceptibility hypothesis centered on pigment biology: neuromelanin's metal-binding capacity is finite and composition-dependent, and differences in eumelanin-like versus pheomelanin-like chemistry may influence metal sequestration efficiency and [[oxidative-stress]] burden
- Strategies should emphasize reducing labile metal pools, preventing microbial selection for virulence, preserving barrier integrity, and dampening neuroinflammatory amplification
- Metal speciation, microbial functional genes, barrier biomarkers, inflammatory readouts, and neuromelanin-sensitive imaging form a natural, integrated endpoint architecture

## Methods/Approach

- **Study type**: Editorial / integrative conceptual framework
- **Published in**: Microbiome Medicine Journal 2025, Volume I, Issue 1
- **Approach**: Synthesizes evidence across [[metallomics]], microbial ecology, neurobiology, and clinical strategy to propose a unified, falsifiable framework
- **Core framework**: Metal dyshomeostasis (especially iron mismanagement) as the initiating pressure that simultaneously destabilizes vulnerable neurons and selects for pathogenic microbial ecologies along the [[gut-brain-axis]]
