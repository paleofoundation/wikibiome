# Brain-Gut-Microbiome Interactions in Obesity and Food Addiction

## Key Findings

- **Systems biology of food addiction**: The brain-gut-microbiome (BGM) axis governs eating behavior through a balance between **homeostatic** (energy need-based) and **hedonic** (reward-based) mechanisms. Food addiction occurs when this balance shifts toward hedonic eating via disruptions at all three BGM levels [[gupta-2020-brain-gut-microbiome-obesity-food-addiction]], expert-opinion.

- **Microbiome drives hedonic eating**:
  - Microbial SCFAs stimulate enterochromaffin cells → GLP-1 and PYY release → satiety signaling. SCFA depletion from dysbiosis removes this brake.
  - Secondary bile acids (produced by microbial [[bile-salt-hydrolase]]) activate FXR and TGR5 → regulate insulin sensitivity and GLP-1 secretion. Antibiotic disruption of bile acid metabolism impairs insulin sensitivity within 1 week.
  - Metabolic [[endotoxemia]] from dysbiosis-driven LPS translocation promotes insulin resistance → hyperinsulinemia → increased hedonic drive for palatable foods.
  - *[[lactobacillus-plantarum]]* in nutrient-scarce environments promotes protein assimilation → branched-chain amino acid production → CNS TOR kinase activation → altered food-seeking behavior (demonstrated in *Drosophila*) [[gupta-2020-brain-gut-microbiome-obesity-food-addiction]].

- **Early life programming**: The gut microbiome and bidirectional BGM interactions are programmed through influences during pregnancy and the first 1,000 days of life. Early perturbations (antibiotics, stress, diet) at any BGM level can bias ingestive behavior toward hedonic-driven eating patterns that persist into adulthood [[gupta-2020-brain-gut-microbiome-obesity-food-addiction]].

- **Food addiction prevalence**: 25-37% of individuals with obesity meet criteria for food addiction (Yale Food Addiction Scale); up to 60% in those who are morbidly obese or post-bariatric surgery patients [[gupta-2020-brain-gut-microbiome-obesity-food-addiction]].
