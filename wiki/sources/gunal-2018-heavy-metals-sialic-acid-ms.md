# Effect of Heavy Metals and Sialic Acid in Multiple Sclerosis

## Summary

This case-control study investigated the effect of [[heavy-metals]] and sialic acid on [[multiple-sclerosis]] (MS). Fifty-two MS patients and 41 healthy controls from Kayseri, Turkey were enrolled. Fasting saliva and urine samples were analyzed for heavy metal and sialic acid levels.

## Key Findings

- Urine levels of Ag, Al, As, Ba, Cd, Cs, Cu, Mn, Ni, Pb, Rb, Sr, and sialic acid were significantly higher in MS patients compared to controls
- Titanium (Ti) and [[iron]] (Fe) levels were significantly lower in MS patients
- Univariate logistic regression showed high sialic acid levels (OR 14.23), [[nickel]] (OR 1.47), [[cadmium]] (OR 1.45), aluminum (OR 1.39), arsenic (OR 1.39), and [[lead]] (OR 1.32) were significant risk factors for MS
- Heavy metals were approximately 1.5 times more risky for MS
- Iron showed a protective association (OR 0.52, 95% CI 0.36-0.73), consistent with lower Fe in MS patients
- Poor bowel habits increased MS risk 4.76-fold, and less vegetable consumption increased risk 6.56-fold

## Mechanistic Insights

- Sialic acid elevation may reflect acute phase [[inflammation]] and metal-induced tissue damage
- Sialic acid binds toxic metals such as Cd and Pb with great affinity, and its elevation may indicate ongoing metal toxicity
- Fe is required for myelin production and [[oxidative-stress]] defense; its depletion in MS may relate to impaired myelination and increased [[ferroptosis]] vulnerability
- Heavy metals can disrupt the gut microbiome, impair intestinal microbial function, and promote [[dysbiosis]]
- The authors note that MS patients consumed fewer vegetables and had poorer bowel habits, suggesting [[gut-brain-axis]] involvement

## Relevance to Gut-Metal-Microbiome Axis

This study provides direct clinical evidence linking elevated urinary [[heavy-metals]] (especially [[nickel]], [[cadmium]], [[lead]]) to MS risk. The finding that poor bowel habits and low vegetable intake are independent MS risk factors supports the [[gut-metal-microbiome]] hypothesis, where metal exposure disrupts the intestinal microbiome, reduces protective [[short-chain-fatty-acids]] production from fiber, and promotes systemic [[inflammation]] through the [[gut-brain-axis]].

## Limitations

- Cross-sectional design cannot establish causality
- All patients were on interferon therapy, which may affect sialic acid and inflammatory markers
- Small sample size (n=93 total)
- CRP levels were not determined
