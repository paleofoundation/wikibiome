# Key Claims

- Intrauterine microbial colonization (IUMC) is significantly increased in [[endometriosis]], detectable by molecular methods even when bacterial culture fails
- *Lactobacillaceae* was significantly decreased (p<0.01) and *Streptococcaceae* significantly increased (p<0.05) in endometrial samples of endometriosis patients
- 16S rDNA metagenome assay detected significantly higher *Streptococcaceae* (p<0.01) and *Staphylococcaceae* (p<0.05) in ovarian endometrioma cystic fluid
- GnRHa treatment was associated with worsening of IUMC and higher occurrence of endometritis
- A wide proportion (0.01-97.83%) of multiple bacteria was detected in both endometrial swabs and cystic fluid

# Methods/Findings

- **Study design**: Case-control biological study at Nagasaki University Hospital
- **Participants**: 32 women with endometriosis and 32 controls, with GnRHa-treated and untreated subgroups
- **Methods**: 16S rDNA metagenome sequence analysis (Illumina MiSeq) and bacterial culture

 - 5 major families detected: Lactobacillaceae, Streptococcaceae, Staphylococcaceae, Enterobacteriaceae, Moraxellaceae
 - GnRHa treatment increased colony formation of *Gardnerella*, *E. coli*, and *Enterococci*
 - Bacterial culture failed to detect single colonies in cystic fluid despite molecular detection
 - Endometritis was higher in GnRHa-treated women (85.7% vs 68.4%, p=0.003)

# Relevance to Wiki

This study extends the [[bacterial-contamination-hypothesis]] by demonstrating that microbial colonization of the intrauterine environment is increased in [[endometriosis]] and is further worsened by GnRHa treatment. The molecular detection of bacteria in ovarian endometrioma cystic fluid suggests that microbes may contribute to the inflammatory milieu within endometriotic cysts. The shift from *Lactobacillaceae* to pathogenic *Streptococcaceae* and *Enterobacteriaceae* parallels the [[dysbiosis]] patterns seen in gut and vaginal niches of endometriosis patients.
