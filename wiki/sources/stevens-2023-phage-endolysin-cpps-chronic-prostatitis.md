## Key Findings

- **First successful human use of a bacteriophage endolysin to treat chronic bacterial prostatitis (CBP).** A 7-day course of rectally applied ORF28 endolysin, derived from *E. faecalis* phage ϕEf11, eliminated a chronic multidrug-resistant (MDR) [[enterococcus-faecalis]] prostate infection to below culturally detectable levels ([[stevens-2023-phage-endolysin-cpps-chronic-prostatitis]], case report, n=1).
- **NIH-CPSI symptom score dropped from 24 (severe) to 6 (mild)** — pain score fell from 7 to 0, urinary symptoms from 8 to 3, quality-of-life impact from 9 to 3. No recurrence was observed over 6 months of follow-up (August 2022 to February 2023) ([[stevens-2023-phage-endolysin-cpps-chronic-prostatitis]], case report).
- **Multiple prior therapies failed**, including repeated courses of ampicillin/sulbactam, moxifloxacin, linezolid, fosfomycin, a personalized three-phage cocktail (genus *Efquatrovirus*), probiotics (lactobacilli), phytopharmaceuticals (Serenoa repens, Urtica dioica, quercetin), and an autovaccine from the patient's own *E. faecalis* strain ([[stevens-2023-phage-endolysin-cpps-chronic-prostatitis]], case report).
- **The patient harbored an MBL2 gene polymorphism** (p.Arg52Cys, genotype A/D) causing mannose-binding lectin deficiency (MBL 375 ng/mL vs. normal >2,880 ng/mL), a predisposing immunological deficit for recurrent urogenital infections ([[stevens-2023-phage-endolysin-cpps-chronic-prostatitis]], case report).
- **When molecular methods (PCR) were applied to CPPS cases** previously classified as non-bacterial by culture, 16S rDNA was detected in prostate biopsies from 77% of cases, indicating most CPPS cases may in fact be CBP ([[stevens-2023-phage-endolysin-cpps-chronic-prostatitis]], case report, citing Krieger et al.).

## Methods

- **Study design:** Single-patient case report (compassionate use).
- **Patient:** 39-year-old Slovakian male with chronic pelvic pain syndrome/chronic bacterial prostatitis since October 2016.
- **Diagnosis:** Cultured ejaculate and expressed prostatic secretions repeatedly confirmed *E. faecalis* infection. MRI confirmed prostatitis. Whole exome sequencing (WES) identified MBL2 deficiency.
- **Endolysin production:** The ORF28 endolysin gene from *E. faecalis* phage ϕEf11 was cloned into a pGEX4T2 expression vector, expressed as a GST fusion protein in *E. coli* BL21/DE3, purified via glutathione resin affinity chromatography, cleaved with thrombin, and sterile-filtered. Final preparation: 0.8 mg/mL, electrophoretically homogeneous at 46.1 kDa.
- **Endolysin characterization:** The ORF28 endolysin possesses multifunctional muralytic activity — N-acetylmuramidase, N-acetylglucosaminidase, and endopeptidase — hydrolyzing *E. faecalis* cell wall peptidoglycan.
- **Phage cocktail (also tested):** Three newly isolated phages (vB_Efa_VP14, VP15, VP16) from genus *Efquatrovirus*, prepared at Science Park Bratislava. Each individually lysed the patient's strain; cocktail was active against 54% of 28 *E. faecalis* strains tested. Applied rectally at 10^9 PFU/mL, 10 mL twice daily for 2 weeks — no clinical effect observed.
- **Endolysin treatment protocol:** 1 mL endolysin diluted 1:5 in saline, 5 mL applied rectally every 12 hours for 7 days.
- **Outcome measures:** NIH Chronic Prostatitis Symptom Index (NIH-CPSI), bacteriological culture of ejaculate and expressed prostatic secretions, MRI of prostate.

## Detailed Summary

The patient presented in October 2016 with neuropathic perineal pain radiating to the scrotum and anogenital area, diagnosed as chronic pelvic pain syndrome. Urological examination revealed an enlarged, painful prostate; MRI confirmed prostatitis. Cultures of ejaculate and expressed prostatic secretions repeatedly grew *E. faecalis*. Initial NIH-CPSI total score was 25 (severe range: 19–31).

**Phase 1 — Treatable infections (2016–2020):** The patient experienced at least 3 exacerbations per year. Treatment with ampicillin/sulbactam or quinolones for 2 weeks typically achieved temporary clinical improvement and bacteriological negativity, but symptoms invariably recurred. Supplementary therapy with silodosin (4 mg/day), Serenoa repens, and quercetin (400 mg twice daily) produced no significant effect. In March 2019, a prolonged multi-antibiotic regimen (linezolid + ampicillin, then linezolid + fosfomycin, then fosfomycin monotherapy — 4 weeks total) achieved a 1-year remission, during which an enterococcal autovaccine was administered.

**Phase 2 — Resistant infections (2020–2022):** In June 2020, recurrence with an *E. faecalis* isolate now resistant to oxacillin, cefoxitin, gentamicin, and tetracycline. MIC values for ampicillin had increased from 1 to 2 mg/L. A commercial phage preparation (Sekstafag, 20 mL rectally twice daily) combined with ampicillin/sulbactam had no clinical effect. The personalized three-phage cocktail from Bratislava also failed despite demonstrated in vitro activity. The patient developed intolerance to quinolones (tachycardia, severe headaches) and severe allergic reactions (whole-body itching, exanthema) to both ampicillin/sulbactam and fosfomycin.

**WES findings (February 2021):** A heterozygous MBL2 variant (c.154C>T/p.Arg52Cys, rs5030737) was identified — classified as "pathogenic" in ClinVar for mannose-binding lectin deficiency. Serum MBL was 375 ng/mL (normal >2,880). This innate immune deficit likely predisposed the patient to recurrent urogenital bacterial invasion.

**Initial endolysin application (November 2021):** During a flare when antibiotics had to be discontinued due to allergic reactions, two doses of endolysin (5 mL of 1:5 dilution, rectally, 12 hours apart) produced symptom relief, but insufficient supply prevented a full course.

**Definitive endolysin treatment (July 2022):** With a full supply of endolysin, the patient received 7 days of treatment (5 mL of 1:5 dilution rectally every 12 hours). NIH-CPSI score dropped from 24 to 6. Post-treatment cultures were sterile (ejaculate) or showed only coagulase-negative staphylococci (prostatic secretion). No adverse reactions were reported. MRI in December 2022 showed 30% consolidation of inflammatory changes compared to September 2020. At the last check-up (February 9, 2023), the patient remained asymptomatic with NIH-CPSI of 6.

**Mechanism of delivery:** The rectal venous plexus communicates with the prostatic venous plexus via the vesical venous plexus, providing anatomic access for rectally applied endolysin. Animal studies confirm rectally applied bacteriophages reach the circulation within minutes.

## Relevance to WikiBiome

- **[[enterococcus-faecalis]]**: This case dramatically illustrates *E. faecalis* as a biofilm-forming pathogen with remarkable environmental hardiness (temperature, pH, osmolality, desiccation extremes) and multidrug resistance capacity. The species is either the first or second most frequently isolated organism from CBP cases. Its ability to form biofilms in prostatic acini and around prostatic calculi shields it from antibiotics — a key ecological concept for WikiBiome's biofilm pages.
- **[[antimicrobial-resistance]]**: The progressive acquisition of resistance during treatment (MIC escalation from AMP 1 to AMP 2, plus resistance to oxacillin, cefoxitin, gentamicin, tetracycline) provides a real-world illustration of the AMR problem.
- **Biofilm ecology**: *E. faecalis* biofilm in the prostate is a concrete example of how biofilm architecture protects pathogens from both antibiotics and host immune defenses, relevant to the [[biofilm]] concept page.
- **Endolysin as antimicrobial concept**: The ORF28 endolysin's triple muralytic activity (N-acetylmuramidase + N-acetylglucosaminidase + endopeptidase) and its ability to circumvent resistance mechanisms represents a novel antimicrobial modality worth documenting as a concept.

## Relevance to Cureva

- **Intervention hypothesis — phage endolysin therapy for biofilm-associated infections:** This case report provides preliminary (n=1) evidence that endolysin therapy can eradicate MDR *E. faecalis* biofilm infections when antibiotics, phage cocktails, probiotics, and autovaccines have all failed. This is directly relevant to the erectile dysfunction signature, as CBP with *E. faecalis* infection causes sexual dysfunction including erectile dysfunction and ejaculatory discomfort.
- **STOP signal — repeated antibiotic courses in biofilm-associated CBP:** The case illustrates that repeated antibiotic therapy drives resistance acquisition while failing to eradicate biofilm-protected organisms. The patient's progressive resistance pattern (ampicillin MIC escalation, new resistances to oxacillin, cefoxitin, gentamicin) and severe allergic reactions from cumulative antibiotic exposure are cautionary.
- **MBL2 deficiency as predisposing factor:** Whole exome sequencing revealing MBL2 deficiency (innate immune deficit) suggests that screening for immunological predispositions may guide treatment strategy in recurrent CBP/CPPS — a potential Cureva pipeline insight.
- **Phage cocktails ineffective in this case:** Despite convincing in vitro activity, the personalized phage cocktail failed clinically. This is a relevant data point for practitioners evaluating phage therapy vs. endolysin therapy.

## Contradictions / Tensions

- **Phage therapy vs. endolysin therapy:** Several prior case reports have demonstrated successful phage therapy for CBP (Letkiewicz et al. 2009, Johri et al. 2021), but in this case, phage cocktails with confirmed in vitro efficacy against the patient's strain were clinically ineffective. The authors note this discrepancy without fully explaining it. Possible factors include biofilm penetration differences between whole phages (which require host cell machinery for replication) and endolysins (which directly hydrolyze peptidoglycan).
- **CPPS nomenclature vs. bacterial etiology:** The paper cites evidence that 77% of "non-bacterial" CPPS cases actually show bacterial 16S rDNA on molecular testing, challenging the CBP/CPPS distinction. This is relevant to how WikiBiome categorizes and discusses chronic prostatitis.

## Open Questions

- **Reproducibility:** This is a single case report (n=1). Can the result be replicated across patients with different *E. faecalis* strains, different biofilm architectures, and different immune backgrounds?
- **Mechanism of endolysin delivery to prostate:** The rectal-to-prostatic venous plexus pathway is anatomically plausible but not directly demonstrated in this study. How much endolysin actually reaches the prostate tissue? What is the pharmacokinetic profile?
- **Biofilm penetration:** The ORF28 endolysin was previously shown to disrupt *E. faecalis* biofilms in vitro (Tinoco et al. 2016). Did the clinical success here depend on biofilm disruption, or primarily on planktonic cell lysis?
- **Resistance development:** Can *E. faecalis* develop resistance to the ORF28 endolysin? The authors' prior work showed that intrinsic resistance is associated with the presence of ϕEf11 prophage — what proportion of clinical *E. faecalis* strains carry this prophage?
- **MBL2 deficiency prevalence in CBP:** How common is MBL2 deficiency or other innate immune deficits among recurrent CBP patients? Could this be a useful screening biomarker?
- **Role of zinc in *E. faecalis* virulence:** *E. faecalis* possesses zinc-dependent metallopeptidases (gelatinase/GelE, serine protease/SprE) that are important virulence factors. This paper does not discuss the metal dependency angle, but future work connecting endolysin therapy to metal-dependent virulence would strengthen the WikiBiome knowledge graph.
