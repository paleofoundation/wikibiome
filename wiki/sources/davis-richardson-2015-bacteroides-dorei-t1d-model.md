## Key Findings

- **B. dorei peaks before autoimmunity**: In Finnish children who later developed T1D, **[[bacteroides]]** dorei dominated the gut microbiome and peaked at 7 months of age — preceding the appearance of islet autoantibodies (Davis-Richardson & Triplett 2015, expert opinion/review)
- **Immunoinhibitory LPS**: B. dorei produces a structurally distinct lipopolysaccharide that acts as a TLR4 antagonist, suppressing rather than activating innate immune responses. This prevents proper immune education during the critical infancy window
- **Geographic disparity explained**: Russian-Karelian children (Bifidobacterium-dominant microbiomes) have 6-fold lower T1D incidence than Finnish children (Bacteroides-dominant), despite shared genetic backgrounds and geography — implicating the microbiome as the differentiating factor
- **Two-step temporal model**: B. dorei's immunoinhibitory LPS prevents immune training in infancy (step 1), then subsequent dysbiosis before seroconversion triggers autoimmunity in an uneducated immune system (step 2)

## Relevance to WikiBiome

Central to the **[[bacteroides]]** entity page — B. dorei as an exemplar of paradoxical pathogenicity through immune suppression rather than activation. The Finland-Russia comparison is a compelling narrative for geographic variation in autoimmune disease.

## Relevance to Cureva

The immunoinhibitory LPS mechanism is a key component of the T1D signature's ecological layer. The two-step temporal model defines the intervention windows: early-life (before 7 months) and pre-seroconversion. Supports the rationale for early-life Bifidobacterium-promoting interventions.
