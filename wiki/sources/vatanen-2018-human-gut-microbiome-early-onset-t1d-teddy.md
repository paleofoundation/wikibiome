# Key Claims

- **T1D is an autoimmune disease targeting pancreatic beta cells** incorporating complex genetic/environmental factors
- **TEDDY study is the largest longitudinal gut microbiome study** of T1D to date with 10,913 metagenomes from ~783 mostly white, non-Hispanic children
- **Species and deficiency of bacteria producing SCFAs** found in cases of T1D or islet autoimmunity
- **Decreased levels of SCFAs** in breast-fed infants found only in adults with T1D (>18 months)
- **Short-chain fatty acids have protective effects** -- short-chain fatty acids in early life are important

# Methods & Findings

The Environmental Determinants of Diabetes in the Young (TEDDY) study:

- Stool samples collected monthly from 3 months through at least 3 years
- Multiple clinical centers (Colorado, Georgia/Florida, Washington, Finland, Germany, Sweden)
- Several thousand metagenomes analyzed for compositional and functional changes

# Relevance to Metallomic Framework

- **[[zinc]]**: SCFA-producing bacteria require zinc-dependent enzymes -- infant zinc status may determine protective SCFA capacity
- **[[iron]]**: Iron fortification timing in infant formula may disrupt SCFA-producing community establishment
- **[[selenium]]**: Selenium in breast milk provides antioxidant protection during critical microbiome development window
- **[[gut-metal-microbiome]]**: The TEDDY longitudinal design enables tracking of metal-microbiome co-development in early life
- **[[dysbiosis]]**: Early-life metal exposure patterns may explain geographic variation in T1D incidence across TEDDY centers
- **[[probiotics]]**: Early probiotic intervention combined with metal optimization could prevent T1D in genetically at-risk infants
