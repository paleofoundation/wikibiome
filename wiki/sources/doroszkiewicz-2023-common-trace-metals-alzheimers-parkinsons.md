# Key Claims

- Disruption of metal homeostasis is a common feature in both AD and PD, affecting disease onset and progression
- Iron, copper, zinc, manganese, selenium, and calcium all show altered distributions in neurodegenerative brain regions
- Metals may serve as potential biomarkers for early diagnosis and disease monitoring in AD and PD
- The interplay between essential and trace metals creates complex patterns of neurotoxicity that differ between AD and PD
- Metal-targeted therapeutic strategies must consider the dual nature of essential metals (necessary for function but toxic in excess)

# Methods/Findings

- **Design**: Review of common and trace metals in AD and PD pathophysiology (Int J Mol Sci, 2023)

 - Iron accumulation in substantia nigra (PD) and hippocampus/cortex (AD)
 - Ferroptosis as iron-dependent cell death mechanism in both diseases
 - Iron-catalyzed Fenton reactions generating hydroxyl radicals
 - Transferrin receptor and ferritin alterations in disease states
 - Iron chelation (deferiprone) showing some benefit in PD trials

 - Decreased cerebral copper in both PD and AD dementia regions
 - Copper-amyloid-beta interactions promoting toxic oligomer formation in AD
 - Ceruloplasmin dysfunction altering copper distribution
 - Wilson's disease as model of copper neurotoxicity

 - Zinc enrichment in amyloid plaques; zinc-induced amyloid-beta aggregation
 - Reduced serum and plasma zinc in PD patients (meta-analysis of 803 PD patients and 796 controls)
 - Zinc transporter dysfunction in AD brains
 - Zinc supplementation: potential benefits must be balanced against aggregation risk

 - Primarily associated with PD-like parkinsonism
 - Accumulates in globus pallidus and striatum
 - Manganese-induced oxidative stress in dopaminergic neurons
 - Occupational and environmental exposure routes

 - Antioxidant role via selenoproteins (glutathione peroxidases)
 - Selenium deficiency associated with increased neurodegeneration risk
 - Some studies suggest selenium supplementation may have neuroprotective effects

 - Calcium dysregulation central to excitotoxicity in neurodegeneration
 - Metal-calcium interactions (e.g., lead mimicking calcium) disrupt signaling
 - NMDA receptor overactivation and mitochondrial calcium overload
