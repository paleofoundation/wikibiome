# Key Claims

- A two-fold increase in blood [[lead]] associated with 2.60-fold increased odds of infertility (95% CI 1.05-6.41) after full adjustment
- A two-fold increase in blood [[cadmium]] associated with 1.84-fold increased odds of infertility (95% CI 1.07-3.15) after full adjustment
- Dose-response relationship: comparing highest vs lowest tertiles, lead OR = 5.62 (95% CI 1.13-27.90), cadmium OR = 2.47 (95% CI 0.6-10.17)
- Even low blood metal levels (geometric mean Pb = 0.50 ug/dL, Cd = 0.26 ug/L) are associated with infertility
- First population-based study examining blood lead/cadmium and self-reported infertility in US women

# Methods/Findings

- **Data**: NHANES 2013-2016, women aged 20-39; n = 124 (82 infertile, 42 pregnant)
- **Measurements**: Blood Pb and Cd by ICP-DRC-MS
- **Infertile group**: Mean Pb 0.55 ug/dL (age-adjusted 0.53), Cd 0.27 ug/L (age-adjusted 0.28)
- **Pregnant group**: Mean Pb 0.40 ug/dL, Cd 0.23 ug/L
- Significant increasing trend in infertility with increasing blood lead tertiles (p = 0.0145)
- Adjusted for age, ethnicity, income, education, marital status, smoking, alcohol, physical activity, BMI
- Small sample size is a key limitation

# Connections

- Demonstrates effects at levels well below current reference values for [[lead]] and [[cadmium]]
- Supports [[canipari-2020-female-fertility-environmental-pollution]] review on metal reproductive toxicity
- Lead may affect fertility via decreased gonadotropins, folliculogenesis disruption, luteal dysfunction
- Cadmium affects ovulation, steroidogenesis, pituitary function, and fertilization
