# Key Claims

- Environmental chemical exposures including [[heavy metals]], endocrine disrupting chemicals (EDCs), and nanoparticles can induce gut [[dysbiosis]] in animal models, favoring pathogenic species that produce virulence factors like lipopolysaccharide
- [[Arsenic Toxicity|Arsenic]] exposure (10 ppm) in mice for 4 weeks leads to distinct beta-diversity clustering, decreased Erysipelotrichaceae, and increased metabolomic alterations including indolelactic acid changes
- Arsenic-induced gut microbiome changes are sex-dependent and genotype-dependent (wild-type vs. IL10-/- mice show different patterns)
- [[Lead Toxicity|Lead]] exposure in mice decreases cultivable anaerobes, increases [[Lachnospiraceae]] loss, and disrupts metabolic pathways involving vitamin E, bile acids, and nitrogen metabolism
- [[Cadmium Toxicity|Cadmium]] exposure at environmentally relevant doses also alters gut microbiota in mice
- The gut-microbiome-brain axis represents a key pathway through which chemical-induced [[dysbiosis]] may lead to neurobehavioral disorders

# Methods/Findings

- **Study type**: Comprehensive review of animal studies on environmental chemicals and gut microbiota
- **Heavy metals and particulate matter studies reviewed**: Summarized in Table 1 covering 12+ publications
- **Arsenic findings**: 4 weeks of 10 ppm As exposure in C57BL/6 mice altered beta-diversity; sex-dependent changes in Akkermansia and Dorea; metabolic pathways including metal resistance, glutathione-regulated potassium efflux, and iron acquisition altered in females
- **Lead findings**: 8-week Pb exposure (100 or 500 ppm) in Balb/C mice -- decreased Lachnospiraceae, Ruminococcaceae, Oscillibacter; increased metabolic pathways for oxidative stress and defense/detoxification
- **Nanoparticle findings**: AgNPs show conflicting results across rodent studies; dose-dependent changes in Firmicutes/Bacteroidetes ratio
- **EDC findings**: BPA and EE exposure alter gut microbiota composition and are associated with metabolic disorders, inflammatory bowel disease
- **Key framework**: Triad relationship between environmental chemical exposure, host genetic/epigenetic/phenotypic background, and gut microbiome changes (Figure 1)
