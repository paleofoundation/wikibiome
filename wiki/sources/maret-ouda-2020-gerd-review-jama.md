# Gastroesophageal Reflux Disease: A Review

## Key Claims

- [[gerd]] affects approximately 20% of the adult population in high-income countries, defined by recurrent troublesome heartburn and regurgitation or GERD-specific complications
- Obesity, tobacco smoking, and genetic predisposition are established risk factors; H. pylori infection may decrease GERD risk through gastric atrophy reducing acid production
- A meta-analysis of 27 studies showed H. pylori eradication increases reflux esophagitis risk (relative risk 1.46, 95% CI 1.16-1.84)
- Up to 70% of patients with typical GERD symptoms have normal endoscopic findings; the Lyon Consensus (2018) defined conclusive evidence as LA grade C/D esophagitis, Barrett's esophagus, or peptic strictures
- PPIs are more effective than histamine receptor antagonists (H2RAs), which are more effective than placebo; the aim is the lowest effective dose
- Antireflux surgery had a decreased risk of esophageal adenocarcinoma (hazard ratio 0.64, 95% CI 0.52-0.78) in a study of 22,321 patients
- The prevalence of erosive esophagitis ranges from 30% to 70% of GERD patients; Barrett's esophagus has been estimated in 5.6% of US adults

## Methods/Findings

- **Study type**: Clinical review (JAMA Review)
- **Search**: PubMed and Cochrane databases (January 2015 - September 2020); 113 reports included (9 RCTs, 23 systematic reviews, 7 clinical practice guidelines)
- **Diagnostic algorithm**: PPI trial -> endoscopy -> esophageal manometry -> pH monitoring for non-responders
- **Treatment hierarchy**: Lifestyle modifications -> PPI standard dose 4-8 weeks -> dose titration -> twice-daily PPI -> antireflux surgery
- **PPI potency equivalents**: Pantoprazole 20mg = omeprazole 4.5mg equivalent; rabeprazole 20mg = omeprazole 36mg equivalent
- **Surgical options**: Laparoscopic Nissen fundoplication preferred; magnetic sphincter augmentation (MSA) emerging
