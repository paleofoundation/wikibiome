## Key Findings

- **Multi-compartment microbiome alterations** — across gut, vaginal, and peritoneal compartments — collectively contribute to the inflammatory tumor microenvironment in ovarian cancer ([[zhang-2024-ovarian-cancer-microbiome-inflammatory-microenvironment|this study]], expert-opinion).
- **Propionibacterium acnes** (now reclassified as Cutibacterium acnes) was identified as a potentially significant organism in the ovarian tumor microenvironment, capable of promoting chronic inflammation.
- **Upward migration of pathogens** from the lower genital tract to the upper genital tract and peritoneal cavity was proposed as a mechanism for microbial colonization of the ovarian tumor microenvironment.
- **Tumor-associated macrophage (TAM) polarization** is influenced by microbial products, with bacterial components driving M2-type immunosuppressive macrophage differentiation that supports tumor immune evasion.
- **[[Lactobacillus]] depletion** in the vaginal compartment was associated with increased pathogenic bacterial diversity and ascending infection risk.

## Methods

- **Study type**: Narrative review synthesizing evidence on the multi-compartment microbiome in ovarian cancer.
- **Scope**: Covers gut microbiome changes in OC, vaginal microbiome alterations, peritoneal/tumor-associated microbiome, and their collective contribution to the inflammatory tumor microenvironment.
- **Evidence base**: Integrates observational studies, 16S rRNA sequencing studies, and functional immunological data from OC cohorts and animal models.

## Detailed Summary

This review presents a comprehensive model of how microbiome alterations across multiple anatomical compartments converge to shape the inflammatory microenvironment of ovarian tumors. The multi-compartment approach is a strength — most microbiome-cancer studies focus on a single site (usually gut), but OC is uniquely positioned at the intersection of gut, vaginal, and peritoneal microbial ecosystems.

The ascending infection hypothesis proposes that bacteria from the vaginal microbiome migrate through the cervix, uterus, and fallopian tubes to reach the ovaries and peritoneal cavity. This pathway is supported by the detection of vaginal bacteria (including Lactobacillus, Gardnerella, and Prevotella species) in fallopian tube and ovarian tissue. When the vaginal microbiome shifts from a protective Lactobacillus-dominant state to a polymicrobial dysbiotic state, the ascending bacterial load increases, potentially contributing to chronic ovarian inflammation.

The role of Propionibacterium acnes is noteworthy. This organism, best known for its association with acne, is increasingly recognized as a potential driver of chronic inflammation in deep tissues. Its ability to persist intracellularly and evade immune clearance makes it a candidate for sustained inflammatory stimulation in the tumor microenvironment.

The TAM connection is clinically significant. Tumor-associated macrophages are major components of the OC immune microenvironment, and M2-polarized TAMs are associated with poor prognosis. The finding that microbial products influence TAM polarization provides a mechanistic link between dysbiosis and immune evasion in OC.

The iron connection operates through multiple pathways: (1) iron is essential for macrophage function and M1/M2 polarization; (2) iron-loaded TAMs can serve as iron donors to tumor cells; (3) microbial siderophores in the tumor microenvironment compete for iron, potentially altering the availability of iron for both immune cells and tumor cells.

## Relevance to Cureva

- Multi-compartment microbiome assessment may be necessary for comprehensive OC profiling — not just gut but also vaginal and potentially peritoneal.
- The TAM polarization mechanism identifies a target for microbiome-modulating immunotherapy.
- Vaginal Lactobacillus restoration could be part of a preventive strategy for OC in high-risk individuals.

## Open Questions

- Which specific microbial species or products are most potent at driving M2 TAM polarization in OC.
- Whether vaginal microbiome restoration (Lactobacillus-dominant) can reduce ascending infection and OC risk.
- How peritoneal iron availability influences the microbial community and TAM polarization in the OC microenvironment.
- Whether antibiotic or probiotic intervention targeting the ascending infection pathway can affect OC outcomes.
