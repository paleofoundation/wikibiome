# Fecal Bile Acid Dysmetabolism and Reduced Ursodeoxycholic Acid in Feline CKD

## Key Findings

- **CKD cats had significantly reduced fecal UDCA** (ursodeoxycholic acid) concentrations, indicating impaired microbial bile acid conversion in feline kidney disease.
- **Bile acid dysmetabolism** — defined as less than 50% secondary bile acids in fecal bile acid profiles — was present in 8 of 28 CKD cats (29%), demonstrating that a substantial minority of CKD cats have profoundly disrupted bile acid ecology.
- **Peptacetobacter hiranonis combined relative abundance was significantly reduced** in CKD cats with bile acid dysmetabolism, identifying this organism as the key microbial driver of impaired primary-to-secondary bile acid conversion.
- The **gut-kidney axis mediated through microbial-derived secondary bile acids** is relevant to [[chronic-kidney-disease]] pathophysiology — secondary bile acids regulate FXR and TGR5 signaling that affects renal inflammation and fibrosis.
- Oscillospirales order members were also differentially abundant in CKD cats, consistent with broader microbial community disruption.

## Methods

- **Design**: Prospective cross-sectional study with paired microbiome and bile acid metabolomics analysis.
- **Subjects**: 6 healthy cats and 28 CKD cats (staging per IRIS guidelines).
- **Microbiome**: 16S rRNA amplicon sequencing of fecal samples.
- **Metabolomics**: Targeted bile acid quantification in fecal samples using LC-MS/MS, measuring primary and secondary bile acid species.
- **Analysis**: Differential abundance, bile acid profile classification, correlation between microbial taxa and bile acid metabolism.

## Detailed Summary

This study leveraged the feline CKD model — cats naturally develop CKD with many parallels to human disease — to investigate the relationship between gut microbial bile acid metabolism and kidney disease. The central finding is that CKD disrupts the microbial conversion of primary to secondary bile acids, with P. hiranonis (the primary 7-alpha-dehydroxylating bacterium in cats) being depleted in affected animals. UDCA is of particular interest because it has well-documented cytoprotective, anti-inflammatory, and anti-fibrotic properties mediated through FXR and TGR5 receptor signaling. Its reduction in CKD cats may therefore remove a protective factor against renal inflammation and fibrosis. The 29% prevalence of bile acid dysmetabolism among CKD cats suggests this is a clinically significant subgroup that might benefit from targeted interventions (UDCA supplementation, P. hiranonis probiotics, or bile acid pool modulation). The translational relevance to human CKD is supported by parallel findings showing impaired secondary bile acid production in human kidney disease cohorts.

## Open Questions

- Whether UDCA supplementation improves CKD outcomes in cats or humans with documented bile acid dysmetabolism.
- How feline CKD bile acid profiles compare quantitatively with human CKD bile acid profiles.
- Whether P. hiranonis abundance could serve as a clinical biomarker for bile acid dysmetabolism risk in CKD.
