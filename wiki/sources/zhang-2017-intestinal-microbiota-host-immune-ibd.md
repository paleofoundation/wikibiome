# Key Claims

- The intestinal microbiota is a critical environmental factor influencing nutrient metabolism, immune responses, and host health in [[inflammation|inflammatory bowel disease]]
- [[dysbiosis]] in IBD is characterized by decreased [[faecalibacterium-prausnitzii]] (a butyrate-producing bacterium) and increased sulfate-reducing bacteria, leading to reduced SCFAs and toxic hydrogen sulfide production
- Adherent-invasive [[escherichia-coli]] (AIEC) is specifically associated with ileal Crohn's disease, capable of invading epithelial cells and replicating within macrophages
- [[bacteroides-fragilis]] polysaccharide A (PSA) can prevent colitis by suppressing IL-17 and enhancing IL-10 production, demonstrating commensal protective mechanisms
- Therapeutic strategies targeting the intestinal micro-ecosystem (antibiotics, [[probiotics]], FMT) show promise for IBD management

# Methods/Findings

- **Study type**: Narrative review of microbiota-immune crosstalk in IBD
- **Microbiota in IBD**: Reduced diversity; decreased Bacteroidetes and Firmicutes; increased Proteobacteria and Actinobacteria; reduced SCFA-producing bacteria
- **Immune mechanisms**: Epithelial barrier (mucus, IgA, antimicrobial peptides); NOD2 signaling; TLR activation; Th1/Th17 vs Treg balance (Figure 2)
- **Dysregulated interaction**: IBD patients show increased bacterial adherence to mucosal surface, decreased antimicrobial peptide production (defensins, REG3gamma), reduced IgA
- **Environmental factors**: Diet (high fat, sugar increase risk), stress (CRF signaling activates intestinal inflammation), antibiotics alter microbiota composition
- **Therapeutic approaches**: Antibiotics (metronidazole, ciprofloxacin effective in CD not UC); probiotics (VSL#3, Lactobacilli reduce disease activity in mice); FMT (reduced disease activity in CD, less effective in UC)
