# The Impact of Environmental Factors and Contaminants on Thyroid Function and Disease from Fetal to Adult Life

## Key Findings

- **1.1% annual increase in thyroid disorders**: Environmental factors including climate change, pollution, nutritional changes, and chemical exposure are contributing to a 1.1% annual increase in thyroid disorders and cancer ([[street-2024-environmental-factors-thyroid-function]], expert-opinion).
- **Heavy metals as untested thyroid carcinogens**: Heavy metals ([[arsenic]], [[cadmium]], [[chromium]], mercury, [[nickel]], [[lead]]) have never been systematically tested as potential human thyroid carcinogens; the dose and duration of exposure that might be harmful are not well defined ([[street-2024-environmental-factors-thyroid-function]], expert-opinion).
- **PM2.5 and thyroid cancer**: PM2.5 exposure associated with 18% increased PTC odds per 5 ug/m3 increase over 24 months, rising to 23% after 36 months. A study of 550,000 patients in China found industrial waste gas emissions (particulate matter, sulfur dioxide, nitrogen dioxide) associated with increased thyroid cancer ([[street-2024-environmental-factors-thyroid-function]], expert-opinion).
- **EDC thyroid disruption**: Endocrine-disrupting chemicals including bisphenols, phthalates, perchlorate, and PFAS can mimic or disrupt thyroid hormone synthesis, release, and action. BPA, phthalates, perchlorate, and thiocyanate decrease thyroidal iodine uptake by inhibiting NIS ([[street-2024-environmental-factors-thyroid-function]], expert-opinion).
- **Volcanic areas and TC**: Living in volcanic areas is a risk factor for thyroid cancer due to heavy metal contamination and increased radon exposure. Higher concentrations of heavy metals are found in these regions ([[street-2024-environmental-factors-thyroid-function]], expert-opinion).

## Methods

- **Study type**: Narrative review with literature map (published June 19, 2024; 15 pages with Figure 1 literature network, Figure 2 EDC mechanisms, Table 1 EDC effects summary)
- **Search strategy**: PubMed, Scopus, Mendeley via MeSH keywords; Cochrane Library and WHO/European bibliographic sources; original articles including animal and in vitro studies, reviews, and epidemiological studies from last 10 years; expanded for scarce topics
- **Scope**: Environmental pollutants (climate change, air pollution, nitrates), EDCs (bisphenols, phthalates, perchlorate, PFAS, organophosphates, phytoestrogens), heavy metals, diet/nutrition, food pollution, microplastics, infectious diseases (COVID-19)

## Detailed Summary

**Climate and thyroid function**: Rising temperatures disrupt thyroid's hormonal balance. TSH and fT3 increase during autumn-winter; decrease during spring-summer. Nitrate contamination from climate-driven agricultural changes affects thyroid function. School children exposed to high nitrate levels (75 mg/L) had higher goiter and thyroid disorder risk vs. low-exposure groups (8 mg/L) ([[street-2024-environmental-factors-thyroid-function]], expert-opinion).

**Heavy metals**: Links between thyroid volume and Cr, Se, Zn in hair samples of children documented. Pb and Mn may interfere with thyroid gland directly or by influencing iodine intake. Exposure to combination of heavy metals accounts for toxicity more than single-metal exposure. Carcinogenic mechanisms involve [[oxidative-stress]], DNA damage, interference with DNA repair, deregulated growth control, and DNA methylation changes ([[street-2024-environmental-factors-thyroid-function]], expert-opinion).

**EDCs** (Table 1, Figure 2): Comprehensive mapping of bisphenol effects on thyroid across developmental windows (prenatal through adolescence). BPA increases thyroid volume in 9-11 year olds. Phthalates associated with altered TSH and thyroid hormone levels across multiple studies. PFAS reduce circulating thyroid hormone levels. Triclosan prenatal exposure increases TT4 cord levels and TPOAb at 3 years. Phytoestrogens influence thyroid hormones in adolescents ([[street-2024-environmental-factors-thyroid-function]], expert-opinion).

**Diet and microplastics**: High consumption of animal fats increases thyroid autoantibody production. Ultra-processed food consumption associated with higher subclinical thyroid dysfunction risk. Nano- and microplastics in meat and fish disrupt epithelial barrier. Mercury-contaminated seafood bioaccumulates in thyroid cells ([[street-2024-environmental-factors-thyroid-function]], expert-opinion).

**COVID-19 and thyroid**: SARS-CoV-2 can attack thyroid follicular cells via ACE2. Subacute thyroiditis, Graves disease, and silent thyroiditis reported post-infection. mRNA-based vaccines most frequently associated with adverse thyroid effects, followed by adenovirus-vectored vaccines ([[street-2024-environmental-factors-thyroid-function]], expert-opinion).

## Relevance to Cureva

- Heavy metal exposure assessment should be part of thyroid disease workup — intervention begins with exposure reduction
- The dose-response data for PM2.5 (18% per 5 ug/m3 at 24 months, 23% at 36 months) provides actionable thresholds for environmental risk assessment
- EDC avoidance as a primary prevention strategy for thyroid disease — dietary and lifestyle modification interventions
- The combination-metal toxicity concept suggests chelation or metal restriction strategies should target multiple metals simultaneously

## Contradictions / Tensions

- The review acknowledges that heavy metals have "never been systematically tested as potential human thyroid carcinogens" — the evidence base is predominantly observational and mechanistic
- PM2.5 studies show conflicting results: one US study found negative association with thyroid cancer, contradicting the positive associations found in other studies
- The EDC literature is largely focused on prenatal/childhood exposure with limited adult data

## Open Questions

- Whether heavy metal testing should be standard in thyroid disease workup
- The specific threshold doses for individual metals that trigger thyroid dysfunction
- Whether microplastic exposure is a significant contributor to the rising thyroid disease incidence
- Long-term thyroid effects of COVID-19 vaccination vs. infection
