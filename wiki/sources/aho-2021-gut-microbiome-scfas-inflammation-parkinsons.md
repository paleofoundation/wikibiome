# Relationships of Gut Microbiota, Short-Chain Fatty Acids, Inflammation, and the Gut Barrier in Parkinson's Disease

## Summary

This study integrates gut [[microbiome]] composition, fecal [[short-chain-fatty-acids]], markers of intestinal inflammation (calprotectin), and gut barrier integrity (zonulin) in [[parkinsons-disease]] patients versus controls. It provides one of the most comprehensive multi-omic snapshots of the PD gut environment.

## Key Findings

- Fecal SCFA concentrations (butyrate, acetate, propionate) were reduced in PD patients
- Fecal calprotectin was elevated in PD, indicating intestinal inflammation
- Zonulin levels were increased in PD, indicating compromised gut barrier integrity
- SCFA levels correlated negatively with inflammatory markers and positively with gut barrier integrity
- *Faecalibacterium* and *Roseburia* abundance correlated with higher [[butyrate]] levels
- The pro-inflammatory gut environment in PD is linked to reduced SCFA production
- PD medications (levodopa, COMT inhibitors) influenced some microbiome-metabolite associations

## Relevance to Gut-Metal-Microbiome Axis

This study provides direct evidence linking reduced [[short-chain-fatty-acids]] to intestinal barrier breakdown in [[parkinsons-disease]], creating a pathway for systemic metal entry. Elevated zonulin indicates a "leaky gut" that would permit translocation of dietary metals ([[iron]], [[nickel]], [[lead]], [[manganese]]) and bacterial endotoxins into the systemic circulation. The inverse relationship between SCFAs and [[neuroinflammation]] markers supports the model where [[dysbiosis]]-driven butyrate depletion removes a key anti-inflammatory brake, permitting metal-catalyzed oxidative damage. The calprotectin elevation indicates neutrophil infiltration, which releases lactoferrin and other [[iron]]-binding proteins that could further alter local metal homeostasis in the gut.

## Limitations

- Cross-sectional design
- Fecal SCFAs may not reflect mucosal concentrations
- Relatively small cohort
- Medication effects difficult to fully disentangle
