## Key Claims

- [[nickel-allergy|systemic-nickel-allergy-syndrome]] (SNAS) affects approximately 20% of [[allergic contact dermatitis]] (ACD) patients and involves both Th1 and Th2 cytokine patterns
- SNAS is associated with lactose intolerance in 63-74% of patients, possibly due to nickel-induced brush border enzymatic impairment
- Nickel oral hyposensitization (NiOHT) at 1.5 ug Ni/week is effective and has proven to reduce symptoms and medication needs; clinical benefits maintained for at least 1 year
- The diagnosis of SNAS requires a positive [[nickel-allergy|oral nickel challenge]] after symptom improvement on a [[low-nickel-diet]]; the gold standard is the double-blind placebo-controlled oral nickel challenge

## Methods/Findings

- **Design**: Comprehensive book chapter review in Springer's "Metal Allergy" textbook
- **Prevalence data**: Nickel allergy prevalence ranges from 2.5% (Germany, 1966) to 17.6% (Norway, 2007); higher in women (17.1%) vs men (3%)
- **SNAS symptoms**: Cutaneous (ACD flare-up, widespread eczema, urticaria, angioedema) and gastrointestinal (meteorism, gastric acidity, abdominal colic, diarrhea, vomit, throat acidity)
- **Nickel metabolism**: Average daily intake ~200 ug; only 1-10% absorbed; serum concentrations 1.6-7 ug/L; urinary nickel most reliable biomarker; sweat nickel can be 7-270 ug/L (up to 20x plasma)
- **Immune response**: Both Th1 and Th2 involved; IL-5 most relevant increased cytokine; nickel challenge induces CD4+CD45RO+ cell infiltration in intestinal mucosa with decreased CD8+ cells
- **Nickel content in foods (ug/kg)**: 100 (carrots, lettuce, mushrooms), 200 (apricots, broccoli, corn, onions, peppers), 500 (artichoke, asparagus, beans, cauliflower, mussels, potatoes, peas, spinach, tomatoes), >500 (almonds, chickpeas, cocoa, lentils, oats, peanuts, walnuts)
- **Hyposensitization**: NiOHT validated in phase III trial (141 patients); 1.5 ug Ni/week gave best results; GI symptoms more responsive than cutaneous; mediated by IL-10 increase and T regulatory cells
