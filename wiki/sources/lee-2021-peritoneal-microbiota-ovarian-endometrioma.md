# Key Claims

- The microbiota composition of extracellular vesicles (EVs) in peritoneal fluid is significantly altered in women with advanced stage [[endometriosis]]
- *Acinetobacter*, *Pseudomonas*, *Streptococcus*, and *Enhydrobacter* were significantly increased in the endometriosis group
- *Propionibacterium*, *Actinomyces*, and *Rothia* were significantly decreased in endometriosis
- Significant beta diversity differences were found at order (p=0.005), family (p=0.003), and genus (p<0.001) levels
- Microbiota-derived EVs in peritoneal fluid may play a role in the pathogenesis and/or pathophysiology of [[endometriosis]]

# Methods/Findings

- **Study design**: Case-control study
- **Participants**: 45 women with histological ovarian endometrioma and 45 surgical controls (uterine leiomyoma or benign ovarian cysts)
- **Methods**: EV isolation from peritoneal fluid, 16S rDNA V3-V4 NGS sequencing

 - Alpha diversity showed no significant differences (Shannon p=0.12, Simpson p=0.14)
 - Beta diversity analysis (Bray-Curtis) showed significant differences at order, family, and genus levels
 - At the genus level, *Acinetobacter* was the most abundant taxon in both groups
 - The microbial composition of PF was similar between stage III and IV endometriosis

# Relevance to Wiki

This study is the first to analyze microbiota-derived EVs in peritoneal fluid of endometriosis patients, suggesting that gut microbiota-derived vesicles may traverse the intestinal barrier to directly affect the peritoneal environment. The enrichment of pathogenic bacteria like *Acinetobacter* and *Pseudomonas* in [[endometriosis]] supports the [[bacterial-contamination-hypothesis]] and highlights the role of [[dysbiosis]] in peritoneal inflammation and disease progression.
