# Case Report: Fecal Microbiota Transplantation via Capsules Ameliorated Clinical Outcomes in a Patient with Multiple Sclerosis

## Summary

This case report describes the first combined fecal microbiota transplantation (FMT) approach using both colonoscopy and oral capsules in a 42-year-old woman with secondary progressive MS (SPMS, EDSS 8.0). The patient had documented nickel sulfate and cobalt chloride intolerances associated with clinical symptoms following metal exposure.

## Key Findings

### Patient Background
- 42-year-old female with SPMS, disease onset 2009, wheelchair-restricted since 2018
- Failed multiple therapies: interferon beta-1A, natalizumab, teriflunomide, aubagio, methotrexate, simvastatin
- No immunosuppressive therapy for approximately 10 years
- **Documented intolerances to nickel sulfate and cobalt chloride** associated with clinical symptoms following metal exposure
- Reduced MTHFR enzymatic activity and positive HLA-DQ7 haplotype

### FMT Protocol
- Initial infusion via colonoscopy (200 mL donor microbiota solution)
- Followed by daily oral capsules (3/day) containing frozen microbiota for 3 months
- Clinical follow-up at 30, 60, and 90 days with 16S rRNA sequencing

### Clinical Outcomes
- **30 days**: initial clinical improvement, mild reduction in spasticity, increased standing stability
- **60 days**: regained fine motor skills in right hand, independent face washing and hair combing
- **90 days**: further improvement in GI symptoms, thermoregulation, food tolerance; physiotherapy mostly active mode
- Intestinal permeability improved (lactulose-mannitol test: 193 mg pre-FMT to <0.2 mg at 4 months)

### Microbiome Changes
- Baseline: increased Verrucomicrobiota (17%) and Actinomycetota (17%), reduced Bacteroidota (9%), elevated Bacillota (54%)
- *Akkermansia* reduced from 22.5% (baseline) to 6.6% (most favorable profile at T3, 90 days)
- *Collinsella* dropped from 9.8% to 2.5% then rose to ~15%
- *Blautia* fluctuated between 8-14%
- *Phocaeicola* appeared (0% at T0, peaked at 7.2% at T3)
- Unclassified Eubacteriales increased (1.5% to 10-15.3%)
- Patient's microbiome remained distinct from donor throughout

## Relevance to Gut-Metal-Microbiome Axis

This case report is **exceptionally relevant** to the [[gut-metal-microbiome]] hypothesis:

1. **The patient had documented [[nickel]] and cobalt intolerances** - directly linking metal sensitivity to her MS, supporting the hypothesis that metal exposure contributes to MS through gut-immune disruption
2. **FMT reduced Akkermansia** - the same taxon increased by [[cadmium]] exposure in mouse models and elevated in MS
3. **Improved gut permeability** post-FMT demonstrates that restoring the microbiome can repair the "leaky gut" that allows both metals and inflammatory mediators to access systemic circulation
4. **Both neurological and GI improvements** support the [[gut-brain-axis]] as a therapeutic target
5. **MTHFR enzymatic reduction** connects to impaired methylation and metal detoxification pathways

## Limitations

- Single patient case report; no controls
- Cannot distinguish FMT effects from concurrent physiotherapy
- Patient's microbiome did not converge with donor profile
- Short follow-up (90 days)
- SPMS is typically progressive; spontaneous improvement cannot be excluded
