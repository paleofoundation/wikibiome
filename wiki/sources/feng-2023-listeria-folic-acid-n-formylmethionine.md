# Key Claims

- **L. monocytogenes encodes two enzymes** (FolD and Fhs) that catalyze formation of N10-formyltetrahydrofolate, a critical intermediate in folate metabolism
- **The main role of L. monocytogenes folates** during infection is the generation of N-formylmethionine for bacterial protein synthesis
- **Mutants unable to make folates** had approximately 4-log10 less colony-forming units in livers compared to wild-type
- **Purine auxotrophic mutants** were only 1.5-log10 less virulent, and thymidine mutants were fully virulent
- Extends understanding of folic acid metabolism in pathogens with implications for antifolate drug development

# Methods & Findings

**Experimental microbiology study** comparing growth and virulence of L. monocytogenes folate pathway mutants.

- Created FolD, Fhs, and double mutants
- Compared virulence in mouse infection model
- Demonstrated N-formylmethionine generation as primary folate function during infection
- Purine and thymidine synthesis less critical for virulence than N-formylmethionine
- Results inform antifolate drug development strategies

# Relevance to ASD Framework

- **Listeria** is a TORCH-related pathogen; prenatal infection is an ASD risk factor
- **Folate competition** between host and pathogen relevant to maternal folate status and ASD risk
- **One-carbon metabolism** disruption by Listeria infection may impact fetal neurodevelopment
