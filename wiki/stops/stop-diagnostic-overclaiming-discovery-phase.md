intervention: "Using microbiome-based biomarkers for PDAC diagnosis or clinical decision-making"
conventional_rationale: "Emerging studies identify specific oral/gut bacterial and fungal signatures associated with PDAC, leading to enthusiasm for microbiome-based diagnostics."
why_counterproductive: "The current evidence base for PDAC microbiome signatures is almost entirely at the discovery phase — cross-sectional studies, small cohorts, and Mendelian randomization analyses that establish association but not diagnostic utility. The Meng 2025 JAMA paper (the most cited, identifying a 27-microbe oral panel) is a prospective discovery study, not a validated diagnostic tool. Key problems: (1) Tissue contamination during sequencing is a major confounder in intratumoral microbiome studies; (2) no microbiome-based PDAC biomarker has been validated in an independent prospective cohort with sufficient sensitivity/specificity to meet diagnostic thresholds; (3) the mycobiome data (Malassezia, Candida) is from very small cohorts; (4) reverse causation is plausible — PDAC itself may alter the microbiome, not the reverse. Practitioners who use microbiome signatures to screen for or rule out PDAC, or who treat suspected PDAC-associated dysbiosis as a diagnostic signal, risk both false reassurance and unnecessary intervention in healthy patients."

alternative: "Use microbiome signatures to inform mechanistic hypotheses and guide adjunctive intervention selection — not as diagnostic biomarkers. Standard PDAC screening (CA 19-9, imaging, EUS) remains the clinical standard. If a patient presents with dysbiosis consistent with the PDAC signature (Fusobacterium, Porphyromonas enrichment, SCFA producer depletion), treat the dysbiosis on its merits. Do not use dysbiosis presence or absence to make or exclude a PDAC diagnosis."
