conventional_rationale: "Obesity is paradoxically associated with iron deficiency anemia. Low serum iron, low hemoglobin, and elevated transferrin saturation are commonly observed, leading clinicians to prescribe oral iron supplementation."
why_counterproductive: "In obesity-associated dysbiosis, low serum iron almost always reflects **hepcidin-mediated functional anemia** — a deliberate host defense response to elevated Enterobacteriaceae burden — not true iron deficiency. The obesity microbiome signature shows elevated hepcidin, calprotectin, lipocalin-2, and transferrin alongside iron-siderophore-producing Enterobacteriaceae. In this environment, oral iron supplementation: (1) feeds the already-enriched Enterobacteriaceae and other iron-dependent pathobionts at the gut lumen level; (2) bypasses the host's nutritional immunity response; (3) drives increased LPS (lipopolysaccharide) production by iron-replete Gram-negative bacteria; (4) worsens systemic low-grade inflammation — the core driver of insulin resistance and metabolic dysfunction in obesity. The clinical outcome is a worsening of the underlying dysbiosis while transiently improving the hemoglobin marker that prompted treatment."

alternative: "Assess hepcidin before iron supplementation. Elevated hepcidin (>25 ng/mL) in the presence of anemia indicates functional anemia — treat the underlying dysbiosis, not the iron marker. Support host iron sequestration with [[lactoferrin]] (iron-binding glycoprotein that restricts luminal iron without systemic effects) rather than supplemental iron. If supplementation is clinically required (severe true deficiency confirmed by bone marrow stores, elevated sTfR, normal hepcidin), prefer intravenous iron to bypass the gut lumen."
