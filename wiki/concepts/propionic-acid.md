# Propionic Acid (PPA)

A three-carbon short-chain fatty acid (SCFA) produced primarily by **Bacteroidetes** and certain Firmicutes through succinate and acrylate pathways. At physiological concentrations, propionic acid is a normal fermentation product with beneficial metabolic effects. In excess, it becomes a **potent neurotoxin** -- and its overproduction is one of the most replicated findings in autism spectrum disorder (ASD) microbiome research.

## Production

- **Primary producers**: [[bacteroides-fragilis|Bacteroides]], [[bacteroides-vulgatus|*B. vulgatus*]], *Veillonella*, *Propionibacterium*, and *Megasphaera*.
- **Succinate pathway** (Bacteroidetes): Pyruvate -> oxaloacetate -> succinate -> methylmalonyl-CoA -> propionyl-CoA -> propionate.
- **Acrylate pathway** (some Firmicutes): Lactate -> acrylyl-CoA -> propionyl-CoA -> propionate.
- **Metabolic diversion**: When *Veillonella* and similar taxa are enriched, they divert lactate toward propionate rather than allowing it to be converted to [[butyrate]] by lactate-utilizing genera like [[anaerostipes]]. This shifts the SCFA ratio away from the neuroprotective butyrate and toward the potentially neurotoxic propionate.

## Neurotoxicity

**Intracerebroventricular PPA injection is an established animal model of ASD**, producing behavioral and neuropathological changes that mirror the human condition:

- **Brain morphological changes** -- Reactive gliosis, neuroinflammation, and white matter abnormalities.
- **Mitochondrial dysfunction** -- PPA inhibits the citric acid cycle by forming propionyl-CoA, which depletes free CoA and inhibits citrate synthase.
- **Neuroinflammation** -- PPA activates microglia and increases pro-inflammatory cytokines (TNF-alpha, IL-6) in brain tissue.
- **Oxidative stress** -- PPA increases lipid peroxidation and depletes glutathione in brain regions.
- **Behavioral effects** -- Repetitive behaviors, social interaction deficits, and restricted interests in rodent models.

## Role in Autism Spectrum Disorder

- **Elevated PPA** has been measured in stool and blood of children with ASD compared to neurotypical controls, correlating with symptom severity.
- **Bacteroidetes enrichment** in ASD microbiome profiles is consistent with increased propionate production capacity.
- The **propionic acid model** suggests a mechanism by which gut dysbiosis translates to neurological symptoms: enrichment of propionate-producing taxa -> excess PPA -> transport across the blood-brain barrier -> mitochondrial and neuroinflammatory damage.
- PPA also affects **epigenetic regulation** by inhibiting histone deacetylases (HDACs), potentially altering gene expression in developing neural tissue.

## Dual Nature

- At **physiological levels**, propionate has beneficial effects: hepatic gluconeogenesis regulation, appetite suppression (via GLP-1 and PYY release), and modest anti-inflammatory activity in the gut.
- The pathology emerges from **excess** -- driven by dysbiotic enrichment of propionate-producing taxa, dietary substrate availability (refined carbohydrates favor Bacteroidetes), or impaired hepatic clearance.
- This dual nature makes propionate a poor therapeutic target in isolation; the goal is restoring the SCFA ratio (butyrate:propionate:acetate) rather than eliminating propionate entirely.
