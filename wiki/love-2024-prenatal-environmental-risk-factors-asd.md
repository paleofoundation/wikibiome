# Love et al. 2024 -- Prenatal Environmental Risk Factors for ASD and Their Potential Mechanisms

## Key Claims

- The interplay between genetic predisposition and modern environmental exposures is likely driving a true increase in ASD incidence beyond diagnostic expansion
- Prenatal exposures are critical: infection during pregnancy, gestational diabetes/obesity, maternal SSRI use, antibiotics, and exposure to toxicants all increase ASD risk
- Underlying mechanisms converge on inflammation, mitochondrial dysfunction, [[oxidative-stress]], hormonal disruptions, and gut microbiome alterations
- Heavy metals and persistent organic pollutants accumulate during critical prenatal periods, leading to neurotoxic effects through inflammatory and oxidative pathways
- A meta-analysis of 4 studies found heavy metals with OR 1.5 to 2 for ASD association, though evidence remains limited

## Methods/Findings

- **Type**: Narrative review (13 pages)
- **Scope**: Assesses modifiable prenatal environmental risk factors for ASD with focus on potential mechanisms
- **Key risk factor evidence (Table 1)**:
  - Maternal infection: Inflammation, mitochondrial dysfunction, altered gut permeability
  - Maternal obesity/GDM: Proinflammatory cytokines, altered metabolic hormones, gut microbiome changes
  - SSRI use: Altered serotonin production in offspring, gut microbiome changes
  - Antibiotic use: Increased intestinal permeability, altered maternal LPS translocation
  - Toxicant exposure: Offspring IL-1beta elevation, altered maternal TLR-4 expression
- **Toxicant/metal evidence**:
  - Accumulation of persistent organic and heavy metal pollutants during critical prenatal/early postnatal periods causes neurotoxic effects
  - Stronger associations in males for diesel, [[lead]], manganese, [[mercury]], and methylene chloride (OR 1.5-2)
  - Evidence primarily epidemiological; few preclinical studies on mechanisms
  - Associations may be confounded by geographical and detection heterogeneity
- **Gut microbiome mechanisms**:
  - Maternal obesity/GDM alter gut microbiome composition
  - Altered maternal intestinal permeability leads to LPS translocation
  - Offspring microbiome changes associated with ASD behaviors in animal models
  - *Bifidobacterium*, *Faecalibacterium*, *Bacteroides*, *Clostridium* implicated
- **Oxidative stress**: Multiple pathways converge -- maternal infection, obesity, SSRI use, and toxicant exposure all induce oxidative stress in offspring
