# Tonelli et al. 2023 -- Oral Microbiome in CVD Pathophysiology

## Key Claims

- Validated links exist between oral dysbiosis and CVDs including [[atherosclerosis]], [[coronary artery disease]], stroke, peripheral artery disease, [[heart failure]], infective endocarditis, and rheumatic heart disease
- Oral dysbiosis contributes to CVD through five mechanisms: (a) transmigration of bacteria into systemic circulation, (b) cytokine release/chronic inflammation, (c) molecular mimicry of host proteins, (d) toxin release via leaky periodontium, (e) abnormal lipid profile via LPS-mediated effects
- Periodontitis is associated with a 1.25-fold increased risk of CAD and 2.46-fold increased risk of cerebrovascular stroke; patients with periodontal disease have a 25% increased risk of fatal or nonfatal MI
- Viridans group streptococci (VGS) and *Staphylococcus* spp. are the most common bacterial causes of infective endocarditis (IE), found in 50-80% of positive blood cultures
- Acute rheumatic fever (ARF) is a delayed autoimmune response triggered by Group A beta-hemolytic Streptococcus (GAS) pharyngeal infection; rheumatic heart disease (RHD) affects nearly 40.5 million people globally

## Methods/Findings

- **Type**: Comprehensive review
- **Oral microbiome in CVD-specific conditions**:
  - Atherosclerosis: *P. gingivalis* detected in atherosclerotic plaques; LPS from Gram-negative bacteria activates TLR4-dependent macrophage activation and NF-kappaB pathway
  - Heart failure: chronic low-level inflammation -> cytokine seeding into vasculature -> cardiac fibroblast activation; LPS burden induces TLR4-dependent systemic inflammation -> decompensation
  - IE: VGS express adhesin B binding to platelets, facilitating PAAP-alpha2beta1 integrin interaction -> platelet activation -> fibrin deposition on heart valve endothelium
  - Dental biofilm: *Streptococcus*, *Granulicatella*, *Actinomyces*, *Solobacterium*, *Rothia*, *Gemella*, and *TM7(G-1)* most frequently detected in distal ileum
- **Prevention strategies**: Dental scaling, oral hygiene practices, potential antibiotic prophylaxis for high-risk procedures; oral microbiome monitoring as a potential CVD risk assessment tool
