Bacteroides Thetaiotaomicron

Bacteroides thetaiotaomicron is one of the most abundant and metabolically versatile commensals of the human gut. It is a Gram-negative obligate anaerobe in the family Bacteroidaceae and a member of the dominant Bacteroides genus. It plays a central role in polysaccharide fermentation, mucosal layer maintenance, and symbiotic host-microbe signaling.

Metal Dependencies

B. thetaiotaomicron expresses multiple zinc-dependent enzymes, including zinc metalloproteases involved in polysaccharide cleavage and protein processing. It also requires iron for electron transport. These metal dependencies make it sensitive to changes in luminal metal availability — conditions of zinc depletion or iron restriction may impair its fermentative function while favoring iron-siderophore-equipped competitors.

Key Functions

  • Polysaccharide utilization loci (PULs) — encodes one of the largest repertoires of carbohydrate-active enzymes (CAZymes) in the human gut; degrades complex plant polysaccharides that host enzymes cannot process
  • Mucus layer education — induces appropriate fucosylation of host intestinal mucins, contributing to barrier integrity
  • Host immune calibration — early colonizer that helps train innate immune tolerance; germ-free mice colonized with B. thetaiotaomicron show normalized gut architecture and immune responses
  • SCFA production — ferments polysaccharides to propionate and acetate

Ecological Role

B. thetaiotaomicron is a keystone commensal whose decline is consistently observed in dysbiotic signatures associated with metal-driven ecological disruption. Its depletion is noted across inflammatory bowel disease, colorectal cancer, and other conditions where Enterobacteriaceae outcompete the Bacteroidetes community. The loss of its polysaccharide-fermenting function reduces SCFA output and mucosal support.
